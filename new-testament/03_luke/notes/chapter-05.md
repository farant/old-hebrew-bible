# Luke Chapter 5 -- Translation Notes

## Versification
All 39 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 5:1 -- "stagnum Genesareth" vs. "λίμνην Γεννησαρέτ"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses "stagnum" (lake/pool) and the Greek uses λίμνην (lake). The Hebrew has יָם-גִּנֵּיסָר (Sea of Gennesaret), using יָם (sea) rather than a word for lake.
- **Vulgate**: stabat secus stagnum Genesareth
- **Greek**: ἦν ἑστὼς παρὰ τὴν λίμνην Γεννησαρέτ
- **Hebrew**: וְהוּא עֹמֵד עַל-יַד יָם-גִּנֵּיסָר
- **Note**: The Hebrew's use of יָם (sea) follows the convention of the parallel passages in Matthew and Mark (which use θάλασσα / mare), not Luke's specific word choice of λίμνη/stagnum (lake). Luke deliberately distinguishes the body of water as a lake, not a sea. The Hebrew harmonizes away this Lukan distinctiveness.

### 5:17 -- "virtus Domini erat ad sanandum eos" vs. "εἰς τὸ ἰᾶσθαι αὐτόν"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "virtus Domini erat ad sanandum eos" (the power of the Lord was to heal them, plural). The SBLGNT has δύναμις κυρίου ἦν εἰς τὸ ἰᾶσθαι αὐτόν (the power of the Lord was for him to heal, singular, referring to Jesus). The Hebrew has וַתְּהִי-בוֹ גְּבוּרַת יְהוָֹה לִרְפּוֹא (the power of the LORD was in him to heal).
- **Vulgate**: et virtus Domini erat ad sanandum eos
- **Greek**: καὶ δύναμις κυρίου ἦν εἰς τὸ ἰᾶσθαι αὐτόν
- **Hebrew**: וַתְּהִי-בוֹ גְּבוּרַת יְהוָֹה לִרְפּוֹא
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek (singular, "in him") rather than the Vulgate (plural, "them"). This is a meaningful difference: the Vulgate says God's power was present to heal the crowd, while the Greek/Hebrew say God's power was upon Jesus himself to heal. The Vulgate reading should be followed.

### 5:20 -- "Homo" vs. "Ἄνθρωπε"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "Homo, remittuntur tibi peccata tua" (Man, your sins are forgiven you). The Greek has Ἄνθρωπε (Man). The Hebrew has בֶּן-אָדָם (son of man), which is a different expression entirely.
- **Vulgate**: Homo, remittuntur tibi peccata tua
- **Greek**: Ἄνθρωπε, ἀφέωνταί σοι αἱ ἁμαρτίαι σου
- **Hebrew**: בֶּן-אָדָם נִסְלְחוּ-לְךָ חַטֹּאתֶיךָ
- **Note**: The Hebrew בֶּן-אָדָם (son of man) is a common form of address in Ezekiel (used over 90 times by God addressing the prophet). However, in this context it creates potential confusion with the Christological title "Son of Man" used in 5:24. The Vulgate's simple "Homo" and the Greek Ἄνθρωπε are straightforward addresses meaning "Man" or "Friend."

### 5:38 -- "et utraque conservantur"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "et utraque conservantur" (and both are preserved). The SBLGNT omits this clause. The Hebrew includes וּשְׁנֵיהֶם יַחְדָּו יִשָּׁמֵרוּ (and both together shall be preserved), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: sed vinum novum in utres novos mittendum est, et utraque conservantur.
- **Greek**: ἀλλὰ οἶνον νέον εἰς ἀσκοὺς καινοὺς βλητέον.
- **Hebrew**: אֲבָל יֻתַּן הַיַּיִן הֶחָדָשׁ בְּנֹאדוֹת חֲדָשִׁים וּשְׁנֵיהֶם יַחְדָּו יִשָּׁמֵרוּ
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly includes the clause present in the Vulgate but absent from the SBLGNT.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 5:5 -- מוֹרֶה for "Praeceptor"
- **Hebrew**: מוֹרֶה כָּל-הַלַּיְלָה יָגַעְנוּ
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "Praeceptor" (Master/Teacher) and the Greek has Ἐπιστάτα (Master/Chief, a title unique to Luke). The Hebrew מוֹרֶה (teacher) is good biblical Hebrew (Prov 5:13, Isa 30:20). Luke uses Ἐπιστάτα rather than the more common ῥαββί or διδάσκαλε, but the Hebrew's מוֹרֶה is a reasonable rendering.

### 5:8 -- פֶּטְרוֹס for "Petrus"
- **Hebrew**: שִׁמְעוֹן פֶּטְרוֹס
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the Greek transliteration פֶּטְרוֹס rather than translating the name to its Hebrew equivalent כֵּיפָא (Cephas/Rock). This is consistent throughout the text. Since Luke always uses Πέτρος, not Κηφᾶς, the transliteration is defensible, though כֵּיפָא would be more natural in a Hebrew text.

### 5:12 -- Leprosy vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ כֻּלּוֹ צָרוּעַ... אֲדֹנִי אִם-תַּחְפֹּץ תּוּכַל לְטַהֲרֵנִי
- **Note**: The vocabulary is excellent biblical Hebrew. צָרוּעַ (leprous) echoes the Levitical laws of Lev 13-14. The verb טָהֵר (to cleanse/purify) is the standard Levitical term for ritual purification from leprosy (Lev 14:7). The leper's address אֲדֹנִי (my lord) is appropriate and deferential.

### 5:19 -- הָרְעָפִים for "tegulas"
- **Hebrew**: וַיּוֹרִידֻהוּ עַל-עַרְשׂוֹ בֵּין הָרְעָפִים
- **Note**: רְעָפִים (tiles/rafters) is a rare biblical word. It appears in the plural construct in Song 1:17 (רַהִיטֵנוּ). The choice reflects the Lukan detail that the roof had tiles (κεράμων/tegulas), which is architecturally Greek rather than Palestinian (Mark's parallel has them digging through the roof). The Hebrew handles this Hellenistic architectural detail as well as possible.

### 5:24 -- בֶן-הָאָדָם for "Filius hominis"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי בֶן-הָאָדָם יֶשׁ-לוֹ הַשָּׁלְטָן בָּאָרֶץ לִסְלֹחַ לַחֲטָאִים
- **Note**: The Christological title בֶּן-הָאָדָם (Son of Man) directly echoes Dan 7:13 (כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ, like a son of man), though the Danielic form uses Aramaic. The claim of authority (שָׁלְטָן) to forgive sins on earth is rendered with the same Aramaic-influenced שָׁלְטָן (authority/dominion) that appears in Daniel (Dan 7:14).

### 5:34 -- בְּנֵי הַחֻפָּה for "filios sponsi"
- **Hebrew**: הֲתוּכְלוּ אֲנֹס בְּנֵי הַחֻפָּה לָצוּם
- **Note**: בְּנֵי הַחֻפָּה (sons of the wedding canopy) is a Semitic idiom well attested in rabbinic literature for wedding guests. The Greek υἱοὺς τοῦ νυμφῶνος (sons of the bridal chamber) is itself a Semitism. The Hebrew idiom is natural and appropriate.

### 5:36-39 -- Parable vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: מַטְלִית (patch), בָּלוּי (worn out), נֹאדוֹת (wineskins)
- **Note**: The vocabulary is solidly biblical. נֹאדוֹת (wineskins) appears in Josh 9:4, 13 (the Gibeonite deception). The agricultural and domestic imagery translates naturally into biblical Hebrew.

## Other Notes

### 5:8 -- Peter's confession of sinfulness
Peter's words צֵא-נָא מֵעָלַי כִּי-אִישׁ חוֹטֵא אָנֹכִי (depart from me, for I am a sinful man) echo the reaction of Isaiah before the divine presence in Isa 6:5 (אוֹי-לִי כִי-נִדְמֵיתִי כִּי אִישׁ טְמֵא-שְׂפָתַיִם אָנֹכִי). The use of אָנֹכִי (the emphatic first-person pronoun) heightens the confessional register.

### 5:14 -- Levitical procedure
The instructions to the healed leper -- הֵרָאֵה אֶל-הַכֹּהֵן וְהַקְרֵב קָרְבָּן עַל-טָהֳרָתְךָ כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּה משֶׁה -- follow the procedure of Lev 14:1-32 precisely. The vocabulary (כֹּהֵן, קָרְבָּן, טָהֳרָה, צִוָּה) is pure Levitical Hebrew.

### 5:27-32 -- The call of Levi
The Hebrew renders "publicanus/τελώνης" as מוֹכֵס (tax collector), which is a Mishnaic term. Biblical Hebrew has no exact equivalent for the Greco-Roman tax-farming system. The term מוֹכֵס is attested in later Hebrew and well understood.
