# Luke Chapter 3 -- Translation Notes

## Versification
All 38 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 3:2 -- "sub principibus sacerdotum" vs. "ἐπὶ ἀρχιερέως"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "sub principibus sacerdotum Anna et Caipha" (under the high priests Annas and Caiaphas), using the plural "principibus." The Greek has the singular ἐπὶ ἀρχιερέως (under the high priest), though naming two men. The Hebrew has הַכֹּהֲנִים הַגְּדוֹלִים (the great priests, plural).
- **Vulgate**: sub principibus sacerdotum Anna et Caipha
- **Greek**: ἐπὶ ἀρχιερέως Ἅννα καὶ Καϊάφα
- **Hebrew**: בִּימֵי הַכֹּהֲנִים הַגְּדוֹלִים חָנָן וְקַיָּפָא
- **Note**: The Hebrew plural aligns with the Vulgate's plural. The Hebrew adds בִּימֵי (in the days of) which is not in either the Vulgate ("sub") or the Greek ("ἐπί"), slightly changing the temporal framing.

### 3:4 -- "Dominum" / "κυρίου" / "יְהוָֹה"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "Parate viam Domini" and the Greek Ἑτοιμάσατε τὴν ὁδὸν κυρίου both render Isaiah 40:3. The Hebrew correctly restores the Tetragrammaton פַּנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ יְהוָֹה, matching the original Isaiah text.
- **Note**: This is appropriate since Luke is quoting the OT directly. The Hebrew's restoration of the Tetragrammaton follows established practice for OT quotations.

### 3:4-6 -- Isaiah 40:3-5 quotation extent
- **Issue**: Luke quotes Isa 40:3-5 more extensively than Matthew or Mark. The Vulgate includes the full quotation through "et videbit omnis caro salutare Dei" (3:6). All three sources agree on the extent.
- **Note**: The Hebrew renders the Isaiah quotation using language very close to the Masoretic text of Isaiah 40, which is appropriate and expected.

### 3:19 -- "uxore fratris sui" and the identity of Philip
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "de Herodiade uxore fratris sui" (concerning Herodias, the wife of his brother), without naming the brother. The Greek has τῆς γυναικὸς τοῦ ἀδελφοῦ αὐτοῦ (the wife of his brother), also without naming him. The Hebrew adds פִּילִפּוֹס (Philip), naming the brother.
- **Vulgate**: de Herodiade uxore fratris sui
- **Greek**: περὶ Ἡρῳδιάδος τῆς γυναικὸς τοῦ ἀδελφοῦ αὐτοῦ
- **Hebrew**: עַל-דְּבַר הוֹרוֹדְיָה אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו פִּילִפּוֹס
- **Note**: The addition of "Philip" in the Hebrew is not in the Vulgate or SBLGNT for this verse. It likely comes from harmonization with Mark 6:17. The name should not be present if following the Vulgate strictly.

### 3:22 -- "in te complacui mihi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "Tu es filius meus dilectus, in te complacui mihi" (You are my beloved son, in you I am well pleased). The Greek has Σὺ εἶ ὁ υἱός μου ὁ ἀγαπητός, ἐν σοὶ εὐδόκησα. The Hebrew has אַתָּה בְּנִי יְדִידִי בְּךָ רָצִיתִי. All three agree substantively.
- **Note**: The Hebrew יְדִידִי (my beloved) echoes the term used for Solomon in 2 Sam 12:25 (ידיד יהוה, beloved of the LORD). The verb רָצִיתִי (I was pleased) echoes Ps 44:4 and the language of divine favor throughout the Psalms.

### 3:33 -- Genealogical variants
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "qui fuit Aminadab, qui fuit Aram" while the SBLGNT has τοῦ Ἀμιναδὰβ τοῦ Ἀδμὶν τοῦ Ἀρνὶ, with two names (Admin and Arni) between Amminadab and Hezron. The Hebrew has only בֶּן עַמִּינָדָב בֶּן-אֲרָם (son of Amminadab, son of Aram), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: qui fuit Aminadab, qui fuit Aram, qui fuit Esron
- **Greek**: τοῦ Ἀμιναδὰβ τοῦ Ἀδμὶν τοῦ Ἀρνὶ τοῦ Ἑσρὼμ
- **Hebrew**: בֶּן עַמִּינָדָב בֶּן-אֲרָם בֶּן-חֶצְרוֹן
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate reading here, omitting the extra names Admin and Arni found in the SBLGNT. The name אֲרָם (Aram) matches the OT genealogy in Ruth 4:19 and 1 Chr 2:9-10.

### 3:36 -- Cainan
- **Issue**: The Vulgate, Greek, and Hebrew all include Cainan (קֵינָן) between Arphaxad and Shelah. This name is present in the LXX of Gen 11:12-13 but absent from the Masoretic text of Genesis.
- **Note**: The inclusion of Cainan follows the LXX genealogy. The Hebrew's inclusion of this name is noteworthy since it departs from the Masoretic Genesis genealogy in favor of the Septuagint reading, which is appropriate for a text that Luke composed using the LXX.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:1 -- Political terminology
- **Hebrew**: שַׂר-רֹבַע (tetrarch), הֶגְמוֹן (governor)
- **Note**: שַׂר-רֹבַע literally means "ruler of a quarter," which is a precise calque of the Greek τετραάρχης (ruler of a fourth). This is a post-biblical coinage, unavoidable for a Greco-Roman political term. הֶגְמוֹן is a direct Greek loanword (ἡγεμών), used already in the Mishnah.

### 3:5-6 -- Isaiah 40:4-5 rendering
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-גֶּיא יִנָּשֵׂא וְכָל-הַר וְגִבְעָה יִשְׁפָּלוּ וְהָיָה הֶעָקֹב לְמִישׁוֹר וְהָרְכָסִים לְבִקְעָה
- **Note**: The Hebrew closely follows the Masoretic text of Isa 40:4 rather than translating from the Greek or Latin. This is excellent practice for OT quotations. The vocabulary (גֶּיא, עָקֹב, מִישׁוֹר, רְכָסִים, בִּקְעָה) is taken directly from Isaiah.

### 3:7 -- יַלְדֵי הַצִּפְעוֹנִים for "genimina viperarum"
- **Hebrew**: יַלְדֵי הַצִּפְעוֹנִים
- **Note**: צִפְעוֹנִי (viper) appears in Isa 11:8 and 59:5. The phrase as a whole echoes prophetic invective language. Biblical Hebrew more commonly uses construct chains with בְּנֵי (sons of) for this kind of epithet, but יַלְדֵי (children of) effectively renders γεννήματα (offspring).

### 3:8 -- Stones and sons wordplay
- **Hebrew**: מִן-הָאֲבָנִים הָאֵלֶּה... בָּנִים
- **Note**: The famous Greek wordplay between λίθων (stones) and τέκνα (children) does not have an exact equivalent in Hebrew. However, the Hebrew אֲבָנִים (stones) / בָּנִים (sons) preserves a partial phonetic echo (abanim/banim), which is actually closer than the Greek wordplay. This is a fortunate coincidence of biblical Hebrew.

### 3:17 -- Agricultural vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: הַמִּזְרֶה (winnowing fork), גָּרְנוֹ (threshing floor), דְּגָנוֹ (grain), אוֹצָרוֹ (storehouse), הַמֹּץ (chaff)
- **Note**: Every term here is solidly biblical. מִזְרֶה appears in Isa 30:24 and Jer 15:7. The entire verse reads like prophetic Hebrew, perfectly suited to John the Baptist's oracular style.

### 3:23-38 -- Genealogy format
- **Hebrew**: בֶּן-עֵלִי... בֶּן-מַתָּת... בֶּן-לֵוִי
- **Note**: The repeated בֶּן (son of) construction follows standard biblical genealogical form (cf. Gen 5, 1 Chr 1-9). The Hebrew names are consistently rendered according to their OT equivalents where applicable (שְׁאַלְתִּיאֵל, זְרֻבָּבֶל, דָּוִד, etc.).

## Other Notes

### 3:4-6 -- Significance of the extended Isaiah quotation
Luke alone among the Synoptics extends the Isaiah 40 quotation through verse 5, culminating in "all flesh shall see the salvation of God." The Hebrew renders this as וְרָאוּ כָל-בָּשָׂר אֵת יְשׁוּעַת אֱלֹהִים, where יְשׁוּעַת is phonetically close to the name יֵשׁוּעַ (Jesus). This connection, present in the Hebrew but not the Greek or Latin, reinforces the etymological link between Jesus' name and salvation.

### 3:21-22 -- Baptism and Trinitarian theophany
The baptism narrative presents all three Persons: Jesus is baptized, the Spirit descends, and the Father speaks. The Hebrew renders the Spirit's descent as רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ (the Holy Spirit) and the voice's declaration with בְּנִי יְדִידִי (my beloved son). The phrase בִּדְמוּת גּוּף (in bodily form) for "corporali specie" / σωματικῷ εἴδει is precise biblical Hebrew; דְּמוּת (likeness) is the same word used in Gen 1:26 for the creation of man in God's likeness.
