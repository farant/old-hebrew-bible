# Luke Chapter 4 -- Translation Notes

## Versification
All 44 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 4:4 -- "sed in omni verbo Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "non in solo pane vivit homo, sed in omni verbo Dei" (man does not live by bread alone, but by every word of God). The SBLGNT has only Οὐκ ἐπʼ ἄρτῳ μόνῳ ζήσεται ὁ ἄνθρωπος, omitting "but by every word of God." The Hebrew includes the full clause in brackets: [כִּי עַל-כָּל-מוֹצָא פִי-יְהוָֹה].
- **Vulgate**: Scriptum est : Quia non in solo pane vivit homo, sed in omni verbo Dei.
- **Greek**: Γέγραπται ὅτι Οὐκ ἐπʼ ἄρτῳ μόνῳ ζήσεται ὁ ἄνθρωπος.
- **Hebrew**: הֵן כָּתוּב כִּי לֹא עַל-הַלֶּחֶם לְבַדּוֹ יִחְיֶה הָאָדָם [כִּי עַל-כָּל-מוֹצָא פִי-יְהוָֹה]
- **Note**: The bracketed Hebrew phrase is a direct quotation from Deut 8:3 (כִּי עַל-כָּל-מוֹצָא פִי-יְהוָה יִחְיֶה הָאָדָם). The Vulgate includes this completion; the SBLGNT does not. The brackets correctly signal the textual uncertainty. Following the Vulgate, the phrase should be retained.

### 4:5 -- Order of temptations
- **Issue**: In Luke, the Vulgate presents the temptations in the order: stones to bread (4:3-4), kingdoms from a mountain (4:5-7), pinnacle of the temple (4:9-12). This matches the Greek. Matthew's order differs (mountain second, pinnacle third vs. pinnacle second, mountain third in Matthew). All three sources in Luke agree on this order.
- **Note**: No divergence among the three sources for Luke's order.

### 4:8 -- "Vade retro Satana" / "סוּר מִמֶּנִּי הַשָׂטָן"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "Scriptum est: Dominum Deum tuum adorabis" without "Vade retro Satana" (Get behind me, Satan) in this verse. The SBLGNT also omits it. The Hebrew includes סוּר מִמֶּנִּי הַשָׂטָן (depart from me, Satan) in brackets.
- **Vulgate**: Scriptum est : Dominum Deum tuum adorabis, et illi soli servies.
- **Greek**: Γέγραπται· Κύριον τὸν θεόν σου προσκυνήσεις καὶ αὐτῷ μόνῳ λατρεύσεις.
- **Hebrew**: [סוּר מִמֶּנִּי הַשָׂטָן כִּי] כָתוּב לַיְהוָֹה אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה וְאֹתוֹ לְבַדּוֹ תַּעֲבֹד
- **Note**: The bracketed phrase in Hebrew comes from harmonization with Matt 4:10. Neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT includes it in Luke. Following the Vulgate, it should be omitted.

### 4:18-19 -- Isaiah 61:1-2 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate distributes the Isaiah quotation across two verses and includes "sanare contritos corde" (to heal the brokenhearted). The SBLGNT omits "to heal the brokenhearted" (ἰάσασθαι τοὺς συντετριμμένους τῇ καρδίᾳ). The Hebrew includes לַחֲבשׁ לְנִשְׁבְּרֵי-לֵב (to bind up the brokenhearted), drawn directly from Isa 61:1 MT.
- **Vulgate**: sanare contritos corde, praedicare captivis remissionem, et caecis visum
- **Greek**: ἀπέσταλκέν με κηρύξαι αἰχμαλώτοις ἄφεσιν καὶ τυφλοῖς ἀνάβλεψιν (no "heal the brokenhearted")
- **Hebrew**: שְׁלָחַנִי לַחֲבשׁ לְנִשְׁבְּרֵי-לֵב לִקְרֹא לִשְׁבוּיִם דְּרוֹר
- **Note**: The Hebrew includes the phrase, aligning with the Vulgate against the SBLGNT. The Hebrew here follows the Masoretic text of Isa 61:1 very closely, which is appropriate. The word חֹבֵשׁ (binding/healing) rather than רָפָא (healing) follows the Isaiah original.

### 4:41 -- "Quia tu es Filius Dei" and "Christum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has the demons say "tu es Filius Dei" (you are the Son of God) and notes they knew "ipsum esse Christum" (that he was Christ). The SBLGNT reads Σὺ εἶ ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ, matching. The Hebrew inserts [הַמָּשִׁיחַ] (the Messiah) in brackets within the demons' speech.
- **Vulgate**: Quia tu es Filius Dei... quia sciebant ipsum esse Christum.
- **Greek**: Σὺ εἶ ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ θεοῦ... ὅτι ᾔδεισαν τὸν χριστὸν αὐτὸν εἶναι.
- **Hebrew**: אַתָּה הוּא [הַמָּשִׁיחַ] בֶּן-הָאֱלֹהִים... כִּי יָדְעוּ אֲשֶׁר הוּא הַמָּשִׁיחַ
- **Note**: The brackets around הַמָּשִׁיחַ in the demons' speech indicate a textual variant. The Vulgate distinguishes between what the demons said ("Son of God") and what they knew ("Christ"). Some Greek manuscripts conflate these, adding "Christ" to the demons' direct speech. The Hebrew's brackets correctly flag this.

### 4:44 -- "in synagogis Galilaeae" vs. "τῆς Ἰουδαίας"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "in synagogis Galilaeae" (in the synagogues of Galilee). The SBLGNT has εἰς τὰς συναγωγὰς τῆς Ἰουδαίας (in the synagogues of Judea). The Hebrew has בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת שֶׁבַּגָּלִיל (in the synagogues of the Galilee), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Et erat praedicans in synagogis Galilaeae.
- **Greek**: καὶ ἦν κηρύσσων εἰς τὰς συναγωγὰς τῆς Ἰουδαίας.
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי קוֹרֵא בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת שֶׁבַּגָּלִיל
- **Note**: This is a significant geographical divergence. "Galilee" (Vulgate, Hebrew) vs. "Judea" (SBLGNT). The context strongly favors "Galilee" since Jesus is in Capernaum and the Galilee region throughout this chapter. The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 4:1-2 -- הַשָׂטָן for "diabolus"
- **Hebrew**: וַיְנַסֵּהוּ הַשָׂטָן אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם
- **Note**: Delitzsch consistently renders διάβολος/diabolus as הַשָׂטָן (the Satan/Adversary), the standard biblical Hebrew term (Job 1-2, Zech 3:1-2, 1 Chr 21:1). This is preferable to a transliteration and maintains the OT resonance.

### 4:8 -- Deuteronomy 6:13 quotation
- **Hebrew**: לַיְהוָֹה אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה וְאֹתוֹ לְבַדּוֹ תַּעֲבֹד
- **Note**: Closely follows the Masoretic text of Deut 6:13 (אֶת-יְהוָה אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּירָא וְאֹתוֹ תַעֲבֹד). The Hebrew changes תִּירָא (you shall fear) to תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה (you shall worship), following the Greek/Latin, and adds לְבַדּוֹ (alone), also following the NT sources rather than the MT of Deuteronomy. This is appropriate since Jesus' quotation of the OT here slightly modifies the original.

### 4:18-19 -- Isaiah 61 in Hebrew
- **Hebrew**: רוּחַ אֲדֹנָי יֱהוֹה עָלָי יַעַן מָשַׁח אֹתִי לְבַשֵׂר עֲנָוִים
- **Note**: The opening line is virtually identical to Isa 61:1 MT. The use of אֲדֹנָי יֱהוֹה (Lord GOD) preserves the double divine name of the Isaiah source. The entire passage reads as pure biblical Hebrew because it essentially is -- Delitzsch wisely follows the Masoretic text of Isaiah rather than back-translating from Greek.

### 4:20 -- הַחַזָּן for "ministro"
- **Hebrew**: וַיְשִׁיבֵהוּ אֶל-הַחַזָּן
- **Note**: חַזָּן (attendant/overseer) is a post-biblical term that became standard for the synagogue attendant. It appears in Mishnaic Hebrew (m.Sotah 7:7-8). Biblical Hebrew has no exact equivalent for this role, so the choice is justified.

### 4:32 -- כִּי דְּבָר שִׁלְטוֹן דְּבָרוֹ
- **Hebrew**: כִּי דְּבָר שִׁלְטוֹן דְּבָרוֹ
- **Note**: The phrase is somewhat awkward. The Vulgate has "in potestate erat sermo ipsius" (his word was with authority) and the Greek ἐν ἐξουσίᾳ ἦν ὁ λόγος αὐτοῦ. The Hebrew construction "a word of authority was his word" is redundant with the double use of דָּבָר. A cleaner rendering might be כִּי בְשָׁלְטָן הָיָה דְבָרוֹ.

## Other Notes

### 4:16-21 -- The Nazareth synagogue reading
This is a pivotal passage where Jesus reads from Isaiah and declares its fulfillment. The Hebrew uses בֵּית הַכְּנֵסֶת (house of assembly) for synagoga, which is the standard rabbinic term. The verb גָּלַל (rolled) for "plicuisset" (folded) at 4:20 correctly reflects the use of a scroll rather than a codex.

### 4:25-27 -- Elijah and Elisha typology
The Hebrew renders the Elijah/Elisha references using the standard biblical forms: אֵלִיָּהוּ (Elijah), אֱלִישָׁע (Elisha), צָרְפַתָה (Zarephath, cf. 1 Kgs 17:9), נַעֲמָן הָאֲרַמִּי (Naaman the Syrian/Aramean, cf. 2 Kgs 5:1). The designation הָאֲרַמִּי (the Aramean) rather than "Syrus" reflects the biblical terminology for the region. All proper nouns correctly match their OT forms.
