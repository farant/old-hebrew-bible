# Luke Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 62 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 9:1 — "Apostolis" in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Convocatis autem duodecim Apostolis" (having called together the twelve Apostles). The SBLGNT simply has τοὺς δώδεκα (the twelve). The Hebrew has שְׁנֵים הֶעָשָׂר (the twelve), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Convocatis autem duodecim Apostolis
- **Greek**: Συγκαλεσάμενος δὲ τοὺς δώδεκα
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּקְרָא אֶל-שְׁנֵים הֶעָשָׂר
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Apostolis" is a clarifying addition. The Hebrew omits it, following the Greek. To follow the Vulgate strictly, the Hebrew could include שְׁלִיחִים.

### 9:7 — Bracketed text
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes [עַל-יָדוֹ] in brackets, meaning "by his hand / through him." The Vulgate reads "quæ fiebant ab eo" (which were done by him). The SBLGNT lacks any equivalent.
- **Vulgate**: omnia quæ fiebant ab eo
- **Greek**: τὰ γινόμενα πάντα (all that was happening — no "by him")
- **Hebrew**: אֶת-כָּל-אֲשֶׁר נַעֲשָׂה [עַל-יָדוֹ]
- **Note**: The brackets in the Hebrew indicate this is a variant reading. The Vulgate "ab eo" supports including it; the SBLGNT omits it. The Hebrew brackets appropriately mark the uncertainty.

### 9:35 — "dilectus" vs. "electus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Hic est Filius meus dilectus" (This is my beloved Son). The SBLGNT has ὁ ἐκλελεγμένος (the chosen one). The Hebrew has יְדִידִי (my beloved), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Hic est Filius meus dilectus, ipsum audite
- **Greek**: Οὗτός ἐστιν ὁ υἱός μου ὁ ἐκλελεγμένος, αὐτοῦ ἀκούετε
- **Hebrew**: זֶה-בְּנִי יְדִידִי אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן
- **Note**: This is a significant textual variant. The SBLGNT "chosen" (ἐκλελεγμένος) is the harder reading, supported by early manuscripts. The Vulgate "dilectus" (beloved) harmonizes with the Matthean/Markan parallels and the baptism account. The Hebrew יְדִידִי (my beloved) correctly follows the Vulgate. The word echoes Isa 5:1 (שִׁירַת דּוֹדִי, the song of my beloved) and the naming of Solomon as Jedidiah (2 Sam 12:25).

### 9:42-43 — Vulgate verse division differs from Greek
- **Issue**: The Vulgate places the content about Jesus rebuking the spirit and healing the boy at 9:43 ("Et increpavit Jesus spiritum immundum, et sanavit puerum"), while the SBLGNT has this at 9:42. The Hebrew follows the SBLGNT versification here, placing this material in verse 42.
- **Note**: The content is identical; only the verse boundary differs.

### 9:54-56 — Bracketed expansions
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes extensive bracketed material in 9:54-56. Verse 54 has [כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אֵלִיָּהוּ] (as Elijah did). Verse 55 has [וַיֹּאמַר הֲלֹא יְדַעְתֶּם בְּנֵי רוּחוֹ שֶׁל מִי אַתֶּם] (and he said, do you not know whose spirit's sons you are). Verse 56 has [כִּי בֶן-הָאָדָם לֹא בָא לְאַבֵּד נַפְשׁוֹת אָדָם כִּי אִם-לְהוֹשִׁיעַ] (for the Son of Man did not come to destroy souls but to save).
- **Vulgate**: The Vulgate includes the 9:55-56 expansions: "Nescitis cujus spiritus estis. Filius hominis non venit animas perdere, sed salvare."
- **Greek**: The SBLGNT omits all three expansions.
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly brackets these as textually uncertain but includes them, which matches the Vulgate. The Vulgate attestation supports their inclusion in our text.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:20 — Petrine Confession
- **Hebrew**: מְשִׁיחַ הָאֱלֹהִים אָתָּה
- **Note**: The Hebrew מְשִׁיחַ הָאֱלֹהִים (the Anointed of God) is a faithful rendering of the Vulgate "Christum Dei." The word order places the predicate first for emphasis, which is good Hebrew style for a declaration.

### 9:22 — Passion prediction vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: צָרִיךְ בֶּן-הָאָדָם לֵעָנֹת הַרְבֵּה
- **Note**: צָרִיךְ (it is necessary) is post-biblical, deriving from Mishnaic Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would express this with a verbal construction or an impersonal verb. However, the Vulgate "oportet" (it is necessary) strongly motivates this choice.

### 9:33 — Sukkot allusion
- **Hebrew**: נַעֲשֶׂה-נָּא שָׁלשׁ סֻכּוֹת
- **Note**: The use of סֻכּוֹת (booths/tabernacles) rather than a generic word for shelter is excellent. It directly connects Peter's proposal to the Feast of Tabernacles (Lev 23:42-43), which is a traditional interpretive association with the Transfiguration. The Vulgate "tabernacula" also carries this resonance.

### 9:51 — הֵעָלוֹתוֹ for "assumptionis"
- **Hebrew**: כַּאֲשֶׁר קָרְבוּ יְמֵי הֵעָלוֹתוֹ
- **Note**: הֵעָלוֹתוֹ (his being taken up) from the root עלה captures the Vulgate's "assumptionis" well. The root עלה is used for Elijah's ascent (2 Kings 2:11), providing an apt OT parallel.

### 9:58 — Foxes and birds
- **Hebrew**: לַשּׁוּעָלִים יֵשׁ מְאוּרוֹת וּלְעוֹף הַשָּׁמַיִם קִנִּים
- **Note**: מְאוּרוֹת (dens/holes) is biblical (1 Sam 14:11). קִנִּים (nests) is standard biblical vocabulary (Deut 22:6). The contrast with וּבֶן-הָאָדָם אֵין-לוֹ מָקוֹם לְהָנִיחַ רֹאשׁוֹ captures the pathos well in biblical idiom.

## Other Notes

### 9:23 — צְלוּבוֹ for "crucem"
The Hebrew צְלוּבוֹ (his cross) is derived from the root צלב, which is post-biblical for "to crucify" (the biblical root means "to hang" or "to impale" in a different sense). This is an unavoidable modernism since biblical Hebrew lacks vocabulary for Roman crucifixion.

### 9:28 — "fere dies octo" / "about eight days"
The Vulgate "fere dies octo" (about eight days) matches Luke's unique chronology, distinct from Mark/Matthew's "after six days." The Hebrew כִּשְׁמֹנָה יָמִים (about eight days) faithfully preserves this Lukan peculiarity.
