# Luke Chapter 21 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 38 verses align across all three sources. No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment
### 21:4 — "in munera Dei" / εἰς τὰ δῶρα
- **Issue**: The Vulgate specifies "in munera Dei" (into the offerings of God), while the Greek reads simply εἰς τὰ δῶρα (into the gifts/offerings). The Hebrew הִתְנַדְּבוּ לֵאלֹהִים (donated to God) follows the Vulgate in making the divine recipient explicit.
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate.

### 21:11 — "pestilentiæ et fames" vs. λιμοὶ καὶ λοιμοὶ
- **Issue**: A notable textual variant. The Vulgate reads "pestilentiæ, et fames" (plagues and famines). The Greek varies among manuscripts; SBLGNT has λιμοὶ καὶ λοιμοὶ (famines and plagues). The Hebrew וְרָעָב וָדָבֶר (famine and pestilence) gives both terms but in the Greek order (famine first).
- **Note**: Minor order difference only. Both elements are present in all three traditions.

### 21:19 — "patientia" / ὑπομονή
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "In patientia vestra possidebitis animas vestras" uses "patientia" (patience/endurance). The Greek ὑπομονή has the same range. The Hebrew בְּתוֹחַלְתְּכֶם (in your hope/expectation) uses תּוֹחֶלֶת, which means "hope" or "expectation" (cf. Proverbs 10:28, 11:7) rather than "patient endurance."
- **Vulgate**: In patientia vestra possidebitis animas vestras
- **Greek**: ἐν τῇ ὑπομονῇ ὑμῶν κτήσασθε τὰς ψυχὰς ὑμῶν
- **Hebrew**: בְּתוֹחַלְתְּכֶם קְנוּ לָכֶם אֵת נַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם
- **Note**: תּוֹחֶלֶת does not adequately render "patientia" or ὑπομονή. A better choice would be סַבְלָנוּת or the biblical phrase אֹרֶךְ רוּחַ (longsuffering).

### 21:24 — "tempora nationum" / καιροὶ ἐθνῶν
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "tempora nationum" and Greek καιροὶ ἐθνῶν (times of the nations/Gentiles) are rendered as עִתּוֹת הַגּוֹיִם in Hebrew. The word עִתּוֹת (times/seasons) is the biblical plural of עֵת, attested in 1 Chronicles 29:30, Esther 1:13.
- **Note**: Accurate rendering in good biblical Hebrew.

### 21:36 — "ut digni habeamini" / κατισχύσητε
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ut digni habeamini fugere" (that you may be counted worthy to escape). The Greek (SBLGNT) has κατισχύσητε ἐκφυγεῖν (that you may have strength to escape). The Hebrew תַּעַצְרוּ-כֹחַ (you may gather strength) follows the Greek reading.
- **Vulgate**: ut digni habeamini fugere
- **Greek**: ἵνα κατισχύσητε ἐκφυγεῖν
- **Hebrew**: לְמַעַן תַּעַצְרוּ-כֹחַ לְהִמָּלֵט
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "worthy" is a distinct theological concept from the Greek's "have strength." The Hebrew follows the Greek.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 21:1 — "gazophylacium" / γαζοφυλάκιον
- **Hebrew**: אֲרוֹן הָאוֹצָר (the treasury chest)
- **Note**: אֲרוֹן הָאוֹצָר is a natural Hebrew compound. While אֲרוֹן primarily denotes the Ark in biblical usage, the wider sense of "chest" (cf. 2 Kings 12:10-11 where the collection chest is described) makes this appropriate.

### 21:2 — Small coins
- **Hebrew**: שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת (two perutot)
- **Note**: פְּרוּטָה is a Mishnaic Hebrew coin term, not biblical. This is the smallest denomination in rabbinic coinage. It corresponds well to the Greek λεπτά (the smallest Greek coin) and the Vulgate's "æra minuta" (small copper coins). A necessary post-biblical term.

### 21:20 — "Desolation" / ἐρήμωσις
- **Hebrew**: חָרְבָּנָהּ (its desolation)
- **Note**: חָרְבָּן is post-biblical (common in Mishnaic Hebrew for the destruction of the Temple). The biblical equivalent would be שְׁמָמָה or חָרְבָה. However, חָרְבָּן became the standard Jewish term for the Temple's destruction, making it contextually resonant here.

## Other Notes
### 21:22 — "Dies ultionis"
- The Hebrew יְמֵי נָקָם (days of vengeance) is a direct echo of Isaiah 63:4 (יוֹם נָקָם בְּלִבִּי) and Deuteronomy 32:35 (לִי נָקָם). The Vulgate's "dies ultionis" and the Greek ἡμέραι ἐκδικήσεως are both well served by this prophetic Hebrew phrasing.

### 21:25-27 — Eschatological discourse
- The Hebrew of this section draws effectively on prophetic language: הֶמְיַת הַיָּם (the roaring of the sea, cf. Isaiah 17:12), חֲיָלֵי הַשָּׁמַיִם (the host of heaven, cf. Isaiah 34:4), and בֶעָנָן (in a cloud, cf. Daniel 7:13). Delitzsch's rendering roots the passage firmly in its Old Testament prophetic substrate.
