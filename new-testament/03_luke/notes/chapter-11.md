# Luke Chapter 11 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 54 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 11:2 — Lord's Prayer expansions
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes several bracketed expansions not in the SBLGNT. The Hebrew has [שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם] (who is in heaven) after "Our Father," and [יֵעָשֶׂה רְצוֹנְךָ כְּמוֹ בַשָּׁמַיִם כֵּן בָּאָרֶץ] (your will be done, as in heaven so on earth). The Vulgate in Luke omits both of these expansions, reading simply "Pater, sanctificetur nomen tuum. Adveniat regnum tuum."
- **Vulgate**: Pater, sanctificetur nomen tuum. Adveniat regnum tuum.
- **Greek**: Πάτερ, ἁγιασθήτω τὸ ὄνομά σου· ἐλθέτω ἡ βασιλεία σου
- **Hebrew**: אָבִינוּ [שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם] יִתְקַדַּשׁ שְׁמֶךָ תָּבוֹא מַלְכוּתֶךָ [יֵעָשֶׂה רְצוֹנְךָ כְּמוֹ בַשָּׁמַיִם כֵּן בָּאָרֶץ]
- **Note**: The Hebrew harmonizes with the Matthean version (Matt 6:9-10). The Vulgate of Luke does NOT include "who art in heaven" or "thy will be done," so these bracketed expansions are technically departures from the Vulgate. However, the brackets correctly mark them as additions. Also note: the Hebrew has אָבִינוּ (our Father) where the Vulgate has simply "Pater" (Father) without "our." The SBLGNT also reads simply Πάτερ.

### 11:3 — "quotidianum" vs. "supersubstantialem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Panem nostrum quotidianum da nobis hodie" (our daily bread, give us today). Note the Vulgate of Luke uses "quotidianum" (daily), while the Vulgate of Matthew uses "supersubstantialem" (super-substantial). The Greek ἐπιούσιον is the same in both.
- **Hebrew**: אֶת-לֶחֶם חֻקֵּנוּ תֶּן-לָנוּ יוֹם יוֹם
- **Note**: The Hebrew חֻקֵּנוּ (our allotted portion) captures the sense well. It echoes Prov 30:8 (הַטְרִיפֵנִי לֶחֶם חֻקִּי, feed me my allotted bread), which is likely the OT passage behind the petition. The construction יוֹם יוֹם (day by day) matches the Vulgate of Luke, which has the Greek τὸ καθʼ ἡμέραν (daily).

### 11:4 — Bracketed doxology
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes [כִּי אִם-הַצִּילֵנוּ מִן-הָרָע] (but deliver us from evil) in brackets. The Vulgate of Luke omits this clause entirely: "Et ne nos inducas in tentationem." The SBLGNT also omits it.
- **Vulgate**: Et ne nos inducas in tentationem. (ends here)
- **Hebrew**: וְאַל-תְּבִיאֵנוּ לִידֵי נִסָּיוֹן [כִּי אִם-הַצִּילֵנוּ מִן-הָרָע]
- **Note**: The bracketed clause harmonizes with the Matthean version. Since the Vulgate of Luke omits it, the brackets appropriately mark this as extraneous. The Hebrew correctly signals the textual uncertainty.

### 11:11 — "panem / lapidem" (bread / stone)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "patrem petit panem, numquid lapidem dabit illi?" (if he asks for bread, will he give a stone?). The SBLGNT lacks the bread/stone pair entirely, reading only about the fish/serpent. The Hebrew includes the bread/stone pair: יִשְׁאַל מִמֶּנּוּ בְּנוֹ לֶחֶם וְנָתַן-לוֹ אָבֶן.
- **Vulgate**: Quis autem ex vobis patrem petit panem, numquid lapidem dabit illi? aut piscem, numquid pro pisce serpentem dabit illi?
- **Greek**: τίνα δὲ ἐξ ὑμῶν τὸν πατέρα αἰτήσει ὁ υἱὸς ἰχθύν, καὶ ἀντὶ ἰχθύος ὄφιν αὐτῷ ἐπιδώσει
- **Hebrew**: וּמִי בָּכֶם הָאָב אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁאַל מִמֶּנּוּ בְּנוֹ לֶחֶם וְנָתַן-לוֹ אָבֶן וְאִם-דָּג הֲיִתֶּן-לוֹ נָחָשׁ
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate in including the bread/stone pair, which the SBLGNT omits. This is correct per our methodology.

### 11:13 — "spiritum bonum" vs. "Spiritum Sanctum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "spiritum bonum" (a good spirit). The SBLGNT reads πνεῦμα ἅγιον (Holy Spirit). The Hebrew has אֶת-רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ (the Holy Spirit), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: quanto magis Pater vester de caelo dabit spiritum bonum petentibus se
- **Greek**: πόσῳ μᾶλλον ὁ πατὴρ ὁ ἐξ οὐρανοῦ δώσει πνεῦμα ἅγιον τοῖς αἰτοῦσιν αὐτόν
- **Hebrew**: אַף-כִּי הָאָב מִן-הַשָּׁמַיִם יִתֵּן אֶת-רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ לַשֹּׁאֲלִים מֵאִתּוֹ
- **Note**: The Vulgate "spiritum bonum" (a good spirit) differs from the SBLGNT "Holy Spirit." To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should read רוּחַ טוֹבָה (a good spirit). This is a significant theological difference.

### 11:29 — "[הַנָּבִיא]" (the prophet)
- **Issue**: The Hebrew brackets [הַנָּבִיא] (the prophet) after Jonah. The Vulgate reads "Jonae prophetae" (Jonah the prophet). The SBLGNT simply has Ἰωνᾶ (Jonah).
- **Vulgate**: nisi signum Jonae prophetae
- **Hebrew**: בִּלְתִּי אִם-אוֹת יוֹנָה [הַנָּבִיא]
- **Note**: The Vulgate supports including "the prophet." The Hebrew brackets suggest awareness of the textual variant, but per our methodology the word should not be bracketed since the Vulgate includes it.

### 11:44 — Bracketed expansion
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes [הַסּוֹפְרִים וְהַפְּרוּשִׁים הַחֲנֵפִים] (the scribes and Pharisees, the hypocrites) in brackets. The Vulgate lacks this expansion, reading simply "Vae vobis" (woe to you). The SBLGNT also lacks it.
- **Vulgate**: Væ vobis, quia estis ut monumenta
- **Hebrew**: אוֹי לָכֶם [הַסּוֹפְרִים וְהַפְּרוּשִׁים הַחֲנֵפִים]
- **Note**: The bracketed phrase harmonizes with the Matthean woe oracles (Matt 23). Since neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT includes it here, the brackets appropriately mark it as extraneous.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 11:20 — "digito Dei" / "finger of God"
- **Hebrew**: בְּאֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: Outstanding. This is the unique Lukan reading (Matthew has "Spirit of God"). The phrase בְּאֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים directly echoes Exod 8:15 (אֶצְבַּע אֱלֹהִים הִוא), where the Egyptian magicians acknowledge God's power. This OT allusion is precisely what Luke intends, and the Hebrew captures it perfectly.

### 11:31 — Queen of the South
- **Hebrew**: מַלְכַּת תֵּימָן
- **Note**: מַלְכַּת תֵּימָן (Queen of the South/Yemen) is not the standard OT formulation. The OT has מַלְכַּת-שְׁבָא (Queen of Sheba, 1 Kings 10:1). Delitzsch uses תֵּימָן to match the Greek νότου / Vulgate "austri" (south) rather than the proper name Sheba. Both are defensible, but the OT reference would be more resonant.

### 11:42 — Tithing herbs
- **Hebrew**: הַמְעַשְּׂרִים אֶת-הַמִּנְתָּא וְאֶת-הַפִּיגַם וְאֵת כָּל-הַיָּרָק
- **Note**: מִנְתָּא (mint) is a loanword from Greek/Aramaic. פִּיגַם (rue) is likewise post-biblical. Both are unavoidable since these specific plants are not named in the Hebrew Bible. The verb מעשר (to tithe) is properly biblical (Deut 14:22).

### 11:51 — Abel to Zechariah
- **Hebrew**: מִדַּם-הֶבֶל עַד-דַּם זְכַרְיָהוּ אֲשֶׁר נֶהֱרַג בֵּין הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַבָּיִת
- **Note**: The range "from Abel to Zechariah" spans the Hebrew Bible from Genesis (Gen 4:8) to 2 Chronicles (2 Chr 24:20-21), which is the last book in the Hebrew canon order. The phrase בֵּין הַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלַבָּיִת (between the altar and the house/temple) matches the 2 Chronicles account. Excellent rendering.

## Other Notes

### 11:27-28 — Marian praise and response
The woman's beatitude on Mary's womb and breasts (אַשְׁרֵי הַבֶּטֶן... וְאַשְׁרֵי הַשָּׁדַיִם) and Jesus's response redirecting to those who hear God's word is faithfully preserved. The Hebrew אַשְׁרֵי construction echoes the Psalms and Proverbs beatitudes.
