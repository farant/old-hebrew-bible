# Luke Chapter 18 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 43 verses align across all three sources. No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment
### 18:11 — "apud se" / πρὸς ἑαυτὸν
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "hæc apud se orabat" (prayed these things within himself) and Greek πρὸς ἑαυτὸν ταῦτα προσηύχετο can mean either "prayed to himself" or "stood by himself and prayed." The Hebrew לְבַדּוֹ (by himself / alone) chooses the spatial reading: he stood alone and prayed. This is a valid interpretation but loses the potential reflexive sense (prayed to himself).
- **Note**: The Hebrew reading is defensible but the Vulgate's "apud se" more naturally means "within himself" (inwardly). Minor point.

### 18:12 — "sabbato" / τοῦ σαββάτου
- **Issue**: Both the Vulgate "bis in sabbato" and the Greek δὶς τοῦ σαββάτου literally mean "twice in the Sabbath/week." The Hebrew בַּשָּׁבוּעַ (in the week) is a correct contextual translation, as σάββατον here means "week" rather than the Sabbath day itself.
- **Note**: Good interpretive choice in the Hebrew.

### 18:20 — Commandment order
- **Issue**: The Vulgate lists commandments in this order: non occides, non mœchaberis, non furtum facies, non falsum testimonium dices, honora patrem. The Greek has μὴ μοιχεύσῃς first (do not commit adultery), then murder, theft, false witness, honor parents. The Hebrew follows the Greek order (לֹא תִנְאָף, לֹא תִרְצָח...), not the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: non occides ; non mœchaberis...
- **Greek**: Μὴ μοιχεύσῃς, Μὴ φονεύσῃς...
- **Hebrew**: לֹא תִנְאָף לֹא תִרְצָח...
- **Note**: The Vulgate places "thou shalt not kill" first. A Vulgate-following text should match the Vulgate order.

### 18:32 — "flagellabitur" / ἐμπτυσθήσεται
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "flagellabitur, et conspuetur" (will be scourged and spat upon). The Greek has ἐμπαιχθήσεται καὶ ὑβρισθήσεται καὶ ἐμπτυσθήσεται (mocked, insulted, spat upon). The Hebrew וְהֵתֵלּוּ בוֹ וְהִתְעַלְּלוּ וְיָרְקוּ בְּפָנָיו follows the Greek closely (mocked, abused, spat in his face). The Vulgate includes scourging here which the Greek defers to v. 33.
- **Note**: Minor sequencing difference. The Hebrew aligns with the Greek rather than the Vulgate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 18:2 — "Judge of iniquity"
- **Hebrew**: In 18:6, the unjust judge is rendered דַּיַּן הָעַוְלָה (judge of the injustice).
- **Note**: דַּיָּן is post-biblical but widely used in Mishnaic Hebrew. The biblical term would be שֹׁפֵט, which is used in v. 2 (שׁוֹפֵט). The shift from שׁוֹפֵט (v. 2) to דַּיַּן (v. 6) is notable; both are acceptable.

### 18:13 — "Propitius esto mihi" / ἱλάσθητί μοι
- **Hebrew**: אֱלֹהִים סְלַח-לִי (God, forgive me)
- **Note**: סְלַח is the standard biblical Hebrew for divine forgiveness (cf. Psalm 25:11, Numbers 14:19). The Greek ἱλάσθητι has the sense of "be propitiated / be merciful." The Vulgate "propitius esto" has a propitiation nuance that סְלַח-לִי does not fully convey. However, this is a natural Hebrew prayer form.

### 18:25 — Camel and needle
- **Hebrew**: לַגָּמָל עֲבֹר בְּתוֹךְ-נֶקֶב הַמַּחַט
- **Note**: מַחַט (needle) is not a biblical Hebrew word; it comes from Mishnaic Hebrew. The biblical text does not have an equivalent term. This is a necessary post-biblical borrowing given the subject matter.

## Other Notes
### 18:35 — Approaching vs. leaving Jericho
- The Vulgate says "cum appropinquaret Jericho" (as he approached Jericho). Mark 10:46 has the healing upon leaving Jericho. The Hebrew follows the Vulgate/Luke reading with בְּקָרְבוֹ אֶל-יְרִיחוֹ (as he drew near to Jericho).
