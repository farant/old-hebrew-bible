# Luke Chapter 22 — Translation Notes

## Versification
### 22:43-44 — The Agony in the Garden (bloody sweat)
- These verses are bracketed in the SBLGNT (⸂...⸃) indicating textual uncertainty in the Greek tradition. They are absent from some important Greek manuscripts (P75, Codex Vaticanus). However, they are part of the Vulgate and the Catholic canon, and the Hebrew provides text for both verses.
- **Note**: These verses must be retained. They are authoritative Vulgate text affirmed by the Council of Trent. The Hebrew text is adequate.

### 22:43 — Verse boundary with Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate combines material into v. 43 that the Greek splits: "Apparuit autem illi angelus de cælo, confortans eum. Et factus in agonia, prolixius orabat." The Greek places the agony and prolonged prayer in v. 44. The Hebrew splits the verses differently, placing the angel's appearance in v. 43 and the agony/sweat in v. 44, which follows the Greek division.
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek verse division rather than the Vulgate's.

## Content Alignment
### 22:34 — Peter's denial prediction
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "Et dixit eis:" (And he said to them:) at the end of v. 34, transitioning to the discourse on swords in v. 35. This narrative link is absent from the Greek. The Hebrew does not include this transition.
- **Note**: Minor narrative connector in the Vulgate.

### 22:35 — "Nihil" response placement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate places the disciples' response "Nihil" (Nothing) at the beginning of v. 36: "At illi dixerunt : Nihil. Dixit ergo eis..." The Greek places it at the end of v. 35: "οἱ δὲ εἶπαν· Οὐθενός." The Hebrew follows the Greek placement, including the answer in v. 35.
- **Note**: The Hebrew should be checked to ensure the verse boundary matches the Vulgate.

### 22:44 — Agony description
- **Issue**: The Hebrew renders ἐν ἀγωνίᾳ as חֶבְלֵי-מָוֶת (pangs of death), which is an interpretive expansion. The Vulgate simply says "factus in agonia" (being in agony), and the Greek uses ἀγωνίᾳ (agony/struggle). The Hebrew phrase echoes Psalm 18:5 / 2 Samuel 22:6.
- **Vulgate**: Et factus in agonia, prolixius orabat
- **Greek**: γενόμενος ἐν ἀγωνίᾳ ἐκτενέστερον προσηύχετο
- **Hebrew**: וְחֶבְלֵי-מָוֶת בָּאוּ עָלָיו וַיּוֹסֶף לְהִתְפַּלֵּל בְּחָזְקָה
- **Note**: "Pangs of death" is a stronger rendering than either source, but theologically resonant with the Psalms and fitting for the context.

### 22:66 — Merged verse
- **Issue**: The Hebrew merges the Sanhedrin gathering (v. 66 in Greek/Vulgate) with the question "If you are the Christ, tell us" (v. 67). The current Hebrew for v. 66 contains both the assembly and the question. This compresses two Vulgate verses into one.
- **Note**: The verse boundary should be verified for Vulgate alignment.

### 22:68 — "neque dimittetis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "neque dimittetis" (nor will you release me) at the end of v. 68. The Greek text lacks this addition. The Hebrew includes this: וְלֹא תְשַׁלְּחוּנִי (and you will not release me), matching the Vulgate.
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate on this addition.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 22:15 — "Desiderio desideravi"
- **Hebrew**: נִכְסֹף נִכְסַפְתִּי (I have greatly desired)
- **Note**: This is an excellent rendering using the biblical cognate accusative construction (infinitive absolute + finite verb), mirroring the Vulgate's "Desiderio desideravi" and Greek Ἐπιθυμίᾳ ἐπεθύμησα. The verb כָּסַף (to long for, desire) appears in Genesis 31:30, Psalm 84:3, and Job 14:15. The cognate intensification perfectly captures the emphatic construction.

### 22:19-20 — Institution narrative
- **Hebrew**: זֶה גוּפִי הַנִּתָּן בַּעַדְכֶם... הַבְּרִית הַחֲדָשָׁה בְּדָמִי
- **Note**: The Hebrew בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה (new covenant) directly echoes Jeremiah 31:31 (בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה). The term גּוּף (body) is post-biblical; the biblical Hebrew would be בָּשָׂר (flesh) or גְּוִיָּה (body/corpse). However, גּוּף became standard in Mishnaic Hebrew and is used here for σῶμα / "corpus."

### 22:31 — Sifting as wheat
- **Hebrew**: לִזְרוֹת אֶתְכֶם כַּחִטִּים (to winnow you like wheat)
- **Note**: The verb זָרָה (to winnow/scatter) is well attested in biblical Hebrew (Ruth 3:2, Isaiah 30:24, Jeremiah 4:11). The Greek σινιάσαι (to sift) and Vulgate "cribraret" (to sieve) are slightly different (sifting vs. winnowing), but the Hebrew image of winnowing is authentically agricultural and biblical.

### 22:53 — "Power of darkness"
- **Hebrew**: שִׁלְטוֹן הַחשֶׁךְ (dominion of darkness)
- **Note**: שִׁלְטוֹן is an Aramaic-influenced form (cf. Daniel 3:33 [Aramaic], Ecclesiastes 8:4, 8:8). It corresponds well to the Vulgate's "potestas tenebrarum" and the Greek ἐξουσία τοῦ σκότους.

## Other Notes
### 22:37 — Isaiah 53:12 citation
- The Hebrew וְאֶת-פּשְׁעִים נִמְנָה is a direct citation of Isaiah 53:12 (MT). Both the Vulgate "cum iniquis deputatus est" and the Greek μετὰ ἀνόμων ἐλογίσθη translate from this verse. The Hebrew correctly returns to the prophetic source text.

### 22:42 — Gethsemane prayer
- The Hebrew אָבִי (my Father) renders the Greek Πάτερ and Vulgate "Pater" in the intimate address of the Gethsemane prayer. The phrase אַל-יְהִי כִרְצוֹנִי כִּי אִם-כִּרְצוֹנֶךָ captures the submission formula well.
