# Luke Chapter 17 — Translation Notes

## Versification
### 17:36 — Vulgate includes, SBLGNT omits
- The Vulgate includes verse 36 as part of the eschatological discourse: "duo in agro : unus assumetur, et alter relinquetur" (two in the field: one will be taken, the other left). The SBLGNT omits this verse entirely (empty Greek field in the data). The Hebrew provides text for this verse: שְׁנַיִם יִהְיוּ בַשָׂדֶה וְנֶאֱסַף אֶחָד וְאֶחָד יֵעָזֵב.
- **Note**: This verse is present in the Vulgate and should be retained. The Hebrew text here is adequate. However, the Vulgate's Latin for v. 36 in the data reads "Respondentes dicunt illi : Ubi Domine ?" which is actually the content of v. 37. This appears to be a data alignment error. The Vulgate text "duo in agro : unus assumetur, et alter relinquetur" belongs to 17:36, and the current Latin entry seems to have been shifted from v. 37.

### 17:37 — Verse numbering alignment
- The Vulgate's v. 37 response about the body and eagles is "Ubicumque fuerit corpus, illuc congregabuntur et aquilæ." The Greek has the question ("Where, Lord?") and answer combined in v. 37. The Hebrew correctly renders both the question and answer in this verse.

## Content Alignment
### 17:9 — "I think not" in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate contains "non puto" (I think not) at the start of v. 10, functioning as the answer to v. 9. The Greek text implies a negative answer to the rhetorical question but has no explicit "I think not." The Hebrew adds אָמַרְתִּי לֹא (I said no), which approximates the Vulgate's explicit negation but phrases it differently.
- **Vulgate**: Numquid gratiam habet servo illi... non puto.
- **Greek**: μὴ ἔχει χάριν τῷ δούλῳ...
- **Hebrew**: הֲגַם יוֹדֶה לָעֶבֶד... אָמַרְתִּי לֹא
- **Note**: The Hebrew's "I said no" does not correspond well to either source. The Vulgate's "non puto" is more reflective ("I think not"), and the Greek leaves it implicit.

### 17:21 — "intra vos" / ἐντὸς ὑμῶν
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "intra vos" and Greek ἐντὸς ὑμῶν both mean "within you" or "among you." The Hebrew בְּקִרְבְּכֶם accurately captures both senses, as this biblical Hebrew preposition can mean "in your midst" or "within you."
- **Note**: No correction needed. The Hebrew renders the ambiguity faithfully.

### 17:31 — "in illa hora" vs. "in that day"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "In illa hora" (in that hour), while the Greek reads ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ (in that day). The Hebrew follows the Greek with בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא (in that day).
- **Vulgate**: In illa hora
- **Greek**: ἐν ἐκείνῃ τῇ ἡμέρᾳ
- **Hebrew**: בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "hora" (hour) is a distinct reading. A Vulgate-following text should use בַּשָּׁעָה הַהִיא.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 17:6 — Sycamine tree
- **Hebrew**: הַשִּׁקְמָה (sycamore)
- **Note**: The Greek συκαμίνῳ refers to a mulberry tree, while the Vulgate has "arbori moro" (mulberry tree). The Hebrew שִׁקְמָה is the biblical sycamore-fig (Amos 7:14, 1 Kings 10:27), a different tree. The Delitzsch translation uses a familiar biblical tree species, but it does not match either source precisely.

### 17:7 — "recumbe" rendering
- **Hebrew**: וְהָסֵב (and recline at table)
- **Note**: Good rendering. The hiphil of סבב in the sense of reclining at a meal is appropriate for the Greek ἀνάπεσε / Vulgate "recumbe."

### 17:37 — Eagles or vultures
- **Hebrew**: הַנְּשָׁרִים (the eagles/vultures)
- **Note**: Both the Greek ἀετοί and Latin "aquilæ" literally mean eagles, but in context likely refer to vultures. The Hebrew נֶשֶׁר is the standard biblical term covering both eagles and vultures (cf. Micah 1:16, Job 39:27-30), and is the ideal rendering.

## Other Notes
### 17:11 — Between Samaria and Galilee
- The Hebrew renders "per mediam Samariam et Galilæam" as בֵּין שֹׁמְרוֹן וְהַגָּלִיל (between Samaria and the Galilee). This matches the Greek διὰ μέσον Σαμαρείας καὶ Γαλιλαίας. The geographical note is accurate for Jesus' journey south.

### 17:15 — Voice omission
- The Hebrew renders the leper's return as praising God קוֹל גָּדוֹל (great voice), but the syntax is awkward. The Vulgate's "cum magna voce" and Greek μετὰ φωνῆς μεγάλης are adverbial, while the Hebrew places it at the end without a preposition. Expected: בְּקוֹל גָּדוֹל.
