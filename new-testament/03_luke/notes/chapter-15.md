# Luke Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 32 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment
### 15:8 — Drachma rendering
- **Issue**: The Hebrew uses דַרְכְּמוֹנִים, a direct transliteration of the Greek δραχμάς. The Vulgate retains "drachmas." No divergence, but the Hebrew transliteration is notable as it matches the Greek loanword rather than substituting a native Hebrew coin term.
- **Note**: Acceptable approach since דַּרְכְּמוֹן appears in late biblical Hebrew (Ezra 2:69, Nehemiah 7:70-72), though those passages use the Persian daric, not the Greek drachma.

### 15:16 — "Fill his belly" vs. Vulgate reading
- **Issue**: The Greek reads γεμίσαι τὴν κοιλίαν αὐτοῦ (fill his belly), and the Hebrew follows this with לְמַלֵּא בִטְנוֹ. The Vulgate reads "implere ventrem suum de siliquis" which aligns. However, some Greek manuscripts read χορτασθῆναι ἀπό (to be fed from), which the SBLGNT apparatus notes.
- **Note**: All three sources agree on the general sense. No significant divergence.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 15:4 — Lost sheep parable opening
- **Hebrew**: מִי בָכֶם הָאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר-לוֹ מֵאָה כְבָשִׂים
- **Note**: Excellent biblical Hebrew. The כְּבָשִׂים (lambs) is a good biblical term (2 Samuel 12:4, etc.), though the Greek πρόβατα and Latin "oves" mean sheep generically. The Vulgate's "centum oves" is more accurately rendered by צֹאן (flock) or רְחֵלִים.

### 15:13 — Prodigal living
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא
- **Note**: Excellent biblical pair. זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא comes directly from Deuteronomy 21:20 and Proverbs 23:21, describing the glutton and drunkard. This brilliantly renders the Greek ἀσώτως (prodigally) and the Vulgate's "vivendo luxuriose" through a deeply resonant biblical phrase.

### 15:17 — "Came to himself"
- **Hebrew**: וַיָּשֶׁב אֶל-לִבּוֹ
- **Note**: Beautiful biblical idiom. While not an exact OT parallel, it combines the pattern of שׁוּב (return) with לֵב (heart) in a way that feels authentically biblical. Compare 1 Kings 8:47 (וְהֵשִׁיבוּ אֶל-לִבָּם).

### 15:20 — Father's compassion
- **Hebrew**: וַיֶּהֱמוּ מֵעָיו
- **Note**: Superb. This is the exact biblical idiom for deep compassion (cf. Genesis 43:30, 1 Kings 3:26). The verb הָמָה with מֵעַיִם captures σπλαγχνίσθη / "misericordia motus est" through authentic Hebrew emotional vocabulary.

### 15:22 — Best robe
- **Hebrew**: הַשִׂמְלָה הַיָּפָה
- **Note**: שִׂמְלָה is solidly biblical (Genesis 9:23, Exodus 22:26). The Vulgate's "stolam primam" (first/best robe) and Greek στολὴν τὴν πρώτην suggest "first" robe, while the Hebrew renders "beautiful" robe. Minor semantic shift.

### 15:23 — Fatted calf
- **Hebrew**: עֵגֶל הַמַּרְבֵּק
- **Note**: Directly echoes 1 Samuel 28:24 (עֵגֶל-מַרְבֵּק) and Malachi 3:20. Authentic biblical vocabulary.

## Other Notes
### 15:18 — Sin against heaven
- The phrase חָטָאתִי גַּם לְשָׁמַיִם גַּם לְפָנֶיךָ is significant theologically. The Vulgate's "peccavi in caelum, et coram te" matches. The "heaven" as a circumlocution for God is a feature shared by late Second Temple period texts.
