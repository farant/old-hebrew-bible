# Luke Chapter 2 -- Translation Notes

## Versification
All 52 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 2:5 -- "desponsata sibi uxore"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "desponsata sibi uxore praegnante" (betrothed to him, his wife, being with child), calling Mary explicitly "uxore" (wife). The Greek has only ἐμνηστευμένῃ αὐτῷ (betrothed to him). The Hebrew has הַמְאֹרָשָׂה לוֹ (betrothed to him), following the Greek and omitting "uxore."
- **Vulgate**: ut profiteretur cum Maria desponsata sibi uxore praegnante.
- **Greek**: ἀπογράψασθαι σὺν Μαριὰμ τῇ ἐμνηστευμένῃ αὐτῷ, οὔσῃ ἐγκύῳ.
- **Hebrew**: לְהִתְפָּקֵד עִם-מִרְיָם הַמְאֹרָשָׂה לוֹ וְהִיא הָרָה
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "uxore" affirms Mary's status as Joseph's wife, not merely his betrothed. The Hebrew omits this designation, following the Greek reading.

### 2:14 -- "bonae voluntatis" vs. "εὐδοκίας"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "hominibus bonae voluntatis" (to men of good will), a genitive construction limiting peace to those of good will. The SBLGNT reads ἐν ἀνθρώποις εὐδοκίας (among men of [God's] good pleasure). The Hebrew בְּאַנְשֵׁי רְצוֹנוֹ (among men of His favor) follows the Greek genitive reading.
- **Vulgate**: et in terra pax hominibus bonae voluntatis.
- **Greek**: καὶ ἐπὶ γῆς εἰρήνη ἐν ἀνθρώποις εὐδοκίας.
- **Hebrew**: וּבָאָרֶץ שָׁלוֹם בְּאַנְשֵׁי רְצוֹנוֹ
- **Note**: Both Vulgate and Greek agree on the genitive construction here (the Vulgate "bonae voluntatis" matches the Greek εὐδοκίας). The Hebrew רְצוֹנוֹ (His favor) with the third-person suffix makes the divine referent explicit, which is implied but not stated in the Latin or Greek.

### 2:22 -- "purgationis ejus" vs. "καθαρισμοῦ αὐτῶν"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "purgationis ejus" (her purification, singular), while the Greek reads τοῦ καθαρισμοῦ αὐτῶν (their purification, plural). The Hebrew has טָהֳרָם (their purification, plural), following the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: Et postquam impleti sunt dies purgationis ejus secundum legem Moysi
- **Greek**: Καὶ ὅτε ἐπλήσθησαν αἱ ἡμέραι τοῦ καθαρισμοῦ αὐτῶν κατὰ τὸν νόμον Μωϋσέως
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּמְלְאוּ יְמֵי טָהֳרָם כְּתוֹרַת משֶׁה
- **Note**: The Vulgate singular "ejus" (her) is theologically significant: under Levitical law (Lev 12), only the mother undergoes purification, not the child. The Greek/Hebrew plural may be a scribal accommodation or may include the presentation of the child. The Hebrew follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate.

### 2:33 -- "pater ejus" vs. "ὁ πατὴρ αὐτοῦ"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "pater ejus et mater" (his father and mother). The SBLGNT reads ὁ πατὴρ αὐτοῦ (his father). The Hebrew has וְאָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ (his father and his mother). All three agree in calling Joseph "father."
- **Vulgate**: Et erat pater ejus et mater mirantes
- **Greek**: καὶ ἦν ὁ πατὴρ αὐτοῦ καὶ ἡ μήτηρ
- **Hebrew**: וְאָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ תְּמֵהִים
- **Note**: No divergence here. Some Greek manuscripts read "Joseph" instead of "his father" to avoid implying biological paternity, but the SBLGNT retains "his father," and all three sources agree.

### 2:38 -- "confitebatur Domino" vs. "τῷ θεῷ"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "confitebatur Domino" (gave thanks to the Lord), while the SBLGNT has τῷ θεῷ (to God). The Hebrew has לַיְהוָֹה (to the LORD), using the Tetragrammaton.
- **Vulgate**: confitebatur Domino
- **Greek**: ἀνθωμολογεῖτο τῷ θεῷ (variant: τῷ κυρίῳ)
- **Hebrew**: לְהֹדוֹת לַיְהוָֹה
- **Note**: The SBLGNT reads θεῷ (God), but many manuscripts read κυρίῳ (Lord), matching the Vulgate. The Hebrew uses the Tetragrammaton, which is Delitzsch's standard rendering for both θεός and κύριος when referring to God the Father.

### 2:40 -- "plenus sapientia" and "confortabatur"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "confortabatur plenus sapientia" (was strengthened, full of wisdom). The SBLGNT has ἐκραταιοῦτο πληρούμενον σοφίᾳ (was strengthened, being filled with wisdom). The Hebrew has the bracketed word [בָּרוּחַ] (in spirit) plus וַיִּמָּלֵא חָכְמָה.
- **Vulgate**: Puer autem crescebat, et confortabatur plenus sapientia
- **Greek**: Τὸ δὲ παιδίον ηὔξανεν καὶ ἐκραταιοῦτο πληρούμενον σοφίᾳ
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּגְדַּל הַנַּעַר וַיֶּחֱזַק [בָּרוּחַ] וַיִּמָּלֵא חָכְמָה
- **Note**: The bracketed בָּרוּחַ (in spirit) in the Hebrew reflects a textual variant. Neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT has "in spirit" here (though the parallel in 1:80 does include it). The brackets correctly signal the uncertainty.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 2:1 -- Excellent biblical narrative style
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם וַיֵצֵא צַו מֵאֵת קֵיסַר אוֹגוּסְטוֹס לִמְנוֹת אֶת-כָּל-יֹשְׂבֵי תֵבֵל
- **Note**: The opening וַיְהִי בַּיָּמִים הָהֵם is a classic biblical narrative formula (cf. Judg 17:6, 1 Sam 3:1). The word צַו (decree/command) is good biblical Hebrew (Isa 28:10). The phrase יֹשְׂבֵי תֵבֵל (inhabitants of the world) for "universus orbis" echoes Isa 18:3 and Ps 33:8.

### 2:7 -- אֵבוּס for "praesepio"
- **Hebrew**: וַתַּשְׁכִּיבֵהוּ בָאֵבוּס
- **Note**: אֵבוּס (manger/feeding trough) is excellent biblical Hebrew, appearing in Isa 1:3 (יָדַע שׁוֹר קֹנֵהוּ וַחֲמוֹר אֵבוּס בְּעָלָיו), creating a direct verbal link to the Isaianic passage about the ox knowing its owner's manger.

### 2:9 -- Theophany language
- **Hebrew**: וְהִנֵּה מַלְאַךְ יְהוָֹה נִצָּב עֲלֵיהֶם וּכְבוֹד יְהוָֹה הוֹפִיעַ עֲלֵיהֶם מִסָּבִיב
- **Note**: The verb הוֹפִיעַ (shone forth) for "circumfulsit" (shone around) is excellent theophanic vocabulary, echoing Deut 33:2 (הוֹפִיעַ מֵהַר פָּארָן) and Ps 80:2. The phrase כְּבוֹד יְהוָֹה (the glory of the LORD) is the standard tabernacle/temple theophany expression (Exod 16:10, 40:34-35).

### 2:24 -- Levitical offering terminology
- **Hebrew**: שְׁתֵּי תֹרִים אוֹ שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי יוֹנָה
- **Note**: This directly echoes the Levitical offering for purification in Lev 12:8 (שְׁתֵּי תֹרִים אוֹ שְׁנֵי בְּנֵי יוֹנָה). The Hebrew is virtually identical to the Masoretic text of Leviticus, perfectly capturing the OT quotation.

### 2:29-32 -- The Nunc Dimittis
- **Hebrew**: עַתָּה תִּפְטַר אֶת-עַבְדֶּךָ כִּדְבָרְךָ אֲדֹנָי בְּשָׁלוֹם (2:29), אוֹר לְהָאִיר עֵינֵי הַגּוֹיִם (2:32)
- **Note**: The canticle is well rendered. The phrase אוֹר לְהָאִיר עֵינֵי הַגּוֹיִם (a light to enlighten the eyes of the nations) at 2:32 draws on Isa 42:6 and 49:6 (אוֹר גּוֹיִם). However, the Hebrew adds עֵינֵי (eyes) which is not in any of the source texts. The Vulgate has "lumen ad revelationem gentium" (a light for the revelation of the Gentiles) and the Greek has φῶς εἰς ἀποκάλυψιν ἐθνῶν. The added "eyes" is an unnecessary interpretive expansion.

### 2:35 -- חָרֶב for "gladius"
- **Hebrew**: וּבְנַפְשֵׁךְ תַּחְתֹּר חָרֶב
- **Note**: The Vulgate "gladius" and Greek ῥομφαία both denote a large sword. The Hebrew חָרֶב (sword) is the standard biblical term. The verb תַּחְתֹּר (shall pierce/dig through) is unusual; biblical Hebrew typically uses עָבַר (pass through) with sword imagery. The verb חתר means "to dig through" (Ezek 8:8, 12:5) and creates a more violent image than the source texts.

### 2:49 -- "in his quae Patris mei sunt"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי אֶהְיֶה בַּאֲשֶׁר לְאָבִי
- **Note**: The Vulgate "in his quae Patris mei sunt" (in the things that are my Father's) is ambiguous between "my Father's house" and "my Father's affairs." The Hebrew בַּאֲשֶׁר לְאָבִי (in that which is my Father's) preserves this ambiguity well.

## Other Notes

### 2:11 -- Christological titles
The Hebrew renders "Salvator, qui est Christus Dominus" as מוֹשִׂיעַ אֲשֶׁר הוּא הַמָּשִׁיחַ הָאָדוֹן. The word מוֹשִׁיעַ (savior/deliverer) echoes the Judges-era deliverers (Judg 3:9, 3:15) and the Isaianic title (Isa 43:3, 45:15). The pairing of מָשִׁיחַ (anointed one) with אָדוֹן (lord) places two distinct messianic titles together.

### 2:34-35 -- Simeon's oracle
The Hebrew לְמִכְשׁוֹל וְלִתְקוּמָה (for stumbling and for rising) for "in ruinam et in resurrectionem" uses vocabulary that echoes Isa 8:14-15 (the stone of stumbling). The word תְּקוּמָה (rising/resurrection) is rare in biblical Hebrew but effective. The oracle to Mary about the sword is reminiscent of the suffering expressed in Lamentations.

### 2:41-52 -- Finding in the Temple
The narrative of the boy Jesus in the temple is rendered in classical biblical narrative style throughout. The phrase בְּתוֹךְ הַמּוֹרִים (among the teachers) at 2:46 uses the root ירה, the same root from which תּוֹרָה (Torah) derives, subtly connecting Jesus' teaching activity with Torah itself.
