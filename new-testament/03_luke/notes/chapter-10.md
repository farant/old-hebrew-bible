# Luke Chapter 10 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 42 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 10:1 — "septuaginta duos" (seventy-two) vs. "seventy"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "septuaginta duos" (seventy-two). The SBLGNT has ἑβδομήκοντα δύο (seventy-two) in the main text but marks it as a variant. The Hebrew has שִׁבְעִים (seventy), omitting the "two."
- **Vulgate**: designavit Dominus et alios septuaginta duos
- **Greek**: ἀνέδειξεν ὁ κύριος καὶ ἑτέρους ἑβδομήκοντα δύο
- **Hebrew**: הִבְדִּיל הָאָדוֹן עוֹד שִׁבְעִים אֲחֵרִים
- **Note**: The Hebrew departs from the Vulgate by reading "seventy" rather than "seventy-two." The number seventy resonates with the LXX tradition of the seventy elders (Num 11:16-25) and the seventy translators. However, the Vulgate's "seventy-two" should be followed. The Hebrew should read שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם.

### 10:1 — "binos" (two by two)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "binos" (two by two). The SBLGNT has ἀνὰ δύο δύο (two by two) but the Hebrew has שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם (two by two), which correctly matches both.
- **Note**: Alignment is good. The Hebrew distributive construction echoes Gen 7:9 (שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם בָּאוּ אֶל-נֹחַ).

### 10:17 — "septuaginta duo" again
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Reversi sunt autem septuaginta duo" (the seventy-two returned). The Hebrew has הַשִּׁבְעִים (the seventy), again omitting "two."
- **Vulgate**: septuaginta duo
- **Hebrew**: הַשִּׁבְעִים
- **Note**: Same issue as 10:1. The Hebrew should read הַשִּׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם to follow the Vulgate.

### 10:39 — "Domini" / "Jesu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "secus pedes Domini" (at the feet of the Lord). The SBLGNT reads πρὸς τοὺς πόδας τοῦ Ἰησοῦ (at the feet of Jesus) in the main text. The Hebrew has לְרַגְלֵי יֵשׁוּעַ (at the feet of Jesus), following the SBLGNT rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: secus pedes Domini
- **Greek**: πρὸς τοὺς πόδας τοῦ Ἰησοῦ
- **Hebrew**: לְרַגְלֵי יֵשׁוּעַ
- **Note**: To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should read לְרַגְלֵי הָאָדוֹן (at the feet of the Lord).

### 10:41-42 — "Dominus" / "Jesus" and "porro unum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "respondens dixit illi Dominus" (the Lord answered). The Hebrew has וַיַּעַן יֵשׁוּעַ (and Jesus answered). Also, the Vulgate reads "porro unum est necessarium" (moreover, one thing is necessary), while the SBLGNT has a variant textual tradition. The Hebrew reads וְאֵין צֹרֶךְ אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד (there is no need except for one), which matches the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: respondens dixit illi Dominus... porro unum est necessarium
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּעַן יֵשׁוּעַ... וְאֵין צֹרֶךְ אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד
- **Note**: The speaker identification differs (Lord vs. Jesus), but the theological content of "one thing is necessary" is correctly rendered.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 10:21 — Thanksgiving prayer
- **Hebrew**: אוֹדְךָ אָבִי אֲדוֹן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ כִּי הִסְתַּרְתָּ אֶת-אֵלֶּה מִן-הַחֲכָמִים וְהַנְּבוֹנִים וְגִלִּיתָם לָעֹלֲלִים
- **Note**: The prayer language is rich biblical Hebrew. אֲדוֹן הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהָאָרֶץ echoes Gen 14:19 (קֹנֵה שָׁמַיִם וָאָרֶץ). The pattern of hiding from the wise and revealing to babes echoes Ps 8:3 (מִפִּי עוֹלְלִים וְיֹנְקִים יִסַּדְתָּ עֹז) and Isa 29:14. Excellent.

### 10:27 — Shema and Leviticus 19:18
- **Hebrew**: וְאָהַבְתָּ אֵת יְהוָֹה אֱלֹהֶיךָ בְּכָל-לְבָבְךָ וּבְכָל-נַפְשְׁךָ וּבְכָל-מְאֹדְךָ וּבְכָל-מַדָּעֲךָ וְאֶת-רֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ
- **Note**: The first clause is a near-exact quotation of Deut 6:5 with the addition of וּבְכָל-מַדָּעֲךָ (with all your mind), which represents the Greek ἐν ὅλῃ τῇ διανοίᾳ σου / Vulgate "ex omni mente tua." This fourth element is not in the MT of Deuteronomy but appears in some LXX recensions. The second clause וְאֶת-רֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ is an exact quotation of Lev 19:18.

### 10:30 — Good Samaritan vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: שֹׁדֲדִים (robbers), הִפְשִׁיטֻהוּ (they stripped him), פְּצָעֻהוּ (they wounded him)
- **Note**: The vocabulary is solidly biblical. שֹׁדֲדִים (Jer 12:12), the hiphil of פשט for stripping (1 Sam 31:8), and the root פצע (Song 5:7) all have good biblical attestation. The phrase עוֹמֵד בֵּין-מָוֶת לַחַיִּים for "semivivo" (half-dead) is an idiomatic Hebrew expansion that is more vivid than a literal rendering.

### 10:42 — Post-biblical צֹרֶךְ
- **Hebrew**: וְאֵין צֹרֶךְ אֶלָּא בְּאֶחָד
- **Note**: צֹרֶךְ (need/necessity) is Mishnaic Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use a different construction. The word אֶלָּא (but/except) is also characteristically rabbinic rather than biblical.

## Other Notes

### 10:15 — שְׁאוֹל for "infernum"
The Hebrew שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) for the Vulgate's "infernum" and Greek ᾅδου (Hades) is the correct biblical equivalent. Sheol is the OT underworld (Isa 14:9-15, Num 16:30), and the passage echoes Isa 14:13-15 (the fall of the king of Babylon).

### 10:22 — Father-Son mutual knowledge
The Hebrew הַכֹּל נִמְסַר-לִי מֵאֵת אָבִי captures the Vulgate's "Omnia mihi tradita sunt a Patre meo." This is one of the most theologically dense Johannine-style sayings in the Synoptics, and the Hebrew renders it straightforwardly.
