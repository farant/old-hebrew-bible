# Luke Chapter 7 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 50 verses align across all three sources. However, the Vulgate verse boundary between 7:18 and 7:19 differs from the SBLGNT. In the Greek, 7:18 includes "and John, summoning two of his disciples," while the Vulgate places this material at the start of 7:19 ("Et convocavit duos de discipulis suis Joannes"). The Hebrew follows the Vulgate division, placing the calling of two disciples in verse 19.

## Content Alignment

### 7:19 — "ad Jesum" vs. "ad Dominum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "misit ad Jesum" (sent to Jesus). The SBLGNT reads πρὸς τὸν κύριον (to the Lord). The Hebrew has אֶל-יֵשׁוּעַ (to Jesus), matching the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: misit ad Jesum
- **Greek**: ἔπεμψεν πρὸς τὸν κύριον
- **Note**: The SBLGNT reading "the Lord" is the harder reading and likely original. The Vulgate and Hebrew both have "Jesus," which aligns with Byzantine manuscripts. No action needed since we follow the Vulgate.

### 7:28 — "propheta" (prophet)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "propheta" — "major inter natos mulierum propheta Joanne Baptista nemo est." The SBLGNT lacks the word "prophet." The Hebrew includes [הַמַּטְבִּיל] in brackets, marking "the Baptist" as textually uncertain, but does not include the word "prophet" (נָבִיא) that the Vulgate has.
- **Vulgate**: major inter natos mulierum propheta Joanne Baptista nemo est
- **Greek**: μείζων ἐν γεννητοῖς γυναικῶν Ἰωάννου οὐδείς ἐστιν
- **Hebrew**: אֵין אִישׁ בִּילוּדֵי אִשָּׁה גָּדוֹל מִיּוֹחָנָן [הַמַּטְבִּיל]
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "propheta" should be represented in the Hebrew to follow our methodology. The Hebrew also brackets הַמַּטְבִּיל, which the SBLGNT also lacks, but the Vulgate has "Baptista."

### 7:31 — "Ait autem Dominus" (And the Lord said)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate begins 7:31 with "Ait autem Dominus" (And the Lord said), introducing the speaker. The SBLGNT lacks this introduction entirely. The Hebrew includes וַיֹּאמֶר הָאָדוֹן (and the Lord said), matching the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Ait autem Dominus : Cui ergo similes dicam
- **Greek**: Τίνι οὖν ὁμοιώσω
- **Hebrew**: וַיֹּאמֶר הָאָדוֹן עַתָּה אֶל-מִי אֲדַמֶּה
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate by including the introductory phrase.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 7:12 — Excellent narrative construction
- **Hebrew**: הוּא קָרַב אֶל-שַׁעַר הָעִיר וְהִנֵּה מוֹצִיאִים מֵת בֵּן יָחִיד לְאִמּוֹ וְהִיא אַלְמָנָה
- **Note**: Outstanding biblical narrative. The construction with הִנֵּה (behold) introducing the scene, בֵּן יָחִיד (only son), and אַלְמָנָה (widow) all echo the Elijah narrative in 1 Kings 17:9-24 (the widow of Zarephath), which is the obvious OT typological parallel. Delitzsch's diction is precisely calibrated here.

### 7:27 — Malachi 3:1 quotation
- **Hebrew**: הִנְנִי שֹׁלֵחַ מַלְאָכִי לְפָנֶיךָ וּפִנָּה דַרְכְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ
- **Note**: The first half closely follows the MT of Malachi 3:1 (הִנְנִי שֹׁלֵחַ מַלְאָכִי). The second clause differs from the MT (וּפִנָּה-דֶרֶךְ לְפָנָי, "and he will clear a way before me"), using לְפָנֶיךָ (before you) as in the NT adaptation. This is correct: the Hebrew reflects the christological re-reading of Malachi found in all three Synoptics.

### 7:32 — Post-biblical חִלַּלְנוּ
- **Hebrew**: חִלַּלְנוּ לָכֶם בַּחֲלִילִים
- **Note**: The piel of חלל meaning "to play the flute" is post-biblical. Biblical Hebrew does not use this verb for flute-playing; cf. 1 Sam 10:5, 1 Kings 1:40 where the noun חָלִיל appears but with different verbal constructions. A minor accommodation.

### 7:38 — וַתֵּבְ
- **Hebrew**: וַתַּעֲמֹד לְרַגְלָיו מֵאַחֲרָיו וַתֵּבְ וַתּוֹרֶד דְּמָעוֹת
- **Note**: The form וַתֵּבְ appears to be a truncated waw-consecutive, perhaps from בכה (to weep). The expected form would be וַתֵּבְךְּ. This may be a typographical issue in the Delitzsch text.

## Other Notes

### 7:9 — "Amen dico vobis"
The Hebrew renders the characteristic "amen" saying with אֹמֵר אֲנִי לָכֶם, omitting the "amen" that the Vulgate has. This is a recurring pattern in Delitzsch: he sometimes flattens the solemn "amen" formula.

### 7:47 — Theological emphasis preserved
The Hebrew נִסְלְחוּ לָהּ חַטֹּאתֶיהָ הָרַבּוֹת כִּי הַרְבֵּה אָהֵבָה captures the causal-sequential logic of the Vulgate's "remittuntur ei peccata multa, quoniam dilexit multum" correctly: her sins are forgiven because she loved much.
