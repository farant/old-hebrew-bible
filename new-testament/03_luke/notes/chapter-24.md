# Luke Chapter 24 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 53 verses align across all three sources. No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment
### 24:1 — "et quædam cum eis" in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds וְעִמָּהֶן נָשִׁים אֲחֵרוֹת (and with them other women), which is absent from both the Vulgate and the SBLGNT Greek for this verse. The Vulgate simply says "venerunt ad monumentum, portantes quæ paraverant aromata." The information about other women appears later in v. 10.
- **Note**: This Hebrew addition should be noted as a Delitzsch expansion not supported by the Vulgate.

### 24:3 — "Domini Jesu" / τοῦ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "corpus Domini Jesu" (the body of the Lord Jesus). The SBLGNT Greek varies by manuscript; some have τοῦ κυρίου Ἰησοῦ, others just the shorter reading. The Hebrew includes the full title: גְּוִיַּת הָאָדוֹן יֵשׁוּעַ (the body of the Lord Jesus).
- **Note**: The Hebrew matches the Vulgate's fuller reading.

### 24:36 — "Pax vobis : ego sum, nolite timere"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate contains "Pax vobis : ego sum, nolite timere" (Peace to you; it is I, do not fear). The SBLGNT Greek has only the first part or none depending on manuscripts. The Hebrew includes שָׁלוֹם לָכֶם (Peace to you) but does not include "it is I, do not fear."
- **Vulgate**: Pax vobis : ego sum, nolite timere
- **Greek**: (varies by manuscript)
- **Hebrew**: שָׁלוֹם לָכֶם
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits "ego sum, nolite timere" which is in the Vulgate. A Vulgate-following text should include אֲנִי הוּא אַל-תִּירָאוּ or similar.

### 24:42 — "et favum mellis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "et favum mellis" (and a honeycomb) to the broiled fish. The SBLGNT Greek has only ἰχθύος ὀπτοῦ μέρος (a piece of broiled fish). The Hebrew places the honeycomb in brackets: [וּמְעַט צוּף דְּבָשׁ] (and a little honeycomb), acknowledging the textual uncertainty while including the Vulgate reading.
- **Note**: The bracketed treatment is a reasonable compromise. The Vulgate text should be the primary reading.

### 24:43 — "sumens reliquias dedit eis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "sumens reliquias dedit eis" (taking the remnants, he gave to them) after Jesus ate before them. This is absent from the Greek. The Hebrew does not include this addition.
- **Note**: The Vulgate's addition of giving the remnants to the disciples is absent from the Hebrew.

### 24:46 — "sic oportebat" / "it was necessary"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "sic scriptum est, et sic oportebat Christum pati" (thus it is written, and thus it was necessary for Christ to suffer). The Hebrew renders this as כֵּן כָּתוּב וְכֵן נִגְזָר (thus it is written and thus it was decreed). The verb נִגְזָר (was decreed) is a strong interpretive choice, adding a sense of divine decree not explicit in "oportebat" (it was necessary).
- **Note**: נִגְזָר (from גזר, to cut/decree) appears in Daniel 9:26 (יִכָּרֵת מָשִׁיחַ) and Esther 2:1. The decree/cutting language has messianic resonance.

### 24:51 — Ascension
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT brackets καὶ ἀνεφέρετο εἰς τὸν οὐρανόν (and was carried up into heaven), indicating manuscript uncertainty. The Vulgate includes it: "et ferebatur in cælum." The Hebrew includes it: וַיִּנָּשֵׂא הַשָּׁמָיְמָה (and he was lifted up heavenward).
- **Note**: Retained correctly as Vulgate text. The Hebrew nifal of נָשָׂא (was lifted up) is a good passive rendering.

### 24:52 — Worship
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT brackets προσκυνήσαντες αὐτόν (having worshipped him). The Vulgate includes "adorantes" (worshipping). The Hebrew includes it: הִשְׁתַּחֲווּ-לוֹ (bowed down to him / worshipped him).
- **Note**: Retained correctly. הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה is the standard biblical Hebrew for prostration and worship.

## Hebrew Translation Quality
### 24:13 — Distance to Emmaus
- **Hebrew**: כְּשִׁשִּׁים רִיס (about sixty ris)
- **Note**: The Hebrew transliterates the Greek στάδιους (stadia) as רִיס, which is the standard Mishnaic Hebrew term for a stadium (roughly 185 meters). Attested in Mishnah and Talmud (e.g., Yoma 6:4). While post-biblical, there is no biblical Hebrew unit of this size, making the borrowing necessary.

### 24:25 — "O stulti"
- **Hebrew**: הוֹי חַסְרֵי דַעַת וְכִבְדֵי לֵב (O lacking in knowledge and heavy of heart)
- **Note**: כְּבַד לֵב (heavy of heart) echoes the description of Pharaoh (Exodus 7:14, 8:15 etc.), where it connotes stubbornness. The Vulgate's "tardi corde" (slow of heart) and Greek βραδεῖς τῇ καρδίᾳ (slow of heart) suggest sluggishness in believing rather than the Pharaonic stubbornness. The Hebrew is slightly stronger than the source texts.

### 24:27 — "Interpretabatur" / διερμήνευσεν
- **Hebrew**: וַיְבַאֵר (and he explained)
- **Note**: The piel of בָּאַר (to make clear, explain) appears in Deuteronomy 1:5 (הוֹאִיל מֹשֶׁה בֵּאֵר אֶת-הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת, Moses began to explain this Torah) and Deuteronomy 27:8. This is a fitting biblical verb for scriptural exposition, connecting Jesus' work to Moses' own interpretive activity.

### 24:32 — "Burning heart"
- **Hebrew**: בֹעֵר הָיָה לְבָבֵנוּ (our heart was burning)
- **Note**: The participial construction with הָיָה is natural biblical syntax. The verb בָּעַר (to burn) used for inner spiritual experience echoes Psalm 39:4 (חַם-לִבִּי בְקִרְבִּי... בָּעֲרָה-אֵשׁ) and Jeremiah 20:9 (כְּאֵשׁ בֹּעֶרֶת). Strong biblical resonance.

### 24:44 — Torah, Prophets, and Psalms
- **Hebrew**: בְּתוֹרַת משֶׁה וּבַנְּבִיאִים וּבַתְּהִלִּים
- **Note**: This three-part division corresponds to the Jewish tripartite canon (Torah, Nevi'im, Ketuvim), with תְּהִלִּים (Psalms) representing the Writings (Ketuvim), as is traditional since Psalms is the largest and first book of that section. The Hebrew rendering naturally expresses the concept the Vulgate renders as "in lege Moysi, et prophetis, et Psalmis."

## Other Notes
### 24:47 — "poenitentiam et remissionem peccatorum"
- The Hebrew תְּשׁוּבָה וּסְלִיחַת הַחֲטָאִים (repentance and forgiveness of sins) uses the classic Jewish theological vocabulary. תְּשׁוּבָה (repentance/return) became the standard rabbinic term but is rooted in the prophetic call to "return" (שׁוּב) to God (Hosea 14:2, Joel 2:12-13). סְלִיחָה (forgiveness) appears in Psalm 130:4 and Nehemiah 9:17. The pairing is authentically Jewish-Hebrew.

### 24:50-53 — The Ascension and conclusion
- The Hebrew ending אָמֵן matches the Vulgate's "Amen." The SBLGNT Greek text varies among manuscripts regarding this final word. The Hebrew's inclusion aligns with the Vulgate reading and provides a liturgical close to the Gospel.
