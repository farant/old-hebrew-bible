# Luke Chapter 6 -- Translation Notes

## Versification
All 49 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 6:1 -- "sabbato secundo primo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has the unique reading "in sabbato secundo primo" (on the second-first Sabbath), a famously obscure phrase found only in some Latin manuscripts. The SBLGNT has simply ἐν σαββάτῳ (on a Sabbath), with the variant δευτεροπρώτῳ omitted as textually uncertain. The Hebrew has בַּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת הַשֵּׁנִית לִסְפִירַת הָעֹמֶר (on the second Sabbath of the counting of the Omer).
- **Vulgate**: Factum est autem in sabbato secundo, primo
- **Greek**: Ἐγένετο δὲ ἐν σαββάτῳ (many MSS add δευτεροπρώτῳ)
- **Hebrew**: בַּיוֹם הַשַּׁבָּת הַשֵּׁנִית לִסְפִירַת הָעֹמֶר
- **Note**: The Hebrew interprets "secundo primo" as referring to the second Sabbath of the Omer count, which is one of several scholarly explanations for this difficult phrase. This is an interpretive choice by Delitzsch, not a straightforward translation. The Vulgate reading is retained but the Hebrew's interpretation cannot be verified.

### 6:5 -- "Dominus est Filius hominis etiam sabbati"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "dominus est Filius hominis etiam sabbati" (the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath). The Greek has Κύριός ἐστιν τοῦ σαββάτου ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου. The Hebrew has כִּי בֶּן-הָאָדָם גַּם-אֲדוֹן הַשַּׁבָּת הוּא. All agree substantively.
- **Note**: The Hebrew word order places הוּא (he) at the end for emphasis, effectively rendering "the Son of Man is also lord of the Sabbath -- he." This emphatic pronoun is idiomatic Hebrew and adds force to the declaration.

### 6:10 -- "restituta est manus ejus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "restituta est manus ejus" (his hand was restored). The SBLGNT has ἀπεκατεστάθη ἡ χεὶρ αὐτοῦ (his hand was restored). The Hebrew has וַתֵּרָפֵא יָדוֹ וַתָּשָׁב כָּאַחֶרֶת (his hand was healed and became like the other).
- **Vulgate**: Et extendit : et restituta est manus ejus.
- **Greek**: ὁ δὲ ἐποίησεν, καὶ ἀπεκατεστάθη ἡ χεὶρ αὐτοῦ.
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּעַשׂ כֵּן וַתֵּרָפֵא יָדוֹ וַתָּשָׁב כָּאַחֶרֶת
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds וַתָּשָׁב כָּאַחֶרֶת (and it returned like the other), a detail not in the Vulgate or SBLGNT for this verse. This appears to be harmonization with the parallel in Matt 12:13, which has ἀπεκατεστάθη ὑγιὴς ὡς ἡ ἄλλη (it was restored healthy like the other). The addition should not be present following the Vulgate strictly.

### 6:11 -- "insipientia" vs. "ἀνοίας" vs. "חֵמָה"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "repleti sunt insipientia" (filled with folly/madness). The Greek has ἐπλήσθησαν ἀνοίας (filled with senseless rage). The Hebrew has נִמְלְאוּ חֵמָה (filled with wrath/fury).
- **Vulgate**: Ipsi autem repleti sunt insipientia
- **Greek**: αὐτοὶ δὲ ἐπλήσθησαν ἀνοίας
- **Hebrew**: וְהֵמָּה נִמְלְאוּ חֵמָה
- **Note**: The Latin "insipientia" and Greek "ἀνοίας" both connote madness or senseless fury, not mere anger. The Hebrew חֵמָה (wrath/fury) captures the emotional intensity but loses the nuance of irrationality/madness. A rendering like שִׁגָּעוֹן (madness, cf. Deut 28:28) would be closer to the Vulgate/Greek sense.

### 6:17 -- "in loco campestri"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree: the Vulgate has "in loco campestri" (in a level place), the Greek ἐπὶ τόπου πεδινοῦ (on a level place), the Hebrew בִּמְקוֹם מִישׁוֹר (in a level place). This is Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" as opposed to Matthew's "Sermon on the Mount."
- **Note**: The Hebrew מִישׁוֹר (level ground/plain) is good biblical Hebrew (Ps 27:11, Isa 40:4). No divergence.

### 6:26 -- "omnes" / "πάντες"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "cum benedixerint vobis homines" (when men shall bless you) without "all." The SBLGNT includes πάντες (all): ὅταν καλῶς ὑμᾶς εἴπωσιν πάντες οἱ ἄνθρωποι. The Hebrew has כָּל-הָאֲנָשִׁים (all the men), following the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: Vae cum benedixerint vobis homines
- **Greek**: Οὐαὶ ὅταν καλῶς ὑμᾶς εἴπωσιν πάντες οἱ ἄνθρωποι
- **Hebrew**: אוֹי לָכֶם אִם כָּל-הָאֲנָשִׁים מְשַׁבְּחִים אֶתְכֶם
- **Note**: The Vulgate omits "all" (omnes). The Hebrew follows the Greek in including כָּל (all). Following the Vulgate, "all" should be omitted.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:1 -- מְלִילֹת for "spicas"
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּקְטְפוּ תַלְמִידָיו מְלִילֹת וַיְפָרְכוּ אֹתָן בִּידֵיהֶם
- **Note**: מְלִילֹת (ears of grain, rubbed by hand) appears in Deut 23:26 (כִּי תָבֹא בְּקָמַת רֵעֶךָ וְקָטַפְתָּ מְלִילֹת בְּיָדֶךָ), the very law that is the background to this episode. The verb קָטַף (to pluck) and the noun מְלִילֹת (rubbed ears) together constitute a near-direct allusion to the Deuteronomy text. Excellent choice by Delitzsch.

### 6:12 -- Prayer on the mountain
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּצֵא הָהָרָה לְהִתְפַּלֵּל וַיַּעֲמֹד כָּל-הַלַּיְלָה בַּתְּפִלָּה לֵאלֹהִים
- **Note**: The verb וַיַּעֲמֹד (and he stood) for "erat pernoctans" (was spending the night) shifts the imagery from sleeping outdoors in prayer to standing all night in prayer, which is a stronger image. The Hebrew idiom of standing in prayer is attested (1 Sam 1:26, 1 Kgs 8:22).

### 6:20-26 -- The Beatitudes and Woes
- **Hebrew**: אַשְׁרֵיכֶם (blessed are you), אוֹי לָכֶם (woe to you)
- **Note**: אַשְׁרֵי is the classic biblical beatitude formula (Ps 1:1, Ps 32:1, Ps 84:5-6). אוֹי is the standard prophetic woe oracle formula (Isa 5:8-23, Hab 2:6-19). The pairing of אַשְׁרֵי and אוֹי gives the Hebrew the feel of prophetic speech. Luke's Beatitudes are in the second person (unlike Matthew's third person), and the Hebrew preserves this direct address with the second-person suffix -כֶם.

### 6:29 -- הַלְּחִי for "maxillam"
- **Hebrew**: הַמַּכֶּה אֹתְךָ עַל-הַלְּחִי הַטֵּה-לוֹ גַּם אֶת-הָאַחֶרֶת
- **Note**: לְחִי (cheek/jaw) appears in Judg 15:15-17 (the jawbone of the donkey), 1 Kgs 22:24, and Lam 3:30 (יִתֵּן לְמַכֵּהוּ לֶחִי, let him give his cheek to the one who strikes him). The Lamentations passage is especially resonant as a near-parallel to Jesus' teaching here.

### 6:38 -- Measure for measure
- **Hebrew**: כִּי בַמִּדָּה אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם מוֹדֲדִים יִמַּד לָכֶם
- **Note**: This principle of measure for measure (middah k'neged middah) is a foundational rabbinic concept. The Hebrew formulation is natural and echoes the principle as stated in m.Sotah 1:7. The vocabulary (מִדָּה, מָדַד) is standard biblical Hebrew.

### 6:42 -- הֶחָנֵף for "Hypocrita"
- **Hebrew**: הֶחָנֵף הָסֵר בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה אֶת-הַקּוֹרָה מֵעֵינְךָ
- **Note**: חָנֵף (godless/profane/hypocrite) is the standard Delitzsch rendering for ὑποκριτής/hypocrita. In biblical Hebrew, חָנֵף primarily means "godless/profane" (Job 8:13, 13:16, 15:34, Isa 9:16) rather than "hypocrite" in the theatrical sense. However, the semantic overlap is sufficient and the word became standard for "hypocrite" in later Hebrew usage.

### 6:48-49 -- House on rock vs. sand
- **Hebrew**: עַל-הַצּוּר (on the rock), עַל-הַקַּרְקַע (on the ground)
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "super petram" (on rock) vs. "super terram sine fundamento" (on the ground without a foundation). The Hebrew צוּר (rock/cliff) is excellent, echoing Deut 32:4 where God is called הַצּוּר (the Rock). The contrast between building on צוּר and on קַרְקַע (bare ground) is clear and idiomatic. The word שֵׁטֶף (flood/torrent) at 6:48 is good biblical Hebrew (Dan 9:26, Nah 1:8).

## Other Notes

### 6:13 -- שְׁלִיחִים for "apostolos"
The Hebrew renders ἀποστόλους / apostolos as שְׁלִיחִים (sent ones/emissaries), from the root שׁלח (to send). This is the standard Hebrew equivalent and reflects the concept of שָׁלִיחַ (agent/emissary) well attested in rabbinic law (שְׁלוּחוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כְּמוֹתוֹ, a man's agent is as himself, m.Berakhot 5:5). The word choice correctly prioritizes meaning over transliteration.

### 6:20-26 -- Lukan vs. Matthean Beatitudes
Luke's version of the Beatitudes differs from Matthew's in several ways: they are addressed in the second person, they include material blessings ("you who hunger now" rather than "who hunger for righteousness"), and they include corresponding woes. The Hebrew effectively captures all these Lukan distinctives. The pairing of אַשְׁרֵי and אוֹי recalls the blessings and curses of Deut 27-28.

### 6:31 -- The Golden Rule
The Hebrew renders the Golden Rule as וְכַאֲשֶׁר תִּרְצוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשׂוּ לָכֶם בְּנֵי הָאָדָם כֵּן תַּעֲשׂוּ-לָהֶם גַּם-אַתֶּם. The use of the שֶׁ- relative particle (rather than אֲשֶׁר) is Mishnaic rather than biblical. The famous negative formulation attributed to Hillel (שנאוי עליך לחברך לא תעביד, what is hateful to you, do not do to your fellow) in b.Shabbat 31a provides the rabbinic background for this teaching.
