# Revelation Chapter 1 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 20 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 1:5 — "lavit" vs. "lysanti/lusanti"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *lavit nos* ("washed us"), while the SBLGNT reads *lysanti* ("freed/loosed us"). The Hebrew follows the Greek with *ge'alanu* (redeemed us) rather than "washed." This is a classic textual variant: the Vulgate follows the Byzantine/TR reading *lousanti* (washed), while the SBLGNT follows *lysanti* (loosed/freed).
- **Vulgate**: *et lavit nos a peccatis nostris in sanguine suo*
- **Greek**: *kai lysanti hemas ek ton hamartion hemon en to haimati autou*
- **Hebrew**: *uve'damo ge'alanu mechatto'teinu* — "and by his blood redeemed us from our sins"
- **Note**: The Hebrew *ga'al* (redeem) sides with the Greek *lyo* (loose) rather than the Vulgate's *lavo* (wash). For Vulgate conformity, a word like *rachatz* (wash, cf. Ps 51:4) would be more appropriate. This is a significant textual variant between the Alexandrian/critical tradition and the Vulgate tradition.

### 1:5 — "princeps" vs. "archon"
- **Issue**: Minor. The Vulgate *princeps regum terrae* and Greek *ho archon ton basileon tes ges* agree. The Hebrew renders this as *'elyon lemalkhei-aretz* ("supreme over the kings of the earth"), using *'elyon* (Most High) rather than a more literal "ruler" (*moshel* or *sar*). The word *'elyon* carries strong theological overtones (cf. Gen 14:18-20, Ps 83:19) that go beyond the source texts.
- **Hebrew**: *ve'elyon lemalkhei-aretz*
- **Note**: While theologically resonant, *sar malkhei ha'aretz* would be more literal. The Delitzsch choice adds a Christological elevation not explicitly in the Vulgate or Greek.

### 1:8 — "principium et finis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes *principium et finis* ("beginning and end") after "alpha et omega." The SBLGNT does not have this phrase in 1:8 (it appears only in 21:6 and 22:13 in the critical text). The Hebrew includes *rosh vasof* ("beginning and end"), aligning with the Vulgate/TR tradition.
- **Vulgate**: *Ego sum alpha et omega, principium et finis*
- **Greek**: *Ego eimi to Alpha kai to O* (no "beginning and end")
- **Hebrew**: *Ani ha'Alef veha-Tav rosh vasof*
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate/TR fuller reading. Good.

### 1:9 — "in Christo Jesu" vs. "in Jesu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *in Christo Jesu* while the SBLGNT reads simply *en Iesou*. The Hebrew has the full form *lema'an Yeshua haMashiach*, aligning with the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *et patientia in Christo Jesu*
- **Greek**: *kai hypomone en Iesou*
- **Hebrew**: *uvsavlanut lema'an Yeshua haMashiach*
- **Note**: Hebrew aligns with Vulgate. Good.

### 1:11 — Alpha/Omega self-identification
- **Issue**: The Hebrew and Vulgate/TR include Christ's self-identification "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the First and the Last" at the beginning of v. 11 before the command to write. The SBLGNT omits this, beginning directly with the command to write. The Hebrew includes it: *va-yomer Ani ha'Alef va-Ani ha-Tav haRishon veha-Acharon*.
- **Vulgate**: *dicentis: Quod vides, scribe in libro* (actually the Vulgate also does not include Alpha/Omega here in modern critical editions of the Clementine)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the TR/Byzantine tradition by including this self-identification. The Clementine Vulgate text provided here begins at "Quod vides," so the Hebrew actually has additional material here. This may need review to confirm the exact Clementine reading.

### 1:17 — "et vivus" placement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate places *et vivus* ("and the living one") at the end of v. 17 while the Greek has *kai ho zon* as the beginning of v. 18. The Hebrew puts *veheChay* at the end of v. 17, aligning with the Vulgate versification.
- **Note**: Good Vulgate alignment on verse division.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 1:1 — chazon
- **Hebrew**: *chazon Yeshua haMashiach*
- **Note**: Excellent. *Chazon* (vision/revelation) is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Isa 1:1, Nahum 1:1, Hab 2:2). Preferable to any modern coinage for "apocalypse."

### 1:4 — qehillot for "ecclesiae"
- **Hebrew**: *qehillot*
- **Note**: *Qehillah/qehillot* is post-biblical/Mishnaic for "congregation." Biblical Hebrew would use *'edah* or *qahal*. However, for NT translation this is a well-established convention in the Delitzsch tradition. Cf. Eccl 12:11 *ba'alei asufot*.

### 1:4 — The divine name periphrasis
- **Hebrew**: *haHoveh veHayah veYavo* ("the One who is, who was, and who comes")
- **Note**: A creative and impressive Hebrew rendering of the Greek participial construction *ho on kai ho en kai ho erchomenos*. This plays on the root *h-y-h* (to be) and captures the theological weight of Ex 3:14. Excellent biblical Hebrew construction.

### 1:7 — Zechariah/Daniel allusion
- **Hebrew**: *hinneh hu va 'im ha'ananim... gam-hemmah asher deqaruhu vesafedu 'alav*
- **Note**: Outstanding. Draws directly on Dan 7:13 LXX (*meta ton nephelon*) for "coming with clouds" and Zech 12:10 (*vedaqaru... vesafedu*) for "pierced... mourn." The Hebrew vocabulary matches the OT source texts precisely. *Mishpechot ha'aretz* for "tribes of the earth" echoes Zech 12:12.

### 1:8 — Elohei Tzeva'ot for "omnipotens"
- **Hebrew**: *Elohei Tzeva'ot* ("God of hosts")
- **Note**: The Vulgate has *omnipotens* and the Greek *pantokrator*. Delitzsch renders this with the classic biblical Hebrew *Elohei Tzeva'ot* (cf. 1 Sam 1:3, Isa 6:3), which is the standard LXX translation equivalent (*Pantokrator* often renders *Tzeva'ot* in the LXX). Excellent choice with deep OT resonance.

### 1:9 — savlanut
- **Hebrew**: *savlanut* (patience/endurance)
- **Note**: This is Mishnaic/post-biblical Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use something like *tokheleth* (hope/waiting) or a construction with *savol* (to bear). The abstract noun *savlanut* is not attested in the Hebrew Bible.

### 1:12 — menorot
- **Hebrew**: *sheva menorot zahav*
- **Note**: Excellent. *Menorah/menorot* is pure biblical Hebrew (Ex 25:31ff., Zech 4:2). The connection to the tabernacle/temple lampstand is theologically loaded and appropriate.

### 1:13 — ben-adam
- **Hebrew**: *demut ben-adam*
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Ben-adam* directly echoes Dan 7:13 (*ke-var enash* in Aramaic, rendered *ben-adam* in Hebrew). *Demut* ("likeness") echoes Ezek 1:26 (*demut ke-mar'eh adam*). Multiple OT allusions captured in two words.

### 1:14 — White hair and fiery eyes
- **Hebrew**: *levaniem ke-tzemer tzachar ka-shaleg ve'einav ke-labat esh*
- **Note**: Directly echoes Dan 7:9 (*u-se'ar re'sheh ke-'amar neqe*) for the white hair and Dan 10:6 (*ve'einav ke-lapidei esh*) for the fiery eyes. *Labat esh* is from Ex 3:2 (the burning bush). Excellent OT allusions.

### 1:15 — nechoshet qalal
- **Hebrew**: *nechoshet qalal*
- **Note**: *Nechoshet qalal* ("burnished bronze") is from Ezek 1:7 (the feet of the living creatures). Excellent — captures the Ezekiel theophany connection. *Qol mayim rabbim* ("sound of many waters") is from Ezek 1:24 and 43:2.

### 1:18 — maftechot She'ol vaMavet
- **Hebrew**: *maftechot She'ol vaMavet*
- **Note**: The Vulgate has *claves mortis et inferni*. The Hebrew reverses the order (She'ol then Mavet vs. mortis then inferni), following a more natural Hebrew pairing. *She'ol* for *inferni* (Hades/underworld) is standard biblical Hebrew. *Maftechot* (keys) is attested in Judg 3:25 and 1 Chr 9:27.

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Revelation 1 is among the most OT-allusion-dense chapters in the NT. The Delitzsch translation handles these connections with notable skill:
- **Dan 7:9, 13**: Son of Man, white hair, clouds (vv. 7, 13-14)
- **Dan 10:5-6**: Priestly garment, golden belt, fiery eyes, burnished feet (vv. 13-15)
- **Zech 12:10, 12**: Pierced one, mourning (v. 7)
- **Ezek 1:7, 24, 26**: Bronze feet, sound of waters, human likeness (vv. 13, 15)
- **Ezek 43:2**: Voice like many waters (v. 15)
- **Ex 3:2, 14**: Burning flame, divine name theology (vv. 4, 14)
- **Isa 44:6, 48:12**: First and Last (vv. 11, 17)
- **Ex 19:16**: Trumpet voice (v. 10)

### Theological Note — 1:8
The Hebrew *ne'um YHVH* ("oracle of the LORD") uses the prophetic formula found throughout Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the minor prophets. Applying it here to the speaker (who is identified as Alpha and Omega, contextually Christ) makes a strong implicit claim about Christ's divine identity, as *ne'um YHVH* is exclusively reserved for God in the Hebrew Bible.
