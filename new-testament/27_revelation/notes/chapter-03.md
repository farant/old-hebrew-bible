# Revelation Chapter 3 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 22 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 3:1 — "opera tua" placement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *Scio opera tua*, and the Greek likewise *Oida sou ta erga*. The Hebrew correctly includes *yada'ti et-ma'asekha*. All three align here.
- **Note**: No divergence.

### 3:14 — "principium creaturae Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *principium creaturae Dei*, the Greek *he arche tes ktiseos tou theou*, and the Hebrew *reshit bri'at ha-Elohim* ("the beginning of God's creation") all align. This is a theologically sensitive verse (Arian controversy). The Hebrew *reshit* can mean "beginning" or "firstfruits/chief," paralleling the ambiguity of *arche*.
- **Vulgate**: *qui est principium creaturae Dei*
- **Hebrew**: *reshit bri'at ha-Elohim*
- **Note**: *Reshit* echoes Prov 8:22 (*YHVH qanani reshit darko*, "The LORD possessed me at the beginning of His way"), connecting Christ to personified Wisdom. The Hebrew is well-chosen for this theologically loaded phrase, as *reshit* in Prov 8:22 is understood in Catholic tradition as referring to the eternal generation of the Son.

### 3:16 — "incipiam te evomere"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *incipiam te evomere* ("I will begin to vomit you out"), using the inceptive *incipiam*. The Greek has *mello se emesai* ("I am about to vomit you out"). The Hebrew has *aqia'akha mi-pi* ("I will vomit you from my mouth") without the inceptive/imminent nuance.
- **Vulgate**: *incipiam te evomere ex ore meo*
- **Greek**: *mello se emesai ek tou stomatos mou*
- **Hebrew**: *aqia'akha mi-pi*
- **Note**: The Hebrew lacks the *mello* ("am about to") / *incipiam* ("begin to") nuance which softens the threat slightly. This is a minor but theologically meaningful difference — the Vulgate/Greek suggest imminence rather than accomplished fact.

### 3:17 — "Hosea 12 allusion in Hebrew"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew *matza'ti on li* ("I have found wealth for myself") appears to echo Hos 12:9 (*akh 'ashirti matza'ti on li*). The Vulgate *locupletatus* ("enriched") and Greek *peploutenka* ("I have become rich") do not carry this specific OT allusion.
- **Hebrew**: *akh 'asharti matza'ti on li velo chasarti kol*
- **Note**: The Delitzsch translation introduces a strong Hosea allusion that enriches the text. Hosea 12:9 is spoken by Ephraim boasting of self-made wealth — a perfect thematic parallel with Laodicean self-satisfaction.

### 3:19 — "aemulare" vs. "zeleue"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *aemulare* ("be zealous") and Greek *zeleue* ("be zealous") agree. The Hebrew renders this as *tishqod* ("keep watch/be vigilant"), which shifts the meaning from zeal to watchfulness.
- **Vulgate**: *Aemulare ergo, et poenitentiam age*
- **Hebrew**: *lakhen tishqod vetashub*
- **Note**: *Tishqod* (from *sh-q-d*, "watch/be alert") is a different concept from zeal (*qin'ah*). The Hebrew should ideally use *qaneh* (be zealous) to match the Vulgate. Cf. 1 Kgs 19:10 *qanno qinneti la-YHVH* for biblical Hebrew zeal language.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:2 — she'erit for "cetera"
- **Hebrew**: *chazzeq et ha-she'erit ha-qerovah la-mut*
- **Note**: Excellent. *She'erit* ("remnant") is deeply resonant biblical vocabulary (Isa 10:21-22, Jer 23:3, Mic 2:12). While the Vulgate simply has *cetera* ("the rest/remaining things"), the Hebrew choice evokes the prophetic "remnant theology" — theologically appropriate for a church that is dying.

### 3:3 — ganav for "fur"
- **Hebrew**: *hinneni va 'alekha ka-gannav*
- **Note**: *Gannav* ("thief") is standard biblical Hebrew (Ex 22:1, Joel 2:9). The thief-coming motif connects to Jesus's eschatological teaching (Matt 24:43 / 1 Thess 5:2) and echoes Joel 2:9.

### 3:7 — mafteach David
- **Hebrew**: *asher be-yado mafteach David ha-poteach ve'ein soger ve-soger ve'ein poteach*
- **Note**: Outstanding. This directly echoes Isa 22:22 (*ve-natatti mafteach bet-David al-shikhmo u-fatach ve'ein soger ve-sagar ve'ein poteach*). The Hebrew replicates the Isaiah source nearly verbatim. The key of David passage is one of the most important Christological appropriations from Isaiah.

### 3:12 — amud be-heikhal
- **Hebrew**: *et'nenu le-'amud be-heikhal Elohai*
- **Note**: *'Amud* ("pillar") echoes the temple pillars Jachin and Boaz (1 Kgs 7:21). *Heikhal* ("temple/palace") is standard biblical Hebrew for the inner sanctuary (1 Kgs 6:5, Ps 11:4). The entire verse resonates with Ezekiel's temple vision.

### 3:14 — ha-Amen
- **Hebrew**: *ha-Amen ha-'ed ha-ne'eman veha-amiti*
- **Note**: Using *Amen* as a divine title echoes Isa 65:16 (*Elohei amen*, "God of truth/Amen"). The juxtaposition with *ne'eman* ("faithful") creates a Hebrew wordplay on the root *'-m-n*, from which both *amen* and *ne'eman* derive. Excellent.

### 3:17 — Hosea vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: *umlal... ve-davvai ve-'ani ve-'ivver ve-'erom*
- **Note**: *Umlal* ("wretched") is from Ps 6:3. *Davvai* ("faint/sick") appears in Jer 8:18 and Lam 1:22. *'Ani* ("poor/afflicted") is standard biblical Hebrew. *'Ivver* ("blind") and *'erom* ("naked") are common OT vocabulary. The string of adjectives has a prophetic catalogue quality reminiscent of Isaiah's woe oracles.

### 3:20 — Petach/dofek
- **Hebrew**: *hinneni 'omed ba-petach ve-dofeq*
- **Note**: The knocking at the door echoes Song of Songs 5:2 (*qol dodi dofeq*, "the voice of my beloved is knocking"). *Petach* ("door/opening") is used throughout the OT. The Hebrew beautifully captures the Song of Songs allusion that many commentators identify in this verse, making it more explicit than either the Latin or Greek.

## Other Notes

### Theological Note — 3:14
The title *reshit bri'at ha-Elohim* must be understood in light of Prov 8:22 and Col 1:15-18 (where Christ is *prototokos pases ktiseos*). The Catholic reading, following Nicene orthodoxy, understands *arche/principium/reshit* as "source/origin" rather than "first thing created." The Hebrew *reshit* supports this, as in Gen 1:1 *reshit* indicates primacy and sovereignty, not mere temporal priority.
