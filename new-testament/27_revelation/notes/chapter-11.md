# Revelation Chapter 11 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 19 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 11:1 — "templum Dei" vs. "ton naon tou theou" vs. "heikhal YHVH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *templum Dei* ("temple of God"), Greek *ton naon tou theou* ("the sanctuary of God"). The Hebrew has *heikhal YHVH* ("the temple of the LORD"), using the Tetragrammaton where both the Vulgate and Greek have *Dei/theou* ("God"). This is a significant theological choice by Delitzsch — substituting the divine name.
- **Vulgate**: *templum Dei*
- **Hebrew**: *heikhal YHVH*
- **Note**: The Hebrew elevates the reference by using the covenant name. This is consistent with OT temple language (*heikhal YHVH* appears frequently: 1 Sam 1:9, 3:3, 2 Kgs 18:16, Ps 11:4, Mal 3:1). However, the Vulgate and Greek do not use the divine name here.

### 11:1 — "Surge, et metire" — Vulgate addition
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes *Surge* ("Rise up/Stand up") before *metire* ("measure"). The SBLGNT has *Egeire kai metreson* ("Rise and measure"). The Hebrew has *qum umod* ("rise and measure"). All three include the command to rise. However, the Vulgate reading also includes *et angelus stabat* which is not in the Greek.
- **Hebrew**: *va-ya'amod ha-mal'akh va-yomer qum umod*
- **Note**: The Hebrew includes *va-ya'amod ha-mal'akh* ("and the angel stood"), which appears to follow a TR/Byzantine expansion not present in the SBLGNT. The Vulgate does not explicitly include this angel standing here either. Possible over-translation.

### 11:2 — "atrium" vs. "aulen" vs. "chatzer"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *atrium* ("court"), Greek *aulen* ("court"), Hebrew *chatzer* ("court/enclosure"). All align. *Chatzer* is the standard term for the temple court (Ex 27:9, 1 Kgs 6:36, Ezek 40:17ff.).
- **Note**: Good alignment. The measuring of the temple and exclusion of the outer court echoes Ezek 40-42 (Ezekiel's temple measurement vision).

### 11:3 — "duobus testibus meis" — speaker identity
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *dabo duobus testibus meis* ("I will give to my two witnesses") has a first-person divine speaker ("my witnesses"). The Greek similarly *doso tois dysin martysin mou* ("I will give to my two witnesses"). The Hebrew *venatatti lishnei edai* ("and I will give to my two witnesses") preserves the divine first person. The question is who speaks — Christ, God, or the angel?
- **Note**: The first-person speaker here is typically identified as Christ. The Hebrew *edai* ("my witnesses") uses the standard legal/covenantal witness term (*ed*, cf. Deut 19:15, Isa 43:10, 44:8).

### 11:4 — Zechariah 4 allusion
- **Issue**: The identification of the two witnesses as "two olive trees and two lampstands" is drawn from Zech 4:3, 11-14. The Vulgate *duae olivae et duo candelabra* ("two olives and two lampstands"), Greek *hai dyo elaiai kai hai dyo lychniai* ("the two olive trees and the two lampstands"), and Hebrew *shnei ha-zeitim u-shtei ha-menorot* all align.
- **Vulgate**: *in conspectu Domini terrae* ("before the Lord of the earth")
- **Hebrew**: *lifnei Elohei ha-aretz* ("before the God of the earth")
- **Note**: The Vulgate *Domini* and Greek *kyriou* are rendered *Elohei* ("God of") rather than *Adonei* ("Lord of") in Hebrew. The phrase *Elohei ha-aretz* echoes Zech 4:14 (*shnei venei ha-yitzhar ha-omdim al Adon kol ha-aretz*, "two sons of oil who stand before the Lord of all the earth"). Delitzsch uses *Elohei* where the source has *Adon*/*Dominus*, a minor shift.

### 11:5 — Fire from the mouth
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree: fire proceeds from their mouths and devours enemies. The Hebrew *esh yotze'et mi-pihem okheleth et oyeveihem* echoes 2 Kgs 1:10-12 (fire from heaven consuming Elijah's adversaries) and Jer 5:14 (*hineni noten devarai be-fikha le-esh*, "I will make my words in your mouth fire").
- **Note**: The Jer 5:14 allusion is particularly apt — God makes the prophet's words into fire. The Delitzsch translation captures this.

### 11:6 — Elijah and Moses powers
- **Issue**: The two witnesses exercise powers associated with Elijah (shutting heaven from rain, cf. 1 Kgs 17:1) and Moses (turning water to blood, striking with plagues, cf. Ex 7:17, 19-20). All three traditions align. The Hebrew uses vocabulary from the source texts: *la'atzor et ha-shamayim velo yihyeh matar* echoes 1 Kgs 8:35 (*be-he'atzer shamayim velo yihyeh matar*), and *lehafkham le-dam* echoes Ex 7:17 (*nehefkhu le-dam*).
- **Note**: Outstanding. The Hebrew precisely replicates the OT source vocabulary for both the Elijah and Moses traditions.

### 11:7 — "bestia" vs. "therion" vs. "chayyah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *bestia* ("beast"), Greek *to therion* ("the beast"), Hebrew *ha-chayyah* ("the living creature/beast"). This is the first appearance of "the beast" in Revelation. Using *chayyah* is potentially confusing, as the same word was used for the four holy *chayyot* ("living creatures") at the throne in chapters 4-5. The Greek distinguishes between *zoa* (holy creatures, 4:6ff.) and *therion* (the beast, 11:7ff.), but the Hebrew uses *chayyah/chayyot* for both.
- **Hebrew**: *ha-chayyah ha-olah mi-tehom*
- **Note**: The ambiguity of using *chayyah* for both holy creatures and the beast is a limitation of the Hebrew vocabulary. Context must disambiguate. The phrase *ha-olah mi-tehom* ("ascending from the abyss") clearly marks this as the evil beast, contrasting with the *chayyot* who surround the throne.

### 11:8 — "spiritualiter" vs. "pneumatikos" vs. "kefi ha-ruach"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *spiritualiter* ("spiritually/figuratively"), Greek *pneumatikos* ("spiritually"), Hebrew *kefi ha-ruach* ("according to the spirit/spiritually"). All agree that "Sodom and Egypt" are spiritual/allegorical names for the city. The Hebrew *kefi ha-ruach* captures the sense well.
- **Note**: Good alignment. The identification of Jerusalem with Sodom echoes Isa 1:10 (*qetzinei Sedom... am Amorah*, "rulers of Sodom... people of Gomorrah") and Ezek 16:46-49.

### 11:8 — "ubi et Dominus eorum crucifixus est"
- **Issue**: All three traditions identify the city as where "their Lord was crucified." The Hebrew *asher sham nitzlav gam Adoneihem* ("where also their Lord was crucified") uses *nitzlav* (from the root *tz-l-v*, "crucify"). *Tzlav* is not biblical Hebrew — it enters Hebrew from Aramaic/Syriac and is standard in post-biblical and modern Hebrew for crucifixion.
- **Note**: No biblical Hebrew word for crucifixion exists. *Nitzlav* is the standard Delitzsch-tradition rendering. An OT-flavored alternative might be *talu* ("hung," cf. Deut 21:22-23), which connects to Paul's Gal 3:13 citation.

### 11:11 — "spiritus vitae a Deo" — Ezekiel 37
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *spiritus vitae a Deo intravit in eos* ("the spirit of life from God entered into them"), Greek *pneuma zoes ek tou theou eiselthen en autois*. The Hebrew *va-tavo vahem ruach chayyim me-et ha-Elohim* ("and a spirit of life from God entered them") directly echoes Ezek 37:10 (*va-tavo vahem ha-ruach va-yichyu*, "and the spirit entered them and they lived") from the Valley of Dry Bones.
- **Note**: Outstanding. The Hebrew replicates the Ezekiel 37 resurrection language precisely. *Va-tavo vahem ruach* is nearly verbatim from Ezekiel.

### 11:15 — "Factum est regnum hujus mundi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Factum est regnum hujus mundi Domini nostri et Christi ejus* ("The kingdom of this world has become our Lord's and His Christ's"). The Greek *Egeneto he basileia tou kosmou tou kyriou hemon kai tou christou autou* ("The kingdom of the world has become our Lord's and His Christ's"). The Hebrew *hinneh mamlekhet ha-olam hayetah la-Adoneinu veli-meshicho* ("behold, the kingdom of the world has become our Lord's and His Messiah's").
- **Note**: Good alignment. *Mamlekhet ha-olam* ("kingdom of the world") and *meshicho* ("His anointed") echo Ps 2:2 (*al YHVH ve-al meshicho*, "against the LORD and against His anointed"). The doxology connects to the messianic coronation psalm.

### 11:17 — "qui venturus es" — Vulgate addition
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds *et qui venturus es* ("and who is to come") to the divine title. The SBLGNT omits this third element, reading only *ho on kai ho en* ("who is and who was"). The Hebrew includes the third element: *ha-hoveh ve-hayah ve-yavo* ("who is and was and will come"), following the Vulgate/TR.
- **Vulgate**: *qui es, et qui eras, et qui venturus es*
- **Greek**: *ho on kai ho en* (SBLGNT omits "who is to come")
- **Hebrew**: *ha-hoveh ve-hayah ve-yavo*
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate in including the third member. Good Vulgate alignment. Some scholars note the SBLGNT's omission as theologically significant — at this point in the narrative (the seventh trumpet), the future element is fulfilled; He has already come.

### 11:18 — "exterminandi" vs. "diaphtheirai" vs. "lehashchit"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *exterminandi eos qui corruperunt terram* ("to destroy those who corrupted the earth"), Greek *diaphtheirai tous diaphtheirontas ten gen* ("to destroy the destroyers of the earth"). The Hebrew *ulehashchit et mashchitei ha-aretz* ("to destroy the destroyers of the earth") creates a wordplay on the root *sh-ch-t* ("destroy/corrupt") — the same root in both verb and object.
- **Note**: Excellent. The *sh-ch-t* root wordplay (destroying the destroyers) mirrors the Greek *diaphtheirai tous diaphtheirontas* and echoes Gen 6:11-13, where the earth is *nishchatah* ("corrupted") and God says *mashchitam* ("I will destroy them"). The corruption-destruction wordplay on *sh-ch-t* is a hallmark of Genesis flood language.

### 11:19 — Ark of the Covenant
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree that the ark of the covenant (*arca testamenti* / *he kibotos tes diathekes* / *aron berito*) is visible in the heavenly temple. The Hebrew *aron berito* ("ark of his covenant") echoes Num 10:33, Josh 3:3, and throughout the OT historical books. The manifestation is accompanied by theophanic phenomena (lightning, voices, thunder, earthquake, hail).
- **Note**: The reappearance of the *aron* in the heavenly temple is theologically powerful — the earthly ark was lost after the Babylonian exile (cf. 2 Macc 2:4-8, Jer 3:16). Its heavenly presence affirms the continuity of the covenant.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 11:1 — qaneh for "calamus"
- **Hebrew**: *qaneh vehu ke-matteh* ("a reed, and it was like a staff")
- **Note**: *Qaneh* ("reed") as a measuring instrument echoes Ezek 40:3, 5 (*qaneh ha-middah*, "the measuring reed"). The entire measuring scene reprises Ezekiel's temple measurement vision (Ezek 40-42). *Matteh* ("staff/rod") echoes the rod of Moses and Aaron (Ex 4:2, 7:9, Num 17:21).

### 11:4 — zeitim u-menorot
- **Hebrew**: *shnei ha-zeitim u-shtei ha-menorot*
- **Note**: *Zeitim* ("olive trees") and *menorot* ("lampstands") directly from Zech 4:3 (*shnei zeitim aleha*, "two olive trees by it") and Zech 4:2 (*menorat zahav*, "golden lampstand"). The Zechariah passage is about Zerubbabel (royal) and Joshua (priestly) — the two anointed ones who mediate between God and His people.

### 11:13 — Elohei ha-shamayim
- **Hebrew**: *va-yittenu kavod le-Elohei ha-shamayim*
- **Note**: *Elohei ha-shamayim* ("God of heaven") is a title found in post-exilic texts: Ezra 1:2, 5:12, 6:9, 10, 7:12, 21, 23; Neh 1:4, 5, 2:4, 20; Dan 2:18, 19, 37, 44. It became the standard Jewish designation for God when speaking to or about Gentile audiences. Its use here, following the earthquake, suggests the survivors' acknowledgment of God matches the post-exilic pattern of Gentile recognition.

### 11:15 — yimlokh le-olmei olamim
- **Hebrew**: *vehu yimlokh le-olmei olamim*
- **Note**: *Yimlokh le-olmei olamim* ("He shall reign forever and ever") echoes Ex 15:18 (*YHVH yimlokh le-olam va-ed*, "the LORD shall reign forever and ever") from the Song of the Sea. The seventh trumpet thus announces the fulfillment of the Exodus promise. Also cf. Dan 7:14 (*sholtneh sholtan alam*, "his dominion is an everlasting dominion").

### 11:18 — mashchitei ha-aretz
- **Hebrew**: *ulehashchit et mashchitei ha-aretz*
- **Note**: As noted in Content Alignment, the *sh-ch-t* root wordplay is outstanding. This also echoes Jer 51:25 (*har ha-mashchit*, "destroying mountain" — a title for Babylon). The destroyers of the earth face divine retribution in kind.

### 11:19 — aron berito
- **Hebrew**: *va-yera aron berito be-heikhalo*
- **Note**: *Aron berit YHVH* is the full OT form (Deut 10:8, Josh 3:3, 1 Sam 4:3). Delitzsch shortens to *aron berito* ("ark of his covenant") using a pronoun suffix. The heavenly temple scene recapitulates the Sinai theophany: lightning (*beraqim*), voices (*qolot*), thunder (*re'amim*), earthquake (*ra'ash*), and hail (*barad*) — matching Ex 19:16-18 and the seventh plague (Ex 9:23-24).

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 11 is among the most OT-dense chapters in Revelation:
- **Ezek 40-42**: Temple measurement — v. 1
- **Zech 4:2-14**: Two olive trees and lampstands — v. 4
- **1 Kgs 17:1**: Elijah shutting heaven from rain — v. 6
- **Ex 7:17-20**: Moses turning water to blood — v. 6
- **2 Kgs 1:10-12**: Fire consuming enemies — v. 5
- **Jer 5:14**: Prophet's words as fire — v. 5
- **Ezek 37:10**: Spirit of life entering the dead — v. 11
- **Ps 2:1-2**: Nations raging against the LORD and His anointed — v. 18
- **Gen 6:11-13**: Destroying the destroyers (flood wordplay) — v. 18
- **Ex 15:18**: The LORD reigns forever — v. 15
- **Dan 7:14**: Everlasting dominion — v. 15

### Theological Note — 11:15
The seventh trumpet is the climactic moment of the first half of Revelation. The declaration that *mamlekhet ha-olam hayetah la-Adoneinu veli-meshicho* brings together Ps 2 (messianic coronation), Ex 15:18 (divine kingship), and Dan 7:14 (everlasting dominion). The Hebrew Delitzsch translation captures all three allusions through vocabulary shared with these OT source texts. This is arguably the most theologically condensed verse in these chapters.
