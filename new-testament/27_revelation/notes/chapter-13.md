# Revelation Chapter 13 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The SBLGNT begins chapter 13 at 13:1 with *Kai eidon ek tes thalasses therion anabainon* ("And I saw a beast rising out of the sea"), without the preliminary clause about standing on the seashore. The Vulgate similarly begins 13:1 with *Et vidi de mari bestiam ascendentem* ("And I saw from the sea a beast rising"). The Hebrew, however, prefixes 13:1 with the 12:18 content: *va-ehi omed al chol ha-yam* ("and I was standing on the sand of the sea"), absorbing the previous verse into the opening of chapter 13. See notes at 12:18 for the textual variant (first person vs. third person).

All 18 verses otherwise align across the three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 13:1 — Word order: "capita septem et cornua decem" vs. Greek order
- **Vulgate**: *habentem capita septem, et cornua decem* ("having seven heads and ten horns") — heads first, then horns.
- **Greek**: *echon kerata deka kai kephalas hepta* ("having ten horns and seven heads") — horns first, then heads.
- **Hebrew**: *ve-lah eser qeranaim ve-shiv'ah rashim* ("and it had ten horns and seven heads") — horns first, then heads.
- **Flag**: The Hebrew follows the Greek word order (horns then heads) against the Vulgate (heads then horns). The Daniel source (Dan 7:7) describes horns prominently, so the Greek/Hebrew order may be influenced by the Danielic source. Minor, but since we prioritize the Vulgate, worth noting.

### 13:1 — "nomina blasphemiae" (plural vs. singular)
- **Vulgate**: *nomina blasphemiae* ("names of blasphemy") — plural *nomina*
- **Greek**: *onomata blasphemias* — the SBLGNT has a variant; some manuscripts read singular *onoma*
- **Hebrew**: *shemot giddufim* ("names of blasphemies") — plural, matching Vulgate
- **Note**: Good Vulgate alignment on the plural form.

### 13:2 — Daniel 7 composite beast
- **Vulgate**: *similis erat pardo... pedes ursi... os leonis* ("like a leopard... feet of a bear... mouth of a lion")
- **Greek**: *homoion pardalei... arkou... leontos*
- **Hebrew**: *ke-mar'eh ha-namer... raglei dov... pi aryeh* ("like the appearance of a leopard... feet of a bear... mouth of a lion")
- **Note**: All three align. The Hebrew vocabulary directly echoes Dan 7:4-6 where the four beasts appear: *aryeh* (lion, Dan 7:4), *dov* (bear, Dan 7:5), *namer* (leopard, Dan 7:6). The composite beast of Rev 13 combines all three Danielic kingdoms. The Hebrew uses the exact same animal vocabulary as Daniel, making the allusion transparent.

### 13:2 — "virtutem suam" vs. "ten dynamin autou" vs. "et kocho"
- **Vulgate**: *virtutem suam, et potestatem magnam* ("his power and great authority")
- **Greek**: *ten dynamin autou kai ton thronon autou kai exousian megalen* ("his power and his throne and great authority")
- **Hebrew**: *et kocho ve-et kis'o u-mimshal rav* ("his strength and his throne and great dominion")
- **Note**: Both the Greek and Hebrew include "throne" (*thronon*/*kis'o*), which the Vulgate omits. The Hebrew follows the Greek in including the throne, diverging from the Vulgate. This is a significant detail — the dragon giving his *throne* to the beast amplifies the parody of divine investiture.
- **Flag**: Hebrew includes "throne" following Greek against Vulgate.

### 13:4 — "Quis similis bestiae?" — Exodus echo
- **Hebrew**: *mi yidmeh la-chayyah u-mi yukhal le-hilachem ittah* ("who is like the beast and who can fight against it?")
- **Note**: The question *mi yidmeh* ("who is like?") is a deliberate blasphemous parody of Ex 15:11 (*mi kamokha ba-elim YHVH*, "who is like you among the gods, O LORD?") and Ps 35:10 (*YHVH mi kamokha*, "LORD, who is like you?"). The name *Mikha'el* itself means "who is like God?" — making the beast's worshippers directly counter the archangel's name and mission from chapter 12. The Hebrew preserves this wordplay.

### 13:5 — "[milchamah]" — Hebrew bracketed insertion
- **Hebrew**: *ve-sholtan nittan lah la'asot [milchamah] arba'im u-shnayim chodashim* ("and authority was given to it to make [war] for forty-two months")
- **Note**: The Hebrew includes *milchamah* ("war") in brackets, indicating it may be a textual addition. The Vulgate reads *data est ei potestas facere menses quadraginta duos* ("authority was given to it to act for forty-two months") — without specifying "war." The Greek similarly reads *exousia poiesai menas tesserakonta dyo* ("authority to act for forty-two months"). The bracketed word *milchamah* appears to be an interpolation, possibly influenced by 13:7 (*bellum facere cum sanctis*). This is an expansion beyond both the Vulgate and the SBLGNT.
- **Flag**: Bracketed *milchamah* is an addition not in the Vulgate or Greek.

### 13:5 — "os loquens magna et blasphemias" / Daniel 7:8 allusion
- **Hebrew**: *peh memallel gedolot ve-ne'atzot* ("a mouth speaking great things and blasphemies")
- **Note**: *Peh memallel gedolot* directly echoes Dan 7:8 (*u-fum memallel ravrevan*, "and a mouth speaking great things" in Aramaic) and Dan 7:20 (*u-fum memallel ravrevan*). The Hebrew translates the Aramaic *ravrevan* with *gedolot*, maintaining the Daniel 7 connection.

### 13:8 — "ab origine mundi" / "apo kataboles kosmou" / "mi-yom hivvased tevel"
- **Vulgate**: *Agni, qui occisus est ab origine mundi* ("the Lamb, who was slain from the foundation of the world")
- **Greek**: *tou arniou tou esphagmenou apo kataboles kosmou*
- **Hebrew**: *la-seh ha-tavuach mi-yom hivvased tevel* ("the Lamb who was slaughtered from the day the world was founded")
- **Note**: The phrase *mi-yom hivvased tevel* ("from the day the world was founded") uses the niphal of *y-s-d* ("to found/establish"), echoing Prov 8:23 (*me-olam nissakti me-rosh mi-qadmei aretz*, "from eternity I was set up, from the beginning, before the earth"). *Tevel* ("world") rather than *olam* for *kosmou*/*mundi* is consistent with Delitzsch's usage. The grammatical question of whether *ab origine mundi* modifies "slain" or "written" (i.e., the Lamb slain from the foundation or names written from the foundation) is an ancient debate. The Vulgate syntax most naturally attaches it to "slain," and the Hebrew follows this reading.

### 13:10 — "Qui in captivitatem duxerit" — Jeremiah 15:2 allusion
- **Vulgate**: *Qui in captivitatem duxerit, in captivitatem vadet*
- **Hebrew**: *ha-molikeh shevi yelekh ba-shevi ve-ha-horeg ba-cherev harog yehareg be-charev*
- **Note**: All three sources align. The Hebrew closely echoes Jer 15:2 (*asher la-mavet la-mavet va-asher la-cherev la-cherev va-asher la-ra'av la-ra'av va-asher la-shevi la-shevi*, "those for death to death, those for the sword to the sword, those for famine to famine, those for captivity to captivity") and Jer 43:11. The lex talionis principle is expressed with classic prophetic vocabulary.

### 13:11 — "similia Agni" / "homoia arnio" / "ke-qarnei ha-seh"
- **Hebrew**: *ve-lah shtei qeranaim ke-qarnei ha-seh u-medabberet ka-tannin* ("and it had two horns like the horns of the Lamb and spoke like a dragon")
- **Note**: The Hebrew expands "like a lamb" to "like the horns of the Lamb" (*ke-qarnei ha-seh*), making the comparison specifically about the horns. This is reasonable given the context (it is the horns being described), but the Vulgate and Greek compare the horns to a lamb as a whole (*similia Agni*/*homoia arnio*). Minor expansion.

### 13:14 — "imaginem bestiae" / "eikona to therio" / "tzelem la-chayyah"
- **Hebrew**: *tzelem la-chayyah* ("an image for the beast")
- **Note**: *Tzelem* ("image/idol") is the term used in Dan 3:1-7 for Nebuchadnezzar's golden image (*tzelem di dehav*, "image of gold"). The connection between the beast's image and Nebuchadnezzar's idol is made explicit through shared vocabulary. The command to worship the image on pain of death (13:15) parallels Dan 3:6 exactly.

### 13:15 — "ut quicumque non adoraverint... occidantur"
- **Hebrew**: *ve-khol asher lo yishtachavu le-tzelem ha-chayyah tasgirem la-mavet* ("and all who will not worship the image of the beast, she will deliver them to death")
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses *tasgirem* ("she will hand them over/deliver them"), where the Vulgate has *occidantur* ("they shall be killed") and the Greek *apoktanthosin* ("they should be killed"). The Hebrew verb *hisgir* ("to deliver over") is weaker than "kill" — it suggests handing over to execution rather than the act of killing itself. This slightly softens the force. *Hisgir* appears in Deut 32:30 (God delivering over) and 1 Sam 23:11-12 (Keilah delivering David). The connotation is judicial/political betrayal rather than direct slaughter.

### 13:16-17 — "caracterem" / "charagma" / "tav"
- **Hebrew**: *yattvu tav al yad yeminam o al mitzchtam* ("they shall mark a mark on their right hand or on their foreheads")
- **Note**: *Tav* is used for the mark/brand. This connects to Ezek 9:4-6, where a *tav* (literally the letter tav, the last letter of the Hebrew alphabet) is placed on the foreheads of the righteous as a mark of protection. The beast's *tav* is thus an anti-sign — a parody of the protective mark of Ezekiel. In the paleo-Hebrew/old Hebrew script, the letter tav was written as a cross shape (+), adding further typological depth.

### 13:18 — "sexcenti sexaginta sex" / 666
- **Hebrew**: *shesh me'ot ve-shishim va-shesh* ("six hundred and sixty-six")
- **Note**: All three sources agree on the number 666. The Hebrew renders it as a standard numeral phrase. The Hebrew text includes *mi she-binah lo yechashev* ("whoever has understanding, let him calculate"), using *binah* ("understanding/discernment") for *intellectum*/*noun*. *Binah* is a wisdom term from Prov 1:2, 2:3, 3:5, 4:5, 7 and is paired with *chokhmah* in the preceding clause (*ba-zot chokhmah*, "in this is wisdom"), echoing Prov 4:5, 7 (*qeneh chokhmah qeneh vinah*, "acquire wisdom, acquire understanding").

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 13:1 — chayyah olah min ha-yam
- **Hebrew**: *va-ere chayyah olah min ha-yam* ("and I saw a beast rising from the sea")
- **Note**: *Chayyah olah min ha-yam* echoes Dan 7:3 (*ve-arba chayyvan ravrevan salqan min yamma*, "and four great beasts coming up from the sea"). The Hebrew *olah* ("ascending") rather than *salqan* (Aramaic) properly hebraizes the Danielic source. As noted at 11:7, the use of *chayyah* for both the holy living creatures and the evil beast creates potential ambiguity, but context resolves it.

### 13:2 — mar'eh/raglei/pi
- **Hebrew**: *mar'eha ke-mar'eh ha-namer ve-ragleha raglei dov u-fiha pi aryeh*
- **Note**: The anatomical description (appearance, feet, mouth) follows the same body-part enumeration pattern as Dan 7:4-6. Delitzsch uses *mar'eh* ("appearance"), which is standard biblical Hebrew for visionary descriptions (Dan 8:15, 10:18, Ezek 1:5, 13, 16, 26-28). The construct chains *raglei dov*, *pi aryeh* are properly formed.

### 13:4 — va-yishtachavu
- **Hebrew**: *va-yishtachavu la-tannin... va-yishtachavu la-chayyah*
- **Note**: The hithpael *va-yishtachavu* ("and they worshipped/prostrated") is the standard OT term for prostration worship (Gen 22:5, Ex 4:31, 34:8, 1 Sam 1:3). Its repeated use for idolatrous worship of the beast and dragon deliberately echoes the forbidden worship of other gods (Ex 20:5, *lo tishtachaveh lahem*, "you shall not bow down to them").

### 13:7 — mishpachah va-am ve-lashon va-goy
- **Hebrew**: *al kol mishpachah va-am ve-lashon va-goy* ("over every family and people and tongue and nation")
- **Note**: The four-fold formula echoes Dan 3:4, 7 (*kol ammayya ummayya ve-lishanayya*, "all peoples, nations, and languages") and Dan 7:14. Delitzsch uses *mishpachah* ("family/clan") for *phylen* ("tribe"), which echoes Gen 12:3 (*ve-nivrekhu vekha kol mishpechot ha-adamah*, "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed"). This is a subtle choice — using the Abrahamic promise vocabulary for the anti-Abrahamic dominion of the beast.

### 13:10 — savlanut ve-emunat ha-qedoshim
- **Hebrew**: *ba-zot savlanut ve-emunat ha-qedoshim* ("in this is the patience and faith of the saints")
- **Note**: *Savlanut* ("patience/endurance") is post-biblical Hebrew, not found in the OT. A more biblical alternative might be *tochelet* ("hope/endurance," from Lam 3:26) or *qavvah* ("waiting/hoping," from Isa 40:31). *Emunat* ("faith/faithfulness of") is excellent — from the root *'-m-n* (Hab 2:4, *ve-tzaddiq be-emunato yichyeh*, "the righteous shall live by his faith").

## Other Notes

### Daniel 7 as Structural Background
Chapter 13 is the most Daniel-dependent chapter in Revelation. Nearly every element derives from Daniel 7:
- Beast from the sea (Dan 7:3)
- Composite animal features: lion, bear, leopard (Dan 7:4-6)
- Ten horns (Dan 7:7, 24)
- Mouth speaking great things (Dan 7:8, 20)
- War against the saints (Dan 7:21)
- Authority for a set period (Dan 7:25)
- Universal dominion over all peoples (Dan 7:14 inverted)
The Hebrew translation effectively preserves all these Danielic connections through shared vocabulary.

### The Image Commandment
The command to make an image (*tzelem*) and worship it on pain of death (13:14-15) conflates:
- The golden calf (Ex 32) — idolatrous image made for worship
- Nebuchadnezzar's image (Dan 3) — forced worship of a royal image on pain of death
- The Decalogue prohibition (Ex 20:4-5) — *lo ta'aseh lekha fesel... lo tishtachaveh lahem* ("you shall not make an idol... you shall not bow down to them")

### Red-Text Candidates
No direct speech of the Holy Trinity in this chapter. The beast and its worshippers speak, but God and Christ do not speak directly.

### The Number 666
The Hebrew does not attempt gematria interpretation but faithfully renders the number. In Hebrew gematria, the letters nun-resh-vav-nun qof-samekh-resh (Nero Caesar in Hebrew) yield 666 — this is the standard patristic-era interpretation preserved in some Greek manuscripts. The Delitzsch text simply presents the number without commentary.
