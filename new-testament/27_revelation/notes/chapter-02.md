# Revelation Chapter 2 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 29 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 2:7 — "paradiso Dei mei" vs. "paradiso tou theou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *in paradiso Dei mei* ("in the paradise of my God"), while the SBLGNT reads *en to paradeiso tou theou* ("in the paradise of God") without *mei/mou*. The Hebrew has *be-tokh gan-Elohim* ("in the garden of God"), omitting "my." Some Greek manuscripts (including TR/Byzantine) do include *mou*.
- **Vulgate**: *quod est in paradiso Dei mei*
- **Greek**: *ho estin en to paradeiso tou theou*
- **Hebrew**: *asher be-tokh gan-Elohim*
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits "my" (*Elohai*), siding with the SBLGNT against the Vulgate. For Vulgate conformity, this should read *gan Elohai*.

### 2:9 — "opera tua" addition
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes *et-ma'asekha* ("your works") corresponding to a reading found in some Greek manuscripts but not in the SBLGNT main text. The Vulgate reads *Scio tribulationem tuam* without explicitly adding "opera tua" as a separate item (though "Scio" covers the full knowledge clause).
- **Hebrew**: *yada'ti et-ma'asekha ve'et-tzaratekha*
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the TR/Byzantine tradition which adds "your works" explicitly before the tribulation and poverty. The Vulgate text provided does not include a separate "opera tua" here. Minor issue.

### 2:10 — "diabolus" vs. "malsheen"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *diabolus* and the Greek *diabolos*. The Hebrew renders this as *ha-malsheen* ("the slanderer/accuser"), which is a valid Hebrew semantic equivalent but notably avoids the expected *ha-Satan* which would be the direct OT equivalent. *Malsheen* is a Mishnaic/rabbinic term.
- **Vulgate**: *missurus est diabolus*
- **Hebrew**: *atid ha-malsheen latet mikkem ba-mishmar*
- **Note**: While *malsheen* captures the Greek etymology of *diabolos* (slanderer), biblical Hebrew would simply use *ha-Satan* (cf. Job 1-2, Zech 3:1). The Delitzsch choice preserves a distinction between *diabolos* and *Satanas* that exists in Greek but is artificial in Hebrew.

### 2:13 — "opera tua" addition
- **Issue**: Similar to 2:9, the Hebrew begins with *yada'ti et-ma'asekha* ("I know your works"), following the TR/Byzantine text. The SBLGNT begins *Oida pou katoikeis* ("I know where you dwell") without the preceding "your works."
- **Vulgate**: *Scio ubi habitas*
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds material not in the Vulgate. Minor, as the Vulgate's *Scio* is comprehensive.

### 2:15 — "quæ et ego odi" addition
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds *asher sane'ti* ("which I hate"), following the TR/Byzantine text which includes *ho miso* in v. 15. The SBLGNT and Vulgate do not include this phrase in v. 15 (it appeared in v. 6 for the Nicolaitans).
- **Vulgate**: *ita habes et tu tenentes doctrinam Nicolaitarum*
- **Hebrew**: *asher sane'ti* is added
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows a TR reading not present in the Vulgate. Should be flagged.

### 2:20 — "mulierem" vs. "ten gunaika"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *permittis mulierem Jezabel* and the Greek both refer to a woman Jezebel. The Hebrew similarly has *la-ishah le-Izevel*. However, the SBLGNT reading *ten gunaika* with the article could mean "your wife" (some manuscripts read *sou*), while the Vulgate *mulierem* is simply "woman." The Hebrew *la-ishah* is ambiguous (could be "the woman" or "a woman").
- **Note**: No significant divergence. All three are in basic alignment.

### 2:27 — "sicut et ego accepi" placement
- **Issue**: In the Vulgate, *sicut et ego accepi a Patre meo* begins v. 28. In the Greek, *hos kago eilefa para tou patros mou* is at the end of v. 27. The Hebrew places *ka-asher qibalti gam-anokhi me'et avi* at the end of v. 27, following the Greek versification against the Vulgate.
- **Note**: Versification split point differs. The Hebrew follows Greek chapter divisions rather than Vulgate here.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 2:1 — koh amar formula
- **Hebrew**: *koh amar ha-ochaz bi-mino shiv'at ha-kokhavim*
- **Note**: Excellent. The prophetic messenger formula *koh amar* ("thus says") is used consistently throughout the seven letters, perfectly matching the OT prophetic convention (cf. Isa 7:7, Jer 2:2, Amos 1:3ff.). This is precisely how a Hebrew prophet would introduce divine speech.

### 2:2 — shlihim for "apostolos"
- **Hebrew**: *shlihim* (emissaries/sent ones)
- **Note**: *Shaliah/shlihim* is post-biblical in its technical sense as "apostle," but the root *sh-l-ch* (send) is thoroughly biblical. The concept of a *shaliah* as an authorized envoy is developed in rabbinic literature. A more biblical phrasing might be *sheluchim* (sent ones, cf. Isa 18:2).

### 2:7 — menatzeach for "vincenti"
- **Hebrew**: *ha-menatzeach*
- **Note**: Excellent. *Menatzeach* ("overcomer/victor") is from the root *n-tz-ch*, which appears in the Psalms superscriptions (*la-menatzeach*, "to the choirmaster/director"). While the Psalms usage has a different semantic range, using *menatzeach* for "the one who conquers" is attested in biblical Hebrew (cf. 1 Chr 29:11 *ha-netzach* meaning "victory").

### 2:9 — keneset ha-Satan
- **Hebrew**: *keneset ha-Satan* ("synagogue of Satan")
- **Note**: *Keneset* is Mishnaic Hebrew (cf. *Bet ha-Keneset*). Biblical Hebrew would use *'edah* (assembly/congregation) or *qahal*. However, *keneset* precisely mirrors the Greek *synagoge*, maintaining the synagogue imagery which is central to the theological point.

### 2:14 — Balaam/Balak allusion
- **Hebrew**: *leqach Bil'am asher horah et-Balaq latet mikhshol lifnei benei Yisra'el*
- **Note**: Excellent use of the Balaam narrative vocabulary. *Mikhshol* ("stumbling block") echoes Lev 19:14. *Leqach* ("teaching/doctrine") is good biblical Hebrew (Prov 1:5, 4:2). The entire phrase resonates with Num 25:1-2 and 31:16.

### 2:17 — man ha-ganuz
- **Hebrew**: *min ha-man ha-ganuz* ("from the hidden manna")
- **Note**: *Man* (manna) is of course from Ex 16. *Ganuz* ("hidden/stored") is post-biblical in this exact form, though the root *g-n-z* appears in Ezek 27:24 and Esth 3:9. The concept of hidden manna connects to the tradition of the manna jar placed before the Lord (Ex 16:33-34).

### 2:17 — even levanah
- **Hebrew**: *even levanah* ("white stone")
- **Note**: Good. *Even* (stone) and *levanah* (white) are both standard biblical vocabulary. *Mefutach* ("engraved") for *gegrammenon* echoes Ex 28:36 and 39:30 (the engraving of the priestly diadem), adding sacerdotal resonance.

### 2:27 — Psalm 2 allusion
- **Hebrew**: *ve-ra'am be-shevet barzel ki-khlei yotzer yenufatzu*
- **Note**: Outstanding. This directly quotes Ps 2:9 (*tero'em be-shevet barzel ki-khli yotzer tenappetzem*). The Hebrew NT text preserves the exact Psalm 2 language. This is the strongest possible way to render this allusion.

## Other Notes

### OT Allusions in the Seven Letters
- **2:7**: Tree of life in the garden (Gen 2:9, 3:22-24)
- **2:14**: Balaam/Balak narrative (Num 22-25, 31:16)
- **2:17**: Hidden manna (Ex 16:33-34), white stone with new name (Isa 62:2, 65:15)
- **2:18**: Son of God with fiery eyes (Dan 10:6)
- **2:20**: Jezebel (1 Kgs 16:31, 21:25, 2 Kgs 9:22)
- **2:23**: Searching kidneys and hearts (Jer 11:20, 17:10, Ps 7:10)
- **2:27**: Rod of iron, potter's vessels (Ps 2:9)
- **2:28**: Morning star (Num 24:17, Isa 14:12)
