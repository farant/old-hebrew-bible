# Revelation Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 8 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch). No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 15:1 — "consummata est ira Dei" / "etelesthe ho thymos tou theou" / "kalah za'am Elohim"
- **Vulgate**: *quoniam in illis consummata est ira Dei* ("for in them is completed the wrath of God")
- **Greek**: *hoti en autais etelesthe ho thymos tou theou* ("for in them the wrath of God is completed")
- **Hebrew**: *ki vahen kalah za'am Elohim* ("for in them the fury of God is complete")
- **Note**: All three align. *Za'am* ("fury/indignation") is a strong prophetic term (Isa 10:5, 25, 13:5, 26:20, 30:27, Jer 10:10, 50:25, Nah 1:6, Hab 3:12, Zeph 3:8, Dan 8:19, 11:36). It is distinct from *chemah* ("wrath") and *af* ("anger") — *za'am* connotes divine indignation at its most intense. The phrase *kalah za'am* echoes Dan 11:36 (*ad kalah za'am*, "until the indignation is accomplished") and Isa 10:25 (*ki od me'at mizr'ar ve-khalah za'am*, "for in a very little while the indignation will be ended"). Excellent OT allusion.

### 15:2 — "mare vitreum mistum igne" / "thalassan hyalinen memigmenen pyri"
- **Hebrew**: *ke-yam zekhokhi balul ba-esh* ("like a sea of glass mingled with fire")
- **Note**: *Yam zekhokhi* ("sea of glass") first appeared in 4:6. *Balul ba-esh* ("mingled with fire") uses *balul* (from *b-l-l*, "to mix/mingle"), which appears in Lev 2:4, 5, 7:12, 14:10 for grain offerings mixed with oil (*belulah va-shemen*). The mixing vocabulary carries sacrificial overtones. All three sources align.

### 15:2 — "qui vicerunt bestiam" / "tous nikontas ek tou theriou"
- **Vulgate**: *eos, qui vicerunt bestiam, et imaginem ejus, et numerum nominis ejus*
- **Greek**: *tous nikontas ek tou theriou kai ek tes eikonos autou kai ek tou arithmou tou onomatos autou*
- **Hebrew**: *ve-et ha-mitgabbrim al ha-chayyah ve-al tzalmah ve-al tavah ve-al mispar shemah*
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds *ve-al tavah* ("and against her mark"), which is present in the Vulgate tradition (some Vulgate manuscripts include *et de caractere ejus*) but is absent from the SBLGNT critical text. The Hebrew follows the fuller reading. *Mitgabbrim* ("those prevailing/conquering") is from the root *g-b-r* ("to be strong/mighty"), which appears in Ps 12:5 and Dan 9:27 (*ve-higbir berit*, "he shall make a strong covenant"). A strong choice.

### 15:3 — "canticum Moysi" / "ten oden Mouseos" / "shirat Moshe"
- **Vulgate**: *canticum Moysi servi Dei, et canticum Agni* ("the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb")
- **Greek**: *ten oden Mouseos tou doulou tou theou kai ten oden tou arniou* ("the song of Moses the servant of God and the song of the Lamb")
- **Hebrew**: *et shirat Moshe eved Elohim ve-shirat ha-seh* ("the song of Moses the servant of God and the song of the Lamb")
- **Note**: All three align. *Shirat Moshe* refers to Ex 15:1-18 (the Song of the Sea) or Deut 32:1-43 (the Song of Moses) or both. The Hebrew *shirat Moshe* uses the construct form *shirat* matching the OT pattern exactly (see the superscription tradition). *Eved Elohim* ("servant of God") for Moses echoes Deut 34:5 (*Moshe eved YHVH*), Josh 1:1, 13, 15, and throughout. The pairing of Moses and the Lamb places the Exodus and the Paschal sacrifice in parallel — the same God who delivered Israel at the Sea delivers through the Lamb.

### 15:3 — "Rex saeculorum" vs. "basileus ton aionon" vs. "melekh ha-goyim"
- **Vulgate**: *Rex saeculorum* ("King of the ages")
- **Greek**: the SBLGNT reads *ho basileus ton aionon* ("King of the ages"), though an important variant reads *ton ethnon* ("of the nations")
- **Hebrew**: *melekh ha-goyim* ("King of the nations")
- **Flag**: The Hebrew reads *melekh ha-goyim* ("King of the nations"), which follows the variant Greek reading *ton ethnon* rather than the Vulgate's *saeculorum* ("of the ages"). Since we prioritize the Vulgate, this is a divergence. The Vulgate *Rex saeculorum* would be rendered *melekh ha-olamim* in Hebrew. However, *melekh ha-goyim* does echo Jer 10:7 (*mi lo yira'akha melekh ha-goyim*, "who would not fear you, King of the nations?"), making it a powerful OT allusion — and the entire verse 15:3-4 is heavily influenced by Jer 10:6-7.

### 15:3 — The doxology content
- **Hebrew**: *gedolim ma'asekha ve-nifla'im YHVH Elohim Tzeva'ot tzedeq ve-emet derakhekha melekh ha-goyim*
- **Note**: This composite doxology draws from multiple OT sources:
  - *Gedolim ma'asekha* ("great are your works") — Ps 111:2, 139:14
  - *Ve-nifla'im* ("and wonderful") — Ps 139:14 (*nifla'im ma'asekha*, "wonderful are your works")
  - *YHVH Elohim Tzeva'ot* — standard prophetic divine name (Isa 1:24, Jer 2:19, Amos 3:13, etc.)
  - *Tzedeq ve-emet derakhekha* ("just and true are your ways") — Deut 32:4 (*ha-Tzur tamim pa'olo ki khol derakhav mishpat El emunah ve-ein avel tzaddiq ve-yashar hu*, "the Rock, his work is perfect, for all his ways are justice; a God of faithfulness and without iniquity, just and upright is he")
  - The entire doxology is a mosaic of OT praise language. Delitzsch assembles it with skill.

### 15:4 — "Quis non timebit te, Domine" — Jeremiah 10:7
- **Vulgate**: *Quis non timebit te, Domine, et magnificabit nomen tuum?*
- **Hebrew**: *mi lo yira'akha YHVH ve-lo yitten kavod le-shimkha*
- **Note**: The Hebrew closely follows Jer 10:7 (*mi lo yira'akha melekh ha-goyim ki lekha ya'atah*, "who would not fear you, O King of the nations? For it is your due"). The adaptation replaces Jeremiah's *ki lekha ya'atah* with the Revelation text's reasoning (*ki qadosh attah levaddekha*, "for you alone are holy"). The Vulgate has *Domine* where the Hebrew has *YHVH* — this is consistent with Delitzsch's practice of using the Tetragrammaton for *Kyrios/Dominus* when it appears to refer to God the Father.

### 15:4 — "solus pius" / "monos hosios" / "qadosh attah levaddekha"
- **Vulgate**: *quia solus pius es* ("for you alone are holy/pious")
- **Greek**: *hoti monos hosios* ("for you alone are holy")
- **Hebrew**: *ki qadosh attah levaddekha* ("for holy are you alone")
- **Note**: The Greek *hosios* and Latin *pius* both mean "holy/devout" in a sense of personal moral purity, slightly different from *hagios*/*sanctus* (set apart/sacred). The Hebrew uses *qadosh* ("holy"), which is the standard OT divine attribute (Isa 6:3, *qadosh qadosh qadosh YHVH Tzeva'ot*, "holy, holy, holy is the LORD of hosts"). While *qadosh* more closely maps to *hagios/sanctus*, it is the most natural Hebrew rendering and theologically appropriate.

### 15:5 — "templum tabernaculi testimonii" / "naos tes skenes tou martyriou"
- **Vulgate**: *templum tabernaculi testimonii in caelo* ("the temple of the tabernacle of the testimony in heaven")
- **Greek**: *ho naos tes skenes tou martyriou en to ourano*
- **Hebrew**: *heikhal mishkan ha-edut ba-shamayim* ("the temple of the tabernacle of the testimony in heaven")
- **Note**: All three align. *Mishkan ha-edut* ("tabernacle of the testimony") appears in Num 1:50, 53, 10:11, 17:22-23, 18:2. *Heikhal* ("temple/palace") combined with *mishkan* creates a phrase that bridges the tabernacle (wilderness) and temple (Jerusalem) traditions. The heavenly sanctuary contains both — it is the archetype of which both earthly structures were copies (cf. Heb 8:5, Ex 25:9, 40).

### 15:6 — "vestiti lino mundo et candido" / "linon katharon lampron"
- **Vulgate**: *vestiti lino mundo et candido* ("clothed in clean bright linen")
- **Greek**: the SBLGNT has a variant — some manuscripts read *linon* ("linen"), others *lithon* ("stone/gem"). *Linon* is the standard reading.
- **Hebrew**: *melubashim bad tahor ve-tzach* ("clothed in pure and bright linen")
- **Note**: *Bad* ("linen") is the priestly vestment material (Ex 28:42, 39:28, Lev 6:3, 16:4, 23, 32, Ezek 44:17-18). The high priest wore *bad* when entering the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur (Lev 16:4, *ketonet bad qodesh yilbash*). The seven angels dressed in *bad* evokes a sevenfold Yom Kippur procession. *Tahor ve-tzach* ("pure and bright") — *tahor* is the standard ritual purity term; *tzach* ("bright/dazzling") appears in Song 5:10, Isa 18:4, Lam 4:7.

### 15:7 — "phialas aureas" / "qa'arot zahav"
- **Hebrew**: *sheva qa'arot zahav mele'ot chamat ha-Elohim* ("seven golden bowls full of the wrath of God")
- **Note**: *Qa'arot* ("bowls/dishes") appears in Num 7:13-85 (the tribal leaders' dedicatory offerings included silver *qa'arot*) and Ex 25:29, 37:16 (the table of showbread vessels). The term carries tabernacle/temple service connotations. The bowls of wrath are presented using the vocabulary of sacred offering vessels — divine judgment served in liturgical containers.

### 15:8 — "impletum est templum fumo" — Exodus/Isaiah theophany
- **Vulgate**: *impletum est templum fumo a majestate Dei, et de virtute ejus*
- **Hebrew**: *va-yimmale ha-heikhal ashan mi-kevod Elohim ve-uzzo*
- **Note**: *Va-yimmale ha-heikhal ashan* ("and the temple was filled with smoke") directly echoes Isa 6:4 (*va-yimmale ha-bayit ashan*, "and the house was filled with smoke") from Isaiah's throne-room vision. It also echoes Ex 40:34-35 (*va-yekhas he-anan et ohel mo'ed u-khvod YHVH male et ha-mishkan ve-lo yakhal Moshe lavo el ohel mo'ed*, "the cloud covered the tent of meeting and the glory of the LORD filled the tabernacle, and Moses was not able to enter the tent of meeting"). The statement that *no one could enter the temple* (*ve-lo yakhal ish lavo el ha-heikhal*) directly parallels Ex 40:35 (*ve-lo yakhal Moshe lavo*). The glory-cloud prevents entry.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 15:1 — makot / za'am
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses *makkot* ("plagues/blows") for the Greek *plegas* and Latin *plagas*. This is the exact term from the Exodus plague narrative (Ex 9:14, 11:1, 12:13, *ha-makkah*, "the plague/blow"). The seven last plagues are thus framed as a new Exodus plague cycle — the plagues of Revelation are a recapitulation and intensification of the plagues of Egypt.

### 15:3 — eved Elohim
- **Note**: *Eved Elohim* ("servant of God") applied to Moses is both Mosaic (Deut 34:5, Josh 1:1) and anticipatory — the "servant" title links Moses to the Suffering Servant of Isaiah (Isa 42:1, 49:3, 52:13, 53:11). The pairing *eved Elohim... ve-shirat ha-seh* ("servant of God... and the song of the Lamb") creates a typological bridge between the Mosaic servant and the Paschal Lamb.

### 15:7 — ha-El ha-chai le-olmei olamim
- **Hebrew**: *ha-Elohim ha-chai le-olmei olamim* ("the God who lives forever and ever")
- **Note**: *El chai* / *Elohim chayyim* ("living God") is a major OT divine title: Deut 5:23, Josh 3:10, 1 Sam 17:26, 36, 2 Kgs 19:4, 16, Ps 42:3, 84:3, Jer 10:10, 23:36, Dan 6:21, 27. The phrase *le-olmei olamim* ("forever and ever") is the standard Hebrew superlative for eternity.

### 15:8 — kevod Elohim ve-uzzo
- **Hebrew**: *mi-kevod Elohim ve-uzzo* ("from the glory of God and his might")
- **Note**: *Kevod Elohim* ("glory of God") — *kavod* is the weighty, visible manifestation of divine presence (Ex 16:10, 24:16-17, 33:18, 22, 40:34-35, 1 Kgs 8:11, Ezek 1:28, 10:4, 43:2, 44:4). *Uzzo* ("his might/strength") renders the Vulgate *virtute ejus* and Greek *dynameos autou*. The pairing *kavod + oz* appears in Ps 29:1 (*havu la-YHVH kavod va-oz*, "ascribe to the LORD glory and strength") and Ps 96:7.

## Other Notes

### Red-Text Candidates
- **15:3-4**: The doxology sung by the victors — *gedolim ma'asekha ve-nifla'im...* — is a hymn addressed to God. It is spoken by the redeemed, not by a member of the Trinity. Not red-text. However, the content is a composite of OT divine speech and praise addressed to God.

### The Song of Moses and the Lamb
The explicit mention of *shirat Moshe* connects Revelation 15 to two OT passages:
1. **Exodus 15:1-18** — the Song of the Sea, celebrating deliverance from Egypt through the Red Sea. The sea of glass (15:2) parallels the Sea of Reeds; the victors standing on the sea parallel Israel standing on the far shore.
2. **Deuteronomy 32:1-43** — the Song of Moses, a covenant lawsuit declaring God's justice and warning of judgment. The themes of 15:3-4 (God's just and true ways, all nations coming to worship) are closer to Deut 32 than to Ex 15.

The Lamb's song joins these Mosaic traditions to the Paschal theology. The entire chapter is liturgical — a heavenly Yom Kippur where the angels emerge in priestly linen from the heavenly sanctuary to execute the final plagues.

### OT Allusion Density
- **Ex 15:1-18**: Song of the Sea — v. 3
- **Ex 40:34-35**: Glory filling the tabernacle, Moses unable to enter — v. 8
- **Lev 16:4**: Priestly linen garments — v. 6
- **Num 1:50, 7:13-85**: Tabernacle of the testimony, golden vessels — vv. 5, 7
- **Deut 32:1-43**: Song of Moses — v. 3
- **Isa 6:4**: Temple filled with smoke — v. 8
- **Jer 10:6-7**: Who would not fear you, King of the nations? — v. 4
- **Dan 11:36**: Until the indignation is accomplished — v. 1
- **Ps 111:2, 139:14**: Great and wonderful works — v. 3
