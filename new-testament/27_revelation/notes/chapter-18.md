# Revelation Chapter 18 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 24 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch). No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 18:1 — "potestatem magnam" vs. "exousian megalen" vs. "kabbir-koach"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *habentem potestatem magnam* ("having great power/authority"), Greek *echonta exousian megalen* ("having great authority"). The Hebrew renders this as *kabbir-koach* ("mighty in strength"), which shifts from "authority" to raw "strength/power." The Vulgate *potestas* and Greek *exousia* connote delegated authority, while *koach* is brute force.
- **Vulgate**: *habentem potestatem magnam*
- **Hebrew**: *kabbir-koach*
- **Note**: A more precise rendering might use *shilton* ("dominion/authority") or *memshelet* ("rule"). However, *kabbir* is good biblical Hebrew (Job 34:17, 36:5) and *kabbir-koach* echoes Job 36:5 (*hen El kabbir velo yim'as kabbir koach lev*, "Behold, God is mighty and does not despise any; He is mighty in strength of understanding"). The slight semantic shift is acceptable.

### 18:2 — "neveh se'irim" for "habitatio daemoniorum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *habitatio daemoniorum* ("dwelling of demons"), Greek *katoiketerion daimonion* ("habitation of demons"). The Hebrew has *neveh se'irim* ("habitation of goat-demons/satyrs"). This is a remarkable translation choice. *Se'irim* are the goat-demons of Lev 17:7, 2 Chr 11:15, and crucially Isa 13:21 and 34:14, where they inhabit desolate Babylon and Edom respectively.
- **Vulgate**: *habitatio daemoniorum*
- **Hebrew**: *neveh se'irim*
- **Note**: Outstanding. By using *se'irim* rather than a generic term like *shedim* ("demons," Deut 32:17, Ps 106:37), Delitzsch directly connects to Isaiah's Babylon oracle (Isa 13:21: *ve-se'irim yeraqqedu sham*, "and goat-demons shall dance there"). This makes the OT background of Revelation 18 explicit: the fall of Babylon here reprises Isaiah's prophecy. The Vulgate's *daemonium* is more generic; the Hebrew is more biblically precise.

### 18:2 — Threefold "custodia" — missing third element in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has three *custodia* clauses: *custodia omnis spiritus immundi, et custodia omnis volucris immundae, et odibilis* ("prison of every unclean spirit, prison of every unclean bird, and hateful [creature]"). The SBLGNT includes a variant third element *kai phylake pantos theriou akathartou* ("and prison of every unclean beast") in some manuscripts. The Hebrew has only two: *mishmar lekhol ruach tamei umishmar lekhol of tamei venimas* ("prison of every unclean spirit and prison of every unclean and detested bird").
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows a shorter text tradition, combining the final element (*venimas*, "and detested") with the bird clause. The Vulgate similarly attaches *odibilis* ("hateful") to the bird clause. This is acceptable Vulgate alignment.

### 18:3 — "shatu" (they drank) vs. "peptokan" (have fallen)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads *peptokan panta ta ethne* ("all the nations have fallen"), while the Vulgate reads *biberunt omnes gentes* ("all the nations have drunk"). The Hebrew has *shatu kol ha-goyim* ("all the nations have drunk"), aligning with the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *de vino irae fornicationis ejus biberunt omnes gentes*
- **Greek**: *ek tou oinou tou thymou tes porneias autes peptokan panta ta ethne*
- **Hebrew**: *mi-yein chamat zenutah shatu kol ha-goyim*
- **Note**: Good Vulgate alignment. The Hebrew follows *biberunt* against the SBLGNT's *peptokan*. The variant is significant: "have drunk" (TR/Vulgate) vs. "have fallen" (critical text). Delitzsch correctly follows the Vulgate/TR here.

### 18:4 — "tze'u mimmenah ammi" — Jeremiah/Isaiah echoes
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree on the content. The Hebrew *tze'u mimmenah ammi* ("Go out from her, my people") directly echoes Jer 51:45 (*tze'u mittokhah ammi*, "Go out from the midst of her, my people") and Isa 52:11 (*suru suru tze'u mi-sham*, "Depart, depart, go out from there").
- **Note**: The phrase is virtually a verbatim OT quotation. The divine voice (*ammi*, "my people") identifies the speaker as God.

### 18:5 — "Dominus" vs. "Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *recordatus est Dominus* ("the Lord remembered"), the Greek *emnemoneuse ho theos* ("God remembered"). The Hebrew has *va-yizkor Elohim* ("and God remembered"), following the Greek against the Vulgate's *Dominus*.
- **Vulgate**: *recordatus est Dominus*
- **Hebrew**: *va-yizkor Elohim*
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows Greek *ho theos* rather than Vulgate *Dominus*. For Vulgate conformity, *YHVH* or *Adonai* would be expected. However, the phrase *va-yizkor Elohim* is a powerful OT echo of Gen 8:1 (*va-yizkor Elohim et Noach*, "and God remembered Noah") and Gen 19:29 (*va-yizkor Elohim et Avraham*, "and God remembered Abraham"), both in contexts of divine judgment. The allusion may justify the choice.

### 18:6 — Gemul wordplay
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree. The Hebrew creates a wordplay: *shalmu-lah gemulah she-gamlah lakhem* ("repay her the recompense she recompensed you"), using the root *g-m-l* twice. This mirrors the Greek's repetition (*apodote... apedoken*) and Vulgate's *reddite... reddidit*.
- **Note**: The *g-m-l* root wordplay is excellent biblical Hebrew. Cf. Ps 137:8 (*ashrei she-yeshalem lakh et-gemulekh she-gamalt lanu*, "blessed is he who repays you what you dealt us") — the very psalm about Babylon's fall.

### 18:7 — "ani yashavti malkah" — Isaiah 47 allusion
- **Issue**: All three traditions present Babylon's arrogant self-declaration. The Hebrew *ani yashavti malkah velo ehyeh almanah ve-evel lo er'eh* ("I sit as queen and I will not be a widow and mourning I will not see") is a near-verbatim citation of Isa 47:8 (*ha-omerah bilevavah ani ve-afsi od lo eshev almanah velo eda shakhol*, "who says in her heart, 'I am, and there is none besides me; I shall not sit as a widow, nor know bereavement'").
- **Note**: Outstanding OT allusion. The connection between Babylon in Isaiah 47 and Babylon in Revelation 18 is made explicit through shared vocabulary.

### 18:8 — "YHVH Elohim" for "Dominus Deus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *fortis est Deus* ("God is strong"), Greek *ischyros kyrios ho theos ho krinas auten* ("strong is the Lord God who judges her"). The Hebrew has *chazaq YHVH Elohim ha-shofet otah* ("strong is the LORD God who judges her"), using the Tetragrammaton.
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses the full divine name *YHVH Elohim* which corresponds well to the Vulgate's *Deus* and Greek's *kyrios ho theos*. The use of the Tetragrammaton adds covenantal weight.

### 18:11 — "masa oniyyotam" for "gomos" (cargo)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *merces eorum* ("their merchandise"), Greek *ton gomon auton* ("their cargo"). The Hebrew has *masa oniyyotam* ("the cargo of their ships"), adding "ships" which is not in the source texts at this verse (ships appear later in v. 17).
- **Hebrew**: *masa oniyyotam*
- **Note**: This is an expansion. The source texts speak only of "cargo/merchandise." Delitzsch anticipates the maritime theme of vv. 17-19 by inserting "ships" here. The Vulgate simply has *merces* ("merchandise"). This is a minor liberty.

### 18:14 — Vocabulary note
- **Issue**: The Hebrew *ha-megadim machmad nafshekh* ("the delicacies, the desire of your soul") for the Vulgate's *poma desiderii animae tuae* ("fruits of the desire of your soul"). *Megadim* ("delicacies/precious fruits") appears in Song 4:13, 16, 7:14. *Machmad nafshekh* echoes Ezek 24:16, 21, 25 (*machmad einekha*, "the delight of your eyes").
- **Note**: Good biblical vocabulary. The Song of Songs connection adds an ironic overtone — the luxuries of Babylon described in the language of erotic desire.

### 18:16 — "shesh" for "byssinum" vs. "butz" in 19:8
- **Issue**: The Hebrew uses *shesh* here for "fine linen" but *butz* in 19:8 for the same Vulgate word *byssinum*. Both are biblical terms for fine linen (*shesh*: Ex 25:4, 26:1, 28:5; *butz*: 1 Chr 4:21, 2 Chr 3:14). The distinction may be intentional: *shesh* for worldly luxury, *butz* for the saints' righteousness.
- **Note**: If intentional, this is a subtle and effective translation choice. If accidental, it is at least not incorrect — both words are excellent biblical Hebrew for fine linen.

### 18:17 — Verse division and content
- **Issue**: The Hebrew in v. 16 includes *ki ve-sha'ah achat hochrav ha-osher ha-gadol ha-zeh* ("for in one hour this great wealth was laid waste"), which in the Greek belongs to v. 17a. The Vulgate similarly begins v. 17 with *quoniam una hora destitutae sunt tantae divitiae*. The Hebrew attaches this clause to v. 16, creating a slight verse-division difference.
- **Note**: Minor versification discrepancy. The content is present but shifted by one verse boundary.

### 18:20 — "ha-shelichim ha-qedoshim" for "sancti apostoli"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *sancti apostoli et prophetae* ("holy apostles and prophets"), the Greek *hoi hagioi kai hoi apostoloi kai hoi prophetai* ("the saints and the apostles and the prophets"). The Hebrew has *ha-shelichim ha-qedoshim veha-nevi'im* ("the holy apostles and the prophets"), grouping "holy" with "apostles" as the Vulgate does.
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate grouping (*sancti apostoli*) rather than the Greek's threefold distinction (*saints, apostles, prophets*). Good Vulgate alignment.

### 18:21 — "pelach rekhev" for "molarem" (millstone)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *lapidem quasi molarem magnum* ("a stone like a great millstone"), Greek *lithon hos mylinon megan* ("a stone like a great millstone"). The Hebrew *even gedolah ke-felach rekhev* ("a great stone like a piece of a millstone") uses *pelach rekhev* ("millstone piece"), echoing Judg 9:53 (*pelach rekhev*, "a piece of a millstone" — the stone that killed Abimelech) and 2 Sam 11:21.
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Pelach rekhev* is the precise OT term from the Abimelech narrative, creating an echo of violent death by stone. The Jeremiah 51 millstone-in-the-river parallel (Jer 51:63-64) is the primary background, but the *pelach rekhev* vocabulary adds the Judges allusion.

### 18:22-23 — Jeremiah 25 allusion chain
- **Issue**: The cessation of music, craftsmen, millstones, lamps, and wedding voices closely parallels Jer 25:10 (*ve-ha'avadti mehem qol sason ve-qol simchah qol chatan ve-qol kallah qol rechayim ve-or ner*, "I will banish from them the sound of joy and gladness, the voice of bridegroom and bride, the sound of millstones and the light of the lamp"). The Hebrew translation uses vocabulary from Jeremiah: *qol rechayim* (v. 22) and *qol chatan ve-qol kallah* (v. 23).
- **Note**: Outstanding. The Hebrew closely follows Jeremiah's vocabulary, making the prophetic source explicit.

### 18:23 — "khin'anayikh" for "mercatores tui"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *mercatores tui erant principes terrae* ("your merchants were the princes of the earth"), Greek *hoi emporoi sou esan hoi megistanes tes ges*. The Hebrew uses *khin'anayikh hayu nikhbaddei eretz* ("your Canaanites/merchants were the honored ones of the earth"). *Kena'ani* literally means "Canaanite" but is used in biblical Hebrew for "merchant/trader" (cf. Zech 14:21, Hos 12:8, Prov 31:24, Isa 23:8).
- **Note**: Excellent double-entendre. *Kena'ani* as "merchant" is attested in classical prophetic Hebrew and carries a judgmental overtone — the merchants of Babylon are equated with the idolatrous Canaanites.

### 18:23 — "veneficiis" vs. "pharmakeia" vs. "kheshafayikh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *in veneficiis tuis* ("in your sorceries"), Greek *en te pharmakeia sou*, Hebrew *bi-khshafayikh* ("in your sorceries"). *Kishuf/keshafim* is the standard biblical Hebrew for sorcery (Ex 7:11, 22:17, 2 Kgs 9:22, Isa 47:9, 12, Mic 5:11, Nah 3:4).
- **Note**: Good alignment. *Keshafim* connects directly to the Isa 47:9, 12 denunciation of Babylon's sorceries, reinforcing the Isaiah 47 framework of the entire chapter.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 18:2 — naflah naflah Bavel
- **Hebrew**: *naflah naflah Bavel ha-gedolah*
- **Note**: The doubled *naflah naflah* ("fallen, fallen") replicates the Hebrew of Isa 21:9 (*naflah naflah Bavel*, "fallen, fallen is Babylon"). This is the defining OT quotation behind Rev 18:2, and the Hebrew translation preserves it exactly. Outstanding.

### 18:4 — tze'u mimmenah ammi
- **Hebrew**: *tze'u mimmenah ammi*
- **Note**: Near-verbatim from Jer 51:45 (*tze'u mittokhah ammi*). See Content Alignment note above.

### 18:6 — maskhah/miskhu
- **Hebrew**: *ba-kos asher maskhah miskhu-lah kiphlayim*
- **Note**: The root *m-s-kh* ("to mix/pour wine") is excellent biblical Hebrew for wine preparation (Prov 9:2, 5, Isa 5:22, Ps 75:9). The Vulgate *miscuit/miscete* ("she mixed/mix for her") is precisely rendered. *Kos* ("cup") echoes the cup of wrath imagery throughout the prophets (Jer 25:15-17, Ps 75:9, Isa 51:17).

### 18:9 — ashan serefatah
- **Hebrew**: *ashan serefatah* ("the smoke of her burning")
- **Note**: Echoes Gen 19:28 (*va-yar ve-hinneh alah qitor ha-aretz ke-qitor ha-kivshan*, "and he looked, and behold, the smoke of the land went up like the smoke of a furnace") — Abraham watching Sodom's destruction. Also Isa 34:10 on Edom: *le-olam ya'aleh ashanah* ("forever shall her smoke go up").

### 18:19 — va-yizrequ afar al roshehem
- **Hebrew**: *va-yizrequ afar al roshehem*
- **Note**: Throwing dust on the head is the classic OT mourning gesture: Josh 7:6, 1 Sam 4:12, 2 Sam 1:2, Job 2:12, Lam 2:10, Ezek 27:30. The last reference is particularly apt — Ezek 27:30 describes merchants mourning the fall of Tyre, the primary OT background for Rev 18's merchant lament. *Va-yizrequ* uses the same verb as Ezek 27:30 (*ve-he'elu afar al roshehem*, actually uses *he'elu*). Still, the vocabulary is solidly from the mourning tradition.

### 18:22 — Musical instruments
- **Hebrew**: *ha-menagnim be-khinor veha-mezamrim u-mechalelim ba-chalilim u-machtzrim ba-chatzotzerot*
- **Note**: The Hebrew expands and specifies the instrument list: *kinnor* (lyre/harp — Gen 4:21, Ps 33:2, 137:2), *chalilim* (pipes — 1 Sam 10:5, 1 Kgs 1:40, Isa 5:12), *chatzotzerot* (trumpets — Num 10:2, 2 Chr 5:12). These are the standard instruments of the OT Temple and festival liturgy. The Greek has *kitharodos, mousikos, auletes, salpistes*; the Hebrew provides OT equivalents for each.

### 18:24 — harugei aretz
- **Hebrew**: *ve-khol harugei aretz*
- **Note**: *Harugei aretz* ("the slain of the earth") uses the passive participle of *h-r-g* ("slay/kill"), which is standard biblical Hebrew for those killed violently (cf. Isa 14:19, *harugei cherev*, "those slain by the sword"; Lam 4:9, *harugei ra'av*, "those slain by famine").

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 18 is built almost entirely from OT prophetic oracles against Babylon and Tyre:
- **Isa 13:19-22**: Babylon as habitation of wild creatures and demons — v. 2
- **Isa 21:9**: "Fallen, fallen is Babylon" — v. 2
- **Isa 47:7-9**: Babylon's arrogant self-declaration and sudden widowhood — vv. 7-8
- **Jer 25:10**: Cessation of joyful sounds, millstones, and lamps — vv. 22-23
- **Jer 50:8, 51:6, 45**: "Go out from her, my people" — v. 4
- **Jer 51:63-64**: Stone cast into river, Babylon sinks — v. 21
- **Ezek 26-27**: Tyre lament — merchants, sailors, cargo list — vv. 9-19
- **Ps 137:8**: Recompense wordplay on *g-m-l* — v. 6
- **Gen 19:28**: Smoke of destruction — v. 9

### Red-Text Candidates
- **18:4-5**: The voice from heaven saying "Go out from her, my people" (*tze'u mimmenah ammi*). The first-person possessive *ammi* ("my people") identifies the speaker as God. This is a strong red-text candidate.
- **18:5b**: "And God remembered her iniquities" is narrative, not direct divine speech, so it would not be red text.

### Theological Note — 18:20
The call to heaven, apostles, and prophets to rejoice over Babylon's fall (*rannu aleha ha-shamayim*) echoes Deut 32:43 LXX (*euphranthe, ouranoi*, "Rejoice, O heavens"), which is itself a call for cosmic celebration of divine vindication. The Hebrew *rannu* ("shout for joy") is the standard prophetic term for eschatological celebration (Isa 44:23, 49:13, 54:1).

### Theological Note — 18:13 "nefesh adam" (souls of men)
The cargo list culminates in *avadim ve-nefesh adam* ("slaves and the soul of man"), which is drawn from Ezek 27:13 (*be-nefesh adam*, "in the persons of men" — human trafficking in the Tyre oracle). The phrase *nefesh adam* carries weight beyond "slaves" — it is the trafficking of human souls, the ultimate indictment against Babylon's commercial empire. The Vulgate *mancipiorum et animarum hominum* ("of slaves and of the souls of men") preserves this double reference.
