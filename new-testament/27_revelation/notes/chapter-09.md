# Revelation Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 21 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 9:1 — "cecidisse" vs. "peptokota" — tense
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *cecidisse* (perfect infinitive, "to have fallen") and the Greek *peptokota* (perfect participle, "having fallen") both indicate the star had already fallen when John saw it. The Hebrew *nofel* ("falling") uses a present/active participle, which implies the star is in the process of falling rather than already fallen.
- **Vulgate**: *stellam de caelo cecidisse* (already fallen)
- **Greek**: *astera... peptokota* (already fallen)
- **Hebrew**: *kokhav nofel* (falling — in process)
- **Note**: The Hebrew shifts the aspect from perfective (completed action) to imperfective (ongoing action). For accuracy, *kokhav she-nafal* or *kokhav nofel min ha-shamayim la-aretz* with *she-nafal* would better capture both source texts.

### 9:2 — "aer" vs. "raqia"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *sol et aer* ("sun and air") obscured by smoke. The Greek likewise has *ho helios kai ho aer* ("the sun and the air"). The Hebrew renders *aer* as *raqia* ("firmament/expanse"), which is the Genesis 1:6-8 term for the sky-dome. *Raqia* carries far more cosmological weight than "air."
- **Vulgate**: *obscuratus est sol, et aer*
- **Hebrew**: *va-yechshakh ha-shemesh veha-raqia*
- **Note**: *Raqia* is a theological choice rather than a simple translation. While *avir* (air, attested in post-biblical Hebrew) would be more literal, *raqia* connects the darkening to the primordial cosmology of Genesis 1. An interesting but non-standard rendering.

### 9:6 — "desiderabunt mori" vs. "yish'alu et nafsham lamut"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *desiderabunt mori* ("they will desire to die"). The Greek *epithymesousin apothanein* ("they will long to die"). The Hebrew *yish'alu et nafsham lamut* ("they will ask their soul to die") echoes 1 Kgs 19:4, where Elijah *vayish'al et nafsho lamut* ("asked his soul to die"). This is a deliberate OT allusion not present in the Greek or Latin.
- **Note**: Excellent. The Delitzsch translation imports the Elijah language, creating a powerful intertextual connection.

### 9:11 — "Abaddon" and "Apollyon" — Vulgate addition
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds a Latin name: *latine habens nomen Exterminans* ("in Latin having the name Exterminator/Destroyer"). Neither the Greek nor Hebrew include a Latin name — the Greek gives only the Hebrew (*Abaddon*) and Greek (*Apollyon*) names. The Hebrew similarly has only *Avaddon be-ivrit ve-Apollyon bi-yvanit*.
- **Vulgate**: *hebraice Abaddon, graece autem Apollyon, latine habens nomen Exterminans*
- **Hebrew**: *Avaddon be-ivrit ve-Apollyon bi-yvanit*
- **Note**: The Vulgate's addition of *Exterminans* is a Clementine-tradition insertion. The Hebrew follows the Greek in giving only two names. For Vulgate conformity, a note about the Latin name could be relevant, though translating *Exterminans* into Hebrew would be *ha-Mashhit* ("the Destroyer," cf. Ex 12:23).

### 9:12 — "duo vae" count
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *ecce veniunt adhuc duo vae* ("behold, two woes still come"). The Greek similarly *erchetai eti dyo ouai* ("two woes still come"). The Hebrew adds detail: *oi sheni ushlishi ba'im acharav* ("the second and third woe come after it"), explicitly numbering the remaining woes rather than simply saying "two."
- **Note**: The Hebrew is more interpretive than the Vulgate, which simply says "two woes." Minor expansion.

### 9:13 — "ex quatuor cornibus" vs. "ek ton keraton"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *ex quatuor cornibus altaris aurei* ("from the four horns of the golden altar"). The SBLGNT has a textual variant here — some manuscripts omit *tessaron* ("four"), reading simply *ek ton keraton* ("from the horns"). The Hebrew has *me-arba qarnot mizbach ha-zahav* ("from the four horns of the golden altar"), agreeing with the Vulgate.
- **Note**: Good Vulgate alignment. The four-horned altar is described in Ex 27:2 and 30:2.

### 9:17 — "hyacinthinas" vs. "hyakinthinous" vs. "tekhelet"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *hyacinthinas* ("hyacinth-colored") and Greek *hyakinthinous* ("hyacinth-colored/dark blue") are rendered by the Hebrew *tekhelet* ("blue/violet"). *Tekhelet* is the specific blue dye used in the tabernacle textiles and priestly garments (Ex 25:4, 26:1, 28:31, Num 15:38).
- **Note**: *Tekhelet* carries immense cultic significance in biblical Hebrew. Using it for a demonic cavalry's armor is jarring — this sacred color is being inverted for an infernal context. Whether Delitzsch intended this ironic resonance is unclear.

### 9:20 — Deuteronomy/Psalm idol polemic
- **Issue**: The description of idols that cannot see, hear, or walk is drawn from Ps 115:4-7 / 135:15-17 and Deut 4:28 (*ve-avadtem sham elohim ma'aseh yedei adam etz va-even asher lo yir'un velo yishme'un velo yokhelun velo yerichun*, "and you will serve gods made by human hands, of wood and stone, that neither see nor hear nor eat nor smell"). The Hebrew captures this: *asher lo yir'u velo yishme'u velo yehalekhu*.
- **Note**: The Hebrew vocabulary precisely matches the OT idol polemic tradition. The three verbs (*ra'ah*, *shama*, *halakh* — see, hear, walk) follow the Psalm 115/135 pattern.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:1 — be'er ha-tehom
- **Hebrew**: *mafteach be'er ha-tehom* ("key of the well of the abyss")
- **Note**: *Tehom* ("the deep/abyss") is from Gen 1:2 (*tehom*, "the deep"). *Be'er* ("well/pit") combined with *tehom* creates a powerful compound. The key (*mafteach*) echoes the key of David in 3:7 and the keys of She'ol in 1:18.

### 9:3 — arbeh for "locustae"
- **Hebrew**: *arbeh* ("locusts")
- **Note**: *Arbeh* is the standard biblical Hebrew locust (Ex 10:4, 12-15 — the eighth plague; Joel 1:4, 2:25). The locust plague is one of the most powerful OT judgment images. Joel 1-2 describes an eschatological locust army in terms remarkably similar to Revelation 9.

### 9:7-9 — Joel 2 parallels
- **Hebrew**: *ke-mar'eh susim arukhei milchamah* ("like the appearance of horses prepared for battle")
- **Note**: This is a near-quotation of Joel 2:4 (*ke-mar'eh susim mar'ehu u-khe-farashim ken yerutzun*, "their appearance is like the appearance of horses, and like horsemen they run"). The entire locust-army description in Rev 9:7-9 closely follows Joel 2:4-10. The Hebrew Delitzsch translation recognizes this and uses Joel's vocabulary: *arukhei milchamah* (prepared for battle), *qol kanfeihem ke-qol markevot* (sound of wings like sound of chariots), echoing Joel 2:5.

### 9:11 — Avaddon
- **Hebrew**: *Avaddon* ("Destruction/Place of Ruin")
- **Note**: *Avaddon* appears in Job 26:6, 28:22, 31:12, Ps 88:12, and Prov 15:11 as a synonym for She'ol or the place of destruction. In Job 28:22 it is personified: *Avaddon va-mavet amru* ("Destruction and Death said"). The personification of Abaddon as a king of the abyss extends this OT concept.

### 9:14 — nehar Perat
- **Hebrew**: *ha-nahar ha-gadol nehar Perat* ("the great river, the river Euphrates")
- **Note**: *Ha-nahar ha-gadol nehar Perat* is a quotation from Gen 15:18 and Deut 1:7 — the boundary of the Promised Land. The Euphrates as the eastern frontier has deep biblical-geographical significance. Jer 46:10 places a great divine judgment at the Euphrates (*al nehar Perat*).

### 9:20 — atzabbei zahav va-khesef
- **Hebrew**: *la-shedim ve-la-atzabbei zahav va-khesef u-nechoshet ve-even va-etz*
- **Note**: *Atzabbim* ("idols/images") is from Ps 115:4, 135:15 (*atzabbei ha-goyim kesef ve-zahav*, "the idols of the nations are silver and gold"). *Shedim* ("demons") is from Deut 32:17 (*yizbechu la-shedim lo Eloha*, "they sacrificed to demons, not God") and Ps 106:37. The pairing of *shedim* and *atzabbim* in a single verse perfectly captures the OT equation of idol worship with demon worship.

### 9:21 — kishef for "veneficiis"
- **Hebrew**: *likhashef* ("to practice sorcery")
- **Note**: *Kishuf/khashaf* ("sorcery") is from Deut 18:10, 2 Chr 33:6, Mic 5:11. It is one of the prohibited occult practices in the Torah (Deut 18:10-12). The Vulgate *veneficium* ("poisoning/sorcery") is well-captured.

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 9 is dominated by two OT sources:
- **Joel 1-2**: The eschatological locust army — vv. 3-10
- **Ex 10:4-15**: The eighth plague of locusts — v. 3
- **Gen 1:2**: *Tehom* — the primordial abyss — v. 1
- **1 Kgs 19:4**: Elijah's death wish — v. 6
- **Gen 15:18, Deut 1:7**: Euphrates as boundary — v. 14
- **Ps 115:4-7, 135:15-17**: Idol polemic — v. 20
- **Deut 32:17**: Demons behind idols — v. 20

### Theological Note — 9:20
The pairing of *shedim* (demons) with *atzabbim* (idols) in the Hebrew text makes explicit what Paul teaches in 1 Cor 10:20 — that pagan sacrifices are offered to demons. This equation is rooted in Deut 32:17 and Ps 106:37, making it a deeply biblical concept. The Hebrew renders this theological connection more vividly than either the Vulgate or Greek.
