# Revelation Chapter 17 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 18 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch). No versification discrepancies.

The Vulgate 17:8 ends with *videntes bestiam, quae erat, et non est*, omitting the final clause *kai parestai* ("and will be present/come") that the SBLGNT includes. The Hebrew includes the third element (*ve-tavo*, "and will come"), following the Greek. See content alignment note below.

## Content Alignment

### 17:1 — "damnationem meretricis magnae" / "to krima tes pornes tes megales" / "mishpat ha-zonah ha-gedolah"
- **Vulgate**: *damnationem meretricis magnae* ("the condemnation of the great harlot")
- **Greek**: *to krima tes pornes tes megales* ("the judgment of the great prostitute")
- **Hebrew**: *mishpat ha-zonah ha-gedolah* ("the judgment of the great harlot")
- **Note**: *Mishpat* ("judgment") renders *krima/damnationem*. The Vulgate *damnationem* is stronger than *krima* (which is simply "judgment/verdict") — it implies condemnation specifically. *Mishpat* is neutral (it can mean acquittal or condemnation), so it follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate's intensified reading. *Zonah* ("harlot/prostitute") is the standard OT term (Josh 2:1, Judg 11:1, 16:1, Prov 6:26, 7:10, Isa 1:21, Ezek 16:15-35, 23:1-49, Hos 1-3, Nah 3:4). The prophetic tradition of depicting unfaithful cities/nations as harlots is extensive.

### 17:2 — "fornicati sunt reges terrae" / "eporneusan hoi basileis tes ges" / "zanu achareha malkhei ha-adamah"
- **Hebrew**: *asher zanu achareha malkhei ha-adamah* ("after whom the kings of the earth committed fornication")
- **Note**: *Zanu achareha* ("they fornicated after her") uses the characteristic OT idiom *zanah acharei* ("to whore after"), which appears throughout the prophets for religious and political apostasy (Ex 34:15-16, Lev 17:7, 20:5-6, Deut 31:16, Judg 2:17, 8:27, 33, Ezek 6:9, 16:15-34, 23:3-30, Hos 1:2, 2:7, 4:12, 9:1). This is perfect biblical idiom — the very grammar of the phrase (*zanah + acharei*) signals covenantal unfaithfulness.

### 17:3 — "in desertum" / "eis eremon" / "ha-midbarah"
- **Hebrew**: *va-yevi'eni va-ruach ha-midbarah* ("and he brought me in the spirit to the wilderness")
- **Note**: The transport *ba-ruach* ("in the spirit") echoes Ezek 3:12, 14, 8:3, 11:1, 24, 37:1, 43:5. *Ha-midbarah* ("to the wilderness") contrasts with 21:10 where John is taken to a great mountain to see the holy city. The harlot is found in the wilderness (place of desolation and demonic habitation — Isa 13:21-22, 34:14, Mt 12:43), while the bride is found on a mountain (place of divine encounter — Ex 19, Isa 2:2-3).

### 17:3 — "bestiam coccineam" / "therion kokkinon" / "chayyah adummah ka-tola"
- **Hebrew**: *chayyah adummah ka-tola* ("a beast, scarlet as the crimson worm")
- **Note**: *Adummah ka-tola* ("red as the tola-worm") is more specific than the Vulgate *coccineam* ("scarlet") or Greek *kokkinon* ("scarlet"). *Tola* / *tola'at shani* is the scarlet dye of the tabernacle (Ex 25:4, 26:1, 31, 36, 28:5-6, Lev 14:4, 6, 49, 51-52, Num 19:6, Isa 1:18). Adding *ka-tola* ("like the crimson worm") is a Hebrew intensifier that connects the scarlet color to the tabernacle materials — an ironic contrast, as the harlot wears the colors of the sacred.

### 17:4 — "purpura et coccino" / "porphyroun kai kokkinon" / "argaman ve-shani"
- **Hebrew**: *levushah argaman ve-shani* ("her garment is purple and scarlet")
- **Note**: *Argaman* ("purple") and *shani* ("scarlet") are the colors of the tabernacle curtains and priestly garments (Ex 25:4, 26:1, 31, 36, 28:5-6, 8, 15, 33). The harlot is dressed in the sacred vestment colors — she is a counterfeit of the holy. The high priest wore *argaman* and *shani*; the harlot's appropriation of these colors signals religious parody.

### 17:4 — "poculum aureum" / "poterion chrysoun" / "kos zahav"
- **Hebrew**: *u-ve-yadah kos zahav mele'ah to'evot ve-tum'at taznutah* ("and in her hand a golden cup full of abominations and the uncleanness of her fornication")
- **Note**: *Kos zahav* ("golden cup") echoes Jer 51:7 (*kos zahav Bavel be-yad YHVH*, "Babylon was a golden cup in the LORD's hand"). The Jeremiah parallel is exact — the golden cup of Babylon. *To'evot* ("abominations") is the standard OT term for idolatrous and morally repugnant practices (Deut 7:25-26, 12:31, 18:9-12, 1 Kgs 14:24, 2 Kgs 16:3, 21:2, 11, Ezek 5:9, 11, 6:9, 7:3-4, 8, 9, 16:2, 22, 36, 43, 47, 50-51). *Tum'ah* ("uncleanness/impurity") is the Levitical purity term (Lev 5:3, 7:20-21, 15:2-33, 18:19, Num 5:19, 19:13, Ezek 22:15, 36:25, 29).

### 17:5 — "Mysterium" / "mysterion" / "be-derekh sod"
- **Vulgate**: *Mysterium: Babylon magna* ("Mystery: Babylon the great")
- **Greek**: *mysterion, Babylon he megale* ("mystery, Babylon the great")
- **Hebrew**: *shem be-derekh sod Bavel ha-gedolah* ("a name, by way of mystery: Babylon the great")
- **Note**: The Hebrew renders *mysterion* as *be-derekh sod* ("in the way/manner of a secret"), using *sod* ("secret/council/mystery"), which appears in Amos 3:7 (*ki lo ya'aseh Adonai YHVH davar ki im galah sodo el avadav ha-nevi'im*, "for the Lord GOD does nothing without revealing his secret to his servants the prophets"), Dan 2:18-19, 27-30, 47, 4:6 (Aramaic *raz*, equivalent to *sod*), Prov 11:13, 20:19, 25:9. The phrase *be-derekh sod* is a circumlocution that captures the sense of an allegorical/hidden name.

### 17:5 — "mater fornicationum et abominationum terrae"
- **Hebrew**: *em ha-zonot ve-to'avot ha-adamah* ("mother of the harlots and abominations of the earth")
- **Note**: *Em ha-zonot* ("mother of the harlots") uses *em* ("mother") as a title of primacy/origin. In the OT, cities are personified as mothers: Ezek 16:44-45 (*hinneh khol ha-moshel alayikh yimshol lemor ke-immah bittah*, "everyone who uses proverbs will say of you, 'Like mother, like daughter'"), Hos 2:4-5 [2:2-3], 4:5. Babylon as *em ha-zonot* is the mother-city of all spiritual fornication — the source from which all idolatry and apostasy flows.

### 17:6 — "de sanguine sanctorum et de sanguine martyrum Jesu"
- **Vulgate**: *ebriam de sanguine sanctorum, et de sanguine martyrum Jesu* ("drunk with the blood of the saints and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus")
- **Greek**: *methuousan ek tou haimatos ton hagion kai ek tou haimatos ton martyron Iesou*
- **Hebrew**: *shekhurah mi-dam ha-qedoshim u-mi-dam edei Yeshua* ("drunk from the blood of the saints and from the blood of the witnesses of Jesus")
- **Note**: The Hebrew distinguishes two groups: *ha-qedoshim* ("the saints/holy ones") and *edei Yeshua* ("the witnesses of Jesus"). *Edei* ("witnesses of") uses *ed*, the legal/covenantal witness term (Deut 17:6, 19:15, Isa 43:10, 12, 44:8). The Vulgate has *martyrum* (from Greek *martyron*), which by the time of the Clementine text carried the specific sense of those who died for the faith. The Hebrew *edei* ("witnesses") preserves the earlier, broader sense of testimony-bearers.

### 17:7 — "sacramentum" vs. "mysterion" vs. "sod"
- **Vulgate**: *sacramentum mulieris* ("the mystery/sacrament of the woman")
- **Greek**: *to mysterion tes gynaikos* ("the mystery of the woman")
- **Hebrew**: *sod ha-ishah* ("the secret/mystery of the woman")
- **Note**: The Vulgate uses *sacramentum* (a significant Latin theological term meaning "sacred mystery/oath/sacrament") where the Greek has *mysterion*. The Hebrew *sod* captures the sense of hidden meaning to be revealed. In Catholic theological tradition, *sacramentum* carries weight beyond mere "mystery" — it implies a sacred sign that effects what it signifies. The Hebrew *sod* does not carry this sacramental connotation.

### 17:8 — "fuit et non est" / "en kai ouk estin" / "hayetah ve-einennah"
- **Hebrew**: *ha-chayyah asher ra'ita hi hayetah ve-einennah ve-hi ta'aleh mi-tehom ve-tered la-avaddon* ("the beast which you saw — she was and is not, and she will come up from the abyss and go down to destruction")
- **Note**: The formula *hayetah ve-einennah* ("she was and is not") parodies the divine title *ha-hoveh ve-hayah ve-yavo* ("who is and was and is to come"). The beast's existence is defined by absence and futility, contrasting with God's eternal presence. *Tehom* ("abyss/deep") echoes Gen 1:2 (*ve-choshekh al penei tehom*, "and darkness was over the face of the deep") and the primordial chaos. *Avaddon* ("destruction/Abaddon") appears in Job 26:6, 28:22, 31:12, Ps 88:12, Prov 15:11, 27:20 as the realm of destruction and death.

### 17:8 — Vulgate omission of "kai parestai"
- **Vulgate**: *videntes bestiam, quae erat, et non est* — ends here
- **Greek**: *bleponten to therion hoti en kai ouk estin kai parestai* — includes *kai parestai* ("and will be present")
- **Hebrew**: *bi-rotam et ha-chayyah asher hayetah ve-einennah ve-tavo* — includes *ve-tavo* ("and will come")
- **Flag**: The Hebrew includes the third element (*ve-tavo*) following the Greek against the Vulgate, which omits it. This is a case where the Vulgate text is shorter and the Hebrew follows the longer Greek reading.

### 17:9 — "Septem capita septem montes sunt" / "shiv'at ha-rashim shiv'at ha-rim hemmah"
- **Hebrew**: *shiv'at ha-rashim shiv'at ha-harim hemmah asher ha-ishah yoshevet aleihem* ("the seven heads are seven mountains on which the woman sits")
- **Note**: The identification of the seven heads with seven mountains was widely understood in antiquity as a reference to Rome (the city of seven hills). The Hebrew *harim* ("mountains") is a standard term. The Vulgate adds *et reges septem sunt* ("and they are seven kings"), making explicit that the mountains are also kings. The Hebrew places this in 17:10 as a separate sentence, following the Greek more closely.

### 17:9 — "Hic est sensus qui habet sapientiam" / "zeh ha-muvan u-vo chokhmah"
- **Hebrew**: *zeh ha-muvan u-vo chokhmah* ("this is the understanding, and in it is wisdom")
- **Note**: The call for wisdom echoes 13:18 (*ba-zot chokhmah*, "in this is wisdom"). *Muvan* ("understanding/meaning") is from the root *b-y-n* ("to discern/understand"), used in Dan 9:22 (*le-havinekha binah*, "to give you understanding") and Prov 1:2, 2:2-6, 3:5. The pairing of *muvan* and *chokhmah* echoes the Solomonic wisdom tradition.

### 17:12 — "potestatem tamquam reges una hora"
- **Vulgate**: *potestatem tamquam reges una hora accipient post bestiam* ("they will receive authority as kings for one hour with the beast")
- **Greek**: *exousian hos basileis mian horan lambanousin meta tou theriou* ("they receive authority as kings for one hour with the beast")
- **Hebrew**: *raq le-sha'ah achat memshalah ka-melakhim yeqabbelu im ha-chayyah* ("only for one hour dominion as kings they will receive with the beast")
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds *raq* ("only/merely"), which is not in the Vulgate or Greek but emphasizes the brevity. *Sha'ah* ("hour") is biblical Hebrew (Dan 3:6, 15, 4:16 [4:19]). The transience of their power is stressed — *le-sha'ah achat* ("for a single hour") contrasts with God's eternal reign (*le-olmei olamim*).

### 17:14 — "Dominus dominorum et Rex regum" / "kyrios kyrion estin kai basileus basileon"
- **Vulgate**: *quoniam Dominus dominorum est, et Rex regum* ("for he is Lord of lords and King of kings")
- **Greek**: *hoti kyrios kyrion estin kai basileus basileon*
- **Hebrew**: *ki hu Adonei ha-Adonim u-Melekh ha-melakhim* ("for he is Lord of lords and King of kings")
- **Note**: All three align. *Adonei ha-Adonim u-Melekh ha-melakhim* combines Deut 10:17 (*ki YHVH Eloheikhem hu Elohei ha-elohim va-Adonei ha-Adonim*, "for the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords") and Dan 2:47 (*Elahakhon Elah elahin u-Mare malkhim*, "your God is God of gods and Lord of kings"). The title is applied to the Lamb — a striking Christological claim, as these titles belong to YHVH in the OT. The Hebrew makes the divine identity claim explicit by using the exact OT vocabulary.

### 17:14 — "vocati, electi, et fideles" / "qeru'im u-vechirim ve-ne'emanim"
- **Hebrew**: *qeru'im u-vechirim ve-ne'emanim hemmah* ("called and chosen and faithful are they")
- **Note**: *Qeru'im* ("called") — Isa 41:9, 42:6, 48:12, 49:1. *Bechirim* ("chosen/elect") — Isa 42:1, 43:20, 45:4, 65:9, 15, 22. *Ne'emanim* ("faithful/trustworthy") — Num 12:7 (*be-khol beiti ne'eman hu*, "in all my house he is faithful" — said of Moses), 1 Sam 2:35, Neh 13:13, Isa 8:2. The triple designation draws on prophetic election vocabulary.

### 17:15 — "populi sunt, et gentes, et linguae"
- **Vulgate**: *populi sunt, et gentes, et linguae* ("they are peoples, nations, and tongues")
- **Greek**: *laoi kai ochloi eisin kai ethne kai glossai* ("they are peoples and multitudes and nations and tongues")
- **Hebrew**: *ammim va-hamonim hemmah ve-goyim u-leshonot* ("peoples and multitudes are they, and nations and tongues")
- **Note**: The Greek and Hebrew have a four-member list (peoples, multitudes, nations, tongues) while the Vulgate has three (peoples, nations, tongues), omitting *ochloi*/*hamonim* ("multitudes"). The Hebrew follows the Greek in including *hamonim* ("multitudes"), diverging from the Vulgate.
- **Flag**: Hebrew includes *hamonim* following Greek against Vulgate.

### 17:16 — "desolatam... et nudam... manducabunt... igni concremabunt"
- **Hebrew**: *ve-asuha galmudah va-arummah ve-akhelu et besarah ve-otah yisrefu va-esh* ("and they will make her desolate and naked and eat her flesh and burn her with fire")
- **Note**: The destruction of the harlot echoes the punishment for a priest's daughter who commits prostitution (Lev 21:9, *ba-esh tissaref*, "she shall be burned with fire") and the judgment on Oholah and Oholibah in Ezek 23:25-29, 47 (stripping, plundering, burning). *Galmudah* ("desolate/barren") appears in Isa 49:21 and is a powerful choice — it connotes both childlessness and abandonment. *Ve-akhelu et besarah* ("and they will eat her flesh") echoes Ezek 39:17-20 (the great sacrificial feast) and 2 Kgs 9:36 (dogs eating Jezebel's flesh). The Jezebel parallel is especially striking — Jezebel, the archetypal foreign queen who promoted false worship, met this exact fate.

### 17:17 — "Deus enim dedit in corda eorum"
- **Hebrew**: *ki ha-Elohim natan be-libbam la'asot et atzato* ("for God put it in their hearts to do his purpose")
- **Note**: *Natan be-libbam* ("put in their hearts") echoes Neh 2:12, 7:5 (*va-yitten Elohai el libbi*, "and my God put it into my heart") and Ezra 7:27. The principle that God directs even the actions of hostile kings to accomplish his purposes echoes Prov 21:1 (*palgei mayim lev melekh be-yad YHVH al kol asher yachpotz yattenu*, "the king's heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will") and Isa 10:5-7 (Assyria as God's unwitting instrument).

### 17:18 — "civitas magna quae habet regnum super reges terrae"
- **Hebrew**: *ve-ha-ishah asher ra'ita hi ha-ir ha-gedolah asher hi geveret mamlkhot ha-aretz* ("and the woman whom you saw is the great city which is mistress of the kingdoms of the earth")
- **Note**: *Geveret mamlkhot ha-aretz* ("mistress of the kingdoms of the earth") uses *geveret* ("mistress/lady"), which echoes Isa 47:5, 7 (*ki lo tosifi yiqre'u lakh geveret mamlakhot*, "they shall no more call you mistress of kingdoms") — Isaiah's oracle against Babylon. The verbal parallel is nearly exact: Isaiah's *geveret mamlakhot* becomes Revelation's *geveret mamlkhot*. This is a superb allusion linking the Revelation Babylon directly to Isaiah's Babylon.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 17:1 — ha-zonah ha-gedolah ha-yoshevet al mayim rabbim
- **Note**: *Ha-yoshevet al mayim rabbim* ("who sits on many waters") echoes Jer 51:13 (*shokhanti al mayim rabbim*, "you who dwell by many waters" — addressed to Babylon). The Jeremiah parallel is direct — Babylon sitting on the Euphrates and its canal system. *Mayim rabbim* ("many waters") also carries cosmic overtones (Ps 29:3, 93:4, Isa 17:12-13, Hab 3:15).

### 17:3 — mele'at shemot giddufim
- **Hebrew**: *mele'at shemot giddufim* ("full of names of blasphemies")
- **Note**: *Giddufim* ("blasphemies") is from *g-d-f* ("to revile/blaspheme"), which appears in 2 Kgs 19:6, 22, Isa 37:6, 23, Ezek 20:27, Num 15:30. The root is specifically used for blasphemy against God in the OT prophetic texts.

### 17:8 — tehom / avaddon
- **Note**: The pairing of *tehom* ("abyss/deep") and *avaddon* ("destruction/perdition") creates a descent narrative: the beast rises from the primordial deep and descends to the place of destruction. Both are OT underworld/chaos terms. *Tehom* is the pre-creation chaos (Gen 1:2), *avaddon* is the realm of death and annihilation (Job 26:6, 28:22, Ps 88:12, Prov 15:11).

### 17:14 — Adonei ha-Adonim u-Melekh ha-melakhim
- **Note**: This is perhaps the most concentrated Christological title in the Hebrew translation. By placing the Deuteronomy 10:17 YHVH-title on the Lamb, Delitzsch makes an unmistakable identification of the Lamb with the God of Israel. The Hebrew reader hears *Adonei ha-Adonim* and recalls *YHVH... Adonei ha-Adonim* from Deuteronomy. The title transfer from YHVH to the Lamb is a Trinitarian affirmation.

## Other Notes

### Red-Text Candidates
- No direct speech of the Holy Trinity in this chapter. The angel speaks (17:1, 7, 15), and the narrative describes events. Christ is spoken *about* (17:14) but does not speak directly.

### Babylon as Anti-Church
In Catholic exegesis, Babylon the Great is understood as the worldly power that persecutes the faithful across all ages — not identified with any single historical city or institution, but as a type of the City of Man in opposition to the City of God (cf. Augustine, *De Civitate Dei*). The harlot seated on the beast represents the alliance of worldly political power with false religion. The destruction of Babylon by the very kings who served her (17:16) illustrates the self-destructive nature of evil — the beast and its allies ultimately turn against their own.

### OT Allusion Density
- **Jer 51:7, 13**: Babylon as golden cup, dwelling by many waters — vv. 1, 4
- **Ezek 16, 23**: Oholah and Oholibah — harlot-city judgment — vv. 1-6, 16
- **Isa 47:5, 7**: Mistress of kingdoms — v. 18
- **Deut 10:17**: Lord of lords — v. 14
- **Dan 2:47, 7:7-8**: Lord of kings, ten horns — vv. 12, 14
- **Lev 21:9**: Burning the prostitute — v. 16
- **2 Kgs 9:36**: Jezebel's flesh eaten — v. 16
- **Prov 21:1**: God directing kings' hearts — v. 17
- **Gen 1:2, Job 26:6**: Abyss and Abaddon — v. 8
- **Isa 1:21**: Faithful city become a harlot — v. 1

### Jezebel Typology
The destruction of the harlot (17:16 — stripped, flesh eaten, burned) precisely mirrors the fate of Jezebel (2 Kgs 9:30-37). Jezebel painted her face (cosmetics = external adornment), was thrown from a window, and dogs ate her flesh. The harlot of Revelation is adorned in purple and gold, is stripped by the ten horns, and her flesh is consumed. This is not accidental — Jezebel is already invoked in Rev 2:20 as a type of false prophetess leading God's people into sexual immorality and idolatry. The harlot of chapter 17 is the eschatological Jezebel.
