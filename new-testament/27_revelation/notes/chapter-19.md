# Revelation Chapter 19 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 21 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch). No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 19:1 — "ha-yeshu'ah" for "salus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *salus* ("salvation"), Greek *he soteria* ("the salvation"). The Hebrew has *ha-yeshu'ah* ("the salvation/deliverance"). All three align well. *Yeshu'ah* is the standard biblical Hebrew for divine deliverance (Ex 14:13, 15:2, Ps 3:9, 118:14).
- **Note**: Good alignment. The word *yeshu'ah* also carries a phonetic connection to the name *Yeshua* (Jesus), which is theologically resonant in the Hebrew translation — "the salvation belongs to our God" and "Jesus belongs to our God" form an implicit wordplay.

### 19:2 — "heshiv gemul" for "judicavit/vindicavit"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses *judicavit de meretrice magna* ("he judged the great harlot") and *vindicavit sanguinem* ("he vindicated the blood"). The Hebrew compresses: *heshiv gemul al ha-zonah ha-gedolah* ("he repaid recompense upon the great harlot") and *lidrosh mi-yadah et dam avadav* ("to require from her hand the blood of his servants").
- **Note**: The phrase *lidrosh dam* ("to require blood") is the standard OT phrase for blood vengeance: Gen 9:5 (*et dimkhem le-nafshoteikhem edrosh*, "your blood, for your lives, I will require"), 2 Sam 4:11, Ezek 3:18, 20. The connection to Gen 9:5 places Babylon's judgment within the Noahic covenant framework.

### 19:4 — "chayyot" for "animalia"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *quatuor animalia* ("four living creatures"), Greek *tessara zoa*. The Hebrew *arba ha-chayyot* ("the four living creatures") uses the same term as throughout the translation. As noted previously (ch. 11 notes), *chayyah* serves for both the holy creatures (*zoa*) and the beast (*therion*), though context disambiguates.
- **Note**: Here *chayyot* clearly refers to the holy throne creatures (cf. Ezek 1:5-14). No ambiguity in this context.

### 19:5 — "hallelu et Eloheinu" for "Laudem dicite Deo nostro"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Laudem dicite Deo nostro* ("Say praise to our God"), Greek *Aineite to theo hemon* ("Praise our God"). The Hebrew *hallelu et Eloheinu* ("Praise our God") uses the Psalmic *hallelu* imperative.
- **Note**: *Hallelu* is the standard Psalms praise imperative (Ps 113:1, 117:1, 135:1, 148:1). Perfectly appropriate. The voice from the throne commanding praise connects to the Psalmic liturgy.

### 19:6 — "malakh Eloheinu YHVH Tzevaot" for "regnavit Dominus Deus noster omnipotens"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *regnavit Dominus Deus noster omnipotens* ("the Lord God almighty has reigned"), Greek *ebasileusen kyrios ho theos ho pantokrator* ("the Lord God the Almighty has reigned"). The Hebrew expands this significantly: *malakh Eloheinu YHVH Tzevaot* ("our God, the LORD of hosts, has reigned").
- **Vulgate**: *Dominus Deus noster omnipotens*
- **Hebrew**: *Eloheinu YHVH Tzevaot*
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds the Tetragrammaton and renders *omnipotens/pantokrator* as *Tzevaot* ("of hosts"), which is Delitzsch's standard rendering throughout Revelation. The phrase *YHVH malakh* ("the LORD reigns") is the opening of the enthronement psalms (Ps 93:1, 97:1, 99:1). By placing *malakh* first, Delitzsch directly echoes these psalms. Outstanding.

### 19:7 — "hitqadshah" for "praeparavit se"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *uxor ejus praeparavit se* ("his wife has prepared herself"), Greek *he gyne autou hetoimasen heauten* ("his wife has made herself ready"). The Hebrew uses *ishto hitqadshah* ("his wife has sanctified herself"), using the root *q-d-sh* ("sanctify/consecrate") rather than a more neutral "prepare" (*hikhinah*).
- **Vulgate**: *praeparavit se*
- **Hebrew**: *hitqadshah*
- **Note**: *Hitqadshah* carries stronger ritual/covenantal connotations than the source texts. It echoes the bridal purification before the Sinai theophany (Ex 19:10, 14-15: *lekh el ha-am ve-qiddashtam*, "go to the people and consecrate them"). This is a theological intensification: the Bride of the Lamb undergoes the same consecration as Israel before meeting God at Sinai. An effective Delitzsch choice, though not strictly literal.

### 19:8 — "butz tahor va-tzach" for "byssino splendenti et candido"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *byssino splendenti et candido* ("splendid and white fine linen"), Greek *byssinon lampron katharon* ("bright clean fine linen"). The Hebrew *butz tahor va-tzach* ("clean and bright fine linen") uses *butz* (fine linen, 1 Chr 4:21, 2 Chr 3:14), *tahor* (clean/pure, ritual term), *tzach* (bright/radiant, Song 5:10).
- **Note**: *Tzach* from Song 5:10 (*dodi tzach ve-adom*, "my beloved is radiant and ruddy") adds a bridal/Song of Songs dimension to the priestly garments. Both *tahor* and *tzach* are biblical Hebrew.

### 19:8 — "tzidqot ha-qedoshim" for "justificationes sanctorum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *justificationes... sanctorum* ("the righteous deeds of the saints"), Greek *ta dikaiomata ton hagion*. The Hebrew *tzidqot ha-qedoshim* ("the righteous acts of the saints") is precise.
- **Note**: *Tzedaqah/tzedaqot* as "righteous acts" is standard biblical Hebrew (Judg 5:11: *tzidqot YHVH*, "the righteous acts of the LORD"; 1 Sam 12:7, Mic 6:5). Good.

### 19:9 — "mishteh chatunat ha-seh" for "coenam nuptiarum Agni"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *coenam nuptiarum Agni* ("the supper of the marriage of the Lamb"), Greek *to deipnon tou gamou tou arniou*. The Hebrew *mishteh chatunat ha-seh* ("the banquet of the wedding of the Lamb") uses *mishteh* ("feast/banquet"), the standard term for festive meals (Gen 21:8, 26:30, 29:22, Esth 1:3, 5).
- **Note**: *Mishteh* (from the root *sh-t-h*, "drink") emphasizes the celebratory drinking aspect. This is the standard OT term for a wedding feast (Gen 29:22, Judg 14:10).

### 19:11 — "ne'eman va-amitti" for "Fidelis et Verax"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Fidelis et Verax* ("Faithful and True"), Greek *pistos... kai alethinos* ("faithful and true"). The Hebrew *ne'eman va-amitti* ("faithful and true") uses *ne'eman* (faithful, Num 12:7, 1 Sam 2:35, Isa 22:23) and *amitti* (true/genuine, from *emet*).
- **Note**: *Ne'eman* echoes the *eved ne'eman* ("faithful servant") language of 1 Sam 2:35 and the *yated ne'emanah* ("faithful peg") of Isa 22:23. Both are good biblical vocabulary.

### 19:13 — "levush me'uddam ba-dam" for "veste aspersa sanguine"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *veste aspersa sanguine* ("a garment sprinkled with blood"), the Greek *himation bebammenon haimati* ("a garment dipped in blood"). The Hebrew *levush me'uddam ba-dam* ("a garment reddened in blood") uses *me'uddam*, from the root *'-d-m* ("be red"), creating a wordplay with *dam* ("blood").
- **Note**: The *'-d-m* / *dam* paronomasia is elegant. The imagery echoes Isa 63:1-3 (*maddua adom li-levushekha*, "why is your apparel red?"), the treading of the winepress passage. Delitzsch's choice captures the Isaianic allusion through shared vocabulary.

### 19:13 — "Devar ha-Elohim" for "Verbum Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Verbum Dei* ("the Word of God"), Greek *ho Logos tou Theou*. The Hebrew *Devar ha-Elohim* ("the Word of God"). All align.
- **Note**: *Devar ha-Elohim* / *Devar YHVH* is the standard prophetic formula throughout the OT (Gen 15:1, 1 Sam 3:1, 1 Kgs 12:22, Isa 1:10, Jer 1:2, Ezek 1:3, Hos 1:1, etc.). As a Christological title, it identifies the rider with the prophetic Word that has been active throughout Israel's history.

### 19:15 — "shevet barzel" and "purat yein" — Psalm 2 and Isaiah 63
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *reget eas in virga ferrea* ("he will rule them with a rod of iron") and *calcat torcular vini furoris irae Dei omnipotentis* ("he treads the winepress of the fury of the wrath of God almighty"). The Hebrew: *yir'em be-shevet barzel* ("he will shepherd/rule them with a rod of iron") and *dorekh purat yein chamat af Elohei ha-Tzevaot* ("he treads the wine-press of the wine of the wrath of the God of hosts").
- **Note**: *Shevet barzel* is verbatim from Ps 2:9 (*tero'em be-shevet barzel*, "you shall break them with a rod of iron"). *Purat/purah* (winepress) echoes Isa 63:3 (*purah darakhti levaddi*, "I have trodden the winepress alone"). Both OT allusions are perfectly captured.

### 19:16 — "Melekh ha-melakhim va-Adonei ha-Adonim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Rex regum et Dominus dominantium* ("King of kings and Lord of lords"). The Hebrew *Melekh ha-melakhim va-Adonei ha-Adonim* ("King of kings and Lord of lords").
- **Note**: *Melekh ha-melakhim* echoes Dan 2:37 (*anta malka melekh malkhayya*, "you, O king, king of kings" — spoken to Nebuchadnezzar, now transferred to Christ). *Adonei ha-Adonim* echoes Deut 10:17 (*ki YHVH Eloheikhem hu Elohei ha-Elohim va-Adonei ha-Adonim*, "for the LORD your God is God of gods and Lord of lords"). The title originally applied to YHVH in Deuteronomy is now applied to Christ — a strong Christological claim.

### 19:17 — "zevach ha-gadol" for "coenam magnam Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *coenam magnam Dei* ("the great supper of God"), Greek *to deipnon to mega tou theou* ("the great supper of God"). The Hebrew *zevach ha-gadol asher le-Elohim* ("the great sacrifice which is for God") uses *zevach* ("sacrifice") rather than *mishteh* ("feast/supper").
- **Note**: This is a significant interpretive choice. *Zevach* frames the battle as a sacrificial offering, echoing Ezek 39:17 (*hikkavtzu uvo'u he'asfu mi-saviv al zivchi asher ani zoveach lakhem zevach gadol*, "assemble and come, gather from all around to my sacrifice that I am sacrificing for you, a great sacrifice"). The Ezekiel 39 Gog/Magog sacrifice is the direct OT background. *Zevach* is more theologically loaded than a neutral "supper/feast" and better captures the sacrificial nature of the scene.

### 19:20 — "agam ha-esh" for "stagnum ignis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *stagnum ignis ardentis sulphure* ("the lake of fire burning with sulfur"), Greek *ten limnen tou pyros*. The Hebrew *agam ha-esh ha-bo'er be-gafrit* ("the lake of fire burning with sulfur") uses *agam* ("pool/lake"), which appears in the OT for natural bodies of water (Isa 35:7, 41:18, Ps 107:35, 114:8).
- **Note**: *Agam* is good biblical Hebrew. The combination with *gafrit* ("sulfur/brimstone") echoes the destruction of Sodom: Gen 19:24 (*YHVH himtir al Sedom ve-al Amorah gafrit va-esh*, "the LORD rained upon Sodom and Gomorrah sulfur and fire").

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 19:1 — Halleluyah
- **Hebrew**: *Halleluyah*
- **Note**: The four Hallelujahs in this chapter (vv. 1, 3, 4, 6) are the only occurrences in the New Testament. The Hebrew preserves the original form — *Hallelu-Yah* ("Praise the LORD") — which is already Hebrew. This is one case where the Hebrew translation is, paradoxically, more "original" than the Greek or Latin, since *Halleluyah* is a Hebrew liturgical exclamation (Ps 104:35, 105:45, 106:1, 48, 111:1, etc.).

### 19:6 — ke-qol mayim rabbim
- **Hebrew**: *u-khe-qol mayim rabbim u-khe-qol re'amim chazaqim*
- **Note**: *Qol mayim rabbim* ("the sound of many waters") is from Ezek 1:24, 43:2 and Ps 93:4. *Qol re'amim* ("the sound of thunders") echoes the Sinai theophany (Ex 19:16) and the throne-room scene of Ezek 1. The combination places this doxology within the prophetic theophanic tradition.

### 19:11 — shofet ve-lochem
- **Hebrew**: *u-ve-tzedeq hu shofet ve-lochem*
- **Note**: *Shofet ve-lochem* ("judging and fighting") combines the roles of the biblical *shofet* (judge-warrior, as in the book of Judges) with the divine warrior. Cf. Isa 11:4 (*ve-shafat be-tzedeq dallim*, "he shall judge the poor in righteousness") and Ex 15:3 (*YHVH ish milchamah*, "the LORD is a man of war"). Christ is presented as both righteous judge and divine warrior.

### 19:14 — Tziv'ot ha-shamayim
- **Hebrew**: *ve-tziv'ot ha-shamayim yotz'im acharav*
- **Note**: *Tziv'ot ha-shamayim* ("the hosts of heaven") echoes the angelic armies of 1 Kgs 22:19, 2 Chr 18:18, Neh 9:6. The phrase *yotz'im acharav* ("going out after him") uses military march language — cf. Judg 4:14 (*YHVH yatza lefanekha*, "the LORD has gone out before you"). The heavenly armies follow their commander.

### 19:15 — cherev chaddah
- **Hebrew**: *cherev chaddah* ("sharp sword")
- **Note**: The Greek has *rhomphaia* (a large two-edged sword/broadsword). The Hebrew *cherev* is the standard biblical sword (Gen 3:24, Ex 5:21, Num 22:23, etc.). *Chaddah* ("sharp") is found in Ezek 5:1 (*cherev chaddah*, "a sharp sword"), Ps 57:5 (*shinannam cherev chaddah*, "their teeth are sharp swords"). Good biblical pairing.

### 19:18 — besar melakhim
- **Hebrew**: *basar melakhim u-vesar sarei alafim u-vesar gibborim*
- **Note**: The repetition of *basar* ("flesh") five times mirrors the Greek's fivefold *sarkas*. The vocabulary is from Ezek 39:18-20, the Gog/Magog feast: *basar gibborim tokhelu ve-dam nesi'ei ha-aretz tishtu* ("you shall eat the flesh of the mighty and drink the blood of the princes of the earth"). *Gibborim* ("mighty men") is the key shared term.

## Other Notes

### Red-Text Candidates
- **19:5**: "Praise our God, all his servants" — the voice from the throne. The speaker's identity is ambiguous (angel? God? Christ?). If from the throne, this could be divine speech.
- **19:7a**: *Nismechah ve-nagilah* is the heavenly multitude's speech, not divine speech.
- **19:9b**: "These are the true words of God" — spoken by the angel, quoting/affirming divine authority. Not directly God's speech.
- **19:11-16**: The entire rider passage describes Christ's actions and titles but is not direct speech. No red text here, though the titles (*Ne'eman va-Amitti*, *Devar ha-Elohim*, *Melekh ha-Melakhim*) are Christological.

### Theological Note — The Four Hallelujahs (19:1, 3, 4, 6)
The four *Halleluyah* exclamations form the only New Testament Hallel. In Jewish liturgy, the Hallel psalms (113-118) are sung at Passover, Sukkot, and Hanukkah. The heavenly Hallel in Revelation 19 celebrates the eschatological Passover — the definitive deliverance from Babylon/Egypt. The Hebrew translation makes this liturgical connection transparent, since *Halleluyah* is already Hebrew and connects seamlessly to the Psalter.

### Theological Note — 19:7-9 Marriage Supper of the Lamb
The *chatunat ha-Seh* ("wedding of the Lamb") is the eschatological fulfillment of the covenant marriage between God and Israel (Hos 2:21-22, Isa 54:5, 62:4-5, Jer 2:2). The Hebrew vocabulary (*chatunnah*, *kallah*, *mishteh*) is from the OT marriage tradition. The Bride's garment of *butz* (fine linen) = *tzidqot ha-qedoshim* (righteous deeds) connects to the priestly garments of the Tabernacle/Temple service, where *butz/shesh* is the standard priestly fabric (Ex 28:39, 39:27-28).

- **Isa 63:1-6**: Garment dipped in blood, treading the winepress — vv. 13, 15
- **Ezek 39:17-20**: The great sacrifice, birds eating flesh — vv. 17-18
- **Deut 10:17**: Lord of lords — v. 16
- **Dan 2:37**: King of kings — v. 16
- **Ps 93:1, 97:1, 99:1**: "The LORD reigns" — v. 6
- **Isa 11:4**: Judging in righteousness — v. 11
- **Gen 19:24**: Sulfur and fire — v. 20
