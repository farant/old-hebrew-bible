# Revelation Chapter 10 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 11 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 10:1 — "amictum nube" vs. "periblamenon nephelen"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *amictum nube* ("clothed in a cloud"), Greek *peribeblegmenon nephelen* ("wrapped in a cloud"), and Hebrew *oteh anan* ("wrapped/clothed in cloud") all agree. The Hebrew *oteh* echoes Ps 104:2 (*oteh or ka-salmah*, "wrapped in light as a garment") — the same verb used of God's self-clothing in light is here applied to the angel.
- **Note**: Subtle but significant OT allusion through the verb *oteh*.

### 10:2 — "libellum" vs. "biblidaridion" vs. "sefer qaton"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *libellum* ("little book") and Greek *biblidaridion* ("little scroll" — a double diminutive) both emphasize the small size. The Hebrew *sefer qaton* ("small scroll") captures this. Later in v. 8, the Vulgate calls it *librum* (full-sized "book") while the Greek retains the diminutive *biblion* and the Hebrew has *ha-sefer ha-qaton* ("the small scroll").
- **Note**: The Vulgate inconsistently shifts from diminutive to full form, while the Greek and Hebrew maintain the diminutive. Minor.

### 10:6 — "tempus non erit amplius" vs. "chronos ouketi estai"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *quia tempus non erit amplius* ("because time will be no more") and Greek *hoti chronos ouketi estai* ("that delay/time will be no more") present a famous interpretive question: does this mean "there will be no more time" (metaphysical) or "there will be no more delay" (practical)? The Hebrew *she-ein od shahut* ("that there is no more delay") uses *shahut* ("delay/tarrying"), clearly choosing the "no more delay" interpretation.
- **Hebrew**: *she-ein od shahut*
- **Note**: *Shahut* is from the root *sh-h-h* ("delay/linger"), attested in 2 Sam 15:28, Gen 19:16 (*va-yitmahemah*, related root). The Hebrew resolves the ambiguity toward "delay" rather than the metaphysical "time ceases." This follows the Greek *chronos* in its secondary sense and aligns with the narrative context (the mystery will be completed, v. 7).

### 10:7 — "mysterium" vs. "mysterion" vs. "sod"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *mysterium Dei* ("mystery of God"), Greek *to mysterion tou theou* ("the mystery of God"), Hebrew *sod ha-Elohim* ("the counsel/secret of God"). *Sod* is a theologically rich word — it means both "secret/mystery" and "intimate council" (cf. Jer 23:18, *mi amad be-sod YHVH*, "who has stood in the council of the LORD"; Amos 3:7, *ki lo ya'aseh Adonai YHVH davar ki im galah sodo el avadav ha-nevi'im*).
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Sod* captures both the Greek *mysterion* sense and the OT prophetic council imagery. Amos 3:7 is a near-perfect parallel: God reveals His *sod* to His servants the prophets — exactly what Rev 10:7 states.

### 10:7 — "evangelizavit" vs. "euengelisen" vs. "bisser"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *evangelizavit* ("proclaimed good news"), Greek *euengelisen* ("announced good news"), Hebrew *bisser* ("brought good news"). *Bisser* is from the root *b-s-r* which appears in Isa 40:9 (*mevaseret Tziyon*, "O Zion, bearer of good tidings"), 52:7 (*mah na'vu al he-harim raglei mevasser*, "how beautiful on the mountains are the feet of the one who brings good news"), and 61:1 (*levasser anavim*, "to bring good news to the poor").
- **Note**: Excellent. The root *b-s-r* is the Hebrew equivalent of *euangelizein* and connects the Revelation prophetic announcement to the Isaianic herald tradition.

### 10:9-10 — Eating the scroll — Ezekiel allusion
- **Issue**: All three traditions describe the same sequence: take the scroll, eat it, sweet in the mouth, bitter in the belly. This is drawn directly from Ezek 2:8-3:3, where Ezekiel eats a scroll that is sweet as honey. The Hebrew vocabulary closely follows Ezekiel: *qach ve-ekhol oto* (cf. Ezek 3:1, *ekhol et ha-megillah hazot*), *matok kidvash* (cf. Ezek 3:3, *va-tehi be-fi kidvash le-matoq*).
- **Note**: The bitterness is Revelation's addition — Ezekiel's scroll was only sweet. The Hebrew *meroriim* ("bitternesses") echoes Ex 12:8 (*al merorim yokhluhu*, "with bitter herbs they shall eat it") and Lam 3:15 (*hisbi'ani va-merorim*, "he has filled me with bitterness").

### 10:11 — "gentibus, et populis, et linguis, et regibus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *gentibus, et populis, et linguis, et regibus multis* ("nations, peoples, tongues, and many kings"). The Greek has *laois kai ethnesin kai glossais kai basileusin pollois* ("peoples, nations, tongues, and many kings") — with a different order (peoples before nations). The Hebrew has *al ammim vegoyim uleshonot umelakhim rabbim* ("concerning peoples, nations, tongues, and many kings"), following the Greek order rather than the Vulgate.
- **Note**: Minor ordering difference. The Hebrew follows the Greek rather than Vulgate word order.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 10:1 — ammudei esh
- **Hebrew**: *ve-raglav ke-ammudei esh* ("and his feet like pillars of fire")
- **Note**: *Ammudei esh* ("pillars of fire") directly echoes Ex 13:21-22 (*ammud esh*, "pillar of fire") from the Exodus wilderness wandering. The angel's legs resemble the theophanic pillar that guided Israel. This creates a visual connection between the mighty angel and the divine presence at the Exodus.

### 10:3 — aryeh
- **Hebrew**: *ka-asher yish'ag ha-aryeh* ("as when a lion roars")
- **Note**: The lion's roar echoes Amos 3:8 (*aryeh sha'ag mi lo yira*, "a lion has roared, who will not fear?") and Hos 11:10 (*ke-aryeh yish'ag*, "he will roar like a lion"). In the prophetic tradition, the lion's roar is God's voice (Amos 1:2, Joel 4:16). Connecting the mighty angel's voice to this tradition hints at a divine or quasi-divine identity.

### 10:5-6 — Oath by the Creator
- **Hebrew**: *va-yishava be-chei olmei ha-olamim asher bara et ha-shamayim... veha-aretz... veha-yam*
- **Note**: The oath formula echoes Dan 12:7 (*va-yishava be-chei ha-olam*, "he swore by the One who lives forever"). The content of the oath — swearing by the Creator of heaven, earth, and sea — echoes Neh 9:6 and the creation account. The triple creation domain (heaven, earth, sea) is a totality formula also found in Ex 20:11.

### 10:9 — qach ve-ekhol
- **Hebrew**: *qach ve-ekhol oto* ("take and eat it")
- **Note**: The imperative *qach ve-ekhol* echoes both Ezek 3:1 (*ekhol et ha-megillah*, "eat this scroll") and the Passover meal commands. The bitterness (*meroriim*) that follows reinforces the Passover connection (Ex 12:8, *merorim*). The sweet-to-bitter sequence also inverts Prov 5:3-4 (*nofet tittofna siftei zarah... ve-acharitah marah*, "honey drips from the lips of the forbidden woman... but her end is bitter").

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 10 is dominated by Ezekiel and Daniel:
- **Ezek 2:8-3:3**: Eating the scroll — vv. 8-10
- **Dan 12:7**: Oath by the eternal — v. 6
- **Ex 13:21-22**: Pillar of fire — v. 1
- **Amos 3:7-8**: Lion's roar, God's counsel to prophets — vv. 3, 7
- **Ex 20:11**: Creator of heaven, earth, sea — v. 6
- **Isa 40:9, 52:7**: Good tidings — v. 7

### Theological Note — 10:7
The statement that the *sod* (mystery/counsel) of God will be completed "as he announced to his servants the prophets" establishes continuity between OT prophecy and the Revelation. The Hebrew vocabulary (*sod*, *bisser*, *avadav ha-nevi'im*) is entirely drawn from the OT prophetic tradition, presenting the Apocalypse as the culmination of Hebrew prophecy rather than a novel genre.
