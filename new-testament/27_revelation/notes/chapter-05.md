# Revelation Chapter 5 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 14 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 5:1 — "librum" vs. "biblion" vs. "sefer"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *librum scriptum intus et foris* ("a book written inside and outside"). The Hebrew has *sefer katuv panim ve-achor* ("a scroll written front and back"). All three are well aligned. The Hebrew *sefer* echoes Ezek 2:9-10, where a scroll is also written *panim ve-achor* ("front and back").
- **Note**: Outstanding. The Hebrew replicates Ezekiel's scroll language exactly.

### 5:4 — "nec videre eum" vs. "oute blepein auto"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *nec videre eum* ("nor to look at it"), and the Greek similarly *oute blepein auto*. The Hebrew adds *veliqro vo* ("nor to read in it") which is not present in either the Vulgate or SBLGNT. This appears to be a Delitzsch addition, perhaps influenced by the scroll context.
- **Vulgate**: *nec videre eum*
- **Hebrew**: *veliqro vo velo lehabbit bo*
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "nor to read in it" beyond both source texts. This should be flagged as an expansion.

### 5:6 — "Agnum" vs. "arnion" vs. "seh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses *Agnum* (Lamb), the Greek *arnion* (little lamb — a diminutive), and the Hebrew *seh* ("lamb/sheep"). The Hebrew *seh* is from Gen 22:7-8 (the Aqedah: *ayeh ha-seh le-olah*, "where is the lamb for the burnt offering?") and Ex 12:3-5 (Passover lamb). *Seh* is a broader term than *keves* (male lamb) or *taleh* (young lamb), but it captures the sacrificial connotation well.
- **Note**: *Seh* is used consistently throughout Revelation for the Lamb. This connects the Christological Lamb to both the Aqedah (Gen 22) and the Passover (Ex 12). Excellent.

### 5:8 — "aperuisset librum" vs. "elaben to biblion"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *cum aperuisset librum* ("when he had opened the book"), but the Greek reads *hote elaben to biblion* ("when he took the book"). The Hebrew has *be-qachto et ha-sefer* ("when he took the scroll"), siding with the Greek. The Vulgate implies the Lamb already opened the scroll; the Greek/Hebrew say he merely took it (the opening begins in chapter 6).
- **Vulgate**: *cum aperuisset librum*
- **Greek**: *hote elaben to biblion*
- **Hebrew**: *be-qachto et ha-sefer*
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek against the Vulgate. This is a meaningful narrative difference — the Vulgate anticipates the opening, while the Greek/Hebrew maintain the dramatic sequence.

### 5:9 — "redemisti nos" vs. "egorasas to theo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *redemisti nos Deo* ("you redeemed us for God"). The SBLGNT reads *egorasas to theo* ("you purchased for God") — without *hemas* ("us"). The critical text omits "us," making the object those "from every tribe." The Hebrew has *qanita le-Elohim* ("you purchased for God"), following the SBLGNT in omitting the first-person pronoun. However, the Vulgate explicitly includes *nos*.
- **Vulgate**: *redemisti nos Deo in sanguine tuo*
- **Greek**: *egorasas to theo en to haimati sou*
- **Hebrew**: *uv'damkha qanita le-Elohim mi-kol-mishpachah*
- **Note**: For Vulgate conformity, the Hebrew should include *otanu* ("us"). This is a significant textual variant — the Vulgate reading implies the elders themselves are among the redeemed, while the critical text leaves the object implicit.

### 5:10 — "regnabimus" vs. "basileuousin"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *regnabimus* ("we shall reign") in first person plural future. The SBLGNT reads *basileuousin* ("they reign") in third person present. The Hebrew has *yimlekhu* ("they shall reign"), following the Greek's third person but using future tense.
- **Vulgate**: *et regnabimus super terram* (first person)
- **Greek**: *kai basileuousin epi tes ges* (third person)
- **Hebrew**: *veyimlekhu al ha-aretz* (third person)
- **Note**: The Hebrew sides with the Greek against the Vulgate on person. This connects to the variant in 5:9 — the Vulgate has the elders singing about themselves ("redeemed us... we shall reign"), while the Greek/Hebrew have them singing about others.

### 5:12 — "divinitatem" vs. "plouton" vs. "osher"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *divinitatem* ("divinity/divine nature"), which is a striking and theologically loaded word. The Greek has *plouton* ("wealth/riches"), and the Hebrew has *osher* ("wealth"). The Vulgate *divinitatem* is a significant departure from the Greek, as it attributes a divine quality rather than merely wealth.
- **Vulgate**: *virtutem, et divinitatem, et sapientiam*
- **Greek**: *dynamin kai plouton kai sophian*
- **Hebrew**: *oz ve-osher ve-chokhmah*
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek *plouton* ("wealth") rather than the Vulgate's remarkable *divinitatem*. This is one of the most theologically significant Vulgate readings in Revelation — Jerome's (or the Clementine tradition's) decision to render *plouton* as *divinitatem* makes an explicit Christological claim about the Lamb's divine nature. The Hebrew does not capture this.

### 5:13 — Vulgate's "potestas" vs. Greek's "kratos"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *potestas* ("power/authority") while the Greek has *kratos* ("dominion/might"). The Hebrew has *oz* ("strength"). All three are in the same semantic range but with slightly different connotations. *Kratos* and *oz* emphasize strength; *potestas* emphasizes authority.
- **Note**: Minor. Within acceptable range.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 5:5 — aryeh mi-shevet Yehudah
- **Hebrew**: *ha-aryeh asher hu mi-shevet Yehudah shoresh David*
- **Note**: Outstanding. "Lion of the tribe of Judah" directly echoes Gen 49:9 (*gur aryeh Yehudah*, "Judah is a lion's cub"). "Root of David" echoes Isa 11:1, 10 (*shoresh Yishai*, "root of Jesse"). The Hebrew creates a dense intertextual web connecting Christ to both the patriarchal blessing and the Isaianic Messiah.

### 5:6 — ke-tabuach for "tamquam occisum"
- **Hebrew**: *ke-tabuach* ("as slaughtered")
- **Note**: Excellent. *Tabuach* is from the root *t-b-ch* ("slaughter"), used for ritual/sacrificial killing (Gen 43:16, 1 Sam 25:11, Prov 9:2). This is stronger than *shachat* (ritual slaughter at the altar) and evokes Isa 53:7 (*ka-seh la-tevach yuval*, "like a lamb led to slaughter"). The sacrificial lamb imagery is powerfully conveyed.

### 5:8 — kinnorot for "citharas"
- **Hebrew**: *ish kinnoro be-yado*
- **Note**: *Kinnor* ("lyre/harp") is David's instrument (1 Sam 16:23, Ps 33:2, 43:4). The connection to Davidic worship is theologically appropriate for a scene honoring the "Root of David." *Qa'arot* ("bowls") for the golden *phialas* echoes the temple vessels (Num 7:13ff.).

### 5:9 — shir chadash
- **Hebrew**: *va-yashiru shir chadash*
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Shir chadash* ("new song") is a direct quotation of Ps 33:3, 40:4, 96:1, 98:1, 144:9, 149:1. This is one of the most recognizable psalm phrases in the Hebrew Bible. The verb *va-yashiru* (narrative form "they sang") echoes the Song of the Sea, Ex 15:1 (*az yashir Mosheh*).

### 5:11 — ribbo revavot ve-alfei alafim
- **Hebrew**: *ribbo revavot ve-alfei alafim* ("myriads of myriads and thousands of thousands")
- **Note**: Directly echoes Dan 7:10 (*elef alfin yeshamshunneih veribbo ribb'van qadamohi yeqomun*, "thousands upon thousands served him, and myriads upon myriads stood before him"). The Danielic throne-room language is precisely replicated.

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 5 draws heavily on:
- **Gen 22:7-8**: The Aqedah lamb (*seh*)
- **Gen 49:9**: Lion of Judah
- **Ex 12:3-5**: Passover lamb
- **Ex 15:1**: Song of Moses form
- **Isa 11:1, 10**: Root of Jesse/David
- **Isa 53:7**: Lamb led to slaughter
- **Dan 7:10**: Myriads serving the throne
- **Ps 33:3, 96:1, 98:1**: New song
- **Ezek 2:9-10**: Scroll written front and back

### Theological Note — 5:12
The Vulgate's *divinitatem* for Greek *plouton* is a translation of major theological significance. Where the Greek lists seven attributes of the Lamb (power, wealth, wisdom, strength, honor, glory, blessing), the Vulgate substitutes "divinity" for "wealth," making an explicit ontological claim about the Lamb. The Hebrew follows the Greek reading. For a project following the Vulgate as authoritative, this verse merits special attention.
