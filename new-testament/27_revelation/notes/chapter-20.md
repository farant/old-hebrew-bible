# Revelation Chapter 20 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 20:7-9 — Vulgate verse division discrepancy
- **Issue**: The Vulgate combines material from the Greek/Hebrew vv. 7-8 into a single long verse 7, and what is Greek/Hebrew v. 9a appears partially in Vulgate v. 8. Specifically:
  - The Vulgate v. 7 includes the content of SBLGNT vv. 7-8 (Satan released, goes out to deceive Gog and Magog, gathers them for battle, their number like the sand of the sea).
  - The Vulgate v. 8 begins with *Et ascenderunt super latitudinem terrae* ("And they went up over the breadth of the earth"), which corresponds to SBLGNT v. 9a.
  - The Vulgate v. 9 begins with *Et descendit ignis a Deo de caelo*, which corresponds to SBLGNT v. 9b, and then continues into material from SBLGNT v. 10.
- The Hebrew follows the Greek versification (v. 7 = Satan released; v. 8 = Gog and Magog gathered; v. 9 = they surround the camp and fire falls). The content is the same across all three traditions but distributed differently.
- **Note**: The Vulgate's verse boundaries here are shifted forward by approximately half a verse relative to the Greek/Hebrew. This should be noted when cross-referencing.

All other verses (1-6, 10-15) align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 20:2 — "ha-malshin" for "diabolus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *diabolus* ("the devil/accuser"), Greek *Diabolos* ("the slanderer/accuser"). The Hebrew has *ha-malshin* ("the informer/slanderer"), from the root *l-sh-n* ("tongue/slander"). This is a post-biblical Hebrew term (*malshin* is rabbinic, Mishnaic Hebrew) rather than biblical Hebrew.
- **Vulgate**: *qui est diabolus et Satanas*
- **Hebrew**: *hu ha-malshin ve-hu ha-Satan*
- **Note**: A more biblical rendering of *diabolos* would be *ha-satan* alone (as in Job 1-2, Zech 3:1) or perhaps *ha-soten* ("the adversary"). *Malshin* accurately captures the Greek *diabolos* ("slanderer") but is not attested in the Hebrew Bible. Delitzsch uses a functionally accurate but linguistically late term.

### 20:2 — "ha-nachash ha-qadmoni" for "serpentem antiquum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *serpentem antiquum* ("the ancient serpent"), Greek *ho ophis ho archaios* ("the ancient serpent"). The Hebrew *ha-nachash ha-qadmoni* ("the primordial/ancient serpent") echoes Gen 3:1 (*ve-ha-nachash hayah arum*, "and the serpent was crafty"). The adjective *qadmoni* ("ancient/eastern/primordial") is found in 1 Sam 24:14, Ezek 47:18, Joel 2:20.
- **Note**: Good. The identification of the dragon with the Genesis serpent is made explicit. *Qadmoni* carries overtones of "from the beginning" (*qedem*), linking to the primordial fall. Cf. Isa 51:9 (*u-khe-yemei qedem*, "as in the days of old") in the context of slaying the sea monster.

### 20:2 — "tannin" for "draconem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *draconem* ("the dragon"), Greek *ton drakonta*. The Hebrew *ha-tannin* ("the sea-monster/dragon") is the standard OT term for the primordial chaos monster: Isa 27:1 (*tannin asher ba-yam*, "the dragon that is in the sea"), Isa 51:9 (*ha-lo at hi ha-machtzevet Rahav mecholelet Tannin*, "was it not you who cut Rahab to pieces, who pierced the dragon?"), Ezek 29:3, 32:2, Ps 74:13.
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Tannin* connects the Revelation dragon to the entire OT mythopoetic tradition of God's victory over the chaos monster. This is precisely the right biblical Hebrew term.

### 20:3 — "tehom" for "abyssum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *abyssum* ("the abyss"), Greek *ten abysson*. The Hebrew *tehom* ("the deep/abyss") is the primordial deep of Gen 1:2 (*ve-choshekh al penei tehom*, "and darkness was upon the face of the deep").
- **Note**: Outstanding. *Tehom* connects the abyss where Satan is imprisoned to the primordial chaos-waters of creation. The sealing of the abyss reprises God's ordering of chaos at creation.

### 20:3 — "lizman mitz'ar" for "modico tempore"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *modico tempore* ("a short time"), Greek *mikron chronon*. The Hebrew *lizman mitz'ar* ("for a brief time") uses *mitz'ar* ("small/insignificant"), a rare biblical term (Isa 63:18, Dan 11:34).
- **Note**: *Zeman* ("time/season") is Aramaic-influenced (Dan 2:16, 21, 7:12, Ezra 5:3) rather than purely biblical Hebrew *et* or *mo'ed*. Acceptable, given Daniel's influence on Revelation's eschatology.

### 20:4 — "ha-harugim" for "decollatorum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *animas decollatorum* ("the souls of those beheaded"), Greek *tas psychas ton pepelekismenon* ("the souls of those who had been beheaded with an axe"). The Hebrew *nafshot ha-harugim* ("the souls of the slain") uses the general term *harugim* ("killed/slain") rather than a specific beheading term.
- **Note**: The Hebrew generalizes. *Harugim* (from *h-r-g*, "slay") does not specify the method of death. A more literal rendering might use a term like *qetulim* or a phrase for beheading, but *harugim* is the standard OT martyrdom vocabulary (Lam 4:9, Isa 14:19) and is acceptable.

### 20:5 — "ha-techiyyah ha-rishonah" for "resurrectio prima"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Haec est resurrectio prima* ("This is the first resurrection"), Greek *haute he anastasis he prote*. The Hebrew *zot ha-techiyyah ha-rishonah* ("this is the first resurrection") uses *techiyyah* ("revival/resurrection"), from the root *ch-y-h* ("live").
- **Note**: *Techiyyah* is not a biblical Hebrew word — it enters Hebrew in the rabbinic period (*techiyyat ha-metim*, "resurrection of the dead," a key rabbinic doctrine). The biblical concept is expressed periphrastically: *va-yichyu* ("and they lived," Ezek 37:10), *ya'iru* ("they shall awake," Dan 12:2). However, *techiyyah* has become the standard Hebrew term and is functional here.

### 20:6 — "kohanim le-Elohim ve-li-meshicho"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *sacerdotes Dei et Christi* ("priests of God and of Christ"), Greek *hiereis tou theou kai tou christou* ("priests of God and of Christ"). The Hebrew *kohanim le-Elohim ve-li-meshicho* ("priests to God and to His Messiah/Christ").
- **Note**: *Kohanim le-Elohim* echoes Ex 19:6 (*mamlekhet kohanim ve-goy qadosh*, "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation"). The combination of God and His Messiah echoes Ps 2:2 (*al YHVH ve-al meshicho*, "against the LORD and against His Anointed"). The saints are installed in the priestly office that Israel was called to at Sinai.

### 20:7-8 — Gog and Magog
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree: Gog and Magog are gathered for battle, their number like the sand of the sea. The Hebrew *Gog u-Magog* and *ke-chol ha-yam* ("like the sand of the sea") directly reference Ezek 38-39 (the Gog/Magog war) and the Abrahamic promise (Gen 22:17, 32:13: *ke-chol asher al sefat ha-yam*, "like the sand on the seashore").
- **Note**: The Hebrew makes both allusions transparent. The irony is sharp: the number promised to Abraham's descendants (*ke-chol ha-yam*) is now attributed to the enemies of God's people.

### 20:8 — "arba kanfot ha-aretz" for "quatuor angulos terrae"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *quatuor angulos terrae* ("the four corners of the earth"), Greek *tais tessarsi goniais tes ges*. The Hebrew *arba kanfot ha-aretz* ("the four corners/wings of the earth") uses the standard OT phrase (Isa 11:12: *me-arba kanfot ha-aretz*, "from the four corners of the earth"; Job 37:3, 38:13).
- **Note**: Verbatim OT language. Good.

### 20:9 — "machaneh ha-qedoshim" and "ha-ir ha-chavivah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *castra sanctorum et civitatem dilectam* ("the camp of the saints and the beloved city"), Greek *ten parembolen ton hagion kai ten polin ten egapemenen*. The Hebrew *machaneh ha-qedoshim ve-et ha-ir ha-chavivah* ("the camp of the saints and the beloved city").
- **Note**: *Machaneh* ("camp") is the standard OT term for Israel's encampment in the wilderness (Ex 14:19-20, Num 2, Deut 23:15). *Ha-ir ha-chavivah* ("the beloved city") — *chavivah* is a rare adjective; the standard biblical term would be *ha-ir ha-ahuvah* or *ha-ir ha-nechevedet*. *Chavivah* is more Mishnaic. However, the concept of Jerusalem as "beloved" is biblical (Ps 87:2, *ohev YHVH sha'arei Tziyon*, "the LORD loves the gates of Zion").

### 20:9 — "me-et ha-Elohim" — bracketed addition
- **Issue**: The Hebrew has *me-et ha-Elohim* ("from God") in brackets, indicating it follows the TR/Vulgate reading (*a Deo*: "fire from God from heaven") which the SBLGNT omits. The Vulgate reads *ignis a Deo de caelo* ("fire from God from heaven").
- **Note**: The brackets indicate Delitzsch's awareness that this phrase is textually disputed. Including it follows the Vulgate. The phrase echoes Gen 19:24 (*YHVH himtir... me-et YHVH min ha-shamayim*, "the LORD rained... from the LORD out of heaven") and 2 Kgs 1:10, 12 (fire from God consuming Elijah's adversaries).

### 20:10 — "yusseru" for "cruciabuntur"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *cruciabuntur* ("they will be tormented"), Greek *basanisthesontai* ("they will be tormented"). The Hebrew *yusseru* ("they will be chastised/disciplined"), from the root *y-s-r* ("discipline/chastise").
- **Note**: *Yussar* carries a corrective connotation ("discipline") that is somewhat softer than the unmitigated "torment" of the Vulgate/Greek. However, *yissurim* ("afflictions/sufferings") in biblical Hebrew can denote severe suffering (Ps 94:12, Prov 3:11, Jer 30:14). The root *y-s-r* is used for divine punishment in Lev 26:18, 28.

### 20:11 — Great White Throne
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *thronum magnum candidum* ("a great white throne"), Greek *thronon megan leukon* ("a great white throne"). The Hebrew *kisse lavan ve-gadol* ("a white and great throne") reverses the adjective order relative to the Vulgate/Greek.
- **Note**: Minor stylistic difference. *Kisse* ("throne") is the standard OT term (1 Kgs 10:18, Isa 6:1, Ps 9:5, 8, Dan 7:9). The white throne connects to Dan 7:9 (*korseyeh shevivei di-nur*, "his throne was fiery flames" — though Daniel's throne is fiery, not white). The whiteness may suggest purity of judgment.

### 20:12 — "sefer ha-chayyim" for "liber vitae"
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree. The Hebrew *sefer ha-chayyim* ("the book of life") echoes Ps 69:29 (*yimmahu mi-sefer chayyim*, "let them be blotted out of the book of the living"), Ex 32:32-33 (*mecheini na mi-sifreka*, "blot me out of your book"), and Dan 12:1 (*kol ha-nimtza katuv ba-sefer*, "everyone found written in the book").
- **Note**: The *sefer ha-chayyim* concept runs through the entire OT — from Moses interceding at Sinai to Daniel's eschatological vision. The Hebrew translation seamlessly connects to this tradition.

### 20:13 — "ha-sheol" for "infernus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *infernus* ("hell/the underworld"), Greek *ho hades* ("Hades"). The Hebrew *ha-sheol* is the standard OT underworld (Gen 37:35, Num 16:30, 33, Deut 32:22, Isa 14:9, 15, Ps 6:6, 16:10, 88:4).
- **Note**: *Sheol* is precisely the right biblical Hebrew equivalent. It carries the full weight of OT underworld theology — the shadowy realm of the dead awaiting judgment.

### 20:14 — "ha-mavet ha-sheni" for "mors secunda"
- **Issue**: All three traditions agree: "the second death." The Hebrew *ha-mavet ha-sheni* is clear and literal.
- **Note**: The concept of the "second death" is not explicitly OT but appears in the Targums (Tg. Deut 33:6, Tg. Isa 22:14, 65:6, 15). Delitzsch's rendering is straightforward.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 20:1 — mafteach ha-tehom
- **Hebrew**: *u-ve-yado mafteach ha-tehom ve-khevel gadol*
- **Note**: *Mafteach* ("key") is post-biblical (Mishnaic Hebrew). The biblical word is *mafteach* in Judg 3:25, so it does have one OT attestation. *Tehom* is excellent (see Content Alignment above). *Khevel* ("chain/rope") is good OT vocabulary (Josh 2:15, 2 Sam 17:13, Ps 140:6).

### 20:2 — va-ya'asrehu
- **Hebrew**: *va-ya'asrehu elef shanim*
- **Note**: The root *'-s-r* ("bind") is standard biblical Hebrew (Gen 42:24, Judg 15:10, 13, 16:11-12). The binding of the dragon echoes the binding of Samson (Judg 16:11-12), but here it is the divine warrior who binds the chaos monster. Also echoes Isa 24:22 (*ve-ussefu asefah assir al bor*, "they will be gathered as prisoners in a pit") — the imprisonment of cosmic powers.

### 20:3 — va-yachtom alav
- **Hebrew**: *va-yisgor alav va-yachtom alav*
- **Note**: The double action of *sagar* ("shut") and *chatam* ("seal") echoes Dan 6:18 (*ve-sum chatam*, "and a stone was placed... and the king sealed it") — the sealing of the lions' den. Also Isa 29:11 (*ke-divrei ha-sefer ha-chatum*, "like the words of a sealed book"). The sealing motif indicates divine authority that cannot be broken.

### 20:8 — ke-chol ha-yam
- **Hebrew**: *asher misparum ke-chol ha-yam*
- **Note**: Verbatim from Gen 22:17, 32:13, Josh 11:4, Judg 7:12, 1 Sam 13:5. The phrase is the standard biblical expression for innumerable multitude. Its use for the enemies of God forms a dark parody of the Abrahamic promise.

### 20:9 — va-tokhel otam
- **Hebrew**: *va-tered esh... va-tokhel otam*
- **Note**: *Esh... va-tokhel* ("fire... and it consumed them") is verbatim from Lev 10:2 (*va-tetze esh mi-lifnei YHVH va-tokhel otam*, "and fire went out from before the LORD and consumed them" — the death of Nadab and Abihu), Num 16:35 (Korah), and 2 Kgs 1:10, 12 (Elijah). The formula is the standard expression for divine fiery judgment.

### 20:12 — va-yishaftu ha-metim
- **Hebrew**: *va-yishaftu ha-metim min ha-katuv ba-sefarim ke-ma'aseihem*
- **Note**: *Ke-ma'aseihem* ("according to their deeds") echoes Ps 28:4 (*ten lahem ke-fo'olam u-khe-roa ma'alleihem*, "give them according to their work and according to the evil of their deeds"), Jer 25:14, and throughout the prophets. The principle of judgment according to works is deeply embedded in OT theology.

## Other Notes

### Red-Text Candidates
No direct divine speech occurs in chapter 20. The chapter is entirely John's narration of the vision. There are no red-text candidates.

### Theological Note — The Millennium (20:1-6)
The *elef shanim* ("thousand years") is the defining passage for eschatological interpretation. Catholic tradition (following Augustine) generally interprets this as the present age of the Church between the first and second comings of Christ — the amillennial reading. The binding of Satan represents the limiting of his power through Christ's victory on the Cross, and the "first resurrection" is baptism/spiritual rebirth. The Hebrew *va-yichyu va-yimlekhu im ha-Mashiach elef shanim* ("and they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years") echoes Daniel's kingdom language (Dan 7:18, 22, 27: *ve-yiqabbelu malkuta qaddishei elyonin*, "the saints of the Most High shall receive the kingdom").

### Theological Note — 20:11-15 General Judgment
The Great White Throne judgment is the Catholic doctrine of the Last Judgment. The opening of *sefarim* ("books," plural) alongside *sefer ha-chayyim* ("the book of life," singular) suggests two criteria: deeds (*ke-ma'aseihem*, "according to their works," vv. 12-13) and election (inscription in the book of life, v. 15). Catholic theology affirms both faith and works as necessary for salvation, and this passage supports that dual criterion.
