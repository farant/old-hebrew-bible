# Revelation Chapter 6 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 17 verses align across all three sources (Vulgate, SBLGNT, Delitzsch).

## Content Alignment

### 6:1 — "Veni, et vide" vs. "Erchou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads *Veni, et vide* ("Come, and see") with two imperatives. The SBLGNT has only *Erchou* ("Come") — the critical text omits *kai ide* ("and see"). The Hebrew has *bo ur'eh* ("come and see"), following the Vulgate/TR fuller reading.
- **Vulgate**: *Veni, et vide*
- **Greek**: *Erchou*
- **Hebrew**: *bo ur'eh*
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate here. Good. The *bo ur'eh* formula appears throughout the seal openings (6:1, 3, 5, 7), matching the Vulgate's consistent *Veni et vide*.

### 6:2 — White horse rider's identity
- **Issue**: No textual divergence. All three traditions describe the white horse rider with bow and crown going forth conquering. The Hebrew *menatzeach ulema'an yenatzeach* ("conquering and in order to conquer") renders the Vulgate *vincens ut vinceret* well.
- **Note**: Alignment is good across all three.

### 6:4 — "gladius magnus" vs. "machaira megale"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *gladius magnus* ("great sword"). The Greek has *machaira megale* ("great sword/dagger"). The Hebrew has *cherev gedolah* ("great sword"). All align. *Cherev* is the standard OT word for sword (Gen 3:24, Ex 5:21, Deut 32:41).
- **Note**: Good alignment. *Cherev* is well-chosen.

### 6:6 — Measures and currency
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *bilibris tritici denario* ("two pounds of wheat for a denarius"). The Greek *choinix sitou denariou* ("a choenix of wheat for a denarius"). The Hebrew has *qav chittim be-dinar* ("a qav of wheat for a dinar"), using the Hebrew dry measure *qav* (2 Kgs 6:25) for the Greek *choinix*. The *dinar* is a transliteration.
- **Vulgate**: *Bilibris tritici denario et tres bilibres hordei denario*
- **Hebrew**: *qav chittim be-dinar ushloshoh qabbim se'orim be-dinar*
- **Note**: The Vulgate *bilibris* (about 2 Roman pounds) and Greek *choinix* (about 1 liter) are different measures. The Hebrew *qav* (from 2 Kgs 6:25) is approximately 1.2 liters, close to the *choinix*. The OT allusion to the Samaria famine (2 Kgs 6:25, *qav chariyyonim*) is apt.

### 6:8 — "equus pallidus" vs. "hippos chloros" vs. "sus yeraqraq"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *pallidus* ("pale"), the Greek *chloros* ("pale green/yellowish-green"), and the Hebrew *yeraqraq* ("greenish/pale"). *Yeraqraq* appears in Lev 13:49 and 14:37 describing a greenish tinge in leprous marks. This is the more precise translation of the Greek *chloros* than the Vulgate's *pallidus*.
- **Hebrew**: *sus yeraqraq*
- **Note**: *Yeraqraq* (an intensive reduplication of *yaroq*, "green") captures the *chloros* color better than *pallidus*. This is the color of decay and death in the Levitical purity context.

### 6:8 — "morte" vs. "thanato"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate lists four means of death: *gladio, fame, et morte, et bestiis* ("sword, famine, death, and beasts"). The *et morte* ("and death") is unusual since "death" is already the rider's name. The Greek has *rhomphaia kai limo kai thanato kai hypo ton therion* ("sword, famine, pestilence, and by beasts"). The Hebrew has *ba-cherev uva-ra'av uva-dever uv'chayyat ha-aretz* ("by sword, famine, pestilence, and beasts of the earth").
- **Vulgate**: *morte* (death)
- **Greek**: *thanato* (death/pestilence)
- **Hebrew**: *dever* (pestilence)
- **Note**: The Hebrew *dever* ("pestilence/plague") is an interpretive translation. In OT usage, *dever* is the standard companion of *cherev* (sword) and *ra'av* (famine) in the prophetic triad of judgment (Jer 14:12, 21:7, 24:10, Ezek 5:12, 14:21). The Hebrew correctly identifies this as the OT pestilence motif. Ezek 14:21 is the closest parallel: *cherev vera'av vechayyah ra'ah vedever* — four judgments matching Revelation's four exactly.

### 6:10 — "Usquequo Domine"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate *Usquequo Domine* and Greek *Heos pote ho despotes* agree. The Hebrew *ad matai... Adonai ha-qadosh veha-amitti* captures this well. *Ad matai* ("how long?") is a classic lament formula (Ps 6:4, 13:2-3, 74:10, 79:5, 89:47).
- **Note**: Good alignment. The lament formula is perfectly biblical.

### 6:11 — "stolae albae" vs. "simlah levanah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *stolae albae* ("white robes") and the Greek *stole leuke*. The Hebrew has *simlah levanah* ("white garment"). *Simlah* is a general outer garment (Gen 9:23, Ex 22:8, Deut 22:17). A more precise rendering of the formal *stola* might be *beged* or *me'il*, but *simlah* is acceptable biblical Hebrew.
- **Note**: Minor. The vocabulary is within range.

### 6:17 — "dies magnus irae ipsorum" — pronoun number
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has *irae ipsorum* ("their wrath") in the plural, referring to both God and the Lamb. The SBLGNT has *orges autou* ("his wrath") in the singular (referring to the Lamb only, or to God). The Hebrew has *yom appo ha-gadol* ("the great day of his wrath") in the singular, following the SBLGNT against the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *dies magnus irae ipsorum* (their, plural)
- **Greek**: *he hemera he megale tes orges auton* (their — but variant: *autou*, his)
- **Hebrew**: *yom appo ha-gadol* (his, singular)
- **Note**: The Vulgate *ipsorum* (plural) is theologically significant: it ascribes wrath jointly to the Father and the Lamb. The Hebrew singular follows the Greek variant *autou*. For Vulgate conformity, *yom appam* (their wrath) would be needed.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:8 — Ezekiel's four judgments
- **Hebrew**: *ba-cherev uva-ra'av uva-dever uv'chayyat ha-aretz*
- **Note**: This is virtually a quotation of Ezek 14:21 (*arba'at shefatai ha-ra'im cherev vera'av vechayyah ra'ah vedever*). The Delitzsch translation recognizes the OT source and uses the exact Ezekiel vocabulary. Outstanding.

### 6:12 — saq se'ar
- **Hebrew**: *va-yiqdar ha-shemesh ke-saq se'ar*
- **Note**: *Saq se'ar* ("sackcloth of hair") is attested in Isa 50:3 (*saq asim kesiutam*, "I make sackcloth their covering"). The darkened sun echoes Joel 2:31 (*ha-shemesh yehafekh le-choshekh*, "the sun shall be turned to darkness") and Isa 13:10. *Va-yiqdar* ("became dark/black") is from *qadar* (cf. Jer 4:28, Mic 3:6).

### 6:13 — te'enah imagery
- **Hebrew**: *ka-asher tinoa te'enah be-ruach chazaqah vehishlikhah paggeha*
- **Note**: The fig tree casting unripe figs (*paggim*) in a strong wind is drawn from the Hebrew of Song 2:13 (*ha-te'enah chantah faggeha*, "the fig tree puts forth her green figs"). *Paggim* (unripe/early figs) is precisely the right biblical term. Nahum 3:12 also uses the falling-fig imagery.

### 6:14 — sefer niglal
- **Hebrew**: *ha-shamayim mashu ke-sefer niglal*
- **Note**: *Sefer niglal* ("scroll being rolled up") echoes Isa 34:4 (*venagollu ka-sefer ha-shamayim*, "and the heavens shall be rolled up like a scroll"). The Hebrew recaptures the Isaiah source text. *Mashu* ("withdrew/departed") is less common; *nehefsekhu* or *nasugu* might be more expected.

### 6:15-16 — "fall on us" / Hosea allusion
- **Hebrew**: *niflu aleinu vekhassunu*
- **Note**: The cry to the mountains and rocks echoes Hos 10:8 (*imru la-harim kassunu vela-geva'ot niflu aleinu*, "they will say to the mountains, Cover us, and to the hills, Fall on us"). The Hebrew captures the Hosea allusion, though it reverses the order (Hosea: cover-fall; Revelation: fall-cover), following the Greek/Latin order.

## Other Notes

### OT Allusion Density
Chapter 6 draws from a wide range of OT judgment texts:
- **Ezek 14:21**: Four judgments (sword, famine, pestilence, beasts) — v. 8
- **Zech 1:8, 6:1-8**: Colored horses — vv. 1-8
- **Joel 2:31**: Sun darkened, moon to blood — v. 12
- **Isa 34:4**: Heavens rolled up like a scroll — v. 14
- **Hos 10:8**: Mountains fall on us — v. 16
- **Isa 2:10, 19, 21**: Hiding in caves from divine terror — v. 15
- **Mal 3:2**: Who can stand? — v. 17

### Theological Note — 6:16
The phrase *chamat ha-seh* ("the wrath of the Lamb") is theologically paradoxical — lambs are associated with meekness and sacrifice, not wrath. The Hebrew *chemah* ("burning anger") is among the strongest OT anger words (Deut 29:27, Jer 21:5, Ezek 5:13), making the juxtaposition with *seh* ("lamb") even more striking in Hebrew than in Greek or Latin.
