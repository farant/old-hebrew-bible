# Matthew Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 38 verses align across all three sources. The SBLGNT, Delitzsch, and Vulgate all contain verses 1-38 with consistent numbering.

## Content Divergences

### 9:1 — "Getting into" vs "Going down into" the boat
- **Issue**: The Greek uses ἐμβάς ("having embarked / gotten into") while the Hebrew uses וַיֵּרֶד ("and he went down"), adding a directional nuance not present in the Greek. The Vulgate has "ascendens" ("going up into"), which is the opposite direction from the Hebrew.
- **Greek**: ἐμβὰς εἰς πλοῖον
- **Latin**: ascendens in naviculam
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּרֶד בָּאֳנִיָּה
- **Note**: The Vulgate uses the diminutive "naviculam" (small boat) where Greek simply has πλοῖον (boat). The Hebrew וַיֵּרֶד ("went down") vs. Latin "ascendens" ("went up") is a notable directional disagreement across the versions, though both are standard idioms for boarding a vessel.

### 9:8 — "Feared" vs "Were astonished"
- **Issue**: The Greek ἐφοβήθησαν means "they feared/were afraid." The Vulgate matches with "timuerunt" (they feared). But Delitzsch renders this as וַיִּשְׁתּוֹמֲמוּ ("they were astonished/amazed"), which is a different emotional response.
- **Greek**: ἐφοβήθησαν
- **Latin**: timuerunt
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּשְׁתּוֹמֲמוּ
- **Note**: Delitzsch's choice softens the response from fear to astonishment. The SBLGNT itself has a textual variant here (the ⸀ marker indicates a variant reading), and some Greek manuscripts do read ἐθαύμασαν ("marveled") instead of ἐφοβήθησαν. Delitzsch appears to follow the variant rather than the main SBLGNT reading.

### 9:13 — Bracketed addition "to repentance"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT omits the phrase εἰς μετάνοιαν ("to repentance") after "sinners," which is a well-known textual addition found in later manuscripts (Byzantine text). The Vulgate also lacks it. However, Delitzsch includes לִתְשׁוּבָה ("to repentance") in brackets.
- **Greek**: οὐ γὰρ ἦλθον καλέσαι δικαίους ἀλλὰ ἁμαρτωλούς (no "to repentance")
- **Latin**: Non enim veni vocare justos, sed peccatores (no "to repentance")
- **Hebrew**: כִּי לֹא-בָאתִי לִקְרֹא אֶת-הַצַּדִּיקִים כִּי אִם-אֶת-הַחַטָּאִים [לִתְשׁוּבָה]
- **Note**: The brackets in the Hebrew indicate Delitzsch recognized this as textually uncertain. It is present in the Textus Receptus and Luke 5:32 parallel. Its presence here aligns with the Byzantine/TR tradition rather than the critical text.

### 9:14 — "Much" / "frequently"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has the variant marker on πολλά ("much/often"). The Vulgate reads "frequenter" (frequently). Delitzsch has הַרְבֵּה ("much/greatly"). The critical text has a variant between πολλά and [no word], while the Vulgate supports the longer reading.
- **Greek**: νηστεύομεν ⸀πολλά
- **Latin**: jejunamus frequenter
- **Hebrew**: צָמִים הַרְבֵּה
- **Note**: Both Hebrew and Latin include the adverb, agreeing with the longer reading. Some important Greek manuscripts omit πολλά.

### 9:18 — "Ruler" vs "One ruler"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "Domine" (Lord) in the ruler's address to Jesus, which is absent from both the Greek and Hebrew texts.
- **Greek**: Ἡ θυγάτηρ μου ἄρτι ἐτελεύτησεν
- **Latin**: Domine, filia mea modo defuncta est
- **Hebrew**: עַתָּה זֶה מֵתָה בִתִּי
- **Note**: The Vulgate's addition of "Domine" reflects the Latin tradition's tendency to add honorific address. Neither Greek nor Hebrew has a vocative here.

### 9:34 — Verse inclusion
- **Issue**: Matt 9:34 is absent from some important Greek manuscripts (notably Codex Bezae, D). The SBLGNT includes it, as do the Vulgate and Delitzsch. This verse is sometimes suspected of being harmonized from Matt 12:24.
- **Greek**: οἱ δὲ Φαρισαῖοι ἔλεγον· Ἐν τῷ ἄρχοντι τῶν δαιμονίων ἐκβάλλει τὰ δαιμόνια.
- **Latin**: Pharisæi autem dicebant : In principe dæmoniorum ejicit dæmones.
- **Hebrew**: וְהַפְּרוּשִׁים אָמְרוּ עַל-יְדֵי שַׂר הַשֵׁדִים מְגָרֵשׁ הוּא אֵת הַשֵׁדִים
- **Note**: All three sources include this verse, but its textual status is debated. The content nearly duplicates 12:24.

### 9:35 — "Every disease and every infirmity among the people"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has a variant marker on μαλακίαν and omits "among the people" (ἐν τῷ λαῷ), while the Vulgate also lacks "in the people." However, Delitzsch adds בָּעָם ("among the people").
- **Greek**: πᾶσαν νόσον καὶ πᾶσαν ⸀μαλακίαν (no "among the people" in main text)
- **Latin**: omnem languorem, et omnem infirmitatem (no "among the people")
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-מַחֲלָה וְכָל-מַדְוֶה בָּעָם
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Byzantine/TR reading by including "among the people," which the critical text and Vulgate lack.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:2 — Excellent biblical phrasing for "paralytic"
- **Hebrew**: נְכֵה אֵבָרִים (lit. "stricken of limbs")
- **Note**: Delitzsch avoids a simple loanword or neologism and instead uses a genuinely biblical-sounding construct. The phrase echoes OT idiom for physical affliction (cf. the pattern in 2 Sam 4:4 נְכֵה רַגְלַיִם). This is a strong choice.

### 9:2 — "Be of good cheer" rendered as חֲזַק
- **Hebrew**: חֲזַק בְּנִי ("be strong, my son")
- **Note**: The Greek θάρσει ("take courage / be of good cheer") is well rendered by חֲזַק, which echoes the biblical exhortation formula (cf. Josh 1:6, 1:9 חֲזַק וֶאֱמָץ). The Vulgate's "confide" (trust/have confidence) carries a slightly different nuance. Delitzsch's choice is excellent biblical Hebrew.

### 9:6 — שָׁלְטָן for "authority"
- **Hebrew**: הַשָׁלְטָן
- **Note**: This is an Aramaic loanword (cf. Dan 7:14 שָׁלְטָן) rather than the more purely biblical Hebrew מֶמְשָׁלָה or סָמְכוּת. However, given that שָׁלְטָן appears in biblical Aramaic sections of Daniel, and Matthew's original audience included Aramaic speakers, this is a defensible and arguably appropriate choice for a late biblical register.

### 9:10 — בַּהֲסִבּוֹ for "reclining at table"
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי בַּהֲסִבּוֹ בְּבֵיתוֹ
- **Note**: The hiphil infinitive construct of סבב in the sense of "reclining" is post-biblical/Mishnaic Hebrew usage (הֵסֵב for reclining at table). Biblical Hebrew would more naturally use יָשַׁב or a different construction. However, since the concept of formal reclining at meals is itself Greco-Roman rather than ancient Israelite, Delitzsch had limited biblical options.

### 9:15 — בְּנֵי הַחֻפָּה for "sons of the bridechamber"
- **Hebrew**: בְּנֵי הַחֻפָּה
- **Note**: This renders Greek υἱοὶ τοῦ νυμφῶνος literally as "sons of the bridal canopy." The term חֻפָּה is genuinely biblical (Joel 2:16, Ps 19:6), making this an excellent equivalence. The construct "sons of X" as an idiom for "those who belong to / participate in X" is thoroughly biblical (cf. בְּנֵי הַנְּבִיאִים).

### 9:20 — כְנַף בִּגְדוֹ for "fringe/hem of his garment"
- **Hebrew**: וַתִּגַּע בִּכְנַף בִּגְדוֹ
- **Note**: כָּנָף ("wing, corner, hem") is the standard biblical term for the corner/fringe of a garment (cf. Num 15:38, 1 Sam 24:5). This perfectly renders κράσπεδον and creates an echo with the tzitzit commandment. Strong biblical choice.

### 9:36 — נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו for "he had compassion"
- **Hebrew**: נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו עֲלֵיהֶם
- **Note**: This phrase ("his compassion was stirred") directly echoes Gen 43:30 (וַיְבַקֵּשׁ לִבְכּוֹת כִּי-נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמָיו) and 1 Kings 3:26. It is a deeply biblical phrase for ἐσπλαγχνίσθη. Excellent choice that a Hebrew reader would immediately recognize.

### 9:37-38 — Harvest imagery
- **Hebrew**: רַב הַקָּצִיר וְהַפֹּעֲלִים מְעַטִּים ... בַּעַל הַקָּצִיר
- **Note**: The harvest metaphor uses thoroughly biblical vocabulary. בַּעַל הַקָּצִיר ("lord/owner of the harvest") for κύριος τοῦ θερισμοῦ is natural and echoes OT construct-state patterns. פֹּעֲלִים for "workers/laborers" is also well attested in biblical Hebrew.

## Other Notes

### 9:27 — Christological title "Son of David"
- The two blind men cry out בֶּן-דָּוִד ("Son of David"), which is rendered identically across all three versions. This is a key messianic title (cf. Isa 11:1, Jer 23:5). The triple agreement here underscores the firm textual tradition for this christological address.

### 9:23 — Musical instruments: "flute players"
- The Greek αὐλητάς ("flute players") is rendered as הַמְחַלֲלִים בַּחֲלִילִים ("those playing on flutes") in Hebrew. The root חלל/חָלִיל is well attested in the OT (1 Sam 10:5, 1 Kings 1:40, Isa 5:12). The Vulgate has "tibicines" (pipe players). All three versions agree on the musical detail of this mourning scene.
