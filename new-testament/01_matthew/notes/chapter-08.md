# Matthew Chapter 8 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All three sources contain 34 verses. Verse numbering aligns across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 8:3 — "cleansed" vs. "healed" in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Greek ἐκαθαρίσθη αὐτοῦ ἡ λέπρα means "his leprosy was cleansed." The Vulgate reads "mundata est lepra ejus" ("his leprosy was cleansed"). The Hebrew reads נִרְפְּאָה צָרַעְתּוֹ ("his leprosy was healed"), using a different verb.
- **Greek**: ἐκαθαρίσθη (was cleansed — from καθαρίζω)
- **Latin**: mundata est (was cleansed)
- **Hebrew**: נִרְפְּאָה (was healed — from רפא)
- **Note**: The Greek and Latin both use "cleanse" language, which is technically correct for leprosy in biblical law (Lev 13-14 uses טהר, "to be clean/pure," not רפא, "to heal," for leprosy). Delitzsch's use of נִרְפְּאָה ("was healed") departs from biblical levitical language, where צָרַעַת is always "cleansed" (טָהֵר), never "healed" (רָפָא). Compare 2 Kings 5:10-14 where Naaman is said to be טָהֵר (clean), not רָפָא (healed). However, in v.2 the Greek and Hebrew both correctly use the cleansing verb (καθαρίσαι / לְטַהֲרֵנִי), making the switch to רפא in v.3 inconsistent.

### 8:8 — "under my roof" — different Hebrew idiom
- **Issue**: The Greek ὑπὸ τὴν στέγην μου ("under my roof") and the Latin "sub tectum meum" ("under my roof") both use the literal roof metaphor. The Hebrew reads בְּצֵל קוֹרָתִי ("in the shadow of my beam/rafter"), using a different but vivid image.
- **Greek**: ἵνα μου ὑπὸ τὴν στέγην εἰσέλθῃς (that you enter under my roof)
- **Latin**: ut intres sub tectum meum (that you enter under my roof)
- **Hebrew**: נְקַלּתִי מֵאֲשֶׁר תָּבֹא בְּצֵל קוֹרָתִי (I am too small/unworthy for you to come under the shadow of my rafter)
- **Note**: Delitzsch's בְּצֵל קוֹרָתִי ("shadow of my beam") is a creative Hebrew idiom. The word קוֹרָה ("beam, rafter") appears in biblical Hebrew (1 Kgs 6:9). The phrase בְּצֵל ("in the shadow of") echoes biblical refuge language (Ps 91:1, Ruth 2:12). The phrase נְקַלּתִי ("I am too small/insignificant") is also notable, rendering οὐκ εἰμὶ ἱκανός ("I am not worthy") with a word that evokes humility before God (cf. Gen 32:11, Jacob's נְקַלּתִי "I am too small").

### 8:17 — Isaiah 53:4 quotation
- **Issue**: The quotation from Isaiah 53:4 differs across the sources. The Greek reads Αὐτὸς τὰς ἀσθενείας ἡμῶν ἔλαβεν καὶ τὰς νόσους ἐβάστασεν ("He took our weaknesses and carried our diseases"), which is a paraphrase closer to the LXX sense. The Hebrew directly quotes the Masoretic text: חֳלָיֵנוּ הוּא נָשָׂא וּמַכְאֹבֵינוּ סְבָלָם.
- **Greek**: Αὐτὸς τὰς ἀσθενείας ἡμῶν ἔλαβεν καὶ τὰς νόσους ἐβάστασεν
- **Latin**: Ipse infirmitates nostras accepit : et ægrotationes nostras portavit
- **Hebrew**: חֳלָיֵנוּ הוּא נָשָׂא וּמַכְאֹבֵינוּ סְבָלָם
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders the OT quotation directly from the Masoretic text of Isaiah 53:4, not back-translating from the Greek. This is appropriate for a Hebrew New Testament and shows Delitzsch's philological precision. The Greek NT author already adapted the Hebrew original, and Delitzsch restores the original Isaianic wording. The Masoretic text has חֳלָיֵנוּ ("our sicknesses") and מַכְאֹבֵינוּ ("our pains/sorrows"), while the Greek has ἀσθενείας ("weaknesses") and νόσους ("diseases").

### 8:28 — Gadarenes vs. Gerasenes vs. Gergesenes
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads Γαδαρηνῶν ("Gadarenes"). The Vulgate reads "Gerasenorum" ("Gerasenes"). The Hebrew reads הַגַּדְרִיִּם ("the Gadrites/Gadarenes"). This is a well-known textual variant across the Synoptic parallels.
- **Greek**: τῶν Γαδαρηνῶν (Gadarenes — SBLGNT)
- **Latin**: Gerasenorum (Gerasenes — Vulgate)
- **Hebrew**: הַגַּדְרִיִּם (Gadarenes — follows Greek)
- **Note**: Three place names appear in the manuscript tradition: Gadara, Gerasa, and Gergesa. Gadara was about 6 miles from the Sea of Galilee; Gerasa (modern Jerash) was about 30 miles away, making it geographically less likely for a story involving the sea. The SBLGNT and Delitzsch agree on Gadara; the Vulgate has Gerasa. Origen proposed Gergesa as a correction. The Hebrew form הַגַּדְרִיִּם is a natural gentilic formation from גָּדָר.

### 8:28 — Two demoniacs (Matthew) vs. one (Mark/Luke)
- **Issue**: All three sources in Matthew 8:28 mention two demoniacs (Greek δύο δαιμονιζόμενοι, Latin "duo habentes dæmonia", Hebrew שְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים אֲחוּזֵי שֵׁדִים). This is consistent within Matthew but differs from the Synoptic parallels in Mark 5:2 and Luke 8:27, which mention only one. This is not a divergence between the three texts here (they agree), but is a notable Synoptic difference for the reviewer.
- **Note**: No textual issue within this verse; all three agree on two demoniacs.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 8:1 — Wayyiqtol narrative continuation
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּרֶד מִן-הָהָר וַיֵּלֶךְ אַחֲרָיו הֲמוֹן עַם רָב
- **Note**: Smooth biblical narrative style with wayyiqtol chain. The phrase הֲמוֹן עַם רָב ("a great multitude of people") combines two words for "crowd/many" (הֲמוֹן + רָב), which is slightly redundant but occurs in biblical Hebrew (cf. 2 Chr 20:2, 32:7). The Greek is simply ὄχλοι πολλοί ("many crowds").

### 8:2 — מְצֹרָע for λεπρός
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ מְצֹרָע
- **Note**: Excellent biblical term. מְצֹרָע is the precise biblical Hebrew word for a person afflicted with צָרַעַת (Lev 13:44-45, 2 Sam 3:29, 2 Kgs 5:1). This is one of the rare cases where the Hebrew NT can use the exact biblical Hebrew term for a concept that the Greek merely transliterates/adapts.

### 8:6 — נְכֵה אֵבָרִים for παραλυτικός
- **Hebrew**: וְהוּא נְכֵה אֵבָרִים
- **Note**: The Greek παραλυτικός ("paralytic") is rendered as נְכֵה אֵבָרִים ("smitten of limbs"). The word נָכֶה ("smitten, stricken") is biblical (2 Sam 4:4, Isa 66:2), and אֵבָרִים ("limbs, members") is post-biblical. Biblical Hebrew would use a more general description. However, the construct phrase effectively conveys the meaning and has a biblical register due to the נְכֵה component.

### 8:8 — נְקַלּתִי for "I am not worthy"
- **Hebrew**: נְקַלּתִי מֵאֲשֶׁר תָּבֹא
- **Note**: As noted above, the use of נְקַלּתִי ("I am too small/insignificant") powerfully echoes Jacob's prayer in Genesis 32:11 (קָטֹנְתִּי מִכֹּל הַחֲסָדִים, "I am too small for all the mercies"). This connects the centurion's humility to the patriarchal model of humility before God. The Greek ἱκανός ("worthy, fit") does not carry this specific resonance.

### 8:12 — הַיְלָלָה וַחֲרוֹק הַשִׁנָּיִם for "weeping and gnashing of teeth"
- **Hebrew**: שָׁם תִּהְיֶה הַיְלָלָה וַחֲרוֹק הַשִׁנָּיִם
- **Note**: יְלָלָה ("wailing, howling") is a strong biblical word (Isa 15:8, Jer 25:36, Zeph 1:10). חֲרוֹק ("gnashing") appears in the Psalms in connection with enemies gnashing their teeth (Ps 35:16, 37:12, 112:10). The phrase as a whole sounds natural in biblical Hebrew prophetic-judgment language. This is a recurring formula in Matthew that Delitzsch renders consistently.

### 8:20 — בֶּן-הָאָדָם for υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου
- **Hebrew**: וּבֶן-הָאָדָם אֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם לְהַנִּיחַ אֶת-רֹאשׁוֹ
- **Note**: בֶּן-הָאָדָם ("son of man") directly corresponds to the Hebrew Bible's use of this phrase. In Ezekiel, בֶּן-אָדָם is the standard form of address from God to the prophet (Ezek 2:1, etc.). In Daniel 7:13, the Aramaic כְּבַר אֱנָשׁ ("like a son of man") is the messianic figure. Delitzsch uses the form with the article (בֶּן-הָאָדָם) throughout Matthew, which parallels Daniel 7:13's definite reference. The phrase לְהַנִּיחַ אֶת-רֹאשׁוֹ ("to rest his head") uses natural biblical Hebrew (cf. Gen 28:11, where Jacob lays his head at Bethel).

### 8:24 — סַעַר for σεισμός
- **Hebrew**: סַעַר גָּדוֹל הָיָה בַיָּם
- **Note**: The Greek σεισμός literally means "earthquake/shaking" but is used here for a violent storm at sea. The Vulgate has "motus magnus" ("great disturbance"). Delitzsch uses סַעַר ("storm, tempest"), which is the standard biblical Hebrew word for a violent wind-storm at sea (Jonah 1:4, 1:12; Ps 107:29). This is a more natural rendering than a literal translation of σεισμός would produce, and directly evokes the Jonah parallel that Matthew likely intends.

### 8:26 — דְּמָמָה רַבָּה for γαλήνη μεγάλη
- **Hebrew**: וַתְּהִי דְּמָמָה רַבָּה
- **Note**: The word דְּמָמָה ("silence, stillness, calm") powerfully evokes the theophany of 1 Kings 19:12 (קוֹל דְּמָמָה דַקָּה, "a still small voice"), creating an implicit theological connection between Jesus calming the storm and the divine revelation to Elijah. The Greek γαλήνη simply means "calm" (a nautical term), but Delitzsch's word choice adds a layer of biblical-theological resonance.

### 8:29 — מָה לָּנוּ וָלָךְ for Τί ἡμῖν καὶ σοί
- **Hebrew**: מָה לָּנוּ וָלָךְ יֵשׁוּעַ בֶּן הָאֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: The phrase מָה לִּי וָלָךְ / מָה לָּנוּ וָלָךְ ("what is there between us and you?") is a well-attested biblical idiom (Judg 11:12, 2 Sam 16:10, 19:23, 2 Kgs 3:13). Delitzsch correctly renders the Semitism back into its original Hebrew form; the Greek Τί ἡμῖν καὶ σοί is itself a Semitic calque. This is one of the clearest cases where the Hebrew NT recovers the original idiom underlying the Greek.

## Other Notes

### 8:5-13 — The Centurion's faith
- This pericope is notable for its narrative theology across all three languages. The centurion, a Gentile, displays greater faith than anyone in Israel (v.10), and Jesus' response (vv.11-12) about many coming from east and west to recline with the patriarchs while the "sons of the kingdom" are cast out carries particular force in a Hebrew translation aimed at Jewish readers. Delitzsch's Hebrew renders the passage without softening its challenging message.

### 8:17 — Hermeneutical approach to OT quotation
- Matthew's use of Isaiah 53:4 applies the Suffering Servant passage to physical healing ministry rather than to vicarious atonement. The Hebrew text makes this especially clear by preserving the Masoretic wording: חֳלָיֵנוּ הוּא נָשָׂא ("our sicknesses he bore") in a context of physical healing. This interpretive tradition (applying Isa 53 to healing) is found in both Jewish and Christian exegesis.

### 8:23-27 — Jonah typology
- The storm-stilling narrative contains multiple lexical echoes of Jonah in Delitzsch's Hebrew: סַעַר גָּדוֹל (cf. Jonah 1:4), יָשֵׁן (sleeping, cf. Jonah 1:5), וַתְּהִי דְּמָמָה (cf. the resolution of the storm in Jonah). These connections, already present in Matthew's Greek narrative, become even more pronounced in Hebrew, where the specific vocabulary directly evokes the Jonah story.
