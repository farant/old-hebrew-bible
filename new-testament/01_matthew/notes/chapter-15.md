# Matthew Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 39 verses align across all three sources. The SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, and Clementine Vulgate all contain verses 1-39 with no missing, extra, or differently-numbered verses.

Note on verses 5-6: The Vulgate appears to merge content from the Greek v.5 and v.6 slightly differently, placing the "and he shall not honor his father" clause differently. However, both verses are present and numbered identically in all three traditions.

## Content Divergences

### 15:4 — "God said" vs. "God commanded"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "ho gar theos eipen" ("for God said"). The Vulgate has "Nam Deus dixit" ("for God said"). However, Delitzsch renders "ki ha-elohim tsivvah lemor" ("for God commanded, saying"). The verb tsivvah (commanded) is stronger than eipen/dixit (said).
- **Greek**: ho gar theos eipen (for God said)
- **Latin**: Nam Deus dixit (for God said)
- **Hebrew**: ki ha-elohim tsivvah lemor (for God commanded, saying)
- **Note**: Delitzsch upgrades "said" to "commanded," which arguably better fits the context (these are commandments from the Decalogue), but it is not what the Greek says. The Greek eipen ("said") is the simple word for speaking. This is a minor but real content change.

### 15:5-6 — Corban/qorban expansion
- **Issue**: Delitzsch significantly expands the text of verse 5. The Greek says: "whoever says to father or mother, 'A gift, whatever from me might benefit you'" -- leaving the apodosis incomplete (an aposiopesis). The Vulgate similarly: "Munus, quodcumque est ex me, tibi proderit." But Delitzsch fills in the ellipsis: "the one who says to his father or mother 'qorban (is) what you would benefit from me' is not obligated in the honor of his father and mother" -- adding "eino hayyav be-khivud aviv ve-immo" which has no equivalent in the Greek or Latin.
- **Greek**: Hos an eipe to patri e te metri: Doron ho ean ex emou opheleches
- **Latin**: Quicumque dixerit patri, vel matri: Munus, quodcumque est ex me, tibi proderit
- **Hebrew**: ha-omer le-aviv ule-immo qorban mah-she-attah neheneh li eino hayyav be-khivud aviv ve-immo
- **Note**: Delitzsch supplies the conclusion of the halakhic argument ("he is not obligated in the honor of his father and mother") which is only implicit in the Greek. This reflects Delitzsch's knowledge of rabbinic interpretation of the corban vow (cf. m. Nedarim 9:1), but it adds content not present in the source text. The Greek deliberately leaves the sentence grammatically incomplete.

### 15:6 — "word of God" vs. "commandment of God"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT critical text reads "ton logon tou theou" ("the word of God"). The Vulgate reads "mandatum Dei" ("the commandment of God"), following a different Greek reading (ten entolen tou theou). Delitzsch has "devar ha-elohim" ("the word of God"), matching the SBLGNT.
- **Greek**: ton logon tou theou (the word of God)
- **Latin**: mandatum Dei (the commandment of God)
- **Hebrew**: devar ha-elohim (the word of God)
- **Note**: The Vulgate follows the variant reading "commandment" while the Greek critical text and Delitzsch both have "word." This is a genuine Vulgate divergence from the critical Greek text.

### 15:8 — Isaiah 29:13 quotation form
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "ho laos houtos tois cheilesin me tima" ("this people honors me with their lips"), following the LXX of Isaiah 29:13. The MT of Isaiah 29:13 has a different opening: "because this people draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips." Delitzsch follows the MT form rather than the LXX/Greek NT form: "niggash ha'am hazeh be-fiv u-vi-sfatav kibbeduni" ("this people draws near with its mouth and with its lips honors me").
- **Greek**: Ho laos houtos tois cheilesin me tima (this people honors me with lips)
- **Latin**: Populus hic labiis me honorat (this people honors me with lips)
- **Hebrew**: niggash ha'am hazeh be-fiv u-vi-sfatav kibbeduni (this people draws near with its mouth and with its lips honors me)
- **Note**: Delitzsch adds "draws near with its mouth" (niggash ... be-fiv) which comes from the MT of Isaiah 29:13 but is absent from both the SBLGNT Greek and the Vulgate. This is the same pattern seen in ch.13 where Delitzsch retranslates OT quotations toward the MT rather than faithfully rendering the LXX-based Greek text. This is a substantive content addition.

### 15:30 — List of afflicted persons: different order
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT lists "mute, blind, lame, crippled" (kophous, typhlous, cholous, kyllous). The Vulgate lists "mutos, caecos, claudos, debiles" (mute, blind, lame, disabled). Delitzsch has "pisehim ivrim ilmim qit'im" (lame, blind, mute, crippled) -- a different order from both Greek and Latin.
- **Greek**: kophous, typhlous, cholous, kyllous (deaf-mute, blind, lame, maimed)
- **Latin**: mutos, caecos, claudos, debiles (mute, blind, lame, feeble)
- **Hebrew**: pisehim ivrim ilmim qit'im (lame, blind, mute, maimed)
- **Note**: The reordering does not change the meaning, but it represents an unexplained deviation from the source text order.

### 15:39 — Magadan / Magedan
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "Magadan." The Vulgate reads "Magedan." Delitzsch writes "Magdan." This place name is notoriously variable across manuscripts. Some Greek manuscripts read "Magdala" (harmonizing with Mark 8:10's "Dalmanutha" or the town of Magdala). All three are variant spellings of the same location.
- **Greek**: Magadan
- **Latin**: Magedan
- **Hebrew**: Magdan
- **Note**: Minor orthographic variation only. No substantive divergence.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 15:2 — "tradition of the elders" as qabbalat ha-zqenim
- **Hebrew**: qabbalat ha-zqenim (the received tradition of the elders)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses qabbalah, which in post-biblical Hebrew specifically means "received tradition" (from the root q-b-l, "receive"). This is actually a very apt choice for paradosin ton presbyteron, since qabbalah became the technical term in rabbinic literature for oral tradition (cf. Avot 1:1: "Moses received [qibbel] Torah from Sinai and transmitted it..."). However, qabbalah in this technical sense is post-biblical and not found in the Hebrew Bible. In biblical Hebrew, the word would not carry this meaning.

### 15:2 — "wash hands" as notlim yedeihem
- **Hebrew**: einam notlim yedeihem la-se'udah (they do not wash their hands for the meal)
- **Note**: The verb natal for ritual handwashing is Mishnaic Hebrew (cf. m. Yadayim, m. Berakhot). Biblical Hebrew would use rahats (wash, Gen 18:4) or use the construction of "pouring water on the hands" (2 Kings 3:11: asher yatsaq mayim al yedei). Delitzsch's use of natal is anachronistic for biblical Hebrew but perfectly at home in the rabbinic world this passage describes. Se'udah (meal/feast) is also Mishnaic rather than biblical (the biblical word would be lehem or mishteh).

### 15:7 — "hypocrites" as hanefim
- **Hebrew**: hanefim (hypocrites/godless ones)
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders hypokritai as hanefim. In biblical Hebrew, hanef means "godless, profane, polluted" (Job 8:13, 13:16, 15:34, 20:5, 27:8; Isaiah 9:16, 10:6, 33:14). This is close but not identical to the Greek hypokrites, which originally means "actor" and in the NT means "pretender/dissembler." The biblical hanef emphasizes godlessness rather than pretense. Nevertheless, hanef became the standard Hebrew translation for hypocrite, and its use in Isaiah 33:14 (hanefim be-tsiyyon) in the context of sinners among God's people makes it contextually fitting.

### 15:9 — Excellent MT quotation
- **Hebrew**: va-tehi yir'atam oti mitsvat anashim melummadah (and their fear of me is a commandment of men, learned by rote)
- **Note**: This is taken almost verbatim from the MT of Isaiah 29:13. The phrase mitsvat anashim melummadah is excellent biblical Hebrew. Delitzsch correctly follows the MT here, though the Greek (didaskontes didaskalias entalmata anthropon, "teaching as doctrines the commandments of men") is slightly different in structure -- it makes the people the subject of teaching, while the MT/Delitzsch makes the fear of God the subject.

### 15:14 — "blind guides of the blind"
- **Hebrew**: madrikhim ivrim hemmah la-ivrim (they are blind guides for the blind)
- **Note**: Madrikhim (guides) is from the hiphil of d-r-kh, well-attested in biblical Hebrew (2 Chr 32:22, Prov 4:11). The construction ivrim hemmah la-ivrim inverts for emphasis, which is a natural Hebrew rhetorical device. The word for "pit" -- shahat -- is an excellent biblical choice (Ps 7:16, 9:16, 35:7, Prov 26:27), more evocative than the Greek bothynon (ditch/pit).

### 15:22 — Canaanite woman description
- **Hebrew**: ishah kena'anit yotset min ha-gevulot ha-hem (a Canaanite woman going out from those borders)
- **Note**: Kena'anit is a thoroughly biblical term (Gen 38:2, 46:10). Delitzsch's whole construction reads like natural biblical narrative. The woman's cry "hanneni adoni ben-David" ("be gracious to me, my lord, son of David") uses the excellent biblical verb hanan (be gracious, Ps 4:2, 6:3, etc.) in the imperative.

### 15:26 — "small dogs" as tse'irei ha-kelavim
- **Hebrew**: tse'irei ha-kelavim (the young ones of the dogs)
- **Note**: The Greek kynariois is a diminutive of kyon (dog), meaning "small dogs" or "puppies" -- likely house dogs as opposed to street dogs. Delitzsch renders this as tse'irei ha-kelavim ("the young/small ones of the dogs"). The word tsa'ir means "young, small" in biblical Hebrew (Gen 19:31, Judg 6:15), so this is a reasonable periphrasis. Biblical Hebrew lacks diminutive morphology, so a descriptive phrase is the only option.

### 15:32 — "my compassion is stirred" -- nikhmeru rahamai
- **Hebrew**: nikhmeru rahamai al ha-am (my compassion is stirred over the people)
- **Note**: This is another outstanding rendering of splanchnizomai. The phrase nikhmeru rahamai echoes Genesis 43:30 (nikhmeru rahamav el ahiv, "his compassion was stirred toward his brother") and 1 Kings 3:26 (nikhmeru rameha al benah). This is arguably the most authentically biblical Hebrew way to express deep compassion, and Delitzsch deploys it perfectly.

## Other Notes

### 15:3-9 — Corban / tradition debate
This passage is a key text for understanding the relationship between written Torah and oral tradition in Second Temple Judaism. All three texts agree on the core argument: human tradition (paradosis / traditio / qabbalah) is being used to override divine commandment (entole / mandatum / mitsvah). Delitzsch's use of qabbalah and the familiar halakhic language makes the Hebrew version read very naturally for anyone familiar with rabbinic literature.

### 15:21-28 — The Canaanite/Syrophoenician woman
This pericope is theologically significant for the question of Jewish-Gentile mission. The woman is called "Canaanite" (Chananaia) in Matthew, while Mark 7:26 calls her "Syrophoenician." All three texts in Matthew agree on "Canaanite." Delitzsch's kena'anit deliberately evokes the ancient biblical inhabitants of the land, which heightens the theological tension of the passage -- Jesus is sent to the lost sheep of Israel, but a descendant of the original pre-Israelite inhabitants receives grace through faith.

### 15:36 — eucharistesas vs. eulogesen
In the first feeding (14:19), the Greek has eulogesen ("he blessed"), and Delitzsch uses va-yevarekh. Here in the second feeding, the Greek has eucharistesas ("having given thanks"), but Delitzsch again uses va-yevarekh ("he blessed"), flattening the distinction between the two Greek verbs. The Vulgate preserves the distinction: benedixit (14:19) vs. gratias agens (15:36). The loss of this distinction in the Hebrew is a minor but real translation issue.
