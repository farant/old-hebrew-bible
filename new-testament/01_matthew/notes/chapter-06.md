# Matthew Chapter 6 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All three sources contain 34 verses. Verse numbering aligns across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 6:1 — "righteousness" vs. "alms" (δικαιοσύνην / צִדְקַתְכֶם / justitiam)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads δικαιοσύνην ("righteousness") as the critical text, though some manuscripts read ἐλεημοσύνην ("almsgiving"). All three sources here agree on the broader term: Greek δικαιοσύνην, Hebrew צִדְקַתְכֶם, and Latin "justitiam." However, it is worth noting that the Hebrew צְדָקָה can mean both "righteousness" and "charitable giving" (as it does in rabbinic Hebrew), so the Hebrew here is semantically ambiguous in a way the Greek and Latin are not.
- **Greek**: τὴν δικαιοσύνην ὑμῶν
- **Latin**: justitiam vestram
- **Hebrew**: צִדְקַתְכֶם
- **Note**: The semantic overlap is actually productive: in v.2, all three shift to the specific term for charitable giving (Greek ἐλεημοσύνην, Hebrew צְדָקָה, Latin eleemosynam), so the Hebrew reads seamlessly from the general principle (v.1) to the specific application (v.2) because צְדָקָה covers both senses.

### 6:4, 6:6, 6:18 — "openly" (בַּגָּלוּי) in Hebrew brackets
- **Issue**: In all three verses, the Hebrew includes the word בַּגָּלוּי ("openly/in the open") in square brackets. The SBLGNT omits ἐν τῷ φανερῷ ("openly") in all three locations. The Vulgate also omits it in all three locations.
- **Greek 6:4**: ἀποδώσει σοι (no ἐν τῷ φανερῷ)
- **Latin 6:4**: reddet tibi (no "in palam" / "manifeste")
- **Hebrew 6:4**: הוּא [בַּגָּלוּי] יִגְמְלֶךָ (brackets mark TR addition)
- **Note**: The Byzantine / Textus Receptus tradition adds "openly" to create an antithesis (do it in secret, God will reward openly). The critical text and the Vulgate agree in omitting it. Delitzsch again helpfully brackets the Byzantine expansion.

### 6:13 — Doxology included in Hebrew with double brackets
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes the doxology כִּי לְךָ הַמַּמְלָכָה וְהַגְּבוּרָה וְהַתִּפְאֶרֶת לְעוֹלְמֵי עוֹלָמִים אָמֵן ("For yours is the kingdom, and the power, and the glory, forever. Amen.") in double brackets [[...]]. The SBLGNT omits the doxology entirely. The Vulgate includes only "Amen."
- **Greek**: ἀλλὰ ῥῦσαι ἡμᾶς ἀπὸ τοῦ πονηροῦ. (ends here in SBLGNT)
- **Latin**: sed libera nos a malo. Amen. (adds only "Amen")
- **Hebrew**: כִּי אִם-תְּחַלְּצֵנוּ מִן-הָרָע [[כִּי לְךָ הַמַּמְלָכָה וְהַגְּבוּרָה וְהַתִּפְאֶרֶת לְעוֹלְמֵי עוֹלָמִים אָמֵן]]
- **Note**: The doxology is absent from the earliest and best Greek manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and from the Vulgate. It entered the Byzantine tradition likely from liturgical use, and is adapted from 1 Chronicles 29:11. Delitzsch marks it with double brackets to signal its secondary status. The Hebrew rendering itself is excellent, closely echoing the language of 1 Chronicles 29:11 (לְךָ יְהוָה הַמַּמְלָכָה).

### 6:11 — ἐπιούσιον / supersubstantialem / חֻקֵּנוּ
- **Issue**: The famously difficult word ἐπιούσιον (which occurs only here and in Luke 11:3 in all of Greek literature) is rendered differently across the three texts. The Vulgate has "supersubstantialem" (a word Jerome coined), while the Hebrew has חֻקֵּנוּ ("our allotment/portion").
- **Greek**: τὸν ἄρτον ἡμῶν τὸν ἐπιούσιον
- **Latin**: Panem nostrum supersubstantialem
- **Hebrew**: אֶת-לֶחֶם חֻקֵּנוּ
- **Note**: Each translation interprets the enigmatic ἐπιούσιον differently. Jerome's "supersubstantialem" suggests "above-substance" or "super-essential." Delitzsch's חֻקֵּנוּ ("our portion/ration") derives from חֹק, meaning "allotted portion, statute" (cf. Prov 30:8 לֶחֶם חֻקִּי "my allotted bread"), which is an excellent biblical parallel. Proverbs 30:8 is in fact the closest Old Testament parallel to this petition, and Delitzsch's choice directly evokes it.

### 6:25 — Hebrew adds "what shall we drink"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT critical text for v.25 includes a question about drinking (τί πίωμεν is in the apparatus as a variant). Some manuscripts omit it. The Hebrew includes מַה-נִּשְׁתֶּה ("what shall we drink") alongside eating and wearing. The Vulgate does not include "what shall we drink" in v.25 (it appears only in v.31).
- **Greek**: μὴ μεριμνᾶτε τῇ ψυχῇ ὑμῶν τί φάγητε...μηδὲ τῷ σώματι ὑμῶν τί ἐνδύσησθε (eat...wear; SBLGNT note: some mss add ἢ τί πίητε)
- **Latin**: ne solliciti sitis animæ vestræ quid manducetis, neque corpori vestro quid induamini (eat...wear; no drinking)
- **Hebrew**: מַה-נֹּאכֵל וּמַה-נִּשְׁתֶּה...מַה-נִּלְבָּשׁ (eat, drink, and wear)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Byzantine/TR reading that includes the drinking reference in v.25. The SBLGNT and Vulgate have a shorter reading. The addition may be harmonized from v.31, where all sources include all three questions.

### 6:28-29 — Verse boundary split
- **Issue**: The Hebrew places the phrase "they toil not, neither do they spin" (לֹא יַעַמְלוּ וְלֹא יִטְווּ) at the start of v.29, while in the Greek and Latin it belongs to v.28. The Hebrew v.28 thus reads shorter (ending with הַצֹּמְחוֹת, "which are growing") and v.29 begins with the labor/spinning clause before continuing with the Solomon comparison.
- **Greek v.28**: ...πῶς αὐξάνουσιν· οὐ κοπιῶσιν οὐδὲ νήθουσιν (grow; they toil not nor spin)
- **Hebrew v.28**: ...הַצֹּמְחוֹת (growing — ends here)
- **Hebrew v.29**: לֹא יַעַמְלוּ וְלֹא יִטְווּ (they toil not nor spin — begins here)
- **Note**: This is not a textual variant but a difference in verse division. The content is the same across all three sources; only the placement of the verse break differs in the Hebrew. Delitzsch likely follows a different versification tradition or made a deliberate editorial choice for readability.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:2 — Trumpet imagery
- **Hebrew**: אַל-תָּרִיעַ לְפָנֶיךָ בַּשׂוֹפָר
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses תָּרִיעַ (hiphil of רוע, "to sound an alarm/shout") with שׂוֹפָר (the ram's horn). The Greek σαλπίσῃς refers to a trumpet blast. Using שׂוֹפָר rather than חֲצֹצְרָה (the silver trumpet) is a good choice, as the שׂוֹפָר is the more culturally prominent instrument in biblical Hebrew. The verb תָּרִיעַ is authentically biblical (Josh 6:5, Num 10:9).

### 6:7 — תְּפַטְפְּטוּ for βατταλογήσητε ("babble")
- **Hebrew**: אַל-תְּפַטְפְּטוּ כַּגּוֹיִם
- **Note**: The rare Greek word βατταλογέω ("to babble, use vain repetitions") is rendered with the post-biblical / rabbinic פִּטְפֵּט ("to chatter, babble"). This is not a biblical Hebrew word; no precise biblical equivalent exists. A biblical alternative might be the concept from Eccl 5:1 (אַל-תְּבַהֵל עַל-פִּיךָ, "do not be hasty with your mouth"), but פִּטְפֵּט more precisely captures the onomatopoeic quality of the Greek.

### 6:9 — Lord's Prayer opening
- **Hebrew**: אָבִינוּ שֶׁבַּשָׁמַיִם יִתְקַדַּשׁ שְׁמֶךָ
- **Note**: The opening אָבִינוּ שֶׁבַּשָׁמַיִם mirrors the standard Jewish liturgical formula (as in the Kaddish and Amidah). The hitpael יִתְקַדַּשׁ ("may [your name] be sanctified") closely parallels the Kaddish's יִתְקַדַּשׁ שְׁמֵהּ רַבָּא. While שֶׁ- (the relative particle) is more commonly mishnaic than biblical (biblical Hebrew would prefer אֲשֶׁר), Delitzsch uses שֶׁבַּשָׁמַיִם throughout Matthew as a fixed formula, consistent with its occasional biblical use (Judg 5:7, Song 1:7).

### 6:12 — חֹבוֹתֵינוּ / חַיָּבֵינוּ for debts/debtors
- **Hebrew**: וּמְחַל-לָנוּ עַל-חֹבוֹתֵינוּ כַּאֲשֶׁר מָחַלְנוּ גַּם-אֲנַחְנוּ לְחַיָּבֵינוּ
- **Note**: The Greek ὀφειλήματα ("debts") and ὀφειλέταις ("debtors") are rendered with חֹבוֹת and חַיָּבִים, both post-biblical / rabbinic terms. Biblical Hebrew would use נְשִׁי / נֹשִׁים (from נשא, "creditor/debtor") or a circumlocution. The verb מָחַל ("to forgive") is also rabbinic; biblical Hebrew would prefer סָלַח or נָשָׂא. However, the rabbinic terminology creates a closer parallel to the Aramaic Kaddish tradition and the actual Semitic idiom that likely underlies the Greek.

### 6:19-20 — סָס וְרָקָב for σής and βρῶσις
- **Hebrew**: סָס וְרָקָב (moth and decay/rot)
- **Note**: The Greek has σής ("moth") and βρῶσις ("eating/rust/corrosion"). The Latin has "ærugo" (rust) and "tinea" (moth). Delitzsch's סָס ("moth") is found in Isaiah 51:8 (כִּי כַבֶּגֶד יֹאכְלֵם עָשׁ, though using עָשׁ there). The word רָקָב ("rottenness, decay") is well attested biblically (Prov 12:4, 14:30; Hab 3:16). This is a strong biblical Hebrew rendering.

### 6:24 — מָמוֹן as loanword
- **Hebrew**: לֹא תּוּכְלוּ עֲבוֹד אֶת-הָאֱלֹהִים וְאֶת-הַמָּמוֹן
- **Note**: The Greek μαμωνᾶ is itself a Semitic loanword (Aramaic ממונא). Delitzsch uses הַמָּמוֹן with the Hebrew article, naturalizing the Aramaic term. This is appropriate; the Latin similarly preserves the loanword as "mammonæ."

### 6:30 — קְטַנֵּי אֱמוּנָה for ὀλιγόπιστοι
- **Hebrew**: קְטַנֵּי אֱמוּנָה
- **Note**: The Greek ὀλιγόπιστοι ("of little faith") is a compound adjective characteristic of Matthew. Delitzsch's construct chain קְטַנֵּי אֱמוּנָה ("small ones of faith") follows biblical Hebrew construct state grammar perfectly. While this exact phrase is not in the Hebrew Bible, the pattern (adjective + אֱמוּנָה) is natural, and the rendering captures the sense precisely. This same phrase recurs in 8:26.

## Other Notes

### 6:9-13 — The Lord's Prayer as a whole
- Delitzsch's rendering of the Lord's Prayer has become the standard Hebrew version used in Jewish-Christian contexts. Its resonance with Jewish liturgical language (Kaddish, Amidah) is noteworthy and likely intentional. The prayer reads naturally as Hebrew liturgical text, which strengthens the case that Jesus originally taught it in a Semitic language.
