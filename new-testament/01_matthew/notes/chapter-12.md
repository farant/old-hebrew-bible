# Matthew Chapter 12 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 50 verses align across all three sources. However, verse 47 has a significant textual-critical issue.

### 12:47 — Disputed verse
- **Issue**: Matt 12:47 is absent from several important Greek manuscripts (including Sinaiticus original hand, and some other witnesses). The SBLGNT includes it but marks it with ⸂⸃ indicating the entire verse is a textual variant. Both the Vulgate and Delitzsch include it.
- **Note**: The verse is likely authentic (present in the majority of manuscripts and required by narrative logic — someone must tell Jesus his family is waiting), but its absence from some key witnesses is noteworthy. It may have been accidentally omitted due to homoioteleuton (similar verse endings in vv. 46 and 47).

## Content Divergences

### 12:6 — "Something greater" vs "someone greater"
- **Issue**: The Greek τοῦ ἱεροῦ μεῖζόν ἐστιν ὧδε is ambiguous — the neuter μεῖζόν could mean "something greater" (than the temple is here) or, less commonly, "someone greater." The Vulgate interprets as personal: "templo major est hic" ("greater than the temple is here [this one]"). Delitzsch uses יֶשׁ-פֹּה גָּדוֹל מִן-הַמִּקְדָּשׁ ("there is here [one/something] greater than the Temple"), which is similarly ambiguous.
- **Greek**: τοῦ ἱεροῦ μεῖζόν ἐστιν ὧδε
- **Latin**: templo major est hic
- **Hebrew**: יֶשׁ-פֹּה גָּדוֹל מִן-הַמִּקְדָּשׁ
- **Note**: The Latin "major" is masculine, making it personal ("someone greater"). The Hebrew גָּדוֹל is also grammatically masculine but can serve as either personal or impersonal in this construction. The Greek neuter may be deliberately ambiguous, allowing both a christological reading (Jesus is greater) and a dispensational one (the kingdom is greater). All three versions preserve at least partial ambiguity.

### 12:15 — "Many followed" vs "great multitudes followed"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads ἠκολούθησαν αὐτῷ πολλοί ("many followed him") with a variant marker on αὐτῷ. Delitzsch has הֲמוֹן עַם רָב ("a great multitude of people"), which is more expansive. The Vulgate has "secuti sunt eum multi" ("many followed him"), matching the Greek.
- **Greek**: ἠκολούθησαν ⸀αὐτῷ πολλοί
- **Latin**: secuti sunt eum multi
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּלֶךְ אַחֲרָיו הֲמוֹן עַם רָב
- **Note**: Delitzsch amplifies "many" (πολλοί) to "a great multitude of people" (הֲמוֹן עַם רָב), following the TR/Byzantine reading ὄχλοι πολλοί ("great crowds"). The critical text has the simpler "many."

### 12:18-21 — Isaiah 42:1-4 quotation
- **Issue**: This is the longest OT quotation in Matthew, from Isaiah 42:1-4. The Greek text of Matthew diverges from the LXX, and Delitzsch's Hebrew diverges from the MT of Isaiah in places, following the NT form of the quotation.
- **Greek (v.18)**: ὁ ἀγαπητός μου εἰς ὃν εὐδόκησεν ἡ ψυχή μου ("my beloved in whom my soul is well pleased")
- **Latin (v.18)**: dilectus meus, in quo bene complacuit animæ meæ
- **Hebrew (v.18)**: יְדִידִי רָצְתָה נַפְשִׁי
- **Note**: The MT of Isa 42:1 reads בְּחִירִי רָצְתָה נַפְשִׁי ("my chosen one, my soul delights in"). Delitzsch uses יְדִידִי ("my beloved") to match the Greek ἀγαπητός rather than the MT's בְּחִירִי ("my chosen"). However, Delitzsch retains the MT phrasing רָצְתָה נַפְשִׁי. This hybrid approach — following the NT Greek where it diverges from the OT Hebrew, but using OT Hebrew phrasing where possible — is characteristic of Delitzsch's translation method.

### 12:20 — "Victory" vs "truth" (Isaiah 42:3)
- **Issue**: The Hebrew MT of Isaiah 42:3 reads לֶאֱמֶת ("unto truth"), but the Greek NT (following its own LXX-influenced tradition) reads εἰς νῖκος ("unto victory"). Delitzsch renders this as לָנֶצַח ("unto victory/forever"), following the NT Greek rather than the MT.
- **Greek**: ἕως ἂν ἐκβάλῃ εἰς νῖκος τὴν κρίσιν
- **Latin**: donec ejiciat ad victoriam judicium
- **Hebrew**: עַד-יוֹצִיא לָנֶצַח מִשְׁפָּט
- **Note**: The word נֶצַח can mean both "forever" and "victory" in biblical Hebrew (cf. 1 Sam 15:29, 1 Chr 29:11, Lam 3:18). The LXX apparently read the Hebrew consonants נצח as "victory" rather than following the MT's אמת ("truth"). Delitzsch's choice to use לָנֶצַח over the MT's לֶאֱמֶת demonstrates his commitment to rendering the NT text faithfully rather than harmonizing back to the OT source.

### 12:35 — "Treasure of the heart" addition
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT does not include "of the heart" (τῆς καρδίας) with "treasure," but Delitzsch has מֵאוֹצַר לִבּוֹ הַטּוֹב ("from the good treasure of his heart"), adding לִבּוֹ ("his heart"). The Vulgate also omits "of the heart."
- **Greek**: ὁ ἀγαθὸς ἄνθρωπος ἐκ τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ θησαυροῦ ἐκβάλλει ἀγαθά
- **Latin**: Bonus homo de bono thesauro profert bona
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ טוֹב מֵאוֹצַר לִבּוֹ הַטּוֹב מוֹצִיא אֶת-הַטּוֹב
- **Note**: The addition of "heart" is found in the TR/Byzantine text and in the Luke 6:45 parallel. The critical text omits it. Delitzsch follows the longer reading.

### 12:40 — "Whale/sea monster" vs "fish"
- **Issue**: The Greek κήτους means "sea monster/whale" (the LXX term used in Jonah). The Vulgate has "ceti" (sea creature). But Delitzsch uses הַדָּג ("the fish"), which matches the MT of Jonah 2:1 (בִּמְעֵי הַדָּגָה / בִּמְעֵי הַדָּג) rather than the Greek/Latin term.
- **Greek**: ἐν τῇ κοιλίᾳ τοῦ κήτους
- **Latin**: in ventre ceti
- **Hebrew**: בִּמְעֵי הַדָּג
- **Note**: This is a case where Delitzsch harmonizes with the OT Hebrew of Jonah rather than translating the Greek literally. The Hebrew דָּג is a generic "fish," not a "sea monster" as in the Greek/Latin. Since Jonah's Hebrew text uses דָּג/דָּגָה, Delitzsch's choice creates smoother intertextual resonance for Hebrew readers.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 12:1 — מְלִילֹת for "ears of grain"
- **Hebrew**: וַיָּחֵלּוּ לִקְטֹף מְלִילֹת
- **Note**: מְלִילוֹת ("ears of grain") appears in Deut 23:26 (כִּי תָבֹא בְּקָמַת רֵעֶךָ וְקָטַפְתָּ מְלִילֹת) in precisely the legal context of plucking grain from a neighbor's field. This is an exceptional lexical choice — it directly connects the disciples' action to the Deuteronomic permission, adding legal depth to the Sabbath controversy. Highly biblical and contextually perfect.

### 12:4 — לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים for "showbread"
- **Hebrew**: אֶת-לֶחֶם הַפָּנִים
- **Note**: This is the standard biblical term for the showbread (Ex 25:30, 1 Sam 21:7). Delitzsch renders the Greek τοὺς ἄρτους τῆς προθέσεως ("the bread of the setting forth") with the proper OT Hebrew technical term. The allusion to 1 Sam 21:2-7 (David at Nob) is preserved naturally.

### 12:11 — כֶּבֶשׂ for "sheep"
- **Hebrew**: אֲשֶׁר-לוֹ כֶּבֶשׂ אֶחָד
- **Note**: כֶּבֶשׂ ("lamb, young sheep") is used where the Greek has πρόβατον ("sheep" generically). The Hebrew כֶּבֶשׂ is more specific, recalling Nathan's parable to David (2 Sam 12:3, כִּבְשָׂה אַחַת קְטַנָּה — "one small ewe lamb"). While the Greek is generic, Delitzsch's choice adds pathos by evoking the Nathan parable.

### 12:20 — קָנֶה רָצוּץ and פִּשְׁתָּה כֵהָה
- **Hebrew**: קָנֶה רָצוּץ לֹא יִשְׁבּוֹר וּפִשְׁתָּה כֵהָה לֹא יְכַבֶּנָּה
- **Note**: This is virtually identical to the MT of Isaiah 42:3 (קָנֶה רָצוּץ לֹא יִשְׁבּוֹר וּפִשְׁתָּה כֵהָה לֹא יְכַבֶּנָּה). Delitzsch wisely preserves the exact OT wording where the NT does not diverge from it. This creates seamless scriptural resonance for Hebrew readers.

### 12:24 — בַעַל זְבוּל for "Beelzebul"
- **Hebrew**: בַעַל זְבוּל שַׂר הַשֵׁדִים
- **Note**: Consistent with 10:25. Delitzsch maintains זְבוּל (with -l, "Lord of the Dwelling") rather than the Vulgate's "Beelzebub" (with -b, "Lord of Flies"). See note at 10:25.

### 12:29 — יִשְׁסֶה for "plunder"
- **Hebrew**: וְאַחַר יִשְׁסֶה אֶת-בֵּיתוֹ
- **Note**: שָׁסָה ("to plunder, despoil") is a strong biblical verb (Isa 10:6, Jer 50:11, Ps 89:42). It renders the Greek διαρπάσει ("will plunder") with an authentically biblical war term. The construct of binding the strong man and plundering his house uses naturally biblical vocabulary throughout (אָסַר, גָּזַל, שָׁסָה).

### 12:31 — גִּדּוּף for "blasphemy"
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-חֵטְא וְגִדּוּף יִסָּלַח לָאָדָם
- **Note**: גִּדּוּף ("blasphemy, reviling") is biblical vocabulary (Isa 43:28, 51:7; Ezek 5:15). It is the standard Hebrew equivalent for βλασφημία. The niphal יִסָּלַח ("will be forgiven") echoes Levitical language (Lev 4:20, 26, 31 — וְנִסְלַח). The entire construction feels naturally biblical.

### 12:34 — יַלְדֵי צִפְעוֹנִים for "brood of vipers"
- **Hebrew**: יַלְדֵי צִפְעוֹנִים
- **Note**: צִפְעוֹנִי ("viper, adder") appears in Isa 11:8, 14:29, 59:5, and Prov 23:32. Delitzsch uses the construct form יַלְדֵי ("offspring of") rather than γεννήματα's literal sense of "produce/generation," making it more vivid. The phrase echoes Isaiah 59:5 (בֵּיצֵי צִפְעוֹנִי — "eggs of vipers") and creates the same prophetic tone of denunciation.

### 12:36 — מִלָּה בְטֵלָה for "idle word"
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-מִלָּה בְטֵלָה
- **Note**: מִלָּה ("word") is more characteristic of later biblical Hebrew / Aramaic influence (Job 4:2, Ps 139:4, cf. Dan 4:30 Aramaic מִלָּה). The more standard classical biblical Hebrew term would be דָּבָר. The adjective בָּטֵל ("idle, vain") is essentially Mishnaic/Aramaic (cf. Ezra 4:21, 24 in Aramaic). The phrase thus has a late-biblical / post-biblical feel, though the Aramaic roots are attested in the biblical text itself.

### 12:42 — מַלְכַּת תֵּימָן for "Queen of the South"
- **Hebrew**: מַלְכַּת תֵּימָן
- **Note**: This directly echoes 1 Kings 10:1 (מַלְכַּת-שְׁבָא, "Queen of Sheba"). Delitzsch uses תֵּימָן ("south/Teman") to match the Greek νότου ("south") rather than שְׁבָא (Sheba). The term תֵּימָן is fully biblical (Gen 36:34, Jer 49:7, Amos 1:12), referring to the southern region. This is an accurate translation that preserves geographic reference without forcing a specific identification with Sheba.

### 12:43-45 — Sustained narrative register
- **Hebrew**: וְהָרוּחַ הַטְּמֵאָה אַחֲרֵי צֵאתָהּ מִן-הָאָדָם תְּשֹׁטֵט בִּמְקֹמוֹת צִיָּה לְבַקֶּשׁ-לָהּ מָנוֹחַ
- **Note**: This parable uses excellent biblical vocabulary. תְּשֹׁטֵט ("wanders about") appears in 2 Chr 16:9, Zech 4:10 for eyes roaming. צִיָּה ("dryness, parched places") is biblical (Ps 63:2, Isa 35:1). מָנוֹחַ ("rest, resting place") echoes Gen 8:9 (the dove finding no rest), Ruth 1:9, and Isa 34:14. The entire passage reads as natural biblical Hebrew narrative.

## Other Notes

### 12:7 — Second quotation of Hosea 6:6
- This is the second time Matthew quotes Hosea 6:6 (the first was 9:13). Delitzsch renders it identically both times: חֶסֶד חָפַצְתִּי וְלֹא זָבַח. This is nearly identical to the MT of Hosea 6:6 (כִּי חֶסֶד חָפַצְתִּי וְלֹא-זָבַח), merely dropping the initial כִּי. The consistency and faithfulness to the OT original is commendable.

### 12:28 — Kingdom of God vs Kingdom of Heaven
- Notably, this verse uses βασιλεία τοῦ θεοῦ ("kingdom of God") rather than Matthew's usual βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν ("kingdom of heaven"). Delitzsch faithfully renders this distinction: מַלְכוּת הָאֱלֹהִים ("kingdom of God") rather than the usual מַלְכוּת הַשָׁמַיִם. The Vulgate has "regnum Dei." This is one of the rare places where Matthew uses "kingdom of God" instead of his preferred circumlocution, possibly because the argument about the Spirit of God requires the explicit divine name.

### 12:31-32 — Unforgivable sin
- The passage on blasphemy against the Holy Spirit is theologically significant. All three versions agree closely. The Hebrew distinction between חֶרְפָּה ("reproach, reviling") for speaking against the Son of Man and מְחָרֵף אֶת-רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ ("reviling the Holy Spirit") uses the same root חרף in both cases, creating a clear Hebrew parallel that underscores the gravity of the distinction. The phrase רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ ("Holy Spirit") matches the standard Hebrew construct found in Isa 63:10-11 and Ps 51:13.

### 12:39-40 — Sign of Jonah
- The Jonah typology is one of Jesus' most explicit self-references to his death and resurrection. The Hebrew בְּלֵב הָאֲדָמָה ("in the heart of the earth") is a striking phrase — biblical Hebrew uses לֵב for the "heart/midst" of things (cf. Ex 15:8 בְּלֵב יָם "in the heart of the sea"). All three versions preserve the three-days-and-three-nights structure, which echoes the Hebrew stylistic pattern of Jonah 2:1.
