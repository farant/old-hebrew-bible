# Matthew Chapter 18 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The SBLGNT contains 35 verse slots, but verse 11 has an empty Greek field. The Delitzsch Hebrew includes bracketed content at verse 11 (`[כִּי בֶן-הָאָדָם בָּא לְהוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת-הָאֹבֵד]`), and the Vulgate includes full content at verse 11. The verse counts nominally align (35 verses in all three), but the content at each verse number is shifted in the Vulgate from verse 11 onward, similar to the pattern in chapter 17.

Specifically, the Vulgate Latin text at verses 11-18 each corresponds to the Greek/Hebrew content of the following verse. For example:
- Vulgate v.11 (`Venit enim Filius hominis salvare quod perierat`) corresponds to the content of the interpolated verse that appears in Hebrew v.11.
- Vulgate v.12 contains the lost sheep parable content that matches Greek v.12.

The alignment self-corrects because the interpolated verse content in the Vulgate absorbs the offset.

## Content Divergences

### 18:11 — Omitted verse (Son of Man came to save the lost)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT omits verse 11 entirely (empty Greek). The Hebrew includes the verse in brackets. The Vulgate includes it.
- **Greek**: [empty]
- **Latin**: `Venit enim Filius hominis salvare quod perierat`
- **Hebrew**: `[כִּי בֶן-הָאָדָם בָּא לְהוֹשִׁיעַ אֶת-הָאֹבֵד]`
- **Note**: Matt 18:11 is absent from the best early manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and others). It is a scribal interpolation drawn from Luke 19:10. The SBLGNT rightly omits it. Delitzsch marks it with brackets to indicate its doubtful status. The Vulgate follows the Byzantine tradition and includes it.

### 18:14 — "Your Father" vs. "my Father"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "your Father" (τοῦ πατρὸς ὑμῶν), while some manuscript traditions read "my Father." The Vulgate has "Patrem vestrum" ("your Father"), matching the SBLGNT. The Hebrew has אֲבִיכֶם ("your Father"), also matching.
- **Greek**: `ἔμπροσθεν τοῦ πατρὸς ὑμῶν τοῦ ἐν οὐρανοῖς`
- **Latin**: `ante Patrem vestrum, qui in cælis est`
- **Hebrew**: `מִלִּפְנֵי אֲבִיכֶם שֶׁבַּשָׁמָיִם`
- **Note**: All three agree on "your Father" here. The SBLGNT apparatus notes a variant reading μου ("my") in some manuscripts. No actual divergence among our three sources, but worth noting the variant exists.

### 18:15 — "Against you" — textual variant
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT includes εἰς σέ ("against you") in the critical text but marks it with textual apparatus symbols (⸂ ⸃), indicating manuscript variation. Some important manuscripts omit "against you." The Vulgate has "in te" ("against you"), and the Hebrew has לְךָ ("to/against you").
- **Greek**: `Ἐὰν δὲ ἁμαρτήσῃ ⸂εἰς σὲ⸃ ὁ ἀδελφός σου`
- **Latin**: `Si autem peccaverit in te frater tuus`
- **Hebrew**: `וְכִי יֶחֱטָא-לְךָ אָחִיךָ`
- **Note**: The presence or absence of "against you" significantly affects the scope of the discipline procedure -- whether it applies only to personal offenses or to any sin by a brother. All three of our texts include it, but the textual uncertainty is notable.

### 18:22 — "Seventy times seven" vs. "seventy-seven times"
- **Issue**: The Greek ἑβδομηκοντάκις ἑπτά is ambiguous: it can mean "seventy times seven" (= 490) or "seventy-seven times." The Vulgate has "septuagies septies" ("seventy times seven"). The Hebrew has שִׁבְעִים וָשֶׁבַע ("seventy and seven" = 77).
- **Greek**: `ἕως ἑβδομηκοντάκις ἑπτά`
- **Latin**: `usque septuagies septies`
- **Hebrew**: `עַד-שִׁבְעִים וָשֶׁבַע`
- **Note**: The Hebrew rendering "seventy-seven" more naturally echoes Genesis 4:24 (Lamech's seventy-seven-fold vengeance), which is likely the intended allusion. The Vulgate's "seventy times seven" follows the multiplicative reading. The Delitzsch Hebrew makes the Genesis 4:24 connection explicit, which may be the better interpretation of the Greek.

### 18:35 — Bracketed addition in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds `[עַל-חַטֹּאתָם]` ("for their sins") at the end of the verse, which has no basis in the Greek or Latin.
- **Greek**: `ἐὰν μὴ ἀφῆτε ἕκαστος τῷ ἀδελφῷ αὐτοῦ ἀπὸ τῶν καρδιῶν ὑμῶν`
- **Latin**: `si non remiseritis unusquisque fratri suo de cordibus vestris`
- **Hebrew**: `אִם-לֹא תִמְחֲלוּ אִישׁ לְאָחִיו בְּכָל-לְבַבְכֶם [עַל-חַטֹּאתָם]`
- **Note**: The bracketed phrase is a Delitzsch gloss, not present in either the Greek or Vulgate. It makes explicit what is implicit ("forgive... their sins"), but it is an addition to the source text. The brackets indicate Delitzsch was aware this was supplementary.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 18:6 — פֶלַח-רֶכֶב for μύλος ὀνικός
- **Hebrew**: `שֶׁיִּתָּלֶה פֶלַח-רֶכֶב עַל-צַוָּארוֹ`
- **Note**: The expression פֶלַח-רֶכֶב ("millstone piece of the rider/upper stone") is a creative construction. In biblical Hebrew, פֶּלַח is used for a millstone piece in Judges 9:53 (פֶלַח רֶכֶב, "a piece of an upper millstone"). Delitzsch's choice directly echoes this passage -- an excellent biblical Hebrew rendering of the Greek "donkey millstone" (μύλος ὀνικός).

### 18:7 — Prophetic "woe" construction
- **Hebrew**: `אוֹי לָעוֹלָם מִן-הַמִּכְשֹׁלִים`
- **Note**: The construction אוֹי + ל + subject + מִן + cause is natural prophetic Hebrew (cf. Isa 3:9, אוֹי לְנַפְשָׁם). The word מִכְשׁוֹל ("stumbling block") is good biblical Hebrew (Lev 19:14, Isa 57:14). The repetitive structure mirrors the Greek tripartite woe well.

### 18:9 — גֵּיהִנֹּם for γέεννα
- **Hebrew**: `אֶל-אֵשׁ גֵּיהִנֹּם`
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders γέεννα as גֵּיהִנֹּם, which is the natural Hebrew form underlying the Greek loan. This traces back to גֵּי בֶן-הִנֹּם / גֵּי הִנֹּם (Valley of Hinnom) in Joshua 15:8, 2 Kings 23:10, Jeremiah 7:31. The contracted form גֵּיהִנֹּם is post-biblical (Mishnaic), but since the Greek γέεννα is itself derived from this Hebrew form, the rendering is accurate and appropriate.

### 18:17 — הַקָּהָל for ἐκκλησία
- **Hebrew**: `וְהִגַּדְתָּ אֶל-הַקָּהָל`
- **Note**: Delitzsch's choice of קָהָל ("assembly, congregation") for ἐκκλησία is an excellent biblical Hebrew equivalent. In the LXX, ἐκκλησία frequently translates קָהָל (Deut 23:2-4, 1 Kgs 8:14, etc.). This avoids the anachronistic feel of עֵדָה ("congregation," more specifically the Israelite community in the wilderness) and accurately represents the sense of a gathered community with authority to adjudicate.

### 18:23 — בָּשָׂר וָדָם (flesh and blood)
- **Hebrew**: `לְמֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם שֶׁהָיָה יוֹרֵד לְחֶשְׁבּוֹן עִם-עֲבָדָיו`
- **Note**: The phrase בָּשָׂר וָדָם ("flesh and blood") to qualify the king is a Mishnaic idiom (מֶלֶךְ בָּשָׂר וָדָם, "a king of flesh and blood," as opposed to the King of Kings). This is not biblical Hebrew -- it appears in rabbinic parables as a standard formula. The Greek simply has ἀνθρώπῳ βασιλεῖ ("a man, a king"). Delitzsch is importing rabbinic parable style, which is contextually fitting for a parable but not strictly biblical.

### 18:26 — הַאֲרֶךְ-לִי אַפֶּךָ
- **Hebrew**: `הַאֲרֶךְ-לִי אַפֶּךָ`
- **Note**: The plea "be long of nose toward me" (i.e., "be patient with me") uses the biblical idiom of אֶרֶךְ אַפַּיִם ("long of nostrils / slow to anger"), one of the divine attributes (Exod 34:6, Num 14:18). The Hiphil imperative construction is creative, rendering the Greek Μακροθύμησον ("be patient") by activating a deeply biblical concept. The servant is essentially asking the master to emulate a divine attribute.

### 18:27 — וַיֶּהֱמוּ מְעֵי for σπλαγχνισθείς
- **Hebrew**: `וַיֶּהֱמוּ מְעֵי אֲדֹנֵי הָעֶבֶד`
- **Note**: Rendering σπλαγχνισθείς ("moved with compassion") as "his bowels were stirred" draws on the Hebrew biblical idiom of bowels as the seat of compassion (cf. Jer 31:20, הָמוּ מֵעַי לוֹ, "my bowels are stirred for him"; Isa 63:15). This is outstanding biblical Hebrew that captures the visceral quality of the Greek σπλάγχνα (literally "entrails") better than an abstract word for "compassion" would.

### 18:32 — עֶבֶד בְּלִיַּעַל
- **Hebrew**: `אַתָּה עֶבֶד בְּלִיַּעַל`
- **Note**: Rendering δοῦλε πονηρέ ("wicked servant") as עֶבֶד בְּלִיַּעַל ("servant of Belial/worthlessness") uses a distinctly biblical Hebrew expression. The term בְּלִיַּעַל appears throughout the Hebrew Bible (Deut 13:14, Judg 19:22, 1 Sam 2:12) meaning "worthlessness" or "wickedness." This is a strong, idiomatic choice.

## Other Notes

### 18:10 — Guardian angels
All three texts agree on the substance of verse 10 regarding guardian angels ("their angels always see the face of my Father in heaven"). This is a theologically important verse for angelology across all Christian traditions.

### 18:18 — Binding and loosing
The binding-and-loosing logion at v.18 is significant because it parallels the authority given to Peter alone in 16:19, but here extended to the community. The Hebrew renders well with אסר/התיר (bind/loose), which are the standard rabbinic terms for halakhic prohibition and permission. Delitzsch's vocabulary here would have been immediately intelligible within a Jewish legal framework.

### 18:20 — Divine presence among the gathered
The statement "where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among them" has a close rabbinic parallel in Pirqe Avot 3:2 (where the divine presence rests among those who study Torah together). The Hebrew phrasing בְכָל-מָקוֹם אֲשֶׁר יֵאָסְפוּ resonates naturally with this tradition.
