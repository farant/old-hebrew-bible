# Matthew Chapter 4 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 25 verses align across all three sources (Greek SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, Clementine Vulgate).

## Content Divergences

### 4:1 — "the devil" vs. "the Satan"
- **Issue**: The Greek has τοῦ διαβόλου (the devil, lit. "the slanderer"). The Vulgate has "a diabolo" (by the devil). Delitzsch uses הַשָּׂטָן (the Satan/Adversary), the standard Hebrew term from Job 1-2 and Zechariah 3:1-2.
- **Greek**: πειρασθῆναι ὑπὸ τοῦ διαβόλου
- **Latin**: ut tentaretur a diabolo
- **Hebrew**: לְמַעַן יְנַסֵּהוּ הַשָּׂטָן
- **Note**: This is a consistent translation choice by Delitzsch throughout the chapter — Greek διάβολος becomes הַשָּׂטָן (vv1, 5, 8, 11), while in v10 where the Greek itself has Σατανᾶ (Satana), Delitzsch also uses הַשָּׂטָן. The Hebrew Bible's הַשָּׂטָן with the definite article functions as a title ("the Adversary") in Job and Zechariah, which is appropriate here. Delitzsch consistently uses the Piel יְנַסֵּהוּ (to test him) for πειρασθῆναι (to be tempted), which in biblical Hebrew carries the sense of testing/proving rather than moral temptation (cf. Genesis 22:1, Exodus 17:2).

### 4:4 — Deuteronomy 8:3 quotation
- **Issue**: Delitzsch quotes Deuteronomy 8:3 verbatim from the MT, which has the Tetragrammaton (יְהוָֹה). The Greek has θεοῦ (God) and the Vulgate has "Dei" (God). In the original Deuteronomy context, both MT and LXX disagree — MT has YHWH, LXX has θεοῦ. Matthew's Greek follows the LXX reading.
- **Greek**: διὰ στόματος θεοῦ — "from the mouth of God"
- **Latin**: de ore Dei — "from the mouth of God"
- **Hebrew**: פִי-יְהוָֹה — "the mouth of YHWH"
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the MT of Deuteronomy rather than back-translating from Matthew's Greek. This introduces a substantive difference: the Hebrew NT reads "YHWH" where the Greek NT reads "God." This is theologically significant since the temptation narrative has Jesus quoting Torah — Delitzsch presents Jesus as quoting the Hebrew text exactly as it stands.

### 4:5 — "pinnacle of the temple" rendering
- **Issue**: The Greek τὸ πτερύγιον τοῦ ἱεροῦ (the wing/pinnacle of the temple) is rendered differently across traditions. The Vulgate has "pinnaculum templi." Delitzsch has פִּנַּת-גַּג בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ (the corner of the roof of the Temple).
- **Greek**: ἐπὶ τὸ πτερύγιον τοῦ ἱεροῦ
- **Latin**: super pinnaculum templi
- **Hebrew**: עַל-פִּנַּת-גַּג בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ
- **Note**: Delitzsch expands the phrase with three nouns: פִּנָּה (corner) + גַּג (roof) + בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ (the Temple). The Greek πτερύγιον is a diminutive of πτέρυξ (wing) and its exact architectural referent is debated. Delitzsch's expansion is interpretive — he specifies it as "the corner of the roof" — but uses good biblical vocabulary. The choice of בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ for ἱερόν (rather than הֵיכָל) is the standard biblical Hebrew designation for the Temple complex.

### 4:6 — Psalm 91:11-12 quotation
- **Issue**: The Greek omits part of Psalm 91:11 — the phrase "to guard you in all your ways" (τοῦ διαφυλάξαι σε ἐν πάσαις ταῖς ὁδοῖς σου from LXX) is absent from Matthew's quotation. The Vulgate likewise omits it. Delitzsch's Hebrew also follows Matthew in omitting this line, departing from Psalm 91:11 MT which reads לִשְׁמָרְךָ בְּכָל-דְּרָכֶיךָ.
- **Greek**: Τοῖς ἀγγέλοις αὐτοῦ ἐντελεῖται περὶ σοῦ (omits "in all your ways")
- **Latin**: angelis suis mandavit de te (omits "in all your ways")
- **Hebrew**: מַלְאָכָיו יְצַוֶּה-לָּךְ (omits לִשְׁמָרְךָ בְּכָל-דְּרָכֶיךָ)
- **Note**: All three traditions faithfully reproduce the Satan's selective quotation, which omits the qualifying phrase about God's protection being within "all your ways" — i.e., within the path of obedience. The omission may be theologically significant: the Satan drops the very words that would limit the promise's applicability to acts of faithful obedience rather than reckless testing.

### 4:10 — "Get behind me" vs. "Go away" vs. "Depart from me"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads ⸀Ὕπαγε, Σατανᾶ (Go away, Satan) with a variant marker. Some manuscripts add ὀπίσω μου (behind me), influenced by Matthew 16:23. The Vulgate has "Vade Satana" (Go, Satan) without "behind me." Delitzsch has סוּר מִמֶּנִּי הַשָּׂטָן (depart from me, Satan).
- **Greek**: ⸀Ὕπαγε, Σατανᾶ — "Go away, Satan" (variant noted)
- **Latin**: Vade Satana — "Go, Satan"
- **Hebrew**: סוּר מִמֶּנִּי הַשָּׂטָן — "Depart from me, Satan"
- **Note**: Delitzsch adds מִמֶּנִּי (from me), which is not in the Greek critical text or the Vulgate. The verb סוּר (to turn aside, depart) is a good biblical Hebrew choice (cf. Genesis 19:2, 1 Samuel 15:6), but the addition of "from me" slightly interprets rather than translates. The Greek is simply "go away" without specifying a direction relative to Jesus.

### 4:15-16 — Isaiah 9:1-2 quotation
- **Issue**: Delitzsch's Hebrew is noticeably closer to the MT of Isaiah 8:23-9:1 than to a back-translation of Matthew's Greek. Several differences emerge.
- **Greek**: ὁ λαὸς ὁ καθήμενος ἐν σκοτίᾳ — "the people sitting in darkness"
- **Latin**: populus, qui sedebat in tenebris — "the people who sat in darkness"
- **Hebrew**: הָעָם הָהֹלְכִים בַּחֹשֶׁךְ — "the people walking in darkness"
- **Note**: The Greek/Latin have "sitting" (καθήμενος / sedebat), but Delitzsch follows Isaiah MT which has הָהֹלְכִים (walking). This is a significant verb change. Similarly, the Greek has φῶς εἶδεν (saw a light), the Latin "vidit lucem" (saw a light), but the Hebrew has רָאוּ אוֹר גָּדוֹל (saw a great light) — Delitzsch follows Isaiah MT's word order. Again, Delitzsch's consistent practice is to quote the MT of the OT source rather than mechanically back-translate Matthew's Greek.

### 4:24 — Medical terminology
- **Issue**: The Greek lists various ailments: ποικίλαις νόσοις καὶ βασάνοις (various diseases and torments), δαιμονιζομένους (demon-possessed), σεληνιαζομένους (lunatics/epileptics), and παραλυτικούς (paralytics). Delitzsch renders these with biblical and post-biblical Hebrew terms.
- **Greek**: δαιμονιζομένους καὶ σεληνιαζομένους καὶ παραλυτικούς
- **Latin**: qui daemonia habebant, et lunaticos, et paralyticos
- **Hebrew**: אֲחוּזֵי שֵׁדִים וּמֻכֵּי יָרֵחַ וּנְכֵי אֵבָרִים
- **Note**: Delitzsch's renderings are creative. אֲחוּזֵי שֵׁדִים (seized by demons) uses biblical vocabulary — שֵׁדִים appears in Deuteronomy 32:17 and Psalm 106:37. מֻכֵּי יָרֵחַ (moon-struck) is a literal calque of σεληνιαζομένους, using the Hophal participle of נכה (to strike) with יָרֵחַ (moon). נְכֵי אֵבָרִים (smitten of limbs) for παραλυτικούς is an interpretive but effective rendering — the root נכה is biblical, and אֵבָרִים (limbs) appears in Job 9:26, Ezekiel 17:3.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 4:1 — "nasa haRuach"
- **Hebrew**: נָשָׂא הָרוּחַ אֶת-יֵשׁוּעַ הַמִּדְבָּרָה
- **Note**: The Greek ἀνήχθη (was led up) is passive, with τοῦ πνεύματος as agent. Delitzsch makes the Spirit the active subject: "the Spirit carried Jesus." The verb נָשָׂא (to lift, carry) is a strong choice that echoes Ezekiel's Spirit-transport language (Ezekiel 3:12, 8:3, 11:1), where the Spirit "lifts" the prophet and carries him. This creates a prophetic typological resonance. The directional suffix הַמִּדְבָּרָה (toward the wilderness) is proper biblical Hebrew locative usage.

### 4:2 — Fasting formula
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי אַחֲרֵי צוּמוֹ אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם וְאַרְבָּעִים לָיְלָה וַיִּרְעָב
- **Note**: The phrase "forty days and forty nights" echoes Moses (Exodus 34:28, Deuteronomy 9:9) and Elijah (1 Kings 19:8). Delitzsch's וַיִּרְעָב (and he was hungry) for ἐπείνασεν (he was hungry) is the same verb used of Esau in Genesis 25:29. The entire construction is natural biblical narrative Hebrew.

### 4:9 — "tiqod" for "fall down"
- **Hebrew**: אִם-תִּקֹּד וְתִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לִי
- **Note**: The Greek πεσών (falling) is rendered with תִּקֹּד (bow the head/kneel), from the root קדד which appears in Genesis 24:26, 43:28, Exodus 4:31, 12:27. Combined with תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה (and worship), this creates the classic biblical Hebrew prostration formula found in worship contexts. This is more precise than a simple "fall" (נפל) and better captures the Greek's implication of an act of worship.

### 4:10 — Deuteronomy 6:13 quotation
- **Hebrew**: לַיהוָֹה אֱלֹהֶיךָ תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה וְאוֹתוֹ לְבַדּוֹ תַּעֲבֹד
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the MT of Deuteronomy 6:13 closely but makes one change: the MT has תִּירָא (you shall fear) where Matthew's quotation (and Delitzsch's rendering) has תִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה (you shall worship). This matches the LXX of Deuteronomy (προσκυνήσεις) rather than the MT (תִּירָא). Here Delitzsch follows Matthew's Greek rather than the MT — in contrast to his usual practice — because the difference is theologically essential to the temptation narrative's argument about worship.

### 4:17 — Double infinitive construction
- **Hebrew**: הֵחֵל יֵשׁוּעַ לִקְרֹא קָרוֹא וְאָמוֹר
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the infinitive absolute construction קָרוֹא (calling out), which intensifies the verbal action. This is an acceptable biblical Hebrew construction (cf. Genesis 8:7 — הָלוֹךְ וָשׁוֹב), though the pairing with וְאָמוֹר (and saying) creates an unusually heavy infinitive chain. A simpler construction like וַיִּקְרָא וַיֹּאמַר (and he called out and said) would be more typical of biblical Hebrew narrative.

### 4:18 — Fishing vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: מַשְׁלִיכִים מְצוֹדָה בַּיָּם כִּי דַיָּגִים הֵם
- **Note**: The Greek ἀμφίβληστρον (casting net) becomes מְצוֹדָה (a net/trap). While מְצוּדָה/מְצוֹדָה in the Hebrew Bible usually refers to a hunting net or stronghold (Ecclesiastes 7:26, Ezekiel 12:13), the broader sense of "net/trap" is attested. The word דַיָּגִים (fishermen) does not appear in the Hebrew Bible — the biblical form is דַּוָּגִים (Jeremiah 16:16, Ezekiel 47:10 uses דַּיָּגִים in some manuscripts). Delitzsch's form may reflect later Hebrew influence.

## Other Notes

### 4:3, 6 — "If you are the Son of God"
The Greek εἰ υἱὸς εἶ τοῦ θεοῦ uses a first-class conditional (εἰ + indicative), which assumes the condition is true: "Since you are the Son of God." The Hebrew אִם בֶּן-הָאֱלֹהִים אַתָּה uses אִם, which in biblical Hebrew is typically a neutral or doubtful conditional ("if"). This changes the rhetorical force of the temptation. The Latin "Si Filius Dei es" is similarly ambiguous. A Hebrew rendering with כִּי or הֲלֹא might have better captured the Greek's assumption of truth.

### 4:4, 7, 10 — Jesus' three Scripture quotations
All three of Jesus' responses come from Deuteronomy (8:3, 6:16, 6:13), all from the wilderness-testing narrative of Israel. This creates a typological structure: where Israel failed its wilderness testing, Jesus succeeds. Delitzsch's Hebrew makes this connection particularly vivid because the quotations are recognizable as Deuteronomy to a Hebrew reader. The Satan's quotation comes from Psalm 91:11-12, a psalm of divine protection — but the Satan selectively edits it (see the note on 4:6 above).

### 4:23 — "besorah" for "gospel"
- **Hebrew**: וַיְבַשֵּׂר בְּשׂוֹרַת הַמַּלְכוּת
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses בְּשׂוֹרָה (good news/tidings) for εὐαγγέλιον (gospel). This is the precise Hebrew equivalent — the root בשר in the Piel means "to bear good tidings" (Isaiah 40:9, 52:7, 61:1). The construction וַיְבַשֵּׂר בְּשׂוֹרַת is somewhat redundant (cognate accusative: "he good-newsed the good news"), but this kind of cognate construction is attested in biblical Hebrew (cf. חָלַם חֲלוֹם, "he dreamed a dream," Genesis 37:5).
