# Matthew Chapter 5 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All three sources contain 48 verses. However, the content of verses 4 and 5 is swapped between the Vulgate and the Greek/Hebrew (see below).

## Content Divergences

### 5:4-5 — Beatitudes order swapped in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reverses the order of the second and third beatitudes compared to the Greek (SBLGNT) and the Hebrew (Delitzsch). In the Greek and Hebrew, v.4 is "Blessed are those who mourn" and v.5 is "Blessed are the meek." In the Vulgate, v.4 is "Blessed are the meek" and v.5 is "Blessed are those who mourn."
- **Greek v.4**: μακάριοι οἱ πενθοῦντες, ὅτι αὐτοὶ παρακληθήσονται (those who mourn / shall be comforted)
- **Greek v.5**: μακάριοι οἱ πραεῖς, ὅτι αὐτοὶ κληρονομήσουσι τὴν γῆν (the meek / shall inherit the earth)
- **Latin v.4**: Beati mites : quoniam ipsi possidebunt terram (the meek / shall possess the earth)
- **Latin v.5**: Beati qui lugent : quoniam ipsi consolabuntur (those who mourn / shall be comforted)
- **Hebrew v.4**: אַשְׁרֵי הָאֲבֵלִים כִּי-הֵם יְנֻחָמוּ (follows Greek order: mourners)
- **Hebrew v.5**: אַשְׁרֵי הָעֲנָוִים כִּי-הֵמָּה יִירְשׁוּ אָרֶץ (follows Greek order: the meek)
- **Note**: This is a well-known variant. Many Latin manuscripts and some Greek witnesses (notably D, W, and some Old Latin) place the meek before the mourners. The Clementine Vulgate follows this older Western order. The SBLGNT and Delitzsch both follow the majority Greek manuscript tradition.

### 5:22 — "without cause" (εἰκῇ / חִנָּם)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT text omits εἰκῇ ("without cause"), reading simply "everyone who is angry with his brother." The Hebrew includes חִנָּם ("without cause"), which follows the Byzantine/TR reading. The Vulgate similarly omits the qualifier, reading simply "qui irascitur fratri suo."
- **Greek**: πᾶς ὁ ὀργιζόμενος τῷ ἀδελφῷ αὐτοῦ (no εἰκῇ in SBLGNT critical text)
- **Latin**: omnis qui irascitur fratri suo (no "sine causa")
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-אֲשֶׁר יִקְצֹף עַל-אָחִיו חִנָּם (includes חִנָּם = "without cause")
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Textus Receptus / Byzantine reading that includes "without cause." The SBLGNT and Vulgate agree in omitting it, following the Alexandrian textual tradition (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus). This is a theologically significant variant: without the qualifier, the verse condemns all anger, not merely causeless anger.

### 5:27 — "to those of old" added in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Greek and Latin for v.27 say simply "You have heard that it was said," without specifying "to those of old." The Hebrew adds לָרִאשֹׁנִים ("to the ancients/first ones"), harmonizing with the fuller formula from 5:21 and 5:33.
- **Greek**: Ἠκούσατε ὅτι ἐρρέθη (no τοῖς ἀρχαίοις)
- **Latin**: Audistis quia dictum est antiquis (includes "antiquis")
- **Hebrew**: שְׁמַעְתֶּם כִּי נֶאֱמַר לָרִאשֹׁנִים (includes לָרִאשֹׁנִים)
- **Note**: The SBLGNT omits "to those of old" in v.27, matching the shorter reading. The Vulgate and Delitzsch both include it. The Vulgate reads "antiquis" and the Hebrew reads לָרִאשֹׁנִים. This appears to be a harmonization toward the fuller formula found in vv. 21 and 33.

### 5:44 — Expanded text in Hebrew with brackets
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes bracketed expansions: בָּרֲכוּ אֶת-מְקַלְלֵיכֶם הֵיטִיבוּ לְשׂנְאֵיכֶם ("bless those who curse you, do good to those who hate you") and מַכְאִיבֵיכֶם וְ ("those who hurt you and"). The SBLGNT has the shorter critical reading. The Vulgate includes "benefacite his qui oderunt vos" ("do good to those who hate you") and "calumniantibus vos" ("those who abuse you").
- **Greek**: ἀγαπᾶτε τοὺς ἐχθροὺς ὑμῶν καὶ προσεύχεσθε ὑπὲρ τῶν διωκόντων ὑμᾶς (short reading: love enemies, pray for persecutors)
- **Latin**: diligite inimicos vestros, benefacite his qui oderunt vos, et orate pro persequentibus et calumniantibus vos (expanded: love enemies, do good to haters, pray for persecutors and slanderers)
- **Hebrew**: אֶהֱבוּ אֶת-אֹיְבֵיכֶם [בָּרֲכוּ אֶת-מְקַלְלֵיכֶם הֵיטִיבוּ לְשׂנְאֵיכֶם] וְהִתְפַּלֲלוּ בְּעַד [מַכְאִיבֵיכֶם וְ] רֹדְפֵיכֶם (brackets mark TR expansions)
- **Note**: The Delitzsch edition helpfully marks the Byzantine/TR additions with square brackets, showing awareness that they are not in the critical Greek text. The Vulgate preserves a partially expanded form. This is one of the most well-known cases of Byzantine expansion in the Sermon on the Mount, likely harmonized from Luke 6:27-28.

### 5:47 — "brothers" vs. "Gentiles" (τελῶναι)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads ἀδελφούς ("brothers") while some manuscripts read φίλους ("friends"). The Vulgate reads "fratres vestros" ("your brothers"), agreeing with the SBLGNT. The Hebrew reads אֲחֵיכֶם ("your brothers"), agreeing. However, the Greek says ἐθνικοί ("Gentiles") will do the same, while the Hebrew says הַמֹּכְסִים ("tax collectors"), harmonizing v.47 with v.46. The Vulgate correctly reads "ethnici" ("Gentiles").
- **Greek**: οὐχὶ καὶ οἱ ἐθνικοὶ τὸ αὐτὸ ποιοῦσιν; (the Gentiles)
- **Latin**: nonne et ethnici hoc faciunt? (the Gentiles)
- **Hebrew**: הֲלֹא גַם-הַמֹּכְסִים יַעֲשׂוֹּ-זֹאת (the tax collectors)
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows a Byzantine variant (or possibly harmonizes with v.46) by reading "tax collectors" (מֹּכְסִים) instead of "Gentiles" (גּוֹיִם) in v.47. The SBLGNT and Vulgate agree on "Gentiles." The Byzantine text tends to read τελῶναι (tax collectors) here, harmonizing with v.46.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 5:1 — Excellent biblical Hebrew narrative style
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי כִּרְאוֹתוֹ אֶת-הֲמוֹן הָעָם וַיַּעַל הָהָרָה וַיֵּשֶׁב שָׁם וַיִּגְּשׁוּ אֵלָיו תַּלְמִידָיו
- **Note**: Delitzsch opens with the classic wayyiqtol narrative chain (וַיְהִי...וַיַּעַל...וַיֵּשֶׁב...וַיִּגְּשׁוּ), producing natural biblical Hebrew prose. The directional he on הָהָרָה ("toward the mountain") is an authentic biblical Hebrew feature. תַּלְמִידָיו ("his disciples") is a post-biblical word borrowed from rabbinic Hebrew, but this is unavoidable since there is no precise biblical Hebrew equivalent for μαθηταί.

### 5:3 — עֲנִיֵּי הָרוּחַ for "poor in spirit"
- **Hebrew**: אַשְׁרֵי עֲנִיֵּי הָרוּחַ
- **Note**: Delitzsch's עֲנִיֵּי הָרוּחַ ("poor of spirit") is a strong choice, closely paralleling the Dead Sea Scrolls' ענוי רוח (1QM 14:7) and the biblical construct pattern. The אַשְׁרֵי formula mirrors the Psalms (Ps 1:1, 32:1, etc.), giving the Beatitudes a natural Hebrew Wisdom texture.

### 5:9 — רֹדְפֵי שָׁלוֹם for "peacemakers"
- **Hebrew**: אַשְׁרֵי רֹדְפֵי שָׁלוֹם
- **Note**: The Greek εἰρηνοποιοί is a compound noun ("peace-makers"). Delitzsch renders this as רֹדְפֵי שָׁלוֹם ("pursuers of peace"), drawing on rabbinic idiom (cf. Avot 1:12, Hillel's "be of the disciples of Aaron, loving peace and pursuing peace"). While this is more rabbinic than strictly biblical, the construct is grammatically biblical, and there is no exact biblical Hebrew equivalent for the Greek compound.

### 5:13 — Salt and seasoning vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: וְאִם-הַמֶּלַח הָיָה תָפֵל בַּמֶּה יָמְלָח
- **Note**: תָפֵל for μωρανθῇ ("becomes foolish/tasteless") is well chosen. In biblical Hebrew, תָּפֵל means "unseasoned, insipid" (Job 6:6, Lam 2:14), which captures the double meaning of the Greek μωραίνω (to make foolish / to make tasteless) through a single biblical word. This is one of Delitzsch's more elegant renderings.

### 5:18 — יוֹד and קוֹץ for iota and serif
- **Hebrew**: לֹא תַּעֲבֹר יוֹד אַחַת אוֹ-קוֹץ אֶחָד מִן-הַתּוֹרָה
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses יוֹד (the letter yod, the smallest Hebrew letter) and קוֹץ ("thorn/point," referring to a scribal serif) to translate ἰῶτα and κεραία. This is a culturally appropriate adaptation: in the Greek context, iota is the smallest letter; in Hebrew, yod fills that role. The word קוֹץ for the serif/tittle is drawn from rabbinic terminology for scribal ornamentation on letters.

### 5:22 — סַנְהֶדְרִין as loanword
- **Hebrew**: חַיָּב הוּא לְסַנְהֶדְרִין
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the Aramaic-Greek loanword סַנְהֶדְרִין (Sanhedrin) for συνεδρίῳ. While this is not biblical Hebrew vocabulary (the institution postdates the biblical period), it is the natural Hebrew/Aramaic term and Delitzsch had no genuine biblical alternative. The word בֵּית דִּין ("house of judgment") earlier in the verse for κρίσει is more natively Semitic.

### 5:26 — פְּרוּטָה for κοδράντην (quadrans)
- **Hebrew**: עַד אִם-שִׁלַּמְתָּ אֶת-הַפְּרוּטָה הָאַחֲרוֹנָה
- **Note**: The Greek κοδράντης (quadrans) is a Roman coin. The Vulgate preserves the Latin "quadrantem." Delitzsch uses פְּרוּטָה, a post-biblical rabbinic term for the smallest coin denomination. A more biblical rendering might have used something like גֵּרָה (the smallest biblical weight, Exod 30:13), but פְּרוּטָה communicates the sense clearly to a Hebrew reader.

### 5:29-30 — גּוּף for "body"
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-גּוּפְךָ (both verses)
- **Note**: גּוּף ("body") is post-biblical / mishnaic Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would more naturally use בָּשָׂר ("flesh") or possibly שְׁאֵר ("flesh/body," Lev 18:6). However, the Greek σῶμα means "body" as a whole (not merely flesh), so Delitzsch's choice, while not strictly biblical in register, avoids the ambiguity of בָּשָׂר.

### 5:48 — שְׁלֵמִים / שָׁלֵם for τέλειοι
- **Hebrew**: הֱיוּ שְׁלֵמִים כַּאֲשֶׁר אֲבִיכֶם שֶׁבַּשָׁמַיִם שָׁלֵם הוּא
- **Note**: Excellent choice. The Greek τέλειος means "perfect, complete, whole." The Hebrew שָׁלֵם carries exactly this range of meaning in biblical Hebrew: "whole, complete, having integrity" (cf. Gen 20:5 בְּתָם-לְבָבִי "in the integrity of my heart"; 1 Kgs 8:61 לֵבָב שָׁלֵם "a whole heart"). This is one of the most successful semantic mappings in the chapter.

## Other Notes

### 5:17 — Torah and Prophets
- The phrase הַתּוֹרָה...דִּבְרֵי הַנְּבִיאִים directly mirrors the standard Jewish designation for the first two divisions of the Hebrew canon. Delitzsch adds דִּבְרֵי ("words of") before הַנְּבִיאִים, which is slightly expansive compared to the Greek τοὺς προφήτας but produces more natural Hebrew.

### 5:21-48 — The Antitheses structure
- Delitzsch's rendering of the antithetical formula is consistent throughout: שְׁמַעְתֶּם כִּי נֶאֱמַר...וַאֲנִי אֹמֵר לָכֶם. The shift from נֶאֱמַר (niphal passive, "it was said") to אֹמֵר (active participle, "I say") effectively captures the rhetorical force of Jesus claiming direct authority over against received tradition. The Hebrew participle אֹמֵר conveys habitual/present action, matching the Greek present tense λέγω.
