# Matthew Chapter 7 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All three sources contain 29 verses. Verse numbering aligns across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 7:13-14 — "gate" vs. "door" and structural differences
- **Issue**: The Vulgate in v.14 reads "Quam angusta porta, et arcta via est" ("How narrow the gate and how confined the way"), using the exclamatory "Quam" and treating it as a single sentence. The SBLGNT v.14 begins with ὅτι ("because/that"), reading the verse as a continuation or explanation. The Hebrew begins with וְ ("and"), treating it as a simple continuation.
- **Greek v.14**: ὅτι στενὴ ἡ πύλη... (that/because narrow is the gate)
- **Latin v.14**: Quam angusta porta... (How narrow the gate!)
- **Hebrew v.14**: וְצַר הַפֶּתַח... (and narrow is the entrance)
- **Note**: The SBLGNT critical apparatus notes a variant: some manuscripts read τί ("how!" — exclamatory, like the Vulgate) instead of ὅτι ("because"). The Vulgate appears to follow this variant reading. Delitzsch follows the ὅτι reading with a simple conjunctive ו.

### 7:14 — πύλη / porta / פֶּתַח
- **Issue**: In both v.13 and v.14, Delitzsch uses פֶּתַח ("entrance, opening, doorway") for the Greek πύλη ("gate"). The Vulgate uses "porta" ("gate"). In biblical Hebrew, שַׁעַר is the standard word for "gate" (especially city gates), while פֶּתַח means "opening, entrance, doorway."
- **Hebrew**: וְצַר הַפֶּתַח
- **Note**: The choice of פֶּתַח over שַׁעַר may be deliberate. A שַׁעַר in biblical Hebrew evokes a large city gate, while πύλη in this context seems to be a smaller entrance. פֶּתַח, used for tent openings and doorways throughout the Pentateuch (Gen 18:1, 19:11), conveys a more intimate, smaller entryway, which fits the metaphor of the "narrow" entrance better.

### 7:22 — νίφλָאוֹת vs. δυνάμεις
- **Issue**: The Greek δυνάμεις πολλάς means "many mighty works / miracles." The Latin reads "virtutes multas" ("many powers/virtues"). The Hebrew reads נִפְלָאוֹת רַבּוֹת ("many wonders/marvels").
- **Greek**: τῷ σῷ ὀνόματι δυνάμεις πολλὰς ἐποιήσαμεν
- **Latin**: in nomine tuo virtutes multas fecimus
- **Hebrew**: וּבְשִׁמְךָ עָשִׂינוּ נִפְלָאוֹת רַבּוֹת
- **Note**: While δυνάμεις and "virtutes" emphasize power, Delitzsch's נִפְלָאוֹת emphasizes the wondrous character of the deeds. This is a biblically rich word (Exod 3:20, Ps 77:15, Dan 12:6) and is the standard Hebrew Bible term for God's mighty acts. The semantic shift is minor but worth noting: the Greek stresses power, the Hebrew stresses wonder.

### 7:29 — Vulgate adds "et pharisaei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "et pharisæi" ("and Pharisees") at the end of v.29. The SBLGNT reads only γραμματεῖς αὐτῶν ("their scribes"). The Hebrew reads only כַּסּוֹפְרִים ("like the scribes") without Pharisees.
- **Greek**: οὐχ ὡς οἱ γραμματεῖς αὐτῶν (not as their scribes)
- **Latin**: non sicut scribæ eorum, et pharisæi (not as their scribes and Pharisees)
- **Hebrew**: וְלֹא כַּסּוֹפְרִים (and not like the scribes)
- **Note**: The addition of "and Pharisees" in the Vulgate reflects a variant found in some Greek manuscripts and may be a harmonization with other passages where scribes and Pharisees appear together (e.g., Matt 23). The SBLGNT and Delitzsch agree on the shorter reading.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 7:2 — Measure-for-measure parallelism
- **Hebrew**: כִּי בַמִּשְׁפָּט אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם שֹׁפְטִים תִּשָׁפֵטוּ וּבַמִּדָּה אֲשֶׁר אַתֶּם מֹדֲדִים יִמַּד לָכֶם
- **Note**: This is an outstanding rendering. The root repetition (שֹׁפְטִים / תִּשָׁפֵטוּ and מֹדֲדִים / יִמַּד) mirrors both the Greek polyptoton (κρίματι κρίνετε κριθήσεσθε, μέτρῳ μετρεῖτε μετρηθήσεται) and the well-known rabbinic principle of middah k'neged middah ("measure for measure"). The Hebrew reads as if it could be a genuine wisdom saying from the biblical tradition.

### 7:3-5 — קֵיסָם and קוֹרָה for speck and beam
- **Hebrew**: הַקֵּיסָם (splinter/straw) and הַקּוֹרָה (beam/rafter)
- **Note**: קֵיסָם is a post-biblical word (found in the Talmud). Biblical Hebrew has no exact equivalent for κάρφος ("speck, chip, straw"). The word קוֹרָה ("beam, rafter") is biblical (1 Kgs 6:9, 7:12) and well chosen. The pairing works effectively, even though קֵיסָם is technically rabbinic.

### 7:5 — הֶחָנֵף for ὑποκριτά ("hypocrite")
- **Hebrew**: הֶחָנֵף
- **Note**: Delitzsch consistently renders ὑποκριτής with חָנֵף throughout Matthew. The biblical חָנֵף means "godless, profane, polluted" (Job 8:13, 13:16, 15:34; Isa 9:16, 10:6), which is not a precise equivalent for the Greek ὑποκριτής ("actor, pretender, hypocrite"). The Greek word specifically denotes pretense and play-acting, while the Hebrew connotes moral pollution and godlessness. However, there is no biblical Hebrew word that precisely captures theatrical hypocrisy, and חָנֵף is the closest available term for religious falseness.

### 7:6 — פְנִינֵיכֶם for μαργαρίτας ("pearls")
- **Hebrew**: וְאַל-תַּשְׁלִיכוּ פְנִינֵיכֶם לִפְנֵי הַחֲזִירִים
- **Note**: The Greek μαργαρίτας ("pearls") is rendered with פְּנִינִים. In biblical Hebrew, פְּנִינִים appears in Proverbs 3:15, 8:11, 20:15, 31:10 and Lamentations 4:7, usually translated as "corals" or "rubies" or "pearls" (the exact referent is debated). Delitzsch's choice creates an alliterative effect with לִפְנֵי ("before"), which may be intentional.

### 7:12 — The Golden Rule
- **Hebrew**: כֹּל אֲשֶׁר תִּרְצוּ שֶׁיַּעֲשׂוֹּ לָכֶם בְּנֵי הָאָדָם עֲשׂוֹּ לָהֶם גַּם-אַתֶּם כִּי-זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה וְהַנְּבִיאִים
- **Note**: The construction is somewhat awkward. The שֶׁ- relative clause (שֶׁיַּעֲשׂוֹּ) is more mishnaic than biblical. Biblical Hebrew might prefer כַּאֲשֶׁר תִּרְצוּ אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשׂוּ or a כְּ...כֵּן pattern. The concluding כִּי-זֹאת הַתּוֹרָה וְהַנְּבִיאִים effectively summarizes the connection to Jewish ethical tradition.

### 7:23 — Psalm 6:9 echo
- **Hebrew**: סוּרוּ מִמֶּנִּי פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן
- **Note**: This is a direct quotation of Psalm 6:9 (סוּרוּ מִמֶּנִּי כָּל-פֹּעֲלֵי אָוֶן), only omitting כָּל ("all"). The Greek ἀποχωρεῖτε ἀπʼ ἐμοῦ οἱ ἐργαζόμενοι τὴν ἀνομίαν is itself quoting the LXX of Ps 6:9. Delitzsch correctly renders back to the original Hebrew Psalm, which is the right approach for a Hebrew New Testament.

### 7:24-27 — Parable of the builders
- **Hebrew**: צּוּר (rock) and חוֹל (sand)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses צּוּר ("rock, cliff") for πέτραν and חוֹל ("sand") for ἄμμον. Both are excellent biblical words (צּוּר: Deut 32:4, Ps 18:3, etc.; חוֹל: Gen 22:17, 32:13). The narrative style with wayyiqtol chains (וַיֵּרֶד...וַיִּשְׁטְפוּ...וַיְנַשְׁבוּ) gives the parable a thoroughly biblical Hebrew narrative texture.

### 7:28 — תּוֹרָתוֹ for διδαχῇ ("teaching")
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּשְׁתּוֹמֵם הֲמוֹן הָעָם עַל-תּוֹרָתוֹ
- **Note**: Rendering διδαχή ("teaching, doctrine") as תּוֹרָה ("Torah/instruction") is a theologically loaded choice. In biblical Hebrew, תּוֹרָה is the standard word for authoritative divine instruction (not merely "teaching" in a general sense). By using תּוֹרָתוֹ ("his Torah"), Delitzsch implicitly elevates Jesus' teaching to the level of divine instruction, which fits Matthew's portrayal of Jesus as a new Moses giving Torah from a mountain. The Latin "doctrina" is more neutral.

## Other Notes

### 7:15 — נְּבִיאֵי הַשָׁקֶר for "false prophets"
- The Hebrew נְּבִיאֵי הַשָׁקֶר ("prophets of falsehood") echoes Jeremiah's language about false prophets (Jer 14:14, 23:25-26, where שֶׁקֶר is repeatedly associated with false prophecy). This creates a strong intertextual connection that the Greek ψευδοπροφητῶν, being a single compound word, does not achieve as directly.

### 7:24-27 — Structural note
- The Hebrew parable achieves strong literary parallelism between the two builders. The phrases are closely mirrored: אֲשֶׁר-בָּנָה אֶת-בֵּיתוֹ עַל-הַצּוּר / אֲשֶׁר-בָּנָה אֶת-בֵּיתוֹ עַל-הַחוֹל, and the storm description is nearly identical, with only the final verb and outcome differing (וְלֹא נָפָל / וַיִפֹּל). This tight parallelism is characteristic of biblical Hebrew parable/mashal style.
