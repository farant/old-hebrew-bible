# Matthew Chapter 19 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 30 verses align across all three sources in numbering. No verses are omitted or empty in the SBLGNT for this chapter.

## Content Divergences

### 19:9 — Bracketed clause about marrying the divorced woman
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT includes the clause "and he who marries a divorced woman commits adultery" within textual apparatus marks (⸂ ⸃), indicating significant manuscript variation. The Hebrew includes this clause in brackets. The Vulgate includes it without qualification.
- **Greek**: `καὶ γαμήσῃ ἄλλην μοιχᾶται ⸂καὶ ὁ ἀπολελυμένην γαμήσας μοιχᾶται⸃`
- **Latin**: `et aliam duxerit, moechatur : et qui dimissam duxerit, moechatur`
- **Hebrew**: `וְנֹשֵׂא אַחֶרֶת נֹאֵף הוּא [וְהַנֹּשֵׂא אֶת-הַגְּרוּשָׁה נֹאֵף הוּא]`
- **Note**: The second clause (about marrying a divorced woman) is omitted by important early manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and others). It is likely a scribal harmonization from Matt 5:32 or Luke 16:18. Delitzsch's brackets accurately flag the textual uncertainty. The Vulgate preserves the longer reading of the Byzantine tradition.

### 19:16-17 — "Good Teacher" vs. "Teacher" and the question about goodness
- **Issue**: This is one of the most significant textual divergences between the SBLGNT and the Vulgate in this chapter. The Greek addresses Jesus as Διδάσκαλε ("Teacher") and asks τί ἀγαθὸν ποιήσω ("what good thing shall I do?"). The Vulgate has "Magister bone" ("Good Teacher") and asks "quid boni faciam" ("what good shall I do?"). The Hebrew has רַבִּי [הַטּוֹב] ("Rabbi [the good]"), with brackets around "the good."
- **Greek (v.16)**: `Διδάσκαλε, τί ἀγαθὸν ποιήσω` ("Teacher, what good [thing] shall I do?")
- **Latin (v.16)**: `Magister bone, quid boni faciam` ("Good Teacher, what good shall I do?")
- **Hebrew (v.16)**: `רַבִּי [הַטּוֹב] מַה הַטּוֹב אֲשֶׁר אֶעֱשֶׂה` ("Rabbi [the good], what is the good that I shall do?")
- **Greek (v.17)**: `Τί με ἐρωτᾷς περὶ τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ; εἷς ἐστιν ὁ ἀγαθός` ("Why do you ask me about the good? One is the good.")
- **Latin (v.17)**: `Quid me interrogas de bono? Unus est bonus, Deus` ("Why do you ask me about the good? One is good, God.")
- **Hebrew (v.17)**: `מָה-זֶּה תִּשְׁאָלֵנִי עַל-הַטּוֹב אֵין-טוֹב כִּי אִם-אֶחָד וְהוּא הָאֱלֹהִים`
- **Note**: The Markan/Lukan parallels have "Good Teacher... why do you call me good? No one is good but God alone." The SBLGNT of Matthew redirects: the man asks about "the good thing" and Jesus replies about "the Good One." This is a theologically significant variant. The Vulgate's "Magister bone" follows the harmonized reading found in later manuscripts. Delitzsch navigates this by including הַטּוֹב in brackets, honestly reflecting the textual situation. His response at v.17 follows the SBLGNT reading ("why do you ask me about the good?").

### 19:20 — "From my youth" — addition in Hebrew and Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Greek SBLGNT does not include "from my youth" (ἀπὸ νεότητός μου). The Vulgate has "a juventute mea" ("from my youth"). The Hebrew has מִנְּעוּרָי ("from my youth").
- **Greek**: `Πάντα ταῦτα ἐφύλαξα· τί ἔτι ὑστερῶ;` ("All these I have kept; what do I still lack?")
- **Latin**: `Omnia haec custodivi a juventute mea : quid adhuc mihi deest?`
- **Hebrew**: `אֶת-כָּל-אֵלֶּה שָׁמַרְתִּי מִנְּעוּרָי וּמֶה חָסַרְתִּי עוֹד`
- **Note**: "From my youth" is present in the Markan parallel (Mark 10:20) and in many later Greek manuscripts of Matthew, but absent from the earliest witnesses. Both the Vulgate and Delitzsch include it -- the Vulgate from its Vorlage, Delitzsch presumably from the Textus Receptus or by harmonization with Mark. Notably, Delitzsch does not bracket this addition, unlike his careful bracketing elsewhere.

### 19:29 — "Wife" in the list of things left behind
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT apparatus marks γυναῖκα ("wife") with variant markers. Some manuscripts include "wife" in the list of what one might leave behind for Jesus's name. The Vulgate includes "uxorem" ("wife"). The Hebrew includes וְאֵשֶׁת ("and a wife").
- **Greek**: `ἀφῆκεν οἰκίας ἢ ἀδελφοὺς ἢ ἀδελφὰς ἢ πατέρα ἢ μητέρα ἢ ⸂γυναῖκα ἢ⸃ τέκνα`
- **Latin**: `reliquerit domum, vel fratres, aut sorores, aut patrem, aut matrem, aut uxorem, aut filios`
- **Hebrew**: `אֲשֶׁר עָזַב בָּתִּים וְאַחִים וַאֲחָיוֹת וְאָב וָאֵם וְאֵשֶׁת וּבָנִים`
- **Note**: The inclusion of "wife" is textually uncertain. Luke 18:29 includes it, and it may be a harmonization. All three of our texts include it, but the SBLGNT flags the uncertainty.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 19:1 — Excellent narrative transition
- **Hebrew**: `וַיְהִי כְּכַלּוֹת יֵשׁוּעַ לְדַבֵּר אֵת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה`
- **Note**: The construction וַיְהִי כְּ + infinitive construct is classic biblical narrative formula (cf. Josh 3:14, 1 Sam 18:1). This is the standard way Matthew's discourse-ending formula (Καὶ ἐγένετο ὅτε ἐτέλεσεν) should sound in biblical Hebrew. Delitzsch executes this perfectly.

### 19:4 — הַיֹּצֵר for ὁ κτίσας
- **Hebrew**: `הַיֹּצֵר זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בָּרָא אֹתָם`
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders "the one who created" (ὁ κτίσας) with הַיֹּצֵר ("the Former/Maker"), a participle from the root יצר used of God's creative activity in Genesis 2:7-8, Isaiah 43:1, 44:2. The switch to בָּרָא ("created") in the second clause directly quotes Genesis 1:27 (זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בָּרָא אֹתָם). This dual use of creation vocabulary is skillful: the participle identifies the Creator, and the direct quote preserves the Genesis wording exactly.

### 19:5 — Direct Genesis quotation
- **Hebrew**: `עַל-כֵּן יַעֲזָב-אִישׁ אֶת-אָבִיו וְאֶת-אִמּוֹ וְדָבַק בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ וְהָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד`
- **Note**: This is a nearly verbatim quotation of Genesis 2:24. The Masoretic text reads: `עַל-כֵּן יַעֲזָב-אִישׁ אֶת-אָבִיו וְאֶת-אִמּוֹ וְדָבַק בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ וְהָיוּ לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד`. Delitzsch adds שְׁנֵיהֶם ("the two of them") to match the Greek οἱ δύο, which follows the LXX addition not present in the MT. This is a correct decision for translating the Greek NT.

### 19:6 — Clean biblical legal language
- **Hebrew**: `אֵת אֲשֶׁר חִבֵּר הָאֱלֹהִים אַל-יַפְרֵד הָאָדָם`
- **Note**: The Piel of חבר ("to join, couple") and the Hiphil of פרד ("to separate") form a natural antithetic pair in biblical Hebrew. The use of הָאֱלֹהִים... הָאָדָם ("God... man") creates a powerful universal contrast. The jussive אַל-יַפְרֵד is the correct negative command form. This is clean, authoritative biblical Hebrew.

### 19:7 — סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת
- **Hebrew**: `לָתֶת-לָהּ סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת`
- **Note**: The phrase סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת ("document of cutting off," i.e., certificate of divorce) is directly from Deuteronomy 24:1,3 (סֵפֶר כְּרִיתֻת). Delitzsch uses the exact biblical phrase, which is the source of both the Greek βιβλίον ἀποστασίου and the Latin libellum repudii. Perfect.

### 19:8 — קְשִׁי לְבַבְכֶם for σκληροκαρδίαν
- **Hebrew**: `מִפְּנֵי קְשִׁי לְבַבְכֶם`
- **Note**: The noun construction קְשִׁי לֵבָב ("hardness of heart") is not directly attested in this form in the Hebrew Bible, but the adjective + noun form קְשֵׁה-לֵב / קְשֵׁה-עֹרֶף is well established (Ezek 3:7, Exod 32:9). The abstract noun קְשִׁי appears in Deuteronomy 9:27 (קְשִׁי הָעָם הַזֶּה, "the stubbornness of this people"). Delitzsch's formulation is plausible biblical Hebrew.

### 19:12 — סָרִיס for εὐνοῦχος
- **Hebrew**: `יֵשׁ סָרִיסִים... וְיֵשׁ סָרִיסִים... וְיֵשׁ סָרִיסִים`
- **Note**: The word סָרִיס ("eunuch/court official") is well-established biblical Hebrew (Gen 37:36, 2 Kgs 20:18, Isa 56:3-4, Jer 29:2). The threefold repetition with וְיֵשׁ... וְיֵשׁ creates a dignified, biblical-sounding list structure. Delitzsch's Hebrew for this difficult passage reads very naturally.

### 19:24 — Camel and needle
- **Hebrew**: `נָקֵל לַגָּמָל לַעֲבֹר בְּנֶקֶב הַמַּחַט`
- **Note**: גָּמָל ("camel") is pure biblical Hebrew (Gen 12:16, 24:10, etc.). The word מַחַט ("needle") appears to be more rabbinic than biblical; the biblical corpus uses מַחַט only at Judges 16:14 (where it refers to a weaving implement, not a sewing needle). The construction נֶקֶב הַמַּחַט ("eye of the needle") with נֶקֶב ("hole, perforation") is adequate -- נֶקֶב appears in 2 Kings 12:10 for a hole bored in a chest lid.

### 19:28 — בְּהִתְחַדֵּשׁ הַבְּרִיאָה for παλιγγενεσία
- **Hebrew**: `בְּהִתְחַדֵּשׁ הַבְּרִיאָה`
- **Note**: The Greek παλιγγενεσία ("regeneration, renewal of all things") is a theologically loaded term. Delitzsch renders it as "when the creation is renewed" -- the Hithpael infinitive of חדשׁ ("to renew") with הַבְּרִיאָה ("the creation"). While בְּרִיאָה is a rabbinic term (not found in the Hebrew Bible, which uses בְּרִיָּה or simply the verb בָּרָא), the concept of cosmic renewal resonates with Isaiah 65:17 (שָׁמַיִם חֲדָשִׁים וָאָרֶץ חֲדָשָׁה). A more strictly biblical phrasing might have used something like בְּחַדֵּשׁ-אֵל אֶת-הַכֹּל, but Delitzsch's rendering is clear and functional.

## Other Notes

### 19:3-9 — Divorce discourse and OT quotation
The entire divorce pericope is rich in direct Old Testament quotation. Jesus cites Genesis 1:27 (v.4), Genesis 2:24 (v.5), and the discussion references Deuteronomy 24:1 (v.7). Delitzsch's Hebrew is at its strongest in these verses because he can draw directly on the source texts. The Hebrew of this passage would be almost entirely intelligible to a reader of the Pentateuch alone.

### 19:16-22 — The rich young ruler
The textual situation at vv.16-17 is theologically significant. The Matthean redaction (as preserved in SBLGNT) avoids the implication that Jesus denies being good, by redirecting the question from "Good Teacher" to "what good thing." The Vulgate preserves the more difficult Markan reading ("Good Teacher / no one is good but God"), creating a theological difference between the Latin and Greek traditions of this passage.

### 19:28 — Twelve thrones for twelve tribes
The promise that the Twelve will sit on twelve thrones judging the twelve tribes of Israel is unique to Matthew (cf. Luke 22:30 in a different context). The Hebrew rendering of this verse with its emphasis on כִּסְאוֹת ("thrones") and שִׁבְטֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל ("tribes of Israel") reads naturally within the biblical framework of Israel's tribal governance.
