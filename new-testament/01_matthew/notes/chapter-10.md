# Matthew Chapter 10 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 42 verses align across all three sources. However, there is a noteworthy structural issue: the Vulgate splits the apostle list across verses 2-4 differently from the Greek paragraphing, placing "Jacobus Zebedæi, et Joannes frater ejus" at the start of verse 3, where in the Greek these names belong to the end of verse 2. The verse numbering itself is consistent, but the content boundary within vv. 2-3 shifts.

### 10:2-3 — Apostle list verse boundary
- **Issue**: In the SBLGNT, verse 2 ends with "Ἰάκωβος ὁ τοῦ Ζεβεδαίου καὶ Ἰωάννης ὁ ἀδελφὸς αὐτοῦ" (James son of Zebedee and John his brother), and verse 3 begins with Philip. In the Vulgate, the verse 2 entry ends with "et Andreas frater ejus" and verse 3 begins with "Jacobus Zebedæi, et Joannes frater ejus, Philippus..." The Hebrew follows the Greek ordering.
- **Note**: This is a minor versification discrepancy in how the twelve names are distributed across verse boundaries. It does not affect content.

## Content Divergences

### 10:3 — Lebbaeus / Thaddaeus variants
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads simply Θαδδαῖος ("Thaddaeus"). The Vulgate also reads simply "Thaddæus." But Delitzsch includes the bracketed addition [לַבַּי הַמְכֻנֶּה] תַדָּי ("[Lebbaeus who is surnamed] Thaddaeus"), reflecting the Byzantine/TR conflated reading.
- **Greek**: Θαδδαῖος
- **Latin**: Thaddæus
- **Hebrew**: [לַבַּי הַמְכֻנֶּה] תַדָּי
- **Note**: The brackets indicate Delitzsch knew this was textually uncertain. The conflated reading "Lebbaeus who is surnamed Thaddaeus" is found in the Textus Receptus but not the critical text. This is a significant textual variant affecting apostle identification.

### 10:4 — Κaναναῖος vs קַנָּאִי
- **Issue**: The Greek Καναναῖος (from Aramaic qan'ana, "zealous one") is rendered by Delitzsch as הַקַּנָּאִי ("the Zealot"), which interprets the name rather than transliterating it. The Vulgate transliterates as "Chananæus."
- **Greek**: Σίμων ὁ Καναναῖος
- **Latin**: Simon Chananæus
- **Hebrew**: שִׁמְעוֹן הַקַּנָּאִי
- **Note**: Delitzsch's rendering is interpretive but accurate — the Aramaic qan'ana does mean "zealot." Luke 6:15 and Acts 1:13 use ζηλωτής explicitly. This is a translation choice rather than a textual divergence.

### 10:8 — Word order of miracles
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT order is "heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse lepers, cast out demons." Delitzsch reorders to "heal the sick, cleanse lepers, raise the dead, cast out demons." The Vulgate order is "heal sick, raise dead, cleanse lepers, cast out demons" (matching SBLGNT).
- **Greek**: ἀσθενοῦντας θεραπεύετε, νεκροὺς ἐγείρετε, λεπροὺς καθαρίζετε, δαιμόνια ἐκβάλλετε
- **Latin**: Infirmos curate, mortuos suscitate, leprosos mundate, dæmones ejicite
- **Hebrew**: רִפְאוּ אֵת הַחוֹלִים טַהֲרוּ אֵת הַמְּצֹרָעִים הָקִימוּ אֵת הַמֵּתִים וְאֵת הַשֵׁדִים גָּרֵשׁוּ
- **Note**: The SBLGNT itself has a textual note on the order of "νεκροὺς ἐγείρετε" (the variant marker ⸂⸃), reflecting manuscript variation. Some manuscripts place "raise the dead" after "cleanse lepers," which is the order Delitzsch follows. This is a known textual variant.

### 10:10 — "Food" vs "wages" / "sustenance"
- **Issue**: The Greek τροφῆς means "food/nourishment." The Vulgate has "cibo" (food), matching exactly. Delitzsch renders as דֵּי מִחְיָתוֹ ("sufficient for his sustenance/livelihood"), which is broader than mere "food."
- **Greek**: ἄξιος γὰρ ὁ ἐργάτης τῆς τροφῆς αὐτοῦ
- **Latin**: dignus enim est operarius cibo suo
- **Hebrew**: כִּי-רָאוּי לַפֹּעֵל דֵּי מִחְיָתוֹ
- **Note**: מִחְיָה means "sustenance, livelihood, means of living" — a broader concept than Greek τροφή (food). This shifts the meaning slightly from "the worker deserves his food" to "the worker deserves his livelihood." Cf. the parallel in Luke 10:7 which uses μισθός (wages) instead of τροφή.

### 10:12 — Vulgate addition "dicentes: Pax huic domui"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "dicentes: Pax huic domui" ("saying: Peace to this house"), which is absent from the Greek and Hebrew. This appears to be a harmonization with Luke 10:5.
- **Greek**: εἰσερχόμενοι δὲ εἰς τὴν οἰκίαν ἀσπάσασθε αὐτήν
- **Latin**: Intrantes autem in domum, salutate eam, dicentes : Pax huic domui
- **Hebrew**: וּבְבוֹאֲכֶם אֶל-הַבָּיִת שַׁאֲלוּ-לוֹ לְשָׁלוֹם
- **Note**: The Vulgate explicitly spells out the greeting formula from Luke's parallel, while the Greek simply says "greet it." The Hebrew שַׁאֲלוּ-לוֹ לְשָׁלוֹם ("inquire of its peace") is a standard biblical Hebrew idiom for greeting (cf. 1 Sam 25:5, Judg 18:15) and does not include the Lukan addition.

### 10:25 — Βεελζεβούλ / בַעַל-זְבוּל
- **Issue**: The Greek has Βεελζεβούλ, which the Vulgate renders as "Beelzebub" (with final -b, reflecting the 2 Kings 1:2 form בַּעַל זְבוּב "Lord of Flies"). Delitzsch writes בַעַל-זְבוּל ("Lord of the Dwelling/Temple"), which is closer to the Greek form and likely the more original Aramaic meaning.
- **Greek**: Βεελζεβοὺλ
- **Latin**: Beelzebub
- **Hebrew**: בַעַל-זְבוּל
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Beelzebub" with -b reflects a long Latin/Western tradition going back to the OT pun in 2 Kings 1:2 (Lord of Flies). Delitzsch correctly follows the Greek -λ ending, which likely derives from Aramaic/Hebrew זְבוּל ("dwelling, exalted abode"). This is a case where the Hebrew and Greek agree against the Vulgate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 10:1 — שָׁלְטָן for "authority"
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּתֵּן לָהֶם שָׁלְטָן עַל-רוּחוֹת הַטֻּמְאָה
- **Note**: Again using the Aramaic-origin שָׁלְטָן (as in Dan 7:14) for ἐξουσία. Consistent with chapter 9 usage. See note on 9:6.

### 10:11 — הָגוּן for "worthy"
- **Hebrew**: דִּרְשׁוּ מִי הוּא הָגוּן לָזֶה
- **Note**: הָגוּן is a post-biblical / Mishnaic Hebrew term meaning "fitting, proper, worthy." Biblical Hebrew would more naturally use a form of ראוי or אִישׁ-חַיִל or a relative clause construction. The Greek ἄξιος could be rendered with biblical idiom. This is a minor modernism.

### 10:16 — Excellent biblical parallel construction
- **Hebrew**: הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי שֹׁלֵחַ אֶתְכֶם כִּשְׁלֹחַ כְּבָשִׂים בֵּין זְאֵבִים לָכֵן הֱיוּ עֲרוּמִים כַּנְּחָשִׁים וּתְמִימִים כַּיּוֹנִים
- **Note**: Superb biblical register throughout. עֲרוּמִים ("shrewd, cunning") echoes Gen 3:1 (the serpent was עָרוּם), creating a deliberate intertextual link. תְּמִימִים ("blameless, whole") echoes Gen 6:9, 17:1 and is a key covenantal term. כְּבָשִׂים for πρόβατα (rather than צֹאן) is more specific and vivid. Excellent choices throughout.

### 10:17 — סַנְהֶדְרִיּוֹת for "councils"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי יִמְסְרוּ אֶתְכֶם לְסַנְהֶדְרִיּוֹת
- **Note**: This is a Greek loanword (συνέδρια → סנהדרין), used in its Hebrew plural form. While anachronistic as "biblical Hebrew," it is authentic to the Second Temple period setting of the text and well attested in Mishnaic Hebrew. Delitzsch could not easily avoid it since the institution itself is post-biblical.

### 10:28 — גֵיהִנֹּם for "Gehenna"
- **Hebrew**: בְּגֵיהִנֹּם
- **Note**: The form גֵיהִנֹּם reflects the later contracted form of גֵּי בֶן-הִנֹּם (Valley of the son of Hinnom, cf. Josh 15:8, 2 Kings 23:10). The contracted form is standard in Mishnaic Hebrew and accurately renders the Greek γεέννῃ, which itself derives from this Hebrew toponym.

### 10:29 — אִסָּר for "assarion"
- **Hebrew**: שְׁתֵּי צִפֳּרִים בְּאִסָּר
- **Note**: אִסָּר is a transliteration of the Greek/Latin ἀσσάριον/assarius, a Roman coin. This is unavoidable since biblical Hebrew has no equivalent denomination. Delitzsch handles it cleanly.

### 10:34 — לְהָטִיל for "to cast/bring"
- **Hebrew**: אַל-תַּחְשְׁבוּ כִּי בָאתִי לְהָטִיל שָׁלוֹם בָּאָרֶץ
- **Note**: הִטִּיל ("to cast, throw") is post-biblical Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use הִשְׁלִיךְ or שִׂים or נָתַן. The Greek βαλεῖν (to throw/cast) is naturally rendered, but the Hebrew verb choice is Mishnaic rather than biblical.

### 10:35-36 — Micah 7:6 quotation
- **Hebrew**: כִּי בָאתִי לְהַפְרִיד בֵּין אִישׁ וְאָבִיו ... וְאֹיְבֵי אִישׁ אַנְשֵׁי בֵיתוֹ
- **Note**: Verse 36 (וְאֹיְבֵי אִישׁ אַנְשֵׁי בֵיתוֹ) is a near-exact quotation of Micah 7:6. Delitzsch rightly preserves the OT wording. The construction in v. 35 is appropriately biblical, using הִפְרִיד ("to separate") which appears in biblical Hebrew (Gen 1:4, 14).

### 10:38 — צְלוּבוֹ for "his cross"
- **Hebrew**: וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא-יִקַּח אֶת-צְלוּבוֹ
- **Note**: צְלוּב is derived from the root צלב ("to crucify"), which appears in late/post-biblical Hebrew and Aramaic. Biblical Hebrew has no word for "cross" since crucifixion was not an Israelite practice. Delitzsch's form is the standard Hebrew rendering. The Aramaic cognate appears in Ezra 6:11 (יִצְטְלֵב) in the sense of impalement.

## Other Notes

### 10:2-4 — The apostle list
- This is one of four NT apostle lists (cf. Mark 3:16-19, Luke 6:14-16, Acts 1:13). The Hebrew renders the names with appropriate Hebrew/Aramaic forms where possible: שִׁמְעוֹן (Simon), יַעֲקֹב (James/Jacob), יוֹחָנָן (John), מַתַּי (Matthew), while Greek names are transliterated: פֶטְרוֹס (Petros), אַנְדְּרַי (Andreas), פִילִפּוֹס (Philippos), תּוֹמָא (Thomas). This dual strategy is appropriate.

### 10:20 — Pneumatological significance
- The Greek τὸ πνεῦμα τοῦ πατρὸς ὑμῶν ("the Spirit of your Father") is rendered רוּחַ אֲבִיכֶם ("the Spirit of your Father") in Hebrew. The Vulgate has "Spiritus Patris vestri." All three versions agree exactly in this theologically significant statement about the Holy Spirit speaking through the disciples. This is notable as a clear Trinitarian reference point.

### 10:22 — עֵת קֵץ for "the end"
- The Greek εἰς τέλος ("to the end") is rendered by Delitzsch as עַד-עֵת קֵץ ("until the time of the end"), which adds an apocalyptic overtone not present in the original. The phrase echoes Daniel 8:17, 11:35, 12:4 (עֵת קֵץ). The Vulgate simply has "usque in finem" (unto the end). Delitzsch's choice imports Danielic eschatological vocabulary.
