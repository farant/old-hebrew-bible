# Matthew Chapter 26 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 75 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 26:3 — Scribes in Hebrew, absent from Greek and Latin
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes "scribes" (וְהַסּוֹפְרִים) among those gathered in the high priest's court. Neither the Greek nor the Vulgate mentions scribes here; both read "chief priests and elders."
- **Greek**: οἱ ἀρχιερεῖς καὶ οἱ πρεσβύτεροι τοῦ λαοῦ (the chief priests and the elders of the people)
- **Latin**: principes sacerdotum, et seniores populi
- **Hebrew**: רָאשֵׁי הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַסּוֹפְרִים וְזִקְנֵי הָעָם
- **Note**: Delitzsch's addition of scribes may be harmonized from Mark 14:1 (which includes γραμματέων). This represents a genuine content addition not in the SBLGNT or Vulgate Vorlage for this verse.

### 26:20 — "disciples" in Vulgate, absent from SBLGNT
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "cum duodecim discipulis suis" (with his twelve disciples), while the SBLGNT reads simply "with the twelve" (μετὰ τῶν δώδεκα). The critical apparatus shows the word μαθητῶν is a variant.
- **Greek**: ἀνέκειτο μετὰ τῶν δώδεκα μαθητῶν (the word μαθητῶν is marked with ⸀, indicating a variant)
- **Latin**: discumbebat cum duodecim discipulis suis
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּסֵּב עִם-שְׁנֵים הֶעָשָׂר
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the shorter, critical reading ("the twelve" without "disciples"), while the Vulgate follows the longer Byzantine reading. A minor difference but textually significant.

### 26:27 — "gave thanks" vs. "blessed"
- **Issue**: The Greek reads εὐχαριστήσας (having given thanks) for the cup, while the Hebrew reads וַיְבָרֶךְ (and he blessed), which corresponds more to εὐλογήσας. The Vulgate reads "gratias egit" (gave thanks), matching the Greek.
- **Greek**: καὶ λαβὼν ποτήριον καὶ εὐχαριστήσας (and having taken a cup and given thanks)
- **Latin**: Et accipiens calicem, gratias egit
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּקַּח אֶת-הַכּוֹס וַיְבָרֶךְ (and he took the cup and blessed)
- **Note**: In 26:26 for the bread, the Greek uses εὐλογήσας (blessed) and Delitzsch correctly renders וַיְבָרֶךְ. But here for the cup, the Greek switches to εὐχαριστήσας (gave thanks), yet Delitzsch still uses וַיְבָרֶךְ. This flattens a distinction present in the Greek. While the rabbinic tradition uses בֵּרַךְ for both actions, the loss of the Greek distinction is notable.

### 26:28 — "new covenant" in Hebrew, variant in Greek
- **Issue**: The Hebrew reads דַּם-הַבְּרִית הַחֲדָשָׁה ("blood of the new covenant"), including "new." The SBLGNT apparatus marks τῆς as a variant, and many critical scholars regard "new" (καινῆς) as a secondary addition from Luke 22:20 / 1 Cor 11:25. The Vulgate includes "novi" (new).
- **Greek**: τὸ αἷμά μου τῆς διαθήκης (my blood of the covenant -- with τῆς marked as variant)
- **Latin**: sanguis meus novi testamenti (my blood of the new testament)
- **Hebrew**: דָּמִי דַּם-הַבְּרִית הַחֲדָשָׁה
- **Note**: The Hebrew and Vulgate agree on "new," reflecting the Byzantine/TR tradition. The SBLGNT likely omits καινῆς. This is one of the most theologically significant textual variants in the Eucharistic words.

### 26:59 — "scribes" added in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes "scribes" (וְהַסּוֹפְרִים) alongside the chief priests and the whole council. The Greek reads simply "the chief priests and the whole council." The Vulgate likewise has only "principes sacerdotum, et omne concilium."
- **Greek**: οἱ δὲ ἀρχιερεῖς καὶ τὸ συνέδριον ὅλον
- **Latin**: Principes autem sacerdotum, et omne concilium
- **Hebrew**: וְרָאשֵׁי הַכֹּהֲנִים וְהַסּוֹפְרִים וְכָל-הַסַּנְהֶדְרִין
- **Note**: Again Delitzsch adds scribes, possibly harmonizing from Mark 14:55. This is a pattern in his translation.

### 26:60-61 — Verse division and content arrangement
- **Issue**: The Greek and Hebrew split the content differently from the Vulgate. In the Greek (SBLGNT), verse 60 ends with "two came forward" (ὕστερον δὲ προσελθόντες δύο), and verse 61 begins with their testimony. The Hebrew places "two false witnesses came forward" at the beginning of verse 61 rather than at the end of verse 60, and adds "false witnesses" (עֵדֵי שֶׁקֶר) explicitly. The Vulgate includes "duo falsi testes" (two false witnesses) at the end of verse 60.
- **Greek**: v.60: ...ὕστερον δὲ προσελθόντες δύο / v.61: εἶπαν...
- **Latin**: v.60: ...Novissime autem venerunt duo falsi testes / v.61: et dixerunt...
- **Hebrew**: v.60: ...לֹא מָצָאוּ / v.61: וּבָאַחֲרוֹנָה נִגְּשׁוּ שְׁנֵי עֵדֵי שֶׁקֶר...
- **Note**: The characterization of the final two witnesses as "false" (ψευδομάρτυρες) is present in the Vulgate and in some Greek manuscripts but absent from the SBLGNT critical text at this point (which simply says "two"). Delitzsch follows the longer reading.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 26:6 — Bethany transliteration
- **Hebrew**: בֵּית הִינִי
- **Note**: This is an unusual transliteration. Bethany in Hebrew is typically rendered בֵּית עַנְיָה (from the Aramaic/Hebrew place name meaning "house of affliction" or "house of dates"). Delitzsch's בֵּית הִינִי appears to be a phonetic transliteration of the Greek Βηθανία rather than the Semitic etymology. This is odd for a Hebraist of Delitzsch's caliber.

### 26:15 — "thirty silver"
- **Hebrew**: שְׁלֹשִׁים כָּסֶף
- **Note**: Excellent allusion. This exact phrase echoes Zechariah 11:12-13, the prophetic passage that Matthew himself is alluding to. Delitzsch captures the intertextual resonance perfectly by using the same formulation found in the Hebrew Bible source text.

### 26:18 — "such-and-such a person"
- **Hebrew**: פְּלֹנִי אַלְמֹנִי
- **Note**: Outstanding biblical idiom. The Greek τὸν δεῖνα (a certain person) is rendered with the classic biblical Hebrew expression פְּלֹנִי אַלְמֹנִי (Ruth 4:1, 1 Sam 21:3, 2 Kgs 6:8). This is one of Delitzsch's best choices in the chapter.

### 26:30 — "the Hallel"
- **Hebrew**: אַחֲרֵי קָרְאָם אֶת-הַהַלֵּל
- **Note**: The Greek ὑμνήσαντες (having sung a hymn) is rendered specifically as "reciting the Hallel," which is the actual liturgical practice at Passover (Psalms 113-118). This is an excellent interpretive-cultural translation that brings the reader into the historical setting. The Vulgate's generic "hymno dicto" is flat by comparison.

### 26:36 — Gethsemane as "courtyard"
- **Hebrew**: חָצֵר הַנִּקְרָא גַּת-שְׁמָנֵי
- **Note**: The Greek χωρίον (a place, plot of land, estate) is rendered חָצֵר (courtyard, enclosure). This is reasonable -- χωρίον can mean an enclosed plot -- and it preserves the Hebrew etymology גַּת-שְׁמָנֵי (oil press). However, biblical חָצֵר more typically refers to a walled courtyard of a house or tabernacle. The term כֶּרֶם (vineyard) or simply מָקוֹם might have been more accurate for an agricultural plot.

### 26:38 — "my soul is bitter unto death"
- **Hebrew**: נַפְשִׁי מָרָה-לִי עַד-מָוֶת
- **Note**: Superb biblical Hebrew rendering. This echoes Ruth 1:20 (מָרָא for bitter) and multiple psalmic expressions of anguish. The Greek Περίλυπός ἐστιν ἡ ψυχή μου ἕως θανάτου quotes Psalm 42:6/43:5 (LXX 41:6). Delitzsch's choice of מָרָה captures the biblical emotional register powerfully.

### 26:41 — "spirit willing, flesh weak"
- **Hebrew**: הָרוּחַ חֲפֵצָה וְהַבָּשָׂר רָפֶה
- **Note**: Good vocabulary choices. חָפֵץ (willing, delighting in) is well-attested biblical vocabulary (Isa 53:10, Ps 40:9). רָפֶה (weak, slack) is likewise biblical (Judg 8:3, Isa 35:3). The pair works well together.

### 26:49 — "Peace to you" for "Hail"
- **Hebrew**: שָׁלוֹם לְךָ
- **Note**: The Greek Χαῖρε (Hail! Rejoice!) is rendered שָׁלוֹם לְךָ (Peace to you), which is the natural Hebrew greeting. This is culturally appropriate -- Judas would have greeted Jesus in Hebrew/Aramaic with שלום, not a Greek salutation. Good translational judgment.

### 26:53 — Loanword "legions"
- **Hebrew**: לִגְיוֹנוֹת
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the loanword לִגְיוֹן (from Latin legio via Greek λεγιών), which is attested in rabbinic literature but not in biblical Hebrew. There is no good biblical Hebrew equivalent for this Roman military term, so the loanword is defensible, though it stands out in the biblical-style text.

## Other Notes

### 26:26-29 — The Words of Institution
The three texts are in close agreement for the Eucharistic words, with the notable exception of "new" covenant discussed above. The Hebrew elegantly uses sacrificial and covenantal vocabulary (דָּם, בְּרִית, סְלִיחָה) that roots the passage firmly in the Hebrew Bible's cultic framework.

### 26:31 — Zechariah quotation
The quotation from Zechariah 13:7 is rendered identically in the Hebrew to the Masoretic text: אַכֶּה אֶת-הָרֹעֶה וּתְפוּצֶיןָ הַצֹּאן. The Greek (Πατάξω τὸν ποιμένα, καὶ διασκορπισθήσονται τὰ πρόβατα) follows the LXX adaptation. The Vulgate follows the Greek. This is a case where the Hebrew NT naturally returns to the MT source.
