# Matthew Chapter 24 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 51 verses align across all three sources (SBLGNT, Delitzsch, Vulgate). No versification mismatches.

## Content Divergences

### 24:6 -- "must take place" nuance
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads dei gar genesthai ("for it is necessary to happen"), while the Vulgate has "oportet enim haec fieri." Delitzsch renders ki-hayo tihyeh kol-zot ("for all this will surely happen"), using the infinitive absolute construction (hayo tihyeh) for emphasis. This captures the necessity well but adds kol-zot ("all this"), which is not in the Greek dei gar genesthai.
- **Greek**: dei gar genesthai
- **Latin**: oportet enim haec fieri
- **Hebrew**: ki-hayo tihyeh kol-zot
- **Note**: The infinitive absolute construction is a fine biblical Hebrew rendering of the Greek dei (divine necessity). The addition of kol-zot matches the Latin "haec" but is absent from the SBLGNT at this point in the verse.

### 24:7 -- Pestilence addition
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads limoi kai seismoi ("famines and earthquakes"), without "pestilences." The Vulgate includes "pestilentiae, et fames, et terramotus" (pestilences, and famines, and earthquakes). Delitzsch has ra'av va-dever va-ra'ash ("famine and pestilence and earthquake"), including dever (pestilence), which aligns with the Vulgate/Byzantine tradition but not the critical Greek text.
- **Greek**: limoi kai seismoi (famines and earthquakes)
- **Latin**: pestilentiae, et fames, et terramotus
- **Hebrew**: ra'av va-dever va-ra'ash
- **Note**: The addition of "pestilences" (loimoi/dever) is a well-known Byzantine addition, possibly harmonized from Luke 21:11. Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/TR tradition here. Additionally, dever ("pestilence") is placed between famine and earthquake, matching the Vulgate order rather than the Greek order.

### 24:27 -- "of the Son of Man" phrasing
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds shel ("of") in the construct: bo'o shel-ben-ha'adam. This shel-construction is characteristic of later Hebrew (Mishnaic/Modern) rather than biblical Hebrew, which would use a simple construct chain or a relative clause.
- **Greek**: he parousia tou huiou tou anthropou
- **Latin**: adventus Filii hominis
- **Hebrew**: bo'o shel-ben-ha'adam
- **Note**: This is noted under Hebrew quality below, but it also represents a stylistic divergence from how the other texts handle the genitive.

### 24:31 -- Trumpet description
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads meta salpiggos megales ("with a great trumpet"), marked with a variant. Some manuscripts read meta salpiggos phones megales ("with a trumpet and a great sound/voice"). The Vulgate has "cum tuba, et voce magna" ("with a trumpet and a great voice"), reflecting the longer reading. Delitzsch has be-qol shofar gadol ("with the sound of a great shofar"), which is somewhat ambiguous between the two readings.
- **Greek**: meta salpiggos megales (with variant)
- **Latin**: cum tuba, et voce magna
- **Hebrew**: be-qol shofar gadol
- **Note**: Delitzsch's be-qol can mean "with the sound of" (combining both readings) or simply "with the voice/blast of." The use of shofar rather than chatsotsrah is significant -- shofar is the ram's horn associated with theophany and eschatological events (Isa 27:13, Joel 2:1), while chatsotsrah is the priestly silver trumpet. Shofar is the correct eschatological choice.

### 24:36 -- "nor the Son" -- major textual variant
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT includes oude ho huios ("nor the Son"), marked with a variant indicator. This phrase is absent from many Byzantine manuscripts and the Textus Receptus but present in Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and the parallel in Mark 13:32. The Vulgate omits it: "nisi solus Pater" ("except the Father alone"). Delitzsch includes it: gam-lo habben ("also not the Son").
- **Greek**: oude ho huios (present in SBLGNT, absent in some MSS)
- **Latin**: nisi solus Pater (no "nor the Son")
- **Hebrew**: gam-lo habben bilti avi levaddo
- **Note**: This is one of the most theologically sensitive textual variants in the NT. The phrase "nor the Son" was omitted by some scribes apparently to avoid the implication that the Son is ignorant of the eschaton. Delitzsch follows the critical Greek text in including it, diverging from the Vulgate. This is a significant choice that affects Christological interpretation.

### 24:42 -- "day" vs "hour"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads poia hemera ("which day"), while the Vulgate has "qua hora" ("which hour"). Delitzsch has be-ey-zo sha'ah ("at what hour"), following the Vulgate reading rather than the critical Greek.
- **Greek**: poia hemera (which day)
- **Latin**: qua hora (which hour)
- **Hebrew**: be-ey-zo sha'ah (at what hour)
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/Byzantine reading (hora/sha'ah) against the SBLGNT critical text (hemera/yom). The "day" reading is supported by Sinaiticus and Vaticanus; the "hour" reading likely arises from harmonization with v.44 and 25:13.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 24:2 -- tithparaq (will be pulled apart)
- **Hebrew**: lo tisha'er poh even al-even asher lo titparaq
- **Note**: The hitpael titparaq ("will be torn apart/demolished") is a good biblical Hebrew choice. The root p-r-q appears in biblical Hebrew meaning "to tear away, break off" (Exod 32:2, 1 Kings 19:11). The hitpael form with the sense of architectural destruction is natural.

### 24:3 -- Parousia as bo'akha
- **Hebrew**: u-mah ot bo'akha ve-ot qets ha'olam
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders parousia ("coming/presence/arrival") as bo'akha ("your coming"), which captures the core meaning. However, parousia in Hellenistic Greek carried official connotations (a royal visitation or official arrival of a dignitary). The simple Hebrew bo ("coming") does not carry this formal register. An alternative like hera'otkha ("your appearing") or hivvasetkha ("your being revealed") might capture the theophanic dimension better, though bo is defensible.

### 24:7 -- Echoes of Isaiah and prophetic language
- **Hebrew**: ki yaqum goy al-goy u-mamlakhah al-mamlakhah
- **Note**: Excellent. This closely echoes Isaiah 19:2 (ve-niskeshu ish be-achiv ve-ish be-re'ehu ir be-ir mamlakhah be-mamlakhah) and 2 Chronicles 15:6 (ve-yikkat goy be-goy ve-ir be-ir). The prophetic eschatological language is perfectly at home in biblical Hebrew.

### 24:8 -- "Beginning of birth pains"
- **Hebrew**: ve-khol-elleh raq reshit hachavalim
- **Note**: Chavalim ("birth pains") is excellent biblical Hebrew (Isa 13:8, 26:17; Jer 22:23, 49:24; Hos 13:13). The metaphor of eschatological suffering as birth pangs is thoroughly rooted in the Hebrew prophetic tradition. Reshit hachavalim is a natural Hebrew construction.

### 24:12 -- "love will grow cold"
- **Hebrew**: u-mippeney asher yirbeh happesha tapug ahavat harabbim
- **Note**: The verb tapug ("will grow faint/cold") is from p-w-g, attested in Genesis 45:26 (vayyapag libbo, "his heart grew numb") and Psalm 38:9 (nefugoti, "I am faint"). This is a beautiful biblical Hebrew choice that captures the Greek psygesetai ("will be cooled") with a word that carries deep resonance of emotional numbness and failing vitality.

### 24:15 -- Abomination of desolation
- **Hebrew**: shiqquts meshomem
- **Note**: Delitzsch correctly uses the exact phrase from Daniel 12:11 (shiqquts meshomem) and Daniel 11:31. This is one of the most important OT-NT intertextual links, and Delitzsch preserves it perfectly by using the Daniel source phrase verbatim.

### 24:21 -- "Great tribulation"
- **Hebrew**: ki az tihyeh tsarah gedolah asher kamoha lo-nihyetah me-reshit ha'olam ve-ad-attah ve-khamoha lo tosif
- **Note**: The phrasing closely parallels Daniel 12:1 (ve-haytah et tsarah asher lo nihyetah mi-heyot goy ad ha'et hahi). Delitzsch adapts the Daniel phrase skillfully, maintaining the biblical register. The spelling ha'olam where the Greek has kosmou is standard for Delitzsch.

### 24:27-28 -- Lightning and eagles/vultures
- **Hebrew**: (v.27) ki ka-baraq hayyotse mi-mizrach u-me'ir ad-hamma'arav ken yihyeh gam-bo'o shel-ben-ha'adam
- **Hebrew**: (v.28) ki ba'asher hechalal sham yiqqavetsu hannesharim
- **Note**: The shel construction in v.27 (bo'o shel-ben-ha'adam) is post-biblical; biblical Hebrew would say bo ben-ha'adam or bo'at ben-ha'adam. In v.28, nesharim could mean either "eagles" or "vultures" (as in biblical Hebrew, where the nesher of Lev 11:13 is likely a vulture). The Greek aetoi similarly allows both readings. Delitzsch's hechalal ("the corpse/slain one") is good biblical vocabulary (Num 19:16, Deut 21:1).

### 24:29 -- Cosmic signs
- **Hebrew**: techshakh hashemesh ve-hayyare'ach lo yaggiah oro ve-hakkokhavim yippelu min-hashamayim va-chayeley hashamayim yitmotatu
- **Note**: Strong prophetic Hebrew drawing on Isaiah 13:10 (lo yahel oro, of the moon) and Joel 2:10, 3:4. The verb yitmotatu ("will totter/shake") from the root m-w-t is well-attested in biblical Hebrew for cosmic upheaval (Ps 46:3, 82:5). Chayeley hashamayim ("hosts/powers of heaven") renders dynameis ton ouranon; the Hebrew tseva hashamayim (host of heaven) would be more standard biblical (Deut 4:19, Jer 33:22), but chayeley works.

### 24:30 -- Son of Man coming on clouds
- **Hebrew**: ve-ra'u et-ben-ha'adam ba im-ananey hashamayim bi-gevurah ve-khavod rav
- **Note**: Direct echo of Daniel 7:13 (ve-aru im-ananey shemayya ke-var enash ateh hava). Delitzsch adapts the Aramaic Daniel text into Hebrew naturally. The phrase im-ananey ("with the clouds of") rather than al-ananey ("upon the clouds of") matches the Greek epi ton nephelon less precisely, but follows Daniel 7:13 LXX/Theodotion tradition.

### 24:35 -- Heaven and earth will pass away
- **Hebrew**: hashamayim ve-ha'arets ya'avoru u-devaray lo ya'avorun
- **Note**: Clean, lapidary biblical Hebrew. The parallelism is elegant. The form ya'avorun with the paragogic nun (energic form) lends solemnity and archaism, which is a skillful stylistic touch. This is one of Delitzsch's finest single verses.

### 24:38 -- Days of the flood
- **Hebrew**: ki ka'asher lifney yemey hammabbul hayu okhlim ve-shotim nos'im nashim ve-notenim nashim la-anashim ad-hayyom asher-ba noach el-hattevah
- **Note**: Good narrative Hebrew. Hammabbul ("the flood") is the standard Genesis term (Gen 6:17, 7:6). Tevah ("ark") is the correct Genesis word (Gen 6:14). The marriage phrases nos'im nashim ve-notenim nashim la-anashim ("taking wives and giving wives to men") are somewhat wordy but accurately render the Greek gamountes kai gamizontes.

### 24:45 -- Faithful and wise servant
- **Hebrew**: mi hu efo ha'eved hane'eman ve-hannavon asher hifqido adonav al-avadav
- **Note**: Good. Ne'eman (faithful, cf. Num 12:7, ne'eman be-khol beyti) and navon (discerning/wise, cf. Gen 41:33, 39) are both excellent biblical Hebrew terms. The parable's language sits well in biblical Hebrew, echoing Joseph's appointment over Pharaoh's house.

## Other Notes

### 24:14 -- "Gospel of the kingdom"
Delitzsch renders euangelion as besorat (from the root b-s-r), which is the standard biblical Hebrew for good tidings (Isa 40:9, 52:7, 61:1). This is an apt choice that roots the NT concept in its OT prophetic heritage.

### 24:15 -- "Let the reader understand"
The parenthetical ho anaginoskon noeito ("let the reader understand") is rendered ha-qore yavin. This is likely an editorial aside by the evangelist (or possibly by Jesus referring to reading Daniel). Delitzsch's rendering is straightforward. The verb yavin (from b-y-n, "understand/discern") is standard biblical Hebrew for comprehending a text (Dan 9:2, Neh 8:8).

### 24:28 -- Proverbial saying
The saying about the corpse and the eagles/vultures (v.28) appears to be a proverbial saying that Jesus applies eschatologically. It parallels Job 39:30 (u-va'asher chalalim sham hu, "and where the slain are, there it is" -- referring to the nesher/eagle). Delitzsch's rendering echoes this Job passage, creating an elegant intertextual connection.

### 24:36 -- Christological significance
As noted in the divergences section, the inclusion of gam-lo habben ("nor the Son") is theologically significant. By including this phrase (following the critical Greek against the Vulgate), Delitzsch presents a text where Jesus acknowledges limitation of the incarnate Son's knowledge regarding the eschaton. This aligns with orthodox Christology (the kenosis) but was historically controversial enough to be omitted by Byzantine scribes.
