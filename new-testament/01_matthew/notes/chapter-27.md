# Matthew Chapter 27 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 66 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 27:2 — "Pontius" in Hebrew and Vulgate, absent from Greek
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes the praenomen "Pontius" (פּוֹנְטִיּוֹס) before Pilate's name, as does the Vulgate ("Pontio Pilato"). The SBLGNT Greek has simply Πιλάτῳ τῷ ἡγεμόνι (Pilate the governor).
- **Greek**: παρέδωκαν Πιλάτῳ τῷ ἡγεμόνι
- **Latin**: tradiderunt Pontio Pilato praesidi
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּמְסְרֻהוּ אֶל-פּוֹנְטִיּוֹס פִּילָטוֹס הַהֶגְמוֹן
- **Note**: The full name "Pontius Pilate" appears in Luke 3:1 and in the creedal tradition but is not in the critical Greek text of this verse. The Hebrew and Vulgate agree against the Greek, suggesting Delitzsch may have been influenced by the Vulgate or Byzantine tradition here.

### 27:4 — "innocent blood" vs. "righteous blood"
- **Issue**: The Greek reads αἷμα ἀθῷον (innocent blood), which the Hebrew renders as דָם נָקִי (innocent/clean blood), matching the Greek sense. The Vulgate, however, reads "sanguinem justum" (righteous/just blood), which is a different theological emphasis.
- **Greek**: Ἥμαρτον παραδοὺς αἷμα ἀθῷον (I sinned by betraying innocent blood)
- **Latin**: Peccavi, tradens sanguinem justum (I sinned, betraying righteous blood)
- **Hebrew**: חָטָאתִי כִּי-דָם נָקִי הִסְגָּרְתִּי
- **Note**: The Greek ἀθῷον means "guiltless, innocent" while the Vulgate's "justum" means "just, righteous." The Hebrew דָם נָקִי is a well-known biblical phrase (Deut 19:10, 2 Kgs 21:16, Jer 22:3) meaning "innocent blood." Delitzsch correctly follows the Greek against the Vulgate.

### 27:9 — Attribution to Jeremiah
- **Issue**: All three sources attribute the prophecy to Jeremiah (Ἰερεμίου / Jeremiam / יִרְמְיָה), though the quotation about thirty pieces of silver is from Zechariah 11:12-13, not Jeremiah. This is a famous crux in all text traditions.
- **Note**: This is not a divergence between the texts but a shared difficulty. No text tradition "corrects" the attribution. Some scholars suggest a combined allusion to Jer 18:2-3, 19:1-2 (the potter) and Zech 11:12-13 (the thirty silver pieces).

### 27:16-17 — "Jesus Barabbas" in SBLGNT
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT includes the name "Jesus" before Barabbas in v.16 (Ἰησοῦν Βαραββᾶν) and v.17 (Ἰησοῦν τὸν Βαραββᾶν). Neither the Vulgate nor the Hebrew includes this first name.
- **Greek**: v.16: δέσμιον ἐπίσημον λεγόμενον Ἰησοῦν Βαραββᾶν / v.17: Ἰησοῦν τὸν Βαραββᾶν ἢ Ἰησοῦν τὸν λεγόμενον χριστόν
- **Latin**: v.16: qui dicebatur Barabbas / v.17: Barabbam, an Jesum, qui dicitur Christus
- **Hebrew**: v.16: בַּר-אַבָּא / v.17: אֵת בַּר-אַבָּא אוֹ אֶת-יֵשׁוּעַ
- **Note**: The reading "Jesus Barabbas" is a significant textual variant attested by Origen and found in some manuscripts. The SBLGNT includes it. Both the Vulgate and Delitzsch follow the traditional reading without "Jesus" before Barabbas. If the SBLGNT reading is correct, the scene becomes a dramatic choice between two men named Jesus.

### 27:24 — "righteous man" in Hebrew and Vulgate, "this man" in Greek
- **Issue**: The Hebrew reads דַּם הַצַּדִּיק הַזֶּה ("the blood of this righteous man") and the Vulgate reads "sanguine justi hujus" (the blood of this righteous one). The SBLGNT reads simply τοῦ αἵματος τούτου ("this blood").
- **Greek**: Ἀθῷός εἰμι ἀπὸ τοῦ αἵματος τούτου (I am innocent of this blood)
- **Latin**: Innocens ego sum a sanguine justi hujus (I am innocent of the blood of this righteous one)
- **Hebrew**: נָקִי אָנֹכִי מִדַּם הַצַּדִּיק הַזֶּה
- **Note**: The Vulgate and Hebrew agree on "this righteous one," which is a textual variant present in some Greek manuscripts. The SBLGNT's shorter reading is likely original. This addition makes Pilate's declaration of Jesus's innocence more explicit.

### 27:34 — Vinegar/wine and gall/myrrh
- **Issue**: The Greek reads οἶνον μετὰ χολῆς (wine with gall). The Hebrew reads חֹמֶץ מָסוּךְ בִּמְרוֹרוֹת (vinegar mixed with bitterness). The Vulgate reads "vinum...cum felle" (wine with gall).
- **Greek**: ἔδωκαν αὐτῷ πιεῖν οἶνον μετὰ χολῆς μεμιγμένον
- **Latin**: Et dederunt ei vinum bibere cum felle mistum
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּתְּנוּ-לוֹ לִשְׁתּוֹת חֹמֶץ מָסוּךְ בִּמְרוֹרוֹת
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses חֹמֶץ (vinegar) instead of יַיִן (wine), possibly harmonizing with Psalm 69:21 (וַיִּתְּנוּ בְּבָרוּתִי רֹאשׁ וְלִצְמָאִי יַשְׁקוּנִי חֹמֶץ) and with Mark 15:23 (which has οἶνον ἐσμυρνισμένον). The use of מְרוֹרוֹת (bitterness, bitter herbs) alludes to Passover bitter herbs and Lam 3:15, creating intertextual resonance.

### 27:35 — Bracketed Psalm quotation in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes a lengthy bracketed addition from Psalm 22:19 [18]: [לְמַלּאת אֵת אֲשֶׁר-נֶאֱמַר בְּפִי הַנָּבִיא יְחַלְּקוּ בְגָדַי לָהֶם וְעַל-לְבוּשִׁי יַפִּילוּ גוֹרָל]. This is absent from the SBLGNT but present in the Vulgate.
- **Greek**: σταυρώσαντες δὲ αὐτὸν διεμερίσαντο τὰ ἱμάτια αὐτοῦ βάλλοντες κλῆρον (they crucified him and divided his garments by casting lots)
- **Latin**: ...ut impleretur quod dictum est per prophetam dicentem : Diviserunt sibi vestimenta mea, et super vestem meam miserunt sortem
- **Hebrew**: ...וַיַּפִּלוּ גוֹרָל [לְמַלּאת...גוֹרָל]
- **Note**: The Psalm 22 quotation is a well-known Byzantine/TR interpolation from John 19:24. It is absent from the best Greek manuscripts. The Vulgate includes it; the Hebrew brackets signal Delitzsch's awareness of its secondary status. The Hebrew of the quoted psalm closely follows the MT of Ps 22:19.

### 27:49 — Double-bracketed spear thrust in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes a double-bracketed addition [[וְאִישׁ אַחֵר לָקַח חֲנִית וַיִּדְקֹר אֶת-צַלְעוֹ וַיֵּצְאוּ מַיִם וָדָם]] ("and another man took a spear and pierced his side, and water and blood came out"). This is absent from both the SBLGNT and the Vulgate.
- **Greek**: (absent)
- **Latin**: (absent)
- **Hebrew**: [[וְאִישׁ אַחֵר לָקַח חֲנִית וַיִּדְקֹר אֶת-צַלְעוֹ וַיֵּצְאוּ מַיִם וָדָם]]
- **Note**: This is a very rare textual variant, found in Codex Sinaiticus (01), Codex Vaticanus (03), and a few other witnesses, which place the spear thrust from John 19:34 here in Matthew before the death of Jesus. Delitzsch's double brackets indicate its highly uncertain status. This is one of the most fascinating textual variants in the NT and its inclusion here (even bracketed) is noteworthy.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 27:3 — "repented" vs. "regretted"
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּנָּחֵם (and he repented/was sorry)
- **Note**: The Greek μεταμεληθείς means "regretted, felt remorse" (not μετανοέω which would mean genuine repentance/conversion). The Hebrew נחם in the nifal can mean both "be sorry" (Gen 6:6, 1 Sam 15:35) and "repent" (Jer 31:19). Delitzsch's choice is defensible and well-attested in biblical Hebrew, though the ambiguity of נחם somewhat mirrors the ambiguity of the Greek.

### 27:5 — "strangled himself"
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּחָנַק (and he strangled himself)
- **Note**: The Greek ἀπήγξατο means "hanged himself." The Hebrew חנק (strangle) is rabbinic vocabulary -- it occurs in the Mishnah as one of the four death penalties but is not a biblical Hebrew word. The biblical Hebrew equivalent would be a periphrastic construction. This is a case where Delitzsch draws from post-biblical Hebrew.

### 27:6 — "ark of the offering"
- **Hebrew**: אֲרוֹן הַקָּרְבָּן
- **Note**: The Greek κορβανᾶν (korban treasury) is rendered אֲרוֹן הַקָּרְבָּן (chest of the offering/korban). This cleverly uses biblical Hebrew אָרוֹן (ark, chest -- as in the collection chest of 2 Kgs 12:10) combined with the Hebrew-origin word קָרְבָּן (offering, as in Lev 1:2). This is excellent and culturally grounded.

### 27:24 — Pilate's handwashing
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּרְחַץ אֶת-יָדָיו לְעֵינֵי הָעָם
- **Note**: The language echoes Deuteronomy 21:6-7 (the ritual handwashing over the heifer when a murderer is unknown). This intertextual echo, whether intentional by Matthew or by Delitzsch, enriches the Hebrew text. Pilate performs a ritual recognizable from Torah.

### 27:46 — The cry from the cross
- **Hebrew**: אֵלִי אֵלִי לְמָה שְׁבַקְתָּנִי וְהוּא אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי
- **Note**: Delitzsch preserves both the Aramaic form (שְׁבַקְתָּנִי, from שׁבק "to leave, forsake") and then provides the Hebrew translation (עֲזַבְתָּנִי, from Ps 22:2 [1]). The Greek text itself provides the Aramaic/Hebrew transliteration and translation. The Hebrew translation portion (לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי) exactly matches the Masoretic text of Psalm 22:2. This is one of the most powerful moments in the Hebrew NT.

### 27:51 — Temple veil
- **Hebrew**: פָּרֹכֶת הַהֵיכָל
- **Note**: Perfect biblical vocabulary. פָּרֹכֶת is the exact word used for the Temple veil/curtain in Exodus 26:31-35, 40:21, Leviticus 16:2, etc. Delitzsch's choice is precise and evocative for Hebrew readers who know this word from the Torah's tabernacle descriptions.

### 27:52 — "sleepers of the dust of the ground"
- **Hebrew**: יְשֵׁנֵי אַדְמַת עָפָר
- **Note**: This is drawn directly from Daniel 12:2 (וְרַבִּים מִיְּשֵׁנֵי אַדְמַת-עָפָר יָקִיצוּ, "many of those who sleep in the dust of the earth shall awake"). Delitzsch creates a powerful echo of the only clear OT resurrection passage. The Greek κεκοιμημένων ἁγίων ("sleeping saints") is transformed into deeply resonant biblical Hebrew.

## Other Notes

### 27:9-10 — The Zechariah/Jeremiah composite quotation
The OT quotation attributed to Jeremiah is actually a composite, primarily from Zechariah 11:12-13 with elements possibly from Jeremiah 18-19 (the potter, the field). In the Hebrew, the phrasing closely follows the Masoretic text of Zechariah (שְׁלֹשִׁים הַכֶּסֶף, אֶדֶר הַיְקָר) while adapting it to the narrative context. All three text traditions maintain the Jeremiah attribution without correction.

### 27:62 — "day after Preparation"
- **Hebrew**: מִמָּחֳרַת עֶרֶב הַשַׁבָּת (the day after the sabbath eve)
- **Note**: The Greek μετὰ τὴν παρασκευήν (after the Preparation) and the Vulgate's "post Parasceven" both use the technical term for Friday / the day before the sabbath. Delitzsch renders it descriptively as "the day after sabbath eve" (i.e., Saturday), which is clear but loses the technical term παρασκευή that had become a standard designation. The Hebrew עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת is itself the standard rabbinic equivalent.
