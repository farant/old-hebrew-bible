# Matthew Chapter 2 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 23 verses align across all three sources (Greek SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, Clementine Vulgate).

## Content Divergences

### 2:1 — Word order and emphasis
- **Issue**: The Greek leads with Jesus' birth in Bethlehem, then mentions Herod's days. The Hebrew restructures to lead with the temporal clause (וַיְהִי בִּימֵי הוֹרְדוֹס הַמֶּלֶךְ), creating a different narrative emphasis. The Vulgate follows the Greek more closely ("Cum ergo natus esset Jesus in Bethlehem").
- **Greek**: Τοῦ δὲ Ἰησοῦ γεννηθέντος ἐν Βηθλέεμ ... ἐν ἡμέραις Ἡρῴδου
- **Latin**: Cum ergo natus esset Jesus in Bethlehem Juda in diebus Herodis regis
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי בִּימֵי הוֹרְדוֹס הַמֶּלֶךְ כַּאֲשֶׁר נוֹלַד יֵשׁוּעַ בְּבֵית-לֶחֶם יְהוּדָה
- **Note**: Delitzsch's restructuring uses the classic biblical Hebrew narrative opening וַיְהִי (and it came to pass), echoing constructions like Ruth 1:1 and 1 Samuel 4:1. While this changes the emphasis, it produces more natural biblical Hebrew narrative. The Greek genitive absolute construction has no direct Hebrew equivalent.

### 2:6 — Micah 5:2 quotation divergences
- **Issue**: Matthew's quotation of Micah 5:2 diverges significantly from both the MT and LXX of Micah. Matthew writes "by no means least" (οὐδαμῶς ἐλαχίστη) where Micah has "too small to be" (צָעִיר לִהְיוֹת). Matthew adds "who will shepherd my people Israel" (from 2 Samuel 5:2), which is not in Micah. All three NT texts agree on this composite quotation.
- **Greek**: οὐδαμῶς ἐλαχίστη εἶ ἐν τοῖς ἡγεμόσιν Ἰούδα
- **Latin**: nequaquam minima es in principibus Juda (in brackets in Vulgate, marking it as OT quotation)
- **Hebrew**: אֵינְךָ צָעִיר בְּאַלֻּפֵי יְהוּדָה
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses אַלֻּפֵי (chiefs of), which is a good biblical Hebrew choice echoing Genesis 36 and Zechariah 12:5-6, rather than a mechanical back-translation of ἡγεμόσιν. He uses צָעִיר matching the vocabulary of Micah's Hebrew but reverses its sense with אֵינְךָ (you are not) to follow Matthew's "by no means least" — a skillful hybrid approach.

### 2:11 — "gifts" vs. "offering"
- **Issue**: The Greek uses δῶρα (gifts, plural), the Vulgate has "munera" (gifts), but Delitzsch uses מִנְחָה (offering, singular), which carries sacrificial/cultic overtones in biblical Hebrew (cf. Genesis 4:3-4, Leviticus 2).
- **Greek**: προσήνεγκαν αὐτῷ δῶρα — "they offered him gifts"
- **Latin**: obtulerunt ei munera — "they offered him gifts"
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּקְרִיבוּ לוֹ מִנְחָה — "they brought near to him an offering"
- **Note**: Delitzsch's choice of מִנְחָה (singular, sacrificial offering) rather than מַתָּנוֹת (gifts) or דֹּרוֹן (present) adds a theological layer. Combined with the Hiphil verb הִקְרִיב (to bring near, present as offering), this frames the magi's gifts in priestly/sacrificial language. This may be intentional theological interpretation rather than neutral translation.

### 2:13 — "destroy" vs. "take his life"
- **Issue**: The Greek has τοῦ ἀπολέσαι αὐτό (to destroy it/him). The Vulgate has "ad perdendum eum" (to destroy him). Delitzsch renders this as מְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת-נֶפֶשׁ הַנַּעַר לְקַחְתָּהּ (seeking the life of the child to take it).
- **Greek**: ζητεῖν τὸ παιδίον τοῦ ἀπολέσαι αὐτό
- **Latin**: quærat puerum ad perdendum eum
- **Hebrew**: מְבַקֵּשׁ אֶת-נֶפֶשׁ הַנַּעַר לְקַחְתָּהּ
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the classic biblical idiom בִּקֵּשׁ נֶפֶשׁ (to seek someone's life), found frequently in the David-Saul narrative (1 Samuel 20:1, 22:23, 23:15) and the Moses story (Exodus 4:19). This is an excellent translation that maps a common Greek expression onto its closest biblical Hebrew equivalent, and the Exodus 4:19 parallel (where Pharaoh seeks Moses' life) creates a typological echo that Matthew may have intended.

### 2:18 — Jeremiah 31:15 quotation
- **Issue**: The Greek adds ὀδυρμὸς πολύς (much mourning/lamentation) alongside κλαυθμός (weeping), expanding beyond Jeremiah 31:15 MT. The Hebrew of Delitzsch follows the MT of Jeremiah more closely than the Greek of Matthew, reading נְהִי וּבְכִי תַמְרוּרִים (wailing and bitter weeping), which is very close to Jeremiah 31:14 MT (נְהִי בְּכִי תַמְרוּרִים).
- **Greek**: κλαυθμὸς καὶ ὀδυρμὸς πολύς — "weeping and much mourning"
- **Latin**: ploratus, et ululatus multus — "wailing and much lamentation"
- **Hebrew**: נְהִי וּבְכִי תַמְרוּרִים — "wailing and bitter weeping"
- **Note**: Delitzsch quotes the MT of Jeremiah nearly verbatim rather than back-translating Matthew's Greek. The Greek ὀδυρμὸς πολύς (much mourning) becomes תַמְרוּרִים (bitter), following the Hebrew source text. The Hebrew also adds עַל-בָּנֶיהָ a second time ("refusing to be comforted over her children because they are not"), which matches the MT of Jeremiah but goes beyond what the Greek of Matthew includes. This is another case of Delitzsch privileging the OT Hebrew source over mechanical back-translation from the Greek.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 2:1 — Biblical narrative style
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי בִּימֵי הוֹרְדוֹס הַמֶּלֶךְ
- **Note**: The opening וַיְהִי בִּימֵי (and it came to pass in the days of) is a classic biblical Hebrew narrative formula found in Ruth 1:1, Esther 1:1, Isaiah 7:1. This is excellent biblical register. The word מְגוּשִׁים (magi) is a loan word from Persian (cf. Daniel, Jeremiah 39), appropriately used.

### 2:8 — Natural wayyiqtol narrative chain
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּשְׁלָחֵם בֵּית-לֶחֶם וַיֹּאמַר לְכוּ חִקְרוּ הֵיטֵב עַל-דְּבַר הַנָּעַר וְהָיָה כִּי-תִמְצְאוּן אֹתוֹ וְהִגַּדְתֶּם לִי
- **Note**: The entire verse flows naturally in biblical Hebrew narrative style. The construction וְהָיָה כִּי-תִמְצְאוּן (and it shall be when you find) with weqatal is a classic conditional-temporal construction. The Hiphil imperative חִקְרוּ and the verb הִגַּדְתֶּם are both natural biblical choices.

### 2:22 — "artzot haGalil"
- **Hebrew**: וַיֵּלֵךְ לוֹ אֶל-אַרְצוֹת הַגָּלִיל
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders τὰ μέρη τῆς Γαλιλαίας (the parts/districts of Galilee) as אַרְצוֹת הַגָּלִיל (the lands of the Galil). The plural אַרְצוֹת is somewhat unusual — biblical Hebrew typically refers to the Galilee region in the singular (cf. Joshua 20:7, 21:32, 1 Kings 9:11). The singular אֶרֶץ הַגָּלִיל would be more idiomatic.

### 2:23 — "Notzri" wordplay
- **Hebrew**: כִּי נָצְרִי יִקָּרֵא לוֹ
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders Ναζωραῖος (Nazoraean) as נָצְרִי (Notzri, i.e. Nazarene). This preserves the connection to נְצָרֶת (Nazareth) mentioned earlier in the verse. The wordplay Matthew intends — possibly connecting to נֵצֶר (branch, Isaiah 11:1) or נָזִיר (Nazirite) — is partially preserved in Hebrew through the shared root נצר. This is one of those rare cases where Hebrew actually clarifies the Greek wordplay better than Greek does.

## Other Notes

### 2:15 — Hosea 11:1 and typological exegesis
The quotation מִמִּצְרַיִם קָרָאתִי לִבְנִי ("Out of Egypt I called my son") is identical in all three traditions and matches Hosea 11:1 MT exactly. This is a typological rather than predictive use of the OT — Hosea was referring to Israel's Exodus. Matthew applies it to Jesus as the new Israel. All three textual traditions preserve this without alteration.

### 2:22 — Archelaus
The Hebrew renders βασιλεύει (reigns/rules) as מָלַךְ (reigned as king), but historically Archelaus was an ethnarch, not a king — Rome denied him the royal title. Matthew's Greek uses βασιλεύω loosely. Delitzsch's מָלַךְ carries the full weight of kingship in biblical Hebrew, which slightly overstates Archelaus's actual title, though it follows the Greek faithfully.
