# Matthew Chapter 20 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 34 verses are present in the SBLGNT Greek and the Delitzsch Hebrew. No empty Greek fields in this chapter. The verse numbering aligns across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 20:7 — Hebrew adds "your wages will be given to you"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds the clause וְשְׂכַרְכֶם יֻתַּן לָכֶם ("and your wages will be given to you") which is not in the SBLGNT Greek. The Vulgate also lacks this addition.
- **Greek**: `Ὑπάγετε καὶ ὑμεῖς εἰς τὸν ἀμπελῶνα` ("Go also into the vineyard")
- **Latin**: `Ite et vos in vineam meam` ("Go you also into my vineyard")
- **Hebrew**: `לְכוּ גַם-אַתֶּם אֶל-הַכֶּרֶם וְשְׂכַרְכֶם יֻתַּן לָכֶם` ("Go also into the vineyard, and your wages will be given to you")
- **Note**: The addition of a wage promise appears in some later Greek manuscripts (the Byzantine text/Textus Receptus includes καὶ ὃ ἐὰν ᾖ δίκαιον λήμψεσθε, "and whatever is just you will receive"). Delitzsch follows this longer reading. The SBLGNT omits it based on the earlier manuscripts. The Vulgate also omits this clause at v.7, keeping only the bare command to go into the vineyard.

### 20:16 — "Many are called, few are chosen"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT omits the saying "for many are called but few are chosen" at the end of v.16. The Hebrew includes it in brackets. The Vulgate includes it.
- **Greek**: `οὕτως ἔσονται οἱ ἔσχατοι πρῶτοι καὶ οἱ πρῶτοι ἔσχατοι` (period; no further text)
- **Latin**: `Sic erunt novissimi primi, et primi novissimi. Multi enim sunt vocati, pauci vero electi.`
- **Hebrew**: `כֵּן יִהְיוּ הָאַחֲרוֹנִים רִאשׁוֹנִים וְהָרִאשׁוֹנִים יִהְיוּ אַחֲרוֹנִים [כִּי-רַבִּים הֵם הַקְּרוּאִים וּמְעַטִּים הַנִּבְחָרִים]`
- **Note**: This saying is absent from the earliest manuscripts of Matthew 20:16 but present in Matt 22:14. It was likely added by scribes to harmonize with that later passage. The SBLGNT correctly omits it. Delitzsch's brackets acknowledge the textual doubt. The Vulgate preserves the longer Byzantine reading.

### 20:22-23 — Baptism clause in Hebrew but not in Greek SBLGNT
- **Issue**: The Hebrew at v.22 adds a reference to baptism (וּלְהִטָּבֵל טְבִילָה אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי נִטְבָּל, "and to be baptized with the baptism with which I am baptized") and at v.23 includes a bracketed response about baptism ([וּטְבִילָה אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי נִטְבָּל תִּטָּבֵלוּ], "and the baptism with which I am baptized, you will be baptized"). The SBLGNT Greek and the Vulgate lack these baptism references in Matthew.
- **Greek (v.22)**: `δύνασθε πιεῖν τὸ ποτήριον ὃ ἐγὼ μέλλω πίνειν;` ("Can you drink the cup that I am about to drink?")
- **Latin (v.22)**: `Potestis bibere calicem, quem ego bibiturus sum?`
- **Hebrew (v.22)**: `הֲיָכֹל תּוּכְלוּ לִשְׁתּוֹת אֶת-הַכּוֹס אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי עָתִיד לִשְׁתּוֹתוֹ וּלְהִטָּבֵל טְבִילָה אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי נִטְבָּל`
- **Greek (v.23)**: `Τὸ μὲν ποτήριόν μου πίεσθε` (cup only, no baptism)
- **Latin (v.23)**: `Calicem quidem meum bibetis` (cup only)
- **Hebrew (v.23)**: `הֵן אֶת-כּוֹסִי תִשְׁתּוּ [וּטְבִילָה אֲשֶׁר אֲנִי נִטְבָּל תִּטָּבֵלוּ]`
- **Note**: The baptism language is present in the Markan parallel (Mark 10:38-39) but absent from Matthew in the best manuscripts. Delitzsch harmonizes with Mark here, including the baptism reference at v.22 without brackets but bracketing it at v.23. This is a significant addition not supported by the SBLGNT or the Vulgate. The inconsistent bracketing (unbracketted at v.22, bracketed at v.23) is puzzling and may reflect different editorial decisions at different stages.

### 20:26-27 — Future indicative vs. imperative
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads ἔσται... ἔσται (future indicative: "will be your servant... will be your slave"), while the sense could also be imperative. The Vulgate uses the subjunctive "sit... erit" (mixing moods). The Hebrew uses the jussive יְהִי... יְהִי ("let him be").
- **Greek**: `ὃς ἂν θέλῃ ἐν ὑμῖν μέγας γενέσθαι ἔσται ὑμῶν διάκονος`
- **Latin**: `quicumque voluerit inter vos major fieri, sit vester minister`
- **Hebrew**: `הֶחָפֵץ לִהְיוֹת גָּדוֹל בְּקִרְבְּכֶם יְהִי לָכֶם לִמְשָׁרֵת`
- **Note**: The Hebrew jussive form captures the prescriptive force well. This is not a divergence in content but a translation difference worth noting: the Greek future ("will be") can function as an imperative in Semitic-influenced Greek, and Delitzsch correctly renders it as a jussive command rather than a simple future prediction.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 20:1 — בַּעַל-בָּיִת for οἰκοδεσπότης
- **Hebrew**: `לְאִישׁ בַּעַל-בָּיִת אֲשֶׁר הִשְׁכִּים בַּבֹּקֶר`
- **Note**: The term בַּעַל-בַּיִת ("master of the house") is Mishnaic Hebrew; it does not appear in this form in the Hebrew Bible. Biblical Hebrew would express this differently, perhaps as אֲדוֹן הַבַּיִת (cf. Gen 39:2,16). However, הִשְׁכִּים בַּבֹּקֶר ("rose early in the morning") is excellent biblical Hebrew (Gen 19:27, Judg 6:38, etc.) using the characteristic Hiphil of שׁכם.

### 20:2 — דִּינָר for δηνάριον
- **Hebrew**: `דִּינָר לַיּוֹם`
- **Note**: The loanword דִּינָר is used throughout this chapter. Since the denarius is a Roman coin with no biblical Hebrew equivalent, a loanword is the only practical option. The word is well-established in Mishnaic Hebrew. Delitzsch does not attempt a biblical periphrasis, which is the right choice for clarity.

### 20:3 — בְּטֵלִים for ἀργούς
- **Hebrew**: `אֲחֵרִים עֹמְדִים בְּטֵלִים בַּשּׁוּק`
- **Note**: The adjective בָּטֵל ("idle, unoccupied") is Mishnaic, not biblical Hebrew. The biblical equivalent might be constructed differently (e.g., using רֵיקָם, "empty-handed"). However, the marketplace (שׁוּק) setting evokes Song of Songs 3:2 and Ecclesiastes 12:4-5, and the scene is well painted in Hebrew.

### 20:8 — פְּקִיד for ἐπίτροπος
- **Hebrew**: `וַיֹּאמֶר בַּעַל הַכֶּרֶם אֶל-פְּקִידוֹ`
- **Note**: The word פָּקִיד ("overseer, steward") is biblical Hebrew (Gen 41:34, Neh 11:9, 2 Chr 24:11), making this an excellent choice for the Greek ἐπίτροπος ("steward, manager"). The root פקד is deeply embedded in biblical administrative and religious vocabulary.

### 20:13 — רֵעִי for Ἑταῖρε
- **Hebrew**: `רֵעִי לֹא הוֹנֵיתִי אֹתָךְ`
- **Note**: The address רֵעִי ("my friend/companion") for Ἑταῖρε is functional, but the Greek ἑταῖρε in Matthew carries a subtly ironic tone -- it appears three times in Matthew (20:13, 22:12, 26:50), always in contexts of rebuke or confrontation. The Hebrew רֵעַ does not carry this same edge; it is a warmer, more genuinely friendly term in biblical Hebrew (cf. Exod 33:11, Prov 27:10). The Greek ἑταῖρος is more distant and formal than φίλος. A closer biblical Hebrew equivalent for this ironic-formal address might be harder to find.

### 20:15 — הֲתֵּרַע עֵינְךָ — "evil eye" idiom
- **Hebrew**: `הַאִם-תֵּרַע עֵינְךָ עַל-אֲשֶׁר טוֹב אָנִי`
- **Note**: The Hebrew perfectly captures the "evil eye" idiom. The Greek ὁ ὀφθαλμός σου πονηρός ἐστιν ("is your eye evil?") is itself a Semitic expression for envy/jealousy. Delitzsch renders it with the verb form תֵּרַע עֵינְךָ, echoing Deuteronomy 15:9 (הִשָּׁמֶר לְךָ פֶּן-יִהְיֶה דָבָר עִם-לְבָבְךָ בְלִיַּעַל... וְרָעָה עֵינְךָ). The biblical Hebrew "evil eye" idiom for stinginess/envy is the exact source of the Greek expression, making this a perfect back-translation.

### 20:19 — וְלִצְלֹב for σταυρῶσαι
- **Hebrew**: `וּלְהַכּוֹת אוֹתוֹ בַּשּׁוֹטִים וְלִצְלֹב אוֹתוֹ`
- **Note**: The verb צלב ("to crucify") is not found in biblical Hebrew. It appears in Mishnaic Hebrew and is used in Esther 7:10 in a related but not identical sense (Haman is "hanged/impaled" on the stake). Delitzsch uses the Qal infinitive construct, which functions well. There is no truly biblical Hebrew word for Roman crucifixion since the practice postdates the biblical period. The Targums use the root זקף or צלב for this concept.

### 20:22 — הֲיָכֹל תּוּכְלוּ — emphatic ability construction
- **Hebrew**: `הֲיָכֹל תּוּכְלוּ לִשְׁתּוֹת אֶת-הַכּוֹס`
- **Note**: The infinitive absolute + finite verb construction (יָכֹל תּוּכְלוּ, "can you indeed?") is classic biblical Hebrew emphatic syntax (cf. Gen 43:7, הֲיָדוֹעַ נֵדַע). This is a distinctly biblical construction that modern Hebrew has largely abandoned, showing Delitzsch's commitment to biblical register.

### 20:28 — כֹּפֶר תַּחַת רַבִּים for λύτρον ἀντὶ πολλῶν
- **Hebrew**: `וְלָתֵת אֶת-נַפְשׁוֹ כֹּפֶר תַּחַת רַבִּים`
- **Note**: This is one of Delitzsch's finest renderings. The word כֹּפֶר ("ransom") is deeply biblical (Exod 21:30, 30:12; Job 33:24; Ps 49:8) and carries the specific theological weight of a price paid to redeem a life. The preposition תַּחַת ("in place of, instead of") renders ἀντί with its full substitutionary force. The phrase נָתַן נַפְשׁוֹ ("gave his life/soul") echoes Isaiah 53:10 (אִם-תָּשִׂים אָשָׁם נַפְשׁוֹ) and 53:12 (הֶעֱרָה לַמָּוֶת נַפְשׁוֹ). The whole expression reads as if it could be a verse from the Hebrew Bible itself.

### 20:30 — חָנֵּנוּ-נָא for ἐλέησον ἡμᾶς
- **Hebrew**: `חָנֵּנוּ-נָא אֲדֹנֵינוּ בֶּן-דָּוִד`
- **Note**: The verb חנן ("to be gracious, show mercy") in its imperative form with the -נוּ suffix ("be gracious to us") plus the particle of entreaty נָא is pure biblical Hebrew prayer language (cf. Ps 51:3, חָנֵּנִי אֱלֹהִים; Ps 123:3, חָנֵּנוּ יְהוָה). The address אֲדֹנֵינוּ בֶּן-דָּוִד ("our Lord, Son of David") would resonate powerfully in a biblical Hebrew context.

## Other Notes

### 20:1-16 — Parable of the Workers in the Vineyard
The vineyard imagery (כֶּרֶם) throughout this parable connects to a deep vein of Hebrew Bible metaphor (Isa 5:1-7, the Song of the Vineyard; Ps 80:9-16; Jer 12:10). Delitzsch's Hebrew alternates between כֶּרֶם and כַּרְמוֹ naturally, evoking these associations.

### 20:17-19 — Third passion prediction
This is the most detailed of the three passion predictions in Matthew, specifying crucifixion by the Gentiles. The Hebrew text is notable for its specificity: לְרָאשֵׁי הַכֹּהֲנִים וְלַסּוֹפְרִים ("to the chief priests and the scribes"), וּמָסְרוּ אוֹתוֹ לַגּוֹיִם ("and they will deliver him to the nations/Gentiles"). The term גּוֹיִם for ἔθνεσιν carries its full biblical weight of "the nations" as opposed to Israel.

### 20:28 — Ransom theology
The ransom saying is one of the most theologically significant verses in the Gospel. The Hebrew כֹּפֶר תַּחַת רַבִּים ("a ransom in place of many") resonates with the Day of Atonement theology (Leviticus 16-17) and the Suffering Servant of Isaiah 53. This verse would be relevant for "red text" markup as direct speech of Jesus with high christological content.
