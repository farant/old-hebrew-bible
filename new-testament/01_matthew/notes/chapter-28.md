# Matthew Chapter 28 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 20 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 28:2 — "angel of the Lord" vs. source variation
- **Issue**: All three texts agree on "angel of the Lord" (ἄγγελος κυρίου / Angelus Domini / מַלְאַךְ יְהוָֹה). However, the Hebrew uses the Tetragrammaton יְהוָֹה where the Greek has κυρίου and the Vulgate has "Domini."
- **Greek**: ἄγγελος γὰρ κυρίου
- **Latin**: Angelus enim Domini
- **Hebrew**: מַלְאַךְ יְהוָֹה
- **Note**: Delitzsch's use of the Tetragrammaton for κύριος is a consistent feature of his translation when the reference is to God the Father (as opposed to Jesus as Lord). The phrase מַלְאַךְ יְהוָֹה is a direct biblical formula (Gen 16:7, Exod 3:2, Judg 2:1, etc.). This is a theologically significant translation choice: it identifies the NT "Lord" with the covenant God of Israel.

### 28:6 — "the Lord" in Hebrew, absent from Greek
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds הָאָדוֹן ("the Lord") at the end of the verse where the Greek simply reads ἔκειτο (he lay). The Vulgate reads "positus erat Dominus" (the Lord was placed), matching the Hebrew's addition.
- **Greek**: δεῦτε ἴδετε τὸν τόπον ὅπου ἔκειτο (come, see the place where he lay)
- **Latin**: venite, et videte locum ubi positus erat Dominus (come and see the place where the Lord was placed)
- **Hebrew**: בֹּאנָה וּרְאֶינָה אֶת-הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר שָׁכַב-שָׁם הָאָדוֹן
- **Note**: "The Lord" (ὁ κύριος) is a textual variant found in some manuscripts and in the Byzantine tradition. The SBLGNT does not include it. The Vulgate and Hebrew agree on this expansion, which adds a christological title to the angel's speech.

### 28:9 — Narrative expansion in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew opens 28:9 with a phrase not in the Greek: הֵנָּה הֹלְכוֹת לְסַפֵּר אֶל-תַּלְמִידָיו ("as they were going to tell his disciples"). The Greek begins simply with Ἰησοῦς ὑπήντησεν αὐταῖς ("Jesus met them"). The Vulgate likewise begins directly with "Et ecce Jesus occurrit illis."
- **Greek**: καὶ ἰδοὺ Ἰησοῦς ὑπήντησεν αὐταῖς
- **Latin**: Et ecce Jesus occurrit illis
- **Hebrew**: הֵנָּה הֹלְכוֹת לְסַפֵּר אֶל-תַּלְמִידָיו וְהִנֵּה יֵשׁוּעַ נִקְרֶה אֲלֵיהֶן
- **Note**: The added clause appears to be an explanatory expansion by Delitzsch, perhaps connecting the narrative of v.8 (running to tell the disciples) with v.9 (meeting Jesus on the way). This does not correspond to any known Greek variant and represents a minor departure from the source.

### 28:18 — "authority" vs. "power"
- **Issue**: The Greek ἐξουσία (authority, right) is rendered שָׁלְטָן in Hebrew. The Vulgate uses "potestas" (power, authority). All three are substantively equivalent, but worth noting that שָׁלְטָן is an Aramaic-influenced form.
- **Note**: Not a true content divergence but see Hebrew quality note below.

### 28:19 — Trinitarian formula
- **Issue**: All three texts are in full agreement on the baptismal formula: "in the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit."
- **Greek**: εἰς τὸ ὄνομα τοῦ πατρὸς καὶ τοῦ υἱοῦ καὶ τοῦ ἁγίου πνεύματος
- **Latin**: in nomine Patris, et Filii, et Spiritus Sancti
- **Hebrew**: לְשֵׁם-הָאָב וְהַבֵּן וְרוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ
- **Note**: There is no divergence here, which is itself noteworthy given the significance of this passage. The Hebrew לְשֵׁם (for/in the name of) is the standard Hebrew rendering of εἰς τὸ ὄνομα.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 28:1 — Time expression
- **Hebrew**: וְאַחֲרֵי מוֹצָאֵי הַשַׁבָּת כְּשֶׁהֵאִיר לְאֶחָד בַּשַׁבָּת
- **Note**: This is a mixed construction. מוֹצָאֵי הַשַׁבָּת (the going out of the sabbath) is a rabbinic expression, not found in biblical Hebrew. The construction כְּשֶׁהֵאִיר uses the relative שֶׁ which is predominantly late/post-biblical (it occurs in Song of Songs and Ecclesiastes but is the standard relative particle in Mishnaic Hebrew, not in classical biblical prose). The Greek Ὀψὲ δὲ σαββάτων, τῇ ἐπιφωσκούσῃ εἰς μίαν σαββάτων is itself complex. A more biblical rendering might use בְּאוֹר ... or something with אֲשֶׁר instead of שֶׁ.

### 28:5 — Feminine plural address
- **Hebrew**: אַתֵּן אַל-תִּירֶאןָ
- **Note**: The use of the second person feminine plural (אַתֵּן) and the corresponding verb form is grammatically correct biblical Hebrew for addressing women. This small detail shows Delitzsch's careful attention to biblical Hebrew gender agreement, which modern Hebrew often simplifies.

### 28:9 — "Peace to you" for "Hail"
- **Hebrew**: שָׁלוֹם לָכֵן
- **Note**: As in 26:49, Χαίρετε (Hail! Rejoice!) is rendered as שָׁלוֹם (Peace), the natural Semitic greeting. The feminine plural suffix לָכֵן (to you, f.pl.) is correctly used since Jesus is addressing the women. Good attention to gender and cultural context.

### 28:16 — "eleven" as cardinal number
- **Hebrew**: וְעַשְׁתֵּי עָשָׂר הַתַּלְמִידִים
- **Note**: The number עַשְׁתֵּי עָשָׂר (eleven) is the biblical Hebrew form of eleven (Gen 32:23, Deut 1:2). This is preferable to any post-biblical alternative and shows Delitzsch maintaining classical style.

### 28:17 — "their heart was divided"
- **Hebrew**: וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶם אֲשֶׁר חָלַק לִבָּם (and some of them whose heart was divided)
- **Note**: The Greek ἐδίστασαν (they doubted/hesitated) is rendered with the idiom חָלַק לִבָּם (their heart was divided). This recalls 1 Kings 18:21 (עַד-מָתַי אַתֶּם פֹּסְחִים עַל-שְׁתֵּי הַסְּעִפִּים -- the divided heart at Carmel). The word חלק in connection with the heart is found in Hosea 10:2 (חָלַק לִבָּם). This is an excellent, idiomatically biblical rendering of doubt.

### 28:18 — "authority" as שָׁלְטָן
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-שָׁלְטָן
- **Note**: שָׁלְטָן is an Aramaic loanword that appears in late biblical Hebrew (Dan 3:33, 4:19, 6:27, 7:6,12,14,26,27). Given that it appears prominently in Daniel 7:14 (וְלֵהּ יְהִיב שָׁלְטָן) in the "Son of Man" passage -- and Jesus frequently uses the Son of Man title -- this is a brilliant intertextual choice. The authority given to the Son of Man in Daniel 7 is echoed in Jesus's claim here. The alternative biblical Hebrew word מֶמְשָׁלָה would lack this specific Danielic resonance.

### 28:19 — "make disciples"
- **Hebrew**: וַעֲשׂוֹּ תַלְמִידִים
- **Note**: The Greek μαθητεύσατε (make disciples) is a word with no direct biblical Hebrew equivalent since תַּלְמִיד is not a biblical Hebrew word (it is Mishnaic). Delitzsch's construction עשׂה + תַלְמִידִים (make disciples) is transparent and functional but inherently post-biblical in vocabulary. There is no good way around this; the concept of "making disciples" in this technical sense belongs to Second Temple period vocabulary.

### 28:20 — Closing formula
- **Hebrew**: אָנֹכִי אִתְּכֶם כָּל-הַיָּמִים עַד-קֵץ הָעוֹלָם אָמֵן
- **Note**: Magnificent biblical Hebrew. The phrase אָנֹכִי אִתְּכֶם echoes God's covenant promises to the patriarchs (Gen 26:3 כִּי-אִתְּךָ אָנֹכִי, Gen 28:15 הִנֵּה אָנֹכִי עִמָּךְ). The phrase כָּל-הַיָּמִים (all the days) is standard biblical Hebrew. קֵץ הָעוֹלָם (end of the age) echoes Daniel 12:13 (לְקֵץ הַיָּמִין). The Greek συντελείας τοῦ αἰῶνος is rendered with vocabulary that connects to both patriarchal promises and Danielic eschatology. This is arguably the finest verse in Delitzsch's entire translation of Matthew, and it closes the Gospel with profound resonance.

## Other Notes

### 28:19-20 — The Great Commission
This is the most theologically dense passage in the chapter. All three text traditions are in full agreement on the Trinitarian baptismal formula. The Hebrew rendering is particularly significant because it expresses distinctly Christian theology (the triune name: האָב, הַבֵּן, רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ) in biblical Hebrew forms. רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ as a distinct person alongside the Father and Son is a New Testament innovation that has no exact parallel in the Hebrew Bible, where רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ / רוּחַ אֱלֹהִים is always understood as God's own spirit rather than a separate person.

### 28:1 — Chronological note
The three traditions describe the timing slightly differently: the Greek Ὀψὲ σαββάτων can be read as "late on the sabbath" or "after the sabbath"; the Vulgate "Vespere sabbati" (evening of the sabbath); the Hebrew "after the going out of the sabbath." The ambiguity of the Greek ὀψέ is well known. The Hebrew resolves the ambiguity in favor of "after" the sabbath, which aligns with the following phrase about dawn. The Vulgate's "vespere" (evening) is somewhat misleading since the next clause says "as it was dawning" (quae lucescit).
