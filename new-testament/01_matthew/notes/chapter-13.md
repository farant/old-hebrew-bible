# Matthew Chapter 13 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 58 verses align across all three sources. The SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, and Clementine Vulgate all contain verses 1-58 with no missing, extra, or differently-numbered verses.

## Content Divergences

### 13:35 — Psalm attribution: "prophet" vs. "prophet Isaiah"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "through the prophet" (dia tou prophetou) without naming which prophet. The Vulgate reads "per prophetam dicentem" (through the prophet saying), also unnamed. Some Greek manuscript traditions (and the parallel in certain Vulgate manuscripts) add "Isaiah" (Hesaias), but this is likely secondary since the quotation is from Psalm 78:2 (Asaph), not Isaiah.
- **Greek**: dia tou prophetou legontos
- **Latin**: per prophetam dicentem
- **Hebrew**: ha-navi (the prophet) -- unnamed, matching both Greek and Latin
- **Note**: All three texts agree in leaving the prophet unnamed. This is the correct critical reading. The addition of "Isaiah" in some witnesses is a scribal harmonization that creates a factual error (the quote is from Psalm 78:2, attributed to Asaph).

### 13:14 — Isaiah quotation: imperative vs. future
- **Issue**: The Hebrew of Delitzsch follows the MT of Isaiah 6:9 closely with imperatives ("hear indeed ... see indeed"), while the Greek uses future indicatives following the LXX form ("you will hear ... you will see"). The Vulgate uses futures matching the Greek LXX tradition.
- **Greek**: Akoe akousete kai ou me synete, kai blepontes blepsete kai ou me idete
- **Latin**: Auditu audietis, et non intelligetis : et videntes videbitis, et non videbitis
- **Hebrew**: shim'u shamoa ve-al-tavinu ur'u ra'o ve-al-teda'u
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the MT of Isaiah 6:9 with imperatives (shim'u shamoa / ur'u ra'o), which is a legitimate choice for a Hebrew translation. However, the Greek NT here uses futures (akousete / blepsete), following the LXX form of Isaiah. The Vulgate similarly uses futures (audietis / videbitis). Furthermore, Delitzsch uses ve-al-teda'u ("and do not know") where the Greek has ou me idete ("you will certainly not see"), and the original Isaiah has ve-al-teda'u -- so Delitzsch has actually substituted yod-dalet-ayin (know) for resh-aleph-he (see), which diverges from the Greek.

### 13:15 — Isaiah 6:10 quotation form
- **Issue**: Delitzsch renders this as a near-verbatim quotation of Isaiah 6:10 from the MT, using the hiphil imperative pattern (hashmen ... ha-kbed ... hasha'). The Greek (following the LXX) uses indicative/passive forms (epachynthe ... ekamusan) describing the people's condition as a completed state rather than as a divine command. The Vulgate follows the Greek/LXX pattern with "Incrassatum est enim cor."
- **Greek**: epachynthe gar he kardia tou laou toutou (For the heart of this people has grown thick)
- **Latin**: Incrassatum est enim cor populi hujus (For the heart of this people has been thickened)
- **Hebrew**: ki hashmen lev-ha'am hazeh (For make fat the heart of this people)
- **Note**: This is a significant divergence. The LXX and Vulgate describe the people's heart as already fattened (passive/stative), while the MT (and Delitzsch following it) has the imperative commanding the prophet to fatten their hearts. The Greek NT consistently follows the LXX text of Isaiah here, not the MT, so Delitzsch's choice to follow the MT creates a real content difference with the Greek original he is translating.

### 13:51 — "Jesus said to them" / "Yes, Lord"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT does not contain "Jesus" as the speaker in v.51 -- it reads simply "Did you understand all these things? They say to him: Yes." The Delitzsch Hebrew adds "Jesus" (va-yomer aleihem Yeshua) and the reply is expanded to "hen adonenu" ("Yes, our lord"). The Vulgate is minimal like the Greek: "Intellexistis haec omnia? Dicunt ei: Etiam."
- **Greek**: Synekate tauta panta; legousin auto: Nai.
- **Latin**: Intellexistis haec omnia? Dicunt ei: Etiam.
- **Hebrew**: va-yomer aleihem Yeshua ha-tavinu et-kol-elleh va-yomru elav hen adonenu
- **Note**: Delitzsch adds "Jesus" as subject and expands "yes" (nai / etiam) to "yes, our lord" (hen adonenu). The addition of "adonenu" has no basis in either the Greek or Latin texts. This appears to be Delitzsch's stylistic embellishment to match biblical Hebrew narrative conventions, but it introduces content not present in the source.

### 13:55 — "Joseph" vs. "Joses"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "Ioseph" (Joseph) for the brother of Jesus. The Vulgate also reads "Joseph." However, Delitzsch writes "Yosei" which corresponds to "Joses" -- a variant found in some Greek manuscripts and in the Textus Receptus.
- **Greek**: Iakobos kai Ioseph kai Simon kai Ioudas
- **Latin**: Jacobus, et Joseph, et Simon, et Judas
- **Hebrew**: Ya'akov ve-Yosei ve-Shim'on vi-Yehudah
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the TR/Byzantine reading "Joses" (Yosei) rather than the critical text reading "Joseph" (Yosef). The SBLGNT and Vulgate both have the full form "Joseph."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 13:2 — "boat" vs. "ship"
- **Hebrew**: va-yered el-ha-oniyyah (and he went down to the ship)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses oniyyah, which in biblical Hebrew typically refers to a larger seafaring vessel (cf. Jonah 1:3, 1 Kings 22:49). The Greek ploion can mean either boat or ship, but on the Sea of Galilee a smaller craft is meant. Biblical Hebrew does not have a common diminutive for "boat" so oniyyah is acceptable, but the use of va-yered ("he went down") is a good biblical choice reflecting nautical idiom (cf. Jonah 1:3 va-yered ... ba-oniyyah).

### 13:11 — "mysteries" rendered as sodot
- **Hebrew**: sodot malkhut ha-shamayim (mysteries/secrets of the kingdom of heaven)
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders mysteria as sodot (plural of sod). This is an excellent biblical Hebrew choice. The word sod is used in the Hebrew Bible for divine counsel and hidden purposes (cf. Amos 3:7, Psalm 25:14, Proverbs 3:32). This captures the sense of mysteria far better than a Mishnaic loanword would.

### 13:12 — Use of she- relative particle
- **Hebrew**: ki mi she-yesh-lo naton yinnaten lo ... u-mi she-ein lo gam mah-she-yesh-lo yinnatel mimmennu
- **Note**: The extensive use of the she- relative particle (mi she-yesh-lo, mah-she-yesh-lo) is characteristic of Mishnaic/Rabbinic Hebrew rather than biblical Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use asher constructions. This is one of the most clearly post-biblical constructions in the chapter. A more biblical rendering might use: ki mi asher yesh-lo ... u-mi asher ein lo.

### 13:25 — "tares" rendered as zunin
- **Hebrew**: va-yizra zunin bein ha-hittim (and he sowed zunin among the wheat)
- **Note**: Zunin (tares/darnel) is a word found in Mishnaic Hebrew (m. Kilayim 1:1) but not in the Hebrew Bible. This is unavoidable since the Hebrew Bible does not have a word for zizania (darnel/lolium). Delitzsch's choice is the most natural Hebrew equivalent available.

### 13:30 — Excellent biblical harvest vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: ha-nihu otam ... ad ha-qatsir ... la-qotsrim laqtu ... ve-igdu otam agudot le-sorfam ve-et ha-hittim isfu le-otsari
- **Note**: This verse showcases Delitzsch at his best. The vocabulary is thoroughly biblical: qatsir (harvest, Gen 8:22), qotsrim (reapers, Ruth 2:3), agudot (bundles, Ex 12:22 related), otsari (my storehouse/granary, Joel 1:17). The construction reads naturally as classical Hebrew prose.

### 13:33 — Leaven parable: excellent biblical Hebrew
- **Hebrew**: va-yissa od meshalo (and he took up his parable again)
- **Note**: The phrase va-yissa meshalo is a beautiful biblical idiom drawn from Numbers 23:7, 24:3 (Balaam's oracles). Delitzsch appropriates the prophetic "parable-lifting" language for Jesus's teaching, which is a sophisticated and apt biblical Hebrew rendering of the Greek elalesen ... parabolon.

### 13:47 — "dragnet" as mikhmeret
- **Hebrew**: mikhmeret asher hoshlekhah la-yam (a dragnet that was cast into the sea)
- **Note**: Mikhmeret is used in the Hebrew Bible (Habakkuk 1:15, Isaiah 19:8) for a fishing net, making this an excellent biblical Hebrew word choice. The passive construction hoshlekhah ("was cast") is also properly biblical.

### 13:52 — "scribe trained" as sofer melummad
- **Hebrew**: kol-sofer melummad le-malkhut ha-shamayim
- **Note**: Sofer is the standard biblical term for scribe. The participle melummad ("trained, taught") appears in Isaiah 50:4 (leshon limmudim) and 1 Chronicles 25:7. This is a well-chosen biblical rendering of grammateus matheteutheis.

## Other Notes

### 13:14-15 — Three-way Isaiah 6:9-10 comparison
This passage is significant for textual criticism because all three traditions handle the Isaiah quotation differently. The Greek NT follows the LXX text of Isaiah (passive/stative: "the heart of this people has grown thick"). The Vulgate follows the Greek closely. But Delitzsch retranslates back toward the MT of Isaiah (imperative: "make fat the heart of this people"). This creates a real theological shift: in the LXX/Greek NT reading, the people's insensitivity is described as a fact; in the MT/Delitzsch reading, it is presented as a prophetic commission. This is one of the most important LXX-vs-MT divergences in the entire Old Testament and it resurfaces here in the New Testament quotation.

### 13:35 — Psalm 78:2 attribution
The quotation "I will open my mouth in parables, I will utter things hidden from of old" is from Psalm 78:2. Delitzsch's Hebrew (eftehah be-mashal pi avi'ah hidot minni-qedem) follows the MT of Psalm 78:2 very closely. The Vulgate has "a constitutione mundi" (from the foundation of the world) where the Hebrew/Greek have "from of old" (minni-qedem / apo kataboles), though some Greek witnesses read apo kataboles kosmou ("from the foundation of the world"), which the Vulgate appears to follow.

### 13:39 — Satan vs. Devil
The Greek has ho diabolos ("the devil"), the Latin has "diabolus," but Delitzsch renders this as ha-satan. While "the satan" is the standard Hebrew Bible term for the adversary (Job 1-2, Zechariah 3:1-2, 1 Chronicles 21:1), it is worth noting that the Greek consistently uses diabolos rather than satanas in this verse. Delitzsch's choice is reasonable for a Hebrew Bible register, but it obscures the Greek's specific word choice.
