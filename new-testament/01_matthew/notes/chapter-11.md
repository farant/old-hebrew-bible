# Matthew Chapter 11 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 30 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 11:2 — "Two" disciples vs "his" disciples
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads διὰ τῶν μαθητῶν αὐτοῦ ("through his disciples"), with a variant marker on διά suggesting manuscript variation. The Vulgate reads "duos de discipulis suis" ("two of his disciples"). Delitzsch reads שְׁנַיִם מִתַּלְמִידָיו ("two of his disciples"), agreeing with the Vulgate rather than the SBLGNT main text.
- **Greek**: πέμψας ⸀διὰ τῶν μαθητῶν αὐτοῦ ("sending through his disciples")
- **Latin**: mittens duos de discipulis suis ("sending two of his disciples")
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּשְׁלַח שְׁנַיִם מִתַּלְמִידָיו ("and he sent two of his disciples")
- **Note**: The variant here is between δύο (two) in some manuscripts and the main SBLGNT reading. The Vulgate and Delitzsch follow manuscripts that specify "two" disciples. Luke 7:18-19 confirms the number as two. This is a significant textual variant where Latin and Hebrew align against the SBLGNT main text.

### 11:10 — Malachi 3:1 quotation
- **Issue**: The OT quotation differs between the Greek NT form and the Hebrew OT original. The Greek/NT form has πρὸ προσώπου σου ("before your face") and ἔμπροσθέν σου ("before you"), where the Hebrew original of Malachi 3:1 has לְפָנָי ("before me"). The NT changes the pronouns to address the messenger/forerunner directly.
- **Greek**: Ἰδοὺ ἐγὼ ἀποστέλλω τὸν ἄγγελόν μου πρὸ προσώπου σου, ὃς κατασκευάσει τὴν ὁδόν σου ἔμπροσθέν σου
- **Latin**: Ecce ego mitto angelum meum ante faciem tuam, qui præparabit viam tuam ante te
- **Hebrew**: הִנְנִי שֹׁלֵחַ מַלְאָכִי לְפָנֶיךָ וּפִנָּה דַּרְכְּךָ לְפָנֶיךָ
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses לְפָנֶיךָ ("before you") twice, following the NT adaptation of Malachi rather than the OT original (which has לְפָנָי "before me"). Also note that Delitzsch uses וּפִנָּה ("and he will clear/prepare"), echoing the piel of פנה as in Mal 3:1/Isa 40:3, while the Greek has κατασκευάσει ("will prepare"). The Hebrew rendering here is masterful in its biblical resonance.

### 11:19 — "Works" vs "children" of wisdom
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads ἀπὸ τῶν ἔργων αὐτῆς ("by her works"), with a variant marker. The Vulgate reads "a filiis suis" ("by her children"). Delitzsch reads בְּבָנֶיהָ ("by her children"), following the Vulgate/Lukan parallel reading.
- **Greek**: ἐδικαιώθη ἡ σοφία ἀπὸ τῶν ⸀ἔργων αὐτῆς
- **Latin**: justificata est sapientia a filiis suis
- **Hebrew**: וְנִצְדְּקָה הַחָכְמָה בְּבָנֶיהָ
- **Note**: This is a well-known textual variant. The SBLGNT reads "works" (ἔργων), while the Vulgate and many manuscripts read "children" (τέκνων, matching Luke 7:35). Delitzsch follows the "children" reading with the Vulgate. Both readings make theological sense, but they represent different textual traditions.

### 11:23 — Interrogative vs declarative about Capernaum
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has μὴ ἕως οὐρανοῦ ὑψωθήσῃ; ("Will you be exalted to heaven?"), phrased as a rhetorical question expecting a negative answer. The Vulgate similarly phrases it as a question: "numquid usque in cælum exaltaberis?" However, Delitzsch renders it as a declarative: הַמְרוֹמָמָה עַד-הַשָׁמַיִם ("the one exalted to heaven"), treating it as a description of Capernaum's pride rather than a question.
- **Greek**: μὴ ἕως οὐρανοῦ ὑψωθήσῃ;
- **Latin**: numquid usque in cælum exaltaberis?
- **Hebrew**: הַמְרוֹמָמָה עַד-הַשָׁמַיִם
- **Note**: Delitzsch's rendering follows the Isaiah 14:13-15 parallel more closely, describing Capernaum as one who is (or has been) exalted, and then pronouncing judgment. Some Greek manuscripts (and the TR) also read ἡ ἕως τοῦ οὐρανοῦ ὑψωθεῖσα ("you who have been exalted to heaven"), which matches Delitzsch's approach. This is a case where Delitzsch follows the alternative Greek reading.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 11:5 — Isaiah 35/61 echo
- **Hebrew**: עִוְרִים רֹאִים וּפִסְחִים מְהַלְּכִים מְצֹרָעִים מְטֹהָרִים וְחֵרְשִׁים שׁוֹמְעִים וּמֵתִים קָמִים וַעֲנִיִּים מִתְבַּשְׁרִים
- **Note**: This verse is a deliberate allusion to Isa 35:5-6 and 61:1. Delitzsch renders it with participial chains that echo prophetic Hebrew style beautifully. עִוְרִים רֹאִים echoes Isa 35:5 (עֵינֵי עִוְרִים תִּפָּקַחְנָה). פִּסְחִים מְהַלְּכִים echoes Isa 35:6 (אָז יְדַלֵּג כָּאַיָּל פִּסֵּחַ). The verb מִתְבַּשְׁרִים for "are evangelized" connects to Isa 61:1 (לְבַשֵּׂר עֲנָוִים). The participial style creates an appropriately prophetic register. Excellent work.

### 11:7 — הֲקָנֶה אֲשֶׁר יִנּוֹע בָּרוּחַ for "a reed shaken by the wind"
- **Hebrew**: הֲקָנֶה אֲשֶׁר יִנּוֹע בָּרוּחַ
- **Note**: קָנֶה ("reed") is a standard biblical term (1 Kings 14:15, Isa 42:3). The verb נוּעַ ("to shake, sway") is well attested in biblical Hebrew for swaying/trembling (Isa 7:2, Ps 99:1). The interrogative ה prefixed to the noun is classical biblical Hebrew syntax. Strong rendering.

### 11:11 — בִּילוּדֵי אִשָׁה for "among those born of women"
- **Hebrew**: לֹא קָם בִּילוּדֵי אִשָׁה גָּדוֹל מִיּוֹחָנָן הַמַּטְבִּיל
- **Note**: יְלוּדֵי אִשָׁה ("those born of woman") directly echoes Job 14:1 (אָדָם יְלוּד אִשָׁה) and 15:14, 25:4. This is an excellent biblical Hebrew parallel that a reader steeped in the OT would instantly recognize. הַמַּטְבִּיל ("the Baptizer/Immerser") is a necessary neologism — the concept of ritual baptism as a title is uniquely NT, though the root טבל appears in 2 Kings 5:14 for Naaman's immersion.

### 11:12 — נִתְפְּשָׂה בְיָד חֲזָקָה for βιάζεται
- **Hebrew**: מַלְכוּת הַשָׁמַיִם נִתְפְּשָׂה בְיָד חֲזָקָה
- **Note**: This is one of the most difficult verses in the NT to translate. The Greek βιάζεται can be either middle ("advances forcefully") or passive ("suffers violence"). Delitzsch renders with נִתְפְּשָׂה בְיָד חֲזָקָה ("is seized with a strong hand"), taking the passive interpretation. The phrase בְּיָד חֲזָקָה echoes the Exodus motif (Ex 3:19, 6:1, Deut 5:15), though here applied differently. The addition of "by a strong hand" is interpretive — it is not present in the Greek, which simply has βιάζεται.

### 11:17 — Musical/mourning parallelism
- **Hebrew**: חִלַּלְנוּ לָכֶם בַּחֲלִילִים וְלֹא רִקַּדְתֶּם קוֹנַנּוּ לָכֶם קִינָה וְלֹא סְפַדְתֶּם
- **Note**: Excellent parallelism in biblical style. חִלַּלְנוּ ... בַּחֲלִילִים is a satisfying paronomasia (the verb חלל "to pipe/play" with the noun חָלִיל "flute"). קוֹנַנּוּ ... קִינָה creates another strong alliterative pair (polal of קון "to lament" with קִינָה "lamentation," cf. 2 Sam 1:17, Jer 9:16). סְפַדְתֶּם ("you mourned") echoes the root ספד used for formal mourning (Gen 23:2, 2 Sam 1:12). Thoroughly biblical vocabulary and construction.

### 11:19 — זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא for "glutton and drunkard"
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא
- **Note**: This pair directly quotes Deut 21:20 (זוֹלֵל וְסֹבֵא — "glutton and drunkard"), the charge parents bring against a rebellious son. Delitzsch brilliantly preserves the exact OT diction the Greek φάγος καὶ οἰνοπότης alludes to. A Hebrew reader would immediately hear the Deuteronomic legal accusation behind Jesus' opponents' words, adding a layer of meaning the Greek alone does not convey.

### 11:25 — לָעוֹלֲלִים for "babes/infants"
- **Hebrew**: וְגִלִּיתָם לָעוֹלֲלִים
- **Note**: עוֹלֲלִים ("infants, sucklings") is strong biblical vocabulary (Lam 2:11, 4:4; Joel 2:16; Ps 8:3). It is a more vivid and specifically young age-range term than the Greek νήπιοι ("infants/children"), though both convey the same rhetorical contrast with "the wise and prudent."

### 11:28-30 — Sustained biblical register
- **Hebrew**: פְּנוּ אֵלַי כָּל-הָעֲמֵלִים וְהַטְּעוּנִים וַאֲנִי אָנִיחַ לָכֶם ... קַבְּלוּ עֲלֵיכֶם אֶת-עֻלִי ... וּמִצְאוּ מַרְגּוֹעַ לְנַפְשֹׁתֵיכֶם
- **Note**: This entire passage sustains an elevated biblical register. עֲמֵלִים ("those who toil") echoes Ecclesiastes vocabulary. עֹל ("yoke") is the standard biblical metaphor for servitude or instruction (1 Kings 12:4, Jer 27:8). מַרְגּוֹעַ ("rest") appears in Jer 6:16 in a strikingly parallel context: "find rest for your souls" (מִרְגּוֹעַ לְנַפְשׁוֹתֵיכֶם). Delitzsch clearly modeled this on the Jeremiah passage, creating a powerful intertextual echo. עָנָו וּשְׁפַל רוּחַ ("meek and lowly of spirit") echoes Isa 57:15.

## Other Notes

### 11:13 — Order of "prophets and law"
- The Greek has πάντες ... οἱ προφῆται καὶ ὁ νόμος ("all the prophets and the law"), with "prophets" before "law" — an unusual order, since Jewish convention places Torah before Prophets. Delitzsch follows this unusual order: כָל-הַנְּבִיאִים וְהַתּוֹרָה ("all the prophets and the Torah"). The Vulgate similarly: "prophetæ et lex." All three preserve the marked ordering, which may be theologically significant (emphasizing the prophetic witness to John's role).

### 11:14 — Elijah identification
- The identification of John with Elijah (אֵלִיָּה הֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא, "Elijah who is to come") is a key christological/eschatological statement rooted in Malachi 3:23 (4:5 in English). All three versions agree. The Hebrew wording הֶעָתִיד לָבוֹא uses a participial construction that, while not strictly biblical (עָתִיד in this sense is Mishnaic), effectively conveys the future expectation.

### 11:21-24 — Woe oracles
- The woe oracles against Chorazin, Bethsaida, and Capernaum use the classic prophetic אוֹי ("woe"), matching the Greek Οὐαί and Latin "Væ." The extended comparison with Tyre, Sidon, and Sodom echoes Ezekiel 26-28 (Tyre oracles) and Genesis 19 (Sodom). The Hebrew שָׁבוּ ("they returned/repented") for μετενόησαν ("they repented") uses the standard biblical Hebrew concept of repentance as שׁוּב ("turning/returning"), which is theologically richer than the Greek cognitive framework of μετάνοια ("change of mind").
