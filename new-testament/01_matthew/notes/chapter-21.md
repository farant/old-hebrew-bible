# Matthew Chapter 21 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 46 verses align across all three sources (SBLGNT, Delitzsch, Vulgate). No versification mismatches.

## Content Divergences

### 21:4-5 -- Zechariah quotation fulfillment formula
- **Issue**: The Greek has a simple fulfillment formula (Touto de gegonen hina plerothe), while the Vulgate adds "totum" (Hoc autem totum factum est). The SBLGNT omits "totum/holon" -- this is a minor but real textual difference.
- **Greek**: Touto de gegonen hina plerothe to rhethen
- **Latin**: Hoc autem totum factum est, ut adimpleretur
- **Hebrew**: ve-khol-zot haytah lemallot (follows the Vulgate reading with kol-zot "all this")
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/TR tradition with kol-zot rather than the critical Greek text. The SBLGNT critical apparatus marks the de with a variant indicator.

### 21:7 -- Riding on the animals
- **Issue**: The Greek epekathisen epano auton ("he sat upon them") is ambiguous -- "them" could refer to the garments or the animals. The Vulgate has "eum desuper sedere fecerunt" (they made him sit on top). The Greek has him sitting; the Vulgate makes the disciples the agents of the seating.
- **Greek**: kai epekathisen epano auton
- **Latin**: et eum desuper sedere fecerunt
- **Hebrew**: vayyoshivuhu aleyhem (they caused him to sit upon them -- causative hifil, follows Vulgate)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the hifil vayyoshivuhu, aligning with the Vulgate's causative reading rather than the Greek's simpler intransitive.

### 21:12 -- Temple designation
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads to hieron (temple complex), while the Vulgate has "templum Dei" (temple of God) and Delitzsch has miqdash ha-elohim (sanctuary of God). The addition of "Dei/of God" is a Vulgate/Byzantine addition not in the best Greek manuscripts.
- **Greek**: to hieron (just "the temple")
- **Latin**: templum Dei
- **Hebrew**: miqdash ha-elohim
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/Byzantine reading. Additionally, Delitzsch uses miqdash ("sanctuary") rather than heykal ("temple/palace"), which is a reasonable choice but note that the Hebrew shifts to miqdash in verse 12 then back to discussing the same space.

### 21:16 -- Psalm 8:2 quotation divergence
- **Issue**: A significant divergence. The Greek (following LXX) quotes Psalm 8:3(2) as katertiso ainon ("you have prepared/perfected praise"), while the Hebrew (following MT Ps 8:3) has yissadta oz ("you have established strength/might"). These are fundamentally different readings.
- **Greek**: ek stomatos nepion kai thelazonotn katertiso ainon (from the mouth of infants and nursing babes you have perfected praise)
- **Latin**: ex ore infantium et lactentium perfecisti laudem (follows Greek/LXX)
- **Hebrew**: mippi olelim veyoneqim yissadta oz (from the mouth of babes and sucklings you have established strength)
- **Note**: Delitzsch deliberately follows the MT text of Psalm 8:3 rather than the LXX, which is the text Jesus is actually quoting according to all Greek manuscripts. This is a theologically significant choice -- the Greek/Latin "praise" reading supports the immediate context (children praising in the temple) more directly than the MT "strength" reading. Delitzsch appears to privilege the Hebrew OT source over the NT Greek quotation.

### 21:25 -- Verse split
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT includes the beginning of the internal deliberation within verse 25 (Ean eipomen ex ouranou, erei hemin Dia ti oun ouk episteusate auto). The Vulgate places the same material in verse 25, but both the Greek and Latin include both the question AND the start of the deliberation in v.25. Delitzsch's Hebrew for v.25 is truncated -- it contains the question and the start of the deliberation but omits the "if we say from heaven" reasoning, placing it in v.26 instead.
- **Greek**: (v.25) ...Ean eipomen Ex ouranou, erei hemin Dia ti oun ouk episteusate auto
- **Latin**: (v.26) Si dixerimus, e caelo, dicet nobis : Quare ergo non credidistis illi
- **Hebrew**: (v.26) im-nomar mishamayim ve-amar eleynu maddua efo lo he'emantem bo
- **Note**: Both the Hebrew and the Latin place the "if we say from heaven" clause in verse 26, while the SBLGNT includes it in verse 25. This is a minor versification difference in the internal division of the passage.

### 21:29 -- Two Sons parable textual variant
- **Issue**: There is a major textual variant in this parable. In some manuscripts the first son says "I will not" then repents (as in SBLGNT), while in others the order is reversed. The SBLGNT follows the reading where the first son refuses then goes; the Vulgate agrees (Nolo, then poenitentia motus abiit).
- **Greek**: Ou thelo, hysteron de metameletheis apelthen
- **Latin**: Nolo. Postea autem, poenitentia motus, abiit
- **Hebrew**: lo hafatsti ve-acharey-khen nicham vayelekh
- **Note**: All three texts agree here, but this verse is the site of a famous textual variant where some manuscripts reverse which son said what, affecting the answer in v.31.

### 21:44 -- Disputed verse
- **Issue**: Verse 44 is textually disputed in the Greek tradition. The SBLGNT includes it but marks it with double brackets, indicating significant doubt about its originality. It may be an interpolation from Luke 20:18. The Hebrew also brackets it. The Vulgate includes it without reservation.
- **Greek**: [Kai ho peson epi ton lithon touton synthlasthesetai; eph' hon d' an pese likmhsei auton.]
- **Latin**: Et qui ceciderit super lapidem istum, confringetur : super quem vero ceciderit, conteret eum.
- **Hebrew**: [vehannofel al-ha'even hahi yishaver va'asher tippol alav tishchaqehu]
- **Note**: Delitzsch appropriately marks this verse with brackets, reflecting the textual uncertainty. The verse is absent from key witnesses (D, 33, some Old Latin, Syriac Sinaiticus).

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 21:3 -- tsarikh (modern Hebrew flavor)
- **Hebrew**: ha-adon tsarikh lahem
- **Note**: The word tsarikh is post-biblical Hebrew; in biblical Hebrew one would expect tserekh lo or a verbal construction like tsarekh/hephets. Tsarikh as a predicate adjective meaning "needs" is characteristic of Mishnaic/modern Hebrew rather than biblical idiom.

### 21:5 -- Excellent Zechariah 9:9 echo
- **Hebrew**: imru le-vat-tsiyyon hinneh malkekh yavo lakh ani ve-rokhev al-chamor ve-al-ayir ben-atonot
- **Note**: Delitzsch skillfully renders this as a near-quotation of Zechariah 9:9, using authentic biblical Hebrew vocabulary (ani, rokhev, ayir ben-atonot). This is one of Delitzsch's best renderings, achieving natural biblical Hebrew that simultaneously functions as the Zechariah citation.

### 21:9 -- Hosha-na rendering
- **Hebrew**: hosha-na le-ven-david barukh habba be-shem YHVH hosha-na bammeromim
- **Note**: Excellent. Delitzsch correctly renders Hosanna as the Hebrew original hosha-na ("save, please!") and quotes Psalm 118:26 verbatim (barukh habba be-shem YHVH). The use of the Tetragrammaton is notable and appropriate.

### 21:13 -- me'arat paritsim
- **Hebrew**: ki veyti beyt tefillah yiqqare ve-attem samtem oto lime'arat paritsim
- **Note**: Good biblical Hebrew. Me'arat paritsim ("den of robbers") echoes Jeremiah 7:11 (hame'arah ha-zot...paritsim). Paritsim is authentic biblical vocabulary. Beyt tefillah comes directly from Isaiah 56:7.

### 21:33 -- Vineyard parable vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: ish ba'al-bayit hayah asher nata kerem vayyaas gader saviv lo vayyachtsov yeqev vayyiven migdal betocho vayyittenehu el-koremim vayelekh bederekh merachoq
- **Note**: Outstanding rendering that closely parallels Isaiah 5:1-2 (the Song of the Vineyard). Key biblical terms: nata kerem, yeqev, migdal -- all directly from Isaiah. The phrase vayelekh bederekh merachoq is slightly wordy; the Greek apedemesin could be rendered more concisely as vayyissa, but Delitzsch's expansion is clear.

### 21:42 -- Psalm 118 quotation
- **Hebrew**: even ma'asu habbonim haytah le-rosh pinnah me-et YHVH haytah zot hi niflat be-eyneinu
- **Note**: Delitzsch quotes Psalm 118:22-23 verbatim from the MT, which is the ideal approach for OT citations. The Hebrew reads naturally as biblical poetry.

## Other Notes

### 21:28-32 -- Two Sons parable and Western text
The parable of the Two Sons (21:28-32) is one of the most complex textual problems in the NT. The Western text (Codex D) reverses the order, making the second son the one who refuses then obeys, and changing the answer in v.31 to "the last" (ho hysteros/eschatos). The SBLGNT, Vulgate, and Delitzsch all follow the majority reading, but a reviewer should be aware that some scholars (including those behind the UBS committee) have debated which reading is original.

### 21:43 -- Kingdom transferred to a nation
This verse (the kingdom of God will be taken from you and given to a nation producing its fruit) is unique to Matthew and absent from the Markan and Lukan parallels. All three texts agree on the content. The Hebrew goy ("nation," singular) correctly renders the Greek ethnei (singular).
