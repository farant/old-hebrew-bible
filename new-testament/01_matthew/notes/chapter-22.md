# Matthew Chapter 22 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 46 verses align across all three sources (SBLGNT, Delitzsch, Vulgate). No versification mismatches.

## Content Divergences

### 22:2 -- "man who was a king" vs "king"
- **Issue**: The Greek has anthropo basilei ("a man, a king" or "a royal man"), literally "a human king." The Vulgate renders homini regi. Delitzsch adds basar va-dam ("flesh and blood"), a rabbinic idiom not present in any textual tradition.
- **Greek**: anthropo basilei
- **Latin**: homini regi
- **Hebrew**: le-melekh basar va-dam
- **Note**: Delitzsch inserts the Talmudic expression basar va-dam ("a king of flesh and blood"), which is a standard rabbinic parable opening (mashal le-melekh basar va-dam). While this gives the Hebrew a natural midrashic flavor, it adds content not in the Greek or Latin. This is an interpretive expansion.

### 22:7 -- Vulgate adds "cum audisset"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "cum audisset" (when he heard), absent from the Greek. The SBLGNT reads simply ho de basileus orgisthe ("the king was angry"). Delitzsch follows the Greek, omitting any equivalent of "when he heard."
- **Greek**: ho de basileus orgisthe
- **Latin**: Rex autem cum audisset, iratus est
- **Hebrew**: vayyiqtsof hammelekh
- **Note**: Delitzsch correctly follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate here.

### 22:13 -- Binding order
- **Issue**: The Greek reads desantes autou podas kai cheiras ("having bound his feet and hands" -- feet first). The Vulgate has "ligatis manibus et pedibus" (hands and feet -- hands first). Delitzsch has isru yadav ve-raglav (hands and feet), following the Vulgate word order.
- **Greek**: podas kai cheiras (feet and hands)
- **Latin**: manibus et pedibus (hands and feet)
- **Hebrew**: yadav ve-raglav (his hands and his feet)
- **Note**: A minor divergence. Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/TR order (hands then feet) rather than the SBLGNT critical text (feet then hands).

### 22:20 -- Vulgate adds "Jesus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "Jesus" as the subject (ait illis Jesus), while the SBLGNT has simply kai legei autois ("and he says to them"). Delitzsch follows the Greek, omitting the name.
- **Greek**: kai legei autois
- **Latin**: Et ait illis Jesus
- **Hebrew**: vayyomer aleyhem
- **Note**: Minor. Delitzsch correctly follows the Greek.

### 22:30 -- "angels of God" vs "angels in heaven"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has hos aggeloi theou en to ourano ("like angels of God in heaven"), with variant markers on theou. Some manuscripts read simply "angels in heaven" without "of God." The Vulgate has "sicut angeli Dei in caelo." Delitzsch has ke-malakhey elohim bashamayim, matching the reading with "of God."
- **Greek**: hos aggeloi theou en to ourano (with textual variant on theou)
- **Latin**: sicut angeli Dei in caelo
- **Hebrew**: ke-malakhey elohim bashamayim
- **Note**: All three agree on the "angels of God in heaven" reading, though the SBLGNT flags theou as a variant. The shorter reading (without theou) is found in important witnesses.

### 22:35 -- nomikos (lawyer) vs "doctor of the law"
- **Issue**: The Greek has nomikos ("one learned in the law, lawyer"). The Vulgate renders legis doctor. Delitzsch translates as chakham ("wise man"), which is a significant interpretive shift -- nomikos refers to a Torah-legal expert, not a general sage.
- **Greek**: nomikos (lawyer, legal expert)
- **Latin**: legis doctor (teacher of the law)
- **Hebrew**: chakham (wise man)
- **Note**: Delitzsch's chakham is too generic. Biblical Hebrew offers better options: yodea torah, or even the post-biblical but contextually apt sofer. The Greek nomikos specifies legal expertise, which chakham does not capture.

### 22:36 -- "great commandment" vs "greatest commandment"
- **Issue**: The Greek has poia entole megale en to nomo ("which commandment is great in the law"), using the positive form megale rather than the superlative megiste. The Vulgate uses "magnum" (great, not greatest). Delitzsch has mitsva gedolah, correctly using the positive form.
- **Greek**: poia entole megale
- **Latin**: quod est mandatum magnum
- **Hebrew**: ey-zo hi mitsvah gedolah battorah
- **Note**: All three agree. The question is about which commandment is "great" (qualitative), not "greatest" (superlative), though context implies superlative meaning.

### 22:36 -- "nor the Son" omission in the Vulgate (anticipating 24:36)
This is actually relevant to 24:36, noted here for cross-reference.

### 22:44 -- Psalm 110:1 quotation
- **Issue**: The Greek reads Eipen kyrios to kyrio mou ("The Lord said to my Lord"). The Vulgate has Dixit Dominus Domino meo. Delitzsch renders ne'um YHVH la-adoni ("Oracle of YHVH to my lord"), directly quoting Psalm 110:1 from the MT.
- **Greek**: Eipen kyrios to kyrio mou
- **Latin**: Dixit Dominus Domino meo
- **Hebrew**: ne'um YHVH la-adoni
- **Note**: Delitzsch correctly uses ne'um (prophetic oracle formula) rather than amar, matching the MT of Psalm 110:1 exactly. The use of the Tetragrammaton for the first "Lord" and adoni for the second preserves the crucial theological distinction that the Greek kyrios/kyrios obscures.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 22:1 -- Awkward double introduction
- **Hebrew**: vayyosef yeshua dabber aleyhem bimeshalim vayya'an vayyomar
- **Note**: The Hebrew has a double opening: "and Jesus continued to speak to them in parables, and he answered and said." The vayyosef...dabber construction is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Gen 18:29), but combining it with vayya'an vayyomar creates redundancy. The Greek has a simpler flow: apokritheis...eipen en parabolais.

### 22:12 -- re'i (my friend)
- **Hebrew**: re'i eykh bata hennah
- **Note**: The Greek hetaire ("companion, comrade" -- used in a somewhat formal/ironic sense) is rendered as re'i ("my friend"). Re'i is acceptable biblical Hebrew (cf. 2 Sam 15:37, re'eh), though reyakh or the fuller form re'iy might be more natural. The form works.

### 22:21 -- Excellent rendering of "render unto Caesar"
- **Hebrew**: lakhen tenu le-qeysar et asher le-qeysar ve-le-lohim et asher le-lohim
- **Note**: This is clean, idiomatic biblical Hebrew. The parallel structure with et asher le- is elegant and natural. Delitzsch avoids unnecessarily complex constructions and achieves a memorable formulation.

### 22:24 -- Levirate marriage (yibbum)
- **Hebrew**: ish ki-yamut uvanim eyn-lo ve-yibem achiv et-ishto ve-heqim zera le-achiv
- **Note**: Excellent. Delitzsch uses the technical biblical Hebrew term yibem (to perform levirate marriage), directly from Deuteronomy 25:5. The phrasing closely parallels the Deuteronomy source text and reads as natural biblical legal Hebrew.

### 22:25 -- rosh'n construct
- **Hebrew**: ve-ittanu hayu shivah achim ve-harishon nasa ishah vayyamat
- **Note**: Good narrative Hebrew. The construction with ve-ittanu hayu parallels biblical usage and the waw-consecutive narrative flow is natural.

### 22:29 -- Scriptures as "ha-ketuvim"
- **Hebrew**: to'im attem ba'asher eynkhem yodim et-hakketuvim
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders "the Scriptures" (tas graphas) as ha-ketuvim, which in later Jewish usage specifically denotes the third division of the Tanakh (the Writings). In biblical Hebrew, ketuvim simply means "writings" and could serve, but the technical ambiguity is noteworthy. An alternative like ha-katuv or sifrey ha-qodesh might avoid the later terminological connotation.

### 22:33 -- torato (his Torah)
- **Hebrew**: vayyishma hamon ha'am vayyishtomemu al-torato
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders didache ("teaching") as torah, which in biblical Hebrew simply means "instruction/teaching" (from the root y-r-h, "to teach"). This is a good choice that uses the word in its original biblical sense rather than the later technical sense of "the Torah/Pentateuch."

### 22:37 -- Shema quotation
- **Hebrew**: ve-ahavta et YHVH eloheykha be-khol-levavkha u-ve-khol-nafshekha u-ve-khol-madda'ekha
- **Note**: Delitzsch closely follows Deuteronomy 6:5, with one key difference: the Greek has en hole te dianoia sou ("with all your mind"), which Delitzsch renders as madda'ekha ("your knowledge/mind") instead of the MT's me'odekha ("your might/very much"). This is correct for the NT context -- Jesus (or Matthew) has substituted dianoia for the Hebrew me'od, and Delitzsch follows the Greek here rather than simply quoting the MT, which is the right choice for a translation of the Greek NT.

## Other Notes

### 22:41-46 -- David's Son and David's Lord
This pericope (the question about David's son) is theologically central. Delitzsch's rendering of Psalm 110:1 is particularly significant because it preserves the Hebrew distinction between YHVH (the God of Israel) and adoni (my lord, a human superior) that the Greek kyrios/kyrios collapses. The Hebrew reader immediately grasps the Christological argument: how can David call his own descendant adoni?
