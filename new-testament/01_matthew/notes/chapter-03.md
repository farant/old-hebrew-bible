# Matthew Chapter 3 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 17 verses align across all three sources (Greek SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, Clementine Vulgate).

## Content Divergences

### 3:1-2 — Verse boundary and structure
- **Issue**: The Greek has verse 1 as a complete statement about John's arrival and preaching, then verse 2 as a separate sentence beginning with a variant-marked καί (and). The Hebrew merges the two by ending verse 1 with לֵאמֹר (saying), making verse 2 the direct content of John's proclamation. The Vulgate keeps them as separate sentences.
- **Greek**: v1: παραγίνεται Ἰωάννης ὁ βαπτιστὴς κηρύσσων ... v2: ⸀καὶ λέγων· Μετανοεῖτε
- **Latin**: v1: venit Joannes Baptista praedicans ... v2: et dicens : Poenitentiam agite
- **Hebrew**: v1: קָם יוֹחָנָן הַמַּטְבִּיל וַיְהִי קֹרֵא בְמִדְבַּר יְהוּדָה לֵאמֹר v2: שׁוּבוּ כִּי מַלְכוּת הַשָׁמַיִם קָרְבָה לָבוֹא
- **Note**: The Hebrew handling is natural for biblical narrative, where לֵאמֹר commonly introduces direct speech. The Greek participle λέγων (saying) at the start of v2 serves the same function.

### 3:2 — "Repent" / "Do penance" / "Return"
- **Issue**: A significant theological divergence in translation. The Greek μετανοεῖτε means "change your mind/thinking." The Vulgate renders this as "Poenitentiam agite" (do penance), which shaped Catholic penitential theology. Delitzsch uses שׁוּבוּ (return/turn back), the classic Hebrew prophetic call to repentance.
- **Greek**: Μετανοεῖτε — "change your mind"
- **Latin**: Poenitentiam agite — "do penance"
- **Hebrew**: שׁוּבוּ — "return"
- **Note**: Delitzsch's שׁוּבוּ is arguably the best translation for a Hebrew Bible context. The root שׁוב is the standard prophetic repentance vocabulary (Hosea 14:2, Joel 2:12, Jeremiah 3:12, etc.). This was one of Luther's key criticisms of the Vulgate — that "poenitentiam agite" introduced a sacramental concept not present in the Greek. The Hebrew captures the prophetic overtone that Matthew likely intended given John's role as the prophetic forerunner.

### 3:3 — Isaiah 40:3 quotation
- **Issue**: The Greek reads ὑπό for the preposition introducing Isaiah (variant marked with ⸀), while the standard reading is διά (through). The Hebrew follows the MT of Isaiah 40:3 closely but makes one notable change: where Isaiah MT has פַּנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ יְהוָה יַשְּׁרוּ בָּעֲרָבָה מְסִלָּה לֵאלֹהֵינוּ (make straight in the desert a highway for our God), Delitzsch has יַשְׁרוּ מְסִלּוֹתָיו (make straight HIS paths), following Matthew's Greek rather than Isaiah MT.
- **Greek**: εὐθείας ποιεῖτε τὰς τρίβους αὐτοῦ — "make straight his paths"
- **Latin**: rectas facite semitas ejus — "make straight his paths"
- **Hebrew**: יַשְׁרוּ מְסִלּוֹתָיו — "make straight his highways"
- **Note**: Delitzsch partially follows Isaiah MT (using the vocabulary פַּנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ יְהוָֹה) but departs from it to follow Matthew's Greek for the second half (מְסִלּוֹתָיו "his highways" instead of Isaiah's "a highway for our God"). This is a reasonable compromise — it sounds like Isaiah but carries Matthew's christological reading.

### 3:7 — "his baptism" variant
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has ἐπὶ τὸ βάπτισμα ⸀αὐτοῦ (to his baptism), with a variant marker on αὐτοῦ. Some manuscripts omit "his." The Vulgate has "ad baptismum suum" (to his baptism). The Hebrew omits the possessive entirely: לְהִטָּבֵל (to be immersed).
- **Greek**: ἐπὶ τὸ βάπτισμα ⸀αὐτοῦ — "to his baptism"
- **Latin**: ad baptismum suum — "to his baptism"
- **Hebrew**: לְהִטָּבֵל — "to immerse themselves" (no possessive)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the Niphal infinitive construct, which is clean and natural but loses the possessive "his" that both Greek and Latin include. This changes the nuance slightly — Greek/Latin emphasize it as John's baptism specifically.

### 3:8 — Singular vs. plural "fruit"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads καρπὸν ἄξιον (singular: "fruit worthy"). The Vulgate also has singular "fructum dignum." Delitzsch has פְרִי רָאוּי (singular fruit fitting). All three agree on singular here, though Luke 3:8 has the plural "fruits" — no divergence in Matthew, but worth noting for cross-reference.
- **Note**: No action needed; all three traditions agree.

### 3:11 — "carry his sandals" vs. "untie his sandals"
- **Issue**: Matthew's Greek has τὰ ὑποδήματα βαστάσαι (to carry his sandals), which differs from Mark 1:7/Luke 3:16/John 1:27 where the expression is "untie the strap of his sandals." The Vulgate faithfully renders "calceamenta portare" (to carry sandals). Delitzsch has מִשְּׂאֵת נְעָלָיו (from carrying his sandals).
- **Greek**: τὰ ὑποδήματα βαστάσαι — "to carry the sandals"
- **Latin**: calceamenta portare — "to carry sandals"
- **Hebrew**: מִשְּׂאֵת נְעָלָיו — "from carrying his sandals"
- **Note**: All three Matthew texts agree on "carry" rather than "untie," which is Matthew's distinctive form of this saying. Delitzsch renders it faithfully. The word נְעָלָיו (his sandals) is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Deuteronomy 25:9-10, Ruth 4:7-8).

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:1 — "HaMatbil" for "the Baptist"
- **Hebrew**: יוֹחָנָן הַמַּטְבִּיל
- **Note**: Delitzsch creates הַמַּטְבִּיל (the Immerser/Baptizer) using the Hiphil participle of טבל. This is a natural Hebrew formation, though the word does not appear in the Hebrew Bible. The root טבל appears in the OT only in 2 Kings 5:14 (Naaman's immersion) and a few other places. The Hiphil participle is a reasonable coinage. The verb קָם (arose) for Greek παραγίνεται (appeared/arrived) is acceptable biblical usage though it slightly shifts the sense from "arrived on the scene" to "arose."

### 3:4 — Biblical clothing description
- **Hebrew**: וְיוֹחָנָן לְבוּשׁוֹ שְׂעַר גְּמַלִּים וְאֵזוֹר עוֹר בְּמָתְנָיו
- **Note**: Excellent biblical register. The vocabulary directly echoes 2 Kings 1:8's description of Elijah (אִישׁ בַּעַל שֵׂעָר וְאֵזוֹר עוֹר אָזוּר בְּמָתְנָיו), which is precisely the typological connection Matthew intends. Delitzsch's word choices make this Elijah echo unmistakable to a Hebrew reader.

### 3:7 — "yaldei tzif'onim"
- **Hebrew**: יַלְדֵי צִפְעוֹנִים
- **Note**: For γεννήματα ἐχιδνῶν (brood of vipers), Delitzsch uses יַלְדֵי צִפְעוֹנִים (children of vipers). The word צִפְעוֹנִי is a biblical Hebrew word for a venomous snake (Isaiah 11:8, 59:5, Proverbs 23:32). This is a strong biblical Hebrew choice. However, יַלְדֵי (children of) is slightly different from the Greek γεννήματα (offspring/produce), which has a more agricultural connotation. The biblical Hebrew יִלְדֵי or תּוֹלְדוֹת might have been closer, but the translation is effective.

### 3:10 — "garzen" for axe
- **Hebrew**: הַגַּרְזֶן
- **Note**: The word גַּרְזֶן appears in the Hebrew Bible in Deuteronomy 19:5, 20:19, 1 Kings 6:7, Isaiah 10:15, Jeremiah 10:3. This is a proper biblical Hebrew word choice for ἀξίνη (axe). The entire verse reads naturally in biblical Hebrew idiom.

### 3:12 — Threshing imagery
- **Hebrew**: וּבְיָדוֹ הַמִּזְרֶה וְזָרָה אֶת-גָּרְנוֹ וְאָסַף אֶת-דְּגָנוֹ אֶל-אוֹצָרוֹ וְאֶת-הַמֹּץ יִשְׂרְפֶנּוּ בָאֵשׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תִכְבֶּה
- **Note**: Superb biblical Hebrew for the winnowing metaphor. מִזְרֶה (winnowing fork) is from the root זרה (to scatter/winnow), found in Isaiah 30:24, Jeremiah 15:7. גֹּרֶן (threshing floor), דָּגָן (grain), אוֹצָר (storehouse), מֹץ (chaff) are all standard biblical agricultural vocabulary. The phrase אֵשׁ אֲשֶׁר לֹא תִכְבֶּה (fire which will not be quenched) echoes Isaiah 66:24. This is one of Delitzsch's finest verses in the chapter.

### 3:15 — "na'avah" for "fitting"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי כֵן נָאֲוָה לִשְׁנֵינוּ לְמַלֵּא כָּל-הַצְּדָקָה
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses נָאֲוָה (it is fitting/becoming) for Greek πρέπον (fitting). This is a good biblical word (Song of Songs 1:5, Psalm 33:1, 93:5), though its primary biblical context is aesthetic beauty rather than moral propriety. The Greek πρέπον is about what is proper/fitting, and נָאֲוָה captures this adequately. The Piel infinitive לְמַלֵּא (to fill/fulfill) for πληρῶσαι is standard.

## Other Notes

### 3:16-17 — Trinitarian theophany
These two verses present all three persons of the Trinity simultaneously: Jesus in the water, the Spirit descending as a dove, and the Father's voice from heaven. All three textual traditions preserve this scene identically without substantive variation. The Hebrew renders πνεῦμα θεοῦ as רוּחַ אֱלֹהִים (Spirit of God) — the same phrase as Genesis 1:2, creating a creation-new creation typological link that Hebrew readers would immediately recognize.

### 3:17 — Voice from heaven
- **Hebrew**: זֶה בְּנִי יְדִידִי אֲשֶׁר-רָצִיתִי בּוֹ
- **Note**: The Father's declaration echoes Psalm 2:7 (בְּנִי אַתָּה) and Isaiah 42:1 (the Servant in whom God delights). Delitzsch's יְדִידִי (my beloved) for ἀγαπητός is a strong choice — it is the same root as David's name (דָּוִד from ידד, beloved), connecting Jesus to the Davidic line through wordplay. The Greek ἐν ᾧ εὐδόκησα (in whom I am well pleased) becomes אֲשֶׁר-רָצִיתִי בּוֹ (in whom I have delighted), using the standard biblical Hebrew verb for divine favor (רצה, cf. Psalm 44:4, 149:4).
