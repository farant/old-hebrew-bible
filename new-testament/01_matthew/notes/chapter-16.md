# Matthew Chapter 16 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 28 verses align across all three sources. The SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, and Clementine Vulgate all contain verses 1-28.

However, there is a significant structural note on verses 2b-3:

### 16:2b-3 — Weather signs passage (disputed text)
- **Issue**: The passage about red sky at evening/morning (16:2b-3) is enclosed in critical apparatus marks in the SBLGNT, indicating that its authenticity is disputed. Major manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) omit these verses entirely. The passage is present in the Vulgate and in Delitzsch's Hebrew (following the TR/Byzantine tradition). The SBLGNT includes it but with critical brackets.
- **Note**: If following the shorter reading, verse 3 would be absent entirely, and verse 4 would follow directly after the first clause of verse 2. The Vulgate includes the full passage, and Delitzsch's translation does as well. The critical apparatus of the SBLGNT marks this as textually uncertain.

### 16:3-4 — Vulgate rearranges content
- **Issue**: In the SBLGNT and Delitzsch, verse 3 ends with the rebuke about not discerning the signs of the times, and verse 4 begins with "a wicked and adulterous generation." In the Vulgate, the content about discerning the face of heaven but not the signs of the times appears at the beginning of what is marked as verse 4: "Faciem ergo caeli dijudicare nostis: signa autem temporum non potestis scire?" followed immediately by the "wicked generation" clause. This means the Vulgate essentially places the "signs of the times" rebuke in verse 4, while the Greek and Hebrew have it in verse 3.
- **Greek (v.3)**: ... to men prosopon tou ouranou ginoskete diakrinein, ta de semeia ton kairon ou dynasthe.
- **Latin (v.4 beginning)**: Faciem ergo caeli dijudicare nostis: signa autem temporum non potestis scire?
- **Hebrew (v.3)**: ... hanefim attem makkirim et penei ha-shamayim ve-otot ha-ittim lo takkiru
- **Note**: This is a versification alignment issue. The same content is present in all three, but the Vulgate assigns part of it to a different verse number.

## Content Divergences

### 16:3 — "hypocrites" present in Hebrew but not in SBLGNT main text
- **Issue**: Delitzsch includes "hanefim attem" ("hypocrites you are") in verse 3, which corresponds to the reading "hypokritai" found in some Greek manuscripts and the TR. The SBLGNT main text does not include "hypocrites" here (it is in the disputed 2b-3 section). The Vulgate does not include the word "hypocrites" either.
- **Greek**: (disputed passage, "hypokritai" in some witnesses)
- **Latin**: (no "hypocritae" in this verse)
- **Hebrew**: hanefim attem (hypocrites, you)
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the TR tradition that includes "hypocrites" in this verse, departing from both the SBLGNT critical text and the Vulgate.

### 16:4 — "Jonah the prophet"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads simply "the sign of Jonah" (to semeion Iona). The Vulgate has "signum Jonae prophetae" ("the sign of Jonah the prophet"). Delitzsch has "ot Yonah ha-navi" ("the sign of Jonah the prophet"), agreeing with the Vulgate against the SBLGNT.
- **Greek**: to semeion Iona (the sign of Jonah)
- **Latin**: signum Jonae prophetae (the sign of Jonah the prophet)
- **Hebrew**: ot Yonah ha-navi (the sign of Jonah the prophet)
- **Note**: Both Delitzsch and the Vulgate add "the prophet" to Jonah, following the TR/Byzantine tradition. The SBLGNT critical text omits "the prophet."

### 16:20 — "he is the Christ" vs. "he is Jesus the Christ"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "hoti autos estin ho christos" ("that he is the Christ"). The Vulgate reads "quia ipse esset Jesus Christus" ("that he himself was Jesus Christ"), adding "Jesus." Delitzsch has "ki hu ha-mashiah" ("that he is the Messiah"), matching the SBLGNT without "Jesus."
- **Greek**: hoti autos estin ho christos (that he is the Christ)
- **Latin**: quia ipse esset Jesus Christus (that he was Jesus Christ)
- **Hebrew**: ki hu ha-mashiah (that he is the Messiah)
- **Note**: The Vulgate adds "Jesus" to the confession, making it "Jesus Christ" rather than simply "the Christ." This follows a variant reading not found in the best Greek manuscripts.

### 16:21 — Order of Jewish authorities
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT lists "the elders and chief priests and scribes" (ton presbyteron kai archiereon kai grammateon). The Vulgate lists "a senioribus, et scribis, et principibus sacerdotum" ("from the elders and scribes and chief priests") -- a different order. Delitzsch has "ha-zqenim ve-rashei ha-kohanim ve-ha-sofrim" ("the elders and the chief priests and the scribes"), matching the Greek order.
- **Greek**: ton presbyteron kai archiereon kai grammateon (elders, chief priests, scribes)
- **Latin**: a senioribus, et scribis, et principibus sacerdotum (elders, scribes, chief priests)
- **Hebrew**: ha-zqenim ve-rashei ha-kohanim ve-ha-sofrim (elders, chief priests, scribes)
- **Note**: The Vulgate reverses the order of "scribes" and "chief priests." This is a minor but real divergence from the Greek.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 16:6 — "leaven" as se'or
- **Hebrew**: se'or ha-perushim ve-ha-tsadduqim (the leaven of the Pharisees and the Sadducees)
- **Note**: Se'or is the correct biblical Hebrew word for leaven/sourdough (Exodus 12:15, 12:19, 13:7, Leviticus 2:11). This is distinct from hamets (leavened bread), and Delitzsch correctly uses se'or (the leavening agent itself) rather than hamets (the leavened product), matching the Greek zyme precisely. This is the same word he used in 13:33 for the leaven parable.

### 16:13 — "Caesarea of Philip" as gelilot Qeisariah shel-Filippos
- **Hebrew**: gelilot Qeisariah shel-Filippos (the districts of Caesarea of Philip)
- **Note**: Gelilot (districts/regions) is an excellent biblical word choice (Joshua 22:10-11, Joel 4:4). However, the relative particle shel ("of/belonging to") is Mishnaic/post-biblical Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use the construct chain or asher le- to express possession. The form "Qeisariah shel-Filippos" reads more like a Talmudic place designation than biblical prose.

### 16:16 — Peter's Confession: ha-mashiah ben-elohim hayyim
- **Hebrew**: attah hu ha-mashiah ben-elohim hayyim (You are the Messiah, son of the living God)
- **Note**: This is a strong rendering. Mashiah is the exact biblical Hebrew word behind christos. The phrase elohim hayyim ("the living God") is deeply biblical: it appears in Deuteronomy 5:26, Joshua 3:10, 1 Samuel 17:26, 17:36, Jeremiah 10:10, 23:36, and Daniel 6:21, 27. Delitzsch's word order (ha-mashiah ben-elohim hayyim) creates a natural construct chain. The confession reads as authentic biblical Hebrew.

### 16:17 — "Bar-Yonah" and "flesh and blood"
- **Hebrew**: ashreikha Shim'on Bar-Yonah ki-vasar va-dam lo gillah lekha et-zot ki im-avi she-ba-shamayim
- **Note**: Ashreikha ("blessed are you") is thoroughly biblical (Ps 128:2, Deut 33:29, 1 Kgs 10:8). The Aramaic patronymic Bar-Yonah is preserved correctly. The idiom basar va-dam ("flesh and blood") is Mishnaic (Avot 4:22) rather than biblical -- in biblical Hebrew the pair is basar va-dam in a different context (bloodshed/violence). The relative she-ba-shamayim ("who is in heaven") uses the Mishnaic she- particle rather than biblical asher. A more biblical construction would be avi asher ba-shamayim.

### 16:18 — The Rock saying: petra/petros as tsur/Petros
- **Hebrew**: ki attah Petros ve-al ha-tsur hazeh evneh et-qehillati ve-sha'arei she'ol lo yigberu aleha
- **Note**: This is one of the most theologically significant verses in the NT. Delitzsch renders petra as tsur ("rock/cliff"), which is the standard biblical Hebrew word for a great rock or cliff (Deut 32:4, 32:15, 32:18, Ps 18:3, Isa 26:4). The wordplay between Petros (the name) and petra (the rock) is partially lost since tsur does not sound like Petros. Some Hebrew translations use "kefa" (the Aramaic word behind Petros) for both, which preserves the wordplay better.

The word qehillah for ekklesia ("church/assembly") is an interesting choice. Biblical Hebrew has both qahal (assembly, Deut 5:22, 9:10) and edah (congregation, Num 1:2). Qehillah is found in late biblical Hebrew (Neh 5:7) and is cognate with qahal. It is a defensible choice but edah or qahal might be more recognizably biblical.

Sha'arei she'ol ("gates of Sheol") is superb -- directly paralleling Isaiah 38:10 (sha'arei she'ol) and evoking the biblical cosmology of Sheol's entrance. The Greek pylai hadou is rendered perfectly.

### 16:19 — Keys and binding/loosing
- **Hebrew**: maftekhot malkhut ha-shamayim ... kol-asher te'esor al-ha-arets asur yihyeh ba-shamayim ve-kol-asher tattir al-ha-arets muttar yihyeh ba-shamayim
- **Note**: Maftekhot (keys) appears in Judges 3:25 and Isaiah 22:22 (mafteah), making it a solid biblical word. The binding/loosing language (esor/hittir, asur/muttar) directly corresponds to the Mishnaic technical terms for halakhic prohibition and permission (cf. m. Shabbat, m. Eduyot). In rabbinic Hebrew, "to bind" (asar) means to declare forbidden and "to loose" (hittir) means to declare permitted. Delitzsch brilliantly uses exactly this rabbinic-halakhic terminology, which illuminates the original Semitic background of Jesus's words. The roots themselves (aleph-samekh-resh and nun-tav-resh) are biblical, though the technical halakhic usage is Mishnaic.

### 16:23 — "Get behind me, Satan"
- **Hebrew**: sur me-alai ha-satan mikhshol attah li (depart from upon me, the Satan; a stumbling block you are to me)
- **Note**: The Greek hypage opiso mou, Satana literally means "go behind me, Satan." Delitzsch renders this as sur me-alai ("depart from upon me"), which is stronger -- it means "get away from me" rather than "go behind me." The Greek opiso mou ("behind me") could mean either "get behind me" (take a subordinate position) or "go away from me." Delitzsch chooses the latter interpretation. The word mikhshol (stumbling block) is excellent biblical Hebrew (Lev 19:14, Isa 8:14, Ezek 3:20).

### 16:24 — "cross" as tseluvo
- **Hebrew**: ve-nasa et-tseluvo (and let him carry his cross)
- **Note**: Tselav / tseluvo (his cross) is not a biblical Hebrew word. It derives from the root ts-l-v and enters Hebrew through Aramaic/Syriac (tseliva). There is no biblical Hebrew word for "cross" since crucifixion was not a Near Eastern punishment in the biblical period. Delitzsch's choice is standard but inherently post-biblical.

### 16:26 — "ransom for his soul" -- pidyon nafsho
- **Hebrew**: mah yitten ha-adam pidyon nafsho (what will a man give as a ransom for his soul)
- **Note**: This is an outstanding biblical Hebrew rendering. The phrase directly echoes Psalm 49:8-9 (lo padoh yifdeh ish, lo yitten le-lohim kofro, "No man can redeem his brother, nor give to God a ransom for him"). The word pidyon (ransom/redemption price) appears in Exodus 21:30, Numbers 3:49, and Psalm 49:9. Delitzsch's translation connects Jesus's words explicitly to the Psalm 49 passage about the impossibility of self-redemption, which is theologically illuminating.

## Other Notes

### 16:13-20 — Peter's Confession at Caesarea Philippi
This is one of the most important christological passages in the Synoptics. The triple-text comparison is illuminating:
- The Greek ho christos ho huios tou theou tou zontos ("the Christ, the Son of the living God") uses the full christological title.
- The Latin preserves this exactly: "Christus, Filius Dei vivi."
- The Hebrew ha-mashiah ben-elohim hayyim places the confession squarely in OT theological vocabulary, where "the anointed one" (mashiah) and "the living God" (elohim hayyim) are both loaded biblical terms.

The binding and loosing terminology (16:19) is particularly significant when read in Hebrew, since the rabbinic halakhic background becomes explicit. This is a case where the Hebrew translation actually illuminates the original Semitic context of a saying that was transmitted in Greek.

### 16:18 — Ecclesiology: ekklesia as qehillah
This is one of only two places in the Gospels where ekklesia appears (the other being Matt 18:17). The Hebrew qehillah connects the "church" concept to the biblical qahal YHWH ("assembly of the LORD," Deut 23:2-4, 1 Chr 28:8, Mic 2:5). This lexical choice grounds the NT concept in OT covenant-assembly language, which may reflect the original Aramaic/Hebrew background of Jesus's saying.

### 16:2b-3 — Textual criticism note
The weather-signs passage (red sky at evening/morning) is one of the best-known textual variants in the Gospels. Its absence from the oldest Greek manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and several early versions suggests it may be a later interpolation, possibly drawn from popular proverbial material. Its presence in the Vulgate (and thus in Delitzsch's Vorlage) but its disputed status in the SBLGNT makes this a notable point where the three textual traditions diverge on the question of authenticity rather than content.

### 16:28 — "taste death"
- **Hebrew**: lo yit'amu ta'am mitah (they will not taste the taste of death)
- **Note**: The phrase "taste death" (geusontai thanatou) is rendered with the cognate accusative construction ta'am mitah ("the taste of death"), which is intensifying. The idiom "tasting death" is not found in the Hebrew Bible but is common in rabbinic literature (e.g., b. Berakhot 8a). Delitzsch's double use of the root t-'-m (yit'amu ta'am) creates an emphatic construction that reads naturally in Hebrew literary style.
