# Matthew Chapter 23 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

### 23:14 -- Disputed verse absent from critical Greek text
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has an empty string for verse 14 (the Greek field is blank), reflecting the scholarly consensus that this verse is a scribal interpolation from Mark 12:40 / Luke 20:47. The Vulgate includes the verse in full. Delitzsch includes it in brackets.
- **Greek**: (empty -- verse omitted in critical text)
- **Latin**: Vae vobis scribae et pharisaei hypocritae, quia comeditis domos viduarum, orationes longas orantes! propter hoc amplius accipietis judicium.
- **Hebrew**: [ve-oy lakhem hassofrim vehapperushim hachanefim ki-vol'im attem et-battey ha-almanot uma'arikkim bitfillah lemar'eh eynayim tachat zot mishpat gadol yeter me'od tishafeitu]
- **Note**: This is the most significant versification issue in chapters 21-24. The verse is absent from Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and other early witnesses. Its inclusion in the Vulgate reflects the Textus Receptus tradition. Delitzsch appropriately brackets it, signaling doubt. The total verse count for the chapter is 39 in the Vulgate tradition but effectively 38 in the critical Greek text (with v.14 being a phantom).

All other verses (1-13, 15-39) align across sources, but the numbering after v.13 differs by one verse between the critical Greek tradition (which skips 14) and the Vulgate (which includes it).

## Content Divergences

### 23:4 -- "heavy and hard to bear" vs just "heavy"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has phortia barea ("heavy burdens") with a variant marker on barea, and some manuscripts add kai dysbastakta ("and hard to bear"). The Vulgate includes both: "onera gravia, et importabilia." Delitzsch has only mas'ot kevedim ("heavy burdens"), following the shorter Greek reading.
- **Greek**: phortia barea (with variant -- some add kai dysbastakta)
- **Latin**: onera gravia, et importabilia
- **Hebrew**: mas'ot kevedim
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the shorter critical Greek text. The Vulgate follows the Byzantine/TR tradition with the addition.

### 23:5 -- "phylacteries" and "fringes"
- **Issue**: The Greek uses phylakteria (phylacteries) and kraspeda (fringes/tassels). The Vulgate transliterates phylacteria and uses fimbrias. Delitzsch uses tefilleyhem (their tefillin/phylacteries) and tsitsiyyoteyhem (their fringes), the actual Hebrew religious terms.
- **Greek**: phylakteria / kraspeda
- **Latin**: phylacteria / fimbrias
- **Hebrew**: tefilleyhem / tsitsiyyoteyhem
- **Note**: Delitzsch's use of the proper Hebrew religious terminology (tefillin, tsitsiyyot) is ideal. These are the actual Hebrew words for the objects described. The Greek phylakteria is itself a translation of tefillin, and kraspeda translates tsitsit (cf. Num 15:38-39). This is one of the strongest features of a Hebrew back-translation of the NT.

### 23:8 -- "the Christ" addition
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads heis gar estin hymon ho didaskalos ("for one is your teacher"), with a variant marker. Some manuscripts add ho christos ("the Christ"). The Vulgate has simply "unus est enim magister vester" without "Christus." Delitzsch adds ha-mashiach ("the Messiah"), following the Byzantine addition.
- **Greek**: heis gar estin hymon ho didaskalos (some MSS + ho christos)
- **Latin**: unus est enim magister vester
- **Hebrew**: ki echad hu morekhem hamashiach
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Byzantine/TR reading by including ha-mashiach. The critical text and the Vulgate both lack this addition. This may be an interpolation from v.10 where "the Christ" does appear in all traditions.

### 23:19 -- "fools and blind" vs just "blind"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has a variant marker on the opening word, with some manuscripts reading typhloi ("blind") alone while others have moroi kai typhloi ("fools and blind"). The Vulgate has just "Caeci" (blind). Delitzsch has kesilim ve-ivrim ("fools and blind"), following the longer reading.
- **Greek**: typhloi (with variant; some MSS: moroi kai typhloi)
- **Latin**: Caeci
- **Hebrew**: kesilim ve-ivrim
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the longer Byzantine/TR reading against both the critical Greek and the Vulgate.

### 23:25 -- "self-indulgence" vs "uncleanness"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads akrasias ("self-indulgence/lack of self-control"), while the Vulgate has "immunditia" ("uncleanness"). These are different readings: akrasia = lack of self-restraint; akatharsia = impurity/uncleanness. The Vulgate appears to reflect a Greek Vorlage with akatharsias rather than akrasias.
- **Greek**: akrasias (self-indulgence)
- **Latin**: immunditia (uncleanness = akatharsias)
- **Hebrew**: gargeranut (gluttony/greediness)
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders this as gargeranut, a post-biblical Hebrew word meaning "gluttony" or "greediness." This is an interpretive paraphrase of akrasias (self-indulgence) rather than a direct translation, and it uses non-biblical vocabulary. A more biblical option might be zollelut (from the root z-l-l, "to be worthless/gluttonous," cf. Deut 21:20 zolel ve-sove).

### 23:26 -- Cup and dish
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "the inside of the cup and the dish" (kai tes paropsidos), while the Vulgate agrees (calicis, et paropsidis). Delitzsch has only tokh ha-kos ("the inside of the cup"), omitting any reference to the dish/plate.
- **Greek**: to entos tou poteriou kai tes paropsidos
- **Latin**: quod intus est calicis, et paropsidis
- **Hebrew**: et-tokh ha-kos
- **Note**: Delitzsch omits the paropsis ("dish/plate"), simplifying the text. This is not supported by any known textual variant -- all Greek and Latin witnesses include both cup and dish.

### 23:38 -- "desolate" -- presence or absence
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads aphietai hymin ho oikos hymon eremos, but marks eremos ("desolate") with a variant indicator. Some important manuscripts omit eremos. The Vulgate includes "deserta." Delitzsch includes shamem ("desolate").
- **Greek**: ho oikos hymon eremos (with variant on eremos)
- **Latin**: domus vestra deserta
- **Hebrew**: beytkhem ye'azev lakhem shamem
- **Note**: Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/Byzantine reading including "desolate." The shorter reading (without eremos) is found in Sinaiticus and Vaticanus.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 23:2 -- "Seat of Moses" as kisse Moshe
- **Hebrew**: hassofrim vehapperushim yoshvim al-kisse Moshe
- **Note**: Good. Kisse Moshe ("chair/throne of Moses") is the correct biblical Hebrew rendering and matches the archaeological term for the actual stone seats found in ancient synagogues. The word kisse is well-attested for seats of authority (1 Kings 10:19, etc.).

### 23:4 -- ro'tsim (modern Hebrew)
- **Hebrew**: ve-hem eynam rotsim lehaniam af be-etsba'am
- **Note**: The verb rotsim ("wanting/willing") is characteristic of modern Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would more naturally use avah (lo avu) or chafets (lo chafetsu). The infinitive lehaniam is also somewhat awkward; lehani'am or le-hazzizam ("to move them") might be more natural.

### 23:13 -- First "Woe" oracle
- **Hebrew**: aval oy lakhem hassofrim vehapperushim hachanefim
- **Note**: Delitzsch consistently renders Greek hypokritai as chanefim throughout the woe oracles. In biblical Hebrew, chanef means "profane/godless" (Job 8:13, 13:16; Isa 33:14) rather than "hypocrite" in the theatrical Greek sense. This is actually a defensible choice -- the biblical chanef carries connotations of false piety and inner corruption -- but it shifts the semantic field slightly from pretense/acting (hypokrisis) toward profanity/godlessness (chanuppah).

### 23:15 -- Proselyte terminology
- **Hebrew**: le-ma'an gayyer ish echad ve-khi yitgayyer ta'asso oto le-ven-gehinnom
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the piel gayyer and hitpael yitgayyer from the root g-y-r, which is Mishnaic/Rabbinic Hebrew for making and becoming a proselyte (ger). In biblical Hebrew, ger simply means "sojourner/resident alien," and the conversion concept developed later. However, since the NT Greek proselytos is itself a technical term for a religious convert, Delitzsch's Mishnaic usage is arguably the most accurate available rendering.

### 23:23 -- Tithing of herbs
- **Hebrew**: ki me'assrim attem et-hamminta ve-et-hashevet ve-et-hakammon
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders the three herbs: hedyosmon (mint) = minta (a loanword, not biblical), anethon (dill) = shevet (biblical Hebrew for dill, Isa 28:25-27, excellent choice), kyminon (cumin) = kammon (biblical Hebrew, Isa 28:25-27, excellent choice). The use of shevet and kammon from Isaiah 28 is particularly skillful, grounding the translation in biblical agricultural vocabulary. Only minta is a concession to post-biblical vocabulary; biblical Hebrew lacks a native word for mint.

### 23:24 -- Straining the gnat, swallowing the camel
- **Hebrew**: manhigim ivrim hamesanenim et hayyattush u-vol'im et haggamal
- **Note**: Good. Yattush ("gnat/mosquito") appears in the Talmud but not in biblical Hebrew. Gamal (camel) is thoroughly biblical. The verb mesanenim ("straining/filtering") is post-biblical but no biblical alternative exists for this specific action. The wordplay between gamal and galma that may exist in the Aramaic original is not captured.

### 23:27 -- Whitewashed tombs
- **Hebrew**: ki-domim attem la-qevarim hammesuyadim hannirim na'im michuts ve-tokham male atsmot metim ve-khol-tum'ah
- **Note**: Excellent. Mesuyyadim ("whitewashed/plastered") is from the root s-y-d, attested in biblical Hebrew (Deut 27:2, 4). The image of whitewashed tombs is deeply rooted in Jewish purity law (contact with the dead causing impurity, Num 19:16), and Delitzsch's Hebrew captures this with appropriate vocabulary: atsmot metim, tum'ah.

### 23:32 -- "Fill up the measure"
- **Hebrew**: u-ve-khen malle'u attem se'at avotekhem
- **Note**: Excellent biblical Hebrew. Se'ah is a biblical unit of dry measure (Gen 18:6; 1 Sam 25:18), and "filling the se'ah" evokes the idiom of accumulated guilt reaching its full measure. This resonates with Genesis 15:16 (lo shalem avon ha-emori, "the iniquity of the Amorites is not yet full").

### 23:33 -- Serpents, offspring of vipers
- **Hebrew**: nechashim yaldey tsif'onim
- **Note**: Good biblical Hebrew. Nechashim (serpents) is standard (Gen 3:1). Tsif'oni (viper) appears in Isaiah 11:8, 14:29, 59:5. Yaldey ("children of") is a natural biblical construct for offspring.

### 23:37 -- Hen gathering chicks
- **Hebrew**: ka-tarngolet hammeqabbetset et-efrocheyha tachat kenafeyha
- **Note**: Tarngolet ("hen") is Mishnaic Hebrew, not found in the biblical text. Biblical Hebrew lacks a specific word for a domesticated hen (the rooster appears as sekhvi in Job 38:36 in some readings, but this is disputed). Efrochim ("chicks") does appear in Deuteronomy 22:6, making it good biblical vocabulary. The phrase tachat kenafeyha ("under her wings") beautifully echoes the theological metaphor of divine protection (Ps 91:4, Ruth 2:12).

## Other Notes

### 23:8-10 -- Teacher/Father/Master triad
In these three prohibitions, Delitzsch renders: didaskalos = moreh (v.8 and v.10), pater = av (v.9). This creates an ambiguity because moreh is used for both didaskalos (v.8) and kathegetes (v.10). The Greek distinguishes between didaskalos ("teacher") and kathegetes ("guide/master/instructor"), but Delitzsch collapses this distinction. A possible alternative for kathegetes might be manhig or melammedkhem.

### 23:35 -- Zechariah son of Barachiah
The identification of "Zechariah son of Barachiah" is a well-known crux. This could refer to the prophet Zechariah (Zech 1:1), to Zechariah son of Jehoiada killed in the temple (2 Chr 24:20-21), or to Zechariah son of Bareis killed during the Jewish War (Josephus, War 4.335). All three texts agree on the name. Delitzsch renders Barachias as Berekhyah, the standard Hebrew form, matching Zechariah 1:1.
