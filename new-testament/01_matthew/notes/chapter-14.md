# Matthew Chapter 14 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 36 verses align across all three sources. The SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, and Clementine Vulgate all contain verses 1-36 with no missing, extra, or differently-numbered verses.

## Content Divergences

### 14:3 — Herodias as wife of Philip
- **Issue**: All three texts identify Herodias as "the wife of Philip his brother." Historically, Herodias was the wife of Herod II (sometimes called Herod Philip), not Philip the Tetrarch. This is a well-known historical difficulty in the Synoptics, but all three textual traditions agree here, so it is not a divergence between them.
- **Greek**: dia Herodiada ten gynaika Philippou tou adelphou autou
- **Latin**: propter Herodiadem uxorem fratris sui
- **Hebrew**: biglal Horodiah eshet Filippos ahiv
- **Note**: No textual divergence. All three agree on the content.

### 14:9 — "the king" (basileus)
- **Issue**: The Greek calls Herod "the king" (ho basileus), which is technically inaccurate since Herod Antipas was a tetrarch (tetraarches, as correctly stated in 14:1), not a king. The Vulgate also uses "rex." Delitzsch uses ha-melekh. All three texts use the popular/colloquial title rather than the precise political one.
- **Greek**: kai lypeutheis ho basileus
- **Latin**: Et contristatus est rex
- **Hebrew**: va-yetser la-melekh
- **Note**: This is consistent across all three texts. The shift from "tetrarch" in v.1 to "king" in v.9 reflects the narrative sources and is preserved uniformly.

### 14:24 — "many stadia from land" vs. "in the middle of the sea"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads "stadious pollous apo tes ges apeichen" ("was many stadia distant from the land"). The Vulgate reads "in medio mari" ("in the middle of the sea"), which follows a different Greek textual tradition (meson tes thalasses, found in the Byzantine/TR tradition). Delitzsch follows the Vulgate/Byzantine reading with "ad hatsi ha-yam" ("to the middle of the sea").
- **Greek**: stadious pollous apo tes ges apeichen (many stadia from the land)
- **Latin**: in medio mari (in the middle of the sea)
- **Hebrew**: ba'ah ad hatsi ha-yam (came to the middle of the sea)
- **Note**: This is a genuine textual divergence. The critical Greek text has "many stadia from the land" while the Vulgate and Delitzsch both follow the Byzantine/TR reading "in the middle of the sea." Delitzsch appears to be translating from the TR rather than from a critical text.

### 14:22 — "get into" / "go into" the boat
- **Issue**: The Greek uses embainein eis ploion ("to embark into a boat"). The Vulgate says "ascendere in naviculam" ("to ascend into a small boat"). Delitzsch renders this as "la-redet ba-oniyyah" ("to go down into the ship"), using the verb yarad (descend), which is the opposite direction from the Vulgate's "ascendere" (ascend).
- **Greek**: embainein eis ploion
- **Latin**: ascendere in naviculam
- **Hebrew**: la-redet ba-oniyyah
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses yarad ("descend") which is the standard biblical Hebrew idiom for boarding a ship (cf. Jonah 1:3). The Vulgate uses ascendere, reflecting the Latin nautical idiom of "going up" onto a vessel. Neither the Hebrew nor the Latin matches the neutral Greek embainein ("step into"). This is a purely idiomatic difference, not a content divergence.

### 14:34 — Gennesaret
- **Issue**: All three texts agree on the destination as Gennesaret/Gineisar. No divergence.
- **Greek**: Gennesaret
- **Latin**: Genesar
- **Hebrew**: Ginneisar
- **Note**: Minor spelling variations only, reflecting the same place name.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 14:1 — "tetrarch" as sar-rova' ha-medinah
- **Hebrew**: Hordos sar-rova' ha-medinah (Herod, the prince of a quarter of the province)
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders tetraarches as "sar-rova' ha-medinah" -- literally "prince of a quarter of the province." This is a descriptive periphrasis since biblical Hebrew has no word for "tetrarch." The choice of sar ("prince/official") is appropriate, found throughout the Hebrew Bible for officials of various ranks. The construction is somewhat wordy but accurately conveys the meaning. Rova' (quarter) is biblical (Num 23:10, though in a different sense).

### 14:2 — "powers work in him" as ha-kohot po'alim bo
- **Hebrew**: ha-kohot po'alim bo (the powers work in him)
- **Note**: The Greek dynameis energousin ("the powers are at work") is rendered with kohot ("powers/strengths") and po'alim ("work/act"). While koah is thoroughly biblical (Gen 4:12, Deut 8:17), the plural kohot in this abstract sense of "miracle-working powers" is more characteristic of later Hebrew. The construction reads somewhat woodenly. A more biblical phrasing might use gevurot (mighty deeds) which is the standard biblical Hebrew term for divine mighty acts.

### 14:14 — "his bowels were moved" -- va-yehemu me'av
- **Hebrew**: va-yehemu me'av lahem (and his bowels were moved for them)
- **Note**: This is an outstanding biblical Hebrew rendering of esplanchnisthe ("he was moved with compassion"). The verb hamah with me'im (bowels/innards) directly parallels Isaiah 63:15 (hamon me'eikha ve-rahamekha) and Jeremiah 31:20 (hamu me'ai lo). This is one of Delitzsch's finest choices in the chapter, perfectly capturing the visceral Greek term through authentic biblical idiom.

### 14:19 — Feeding narrative vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: va-yissa einav ha-shamaymah va-yevarekh va-yifros va-yitten
- **Note**: Delitzsch constructs a beautiful chain of wayyiqtol (narrative preterite) verbs: "he lifted his eyes heavenward and blessed and broke and gave." The verb nasa einav is thoroughly biblical (Gen 13:10, 18:2), vayyevarekh is standard for blessing food/God, and the whole chain reads as natural biblical narrative prose. Yifros ("broke/distributed") for klasas is a good choice, paralleling the breaking/distributing sense.

### 14:25 — "the fourth watch" as be-ashmoret ha-revi'it
- **Hebrew**: be-ashmoret ha-revi'it (in the fourth watch)
- **Note**: Ashmoret is the biblical Hebrew word for a night watch (Exodus 14:24, Judges 7:19, 1 Samuel 11:11, Lamentations 2:19). The biblical system uses three watches, while the Greek/Roman system (used in the NT) has four. Delitzsch correctly uses the biblical word ashmoret but applies it to the Roman four-watch system (phulake tetarte). This is a reasonable compromise between biblical vocabulary and NT content.

### 14:26 — "a phantom" as mar'eh-ruah
- **Hebrew**: mar'eh-ruah hu (it is an appearance of a spirit)
- **Note**: The Greek phantasma (apparition/ghost) is rendered as mar'eh-ruah ("a sight/appearance of a spirit"). This is creative since biblical Hebrew has no single word for "ghost/phantom" in this sense. Mar'eh (appearance/vision) is well-attested biblically (Gen 2:9, Ezek 1:1), and ruah can mean "spirit" (1 Sam 28:13-14 for the spirit of Samuel). The compound is somewhat ad hoc but captures the meaning adequately.

### 14:27 — "ego eimi" as ani hu
- **Hebrew**: ki-ani hu (for I am he)
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders ego eimi as ani hu, which is the standard Hebrew equivalent and carries strong biblical resonance. The phrase ani hu appears in Isaiah 41:4, 43:10, 43:13, 46:4, 48:12 as a divine self-identification formula. While the Greek ego eimi in this context may simply mean "it is I" (identifying himself to the frightened disciples), the Hebrew ani hu inevitably evokes the theophanic overtones of Isaiah. This is theologically significant and arguably a good translational choice, since Johannine theology explicitly connects Jesus's ego eimi statements to the divine name.

### 14:31 — "little-faith" as qeton emunah
- **Hebrew**: qeton emunah (small of faith)
- **Note**: The Greek oligopiste (O you of little faith) is rendered as qeton emunah. This is a compact Hebrew construct phrase. While qaton is thoroughly biblical (Gen 32:11, 1 Sam 9:21), the specific compound "small of faith" is a Delitzsch coinage since emunah in the Hebrew Bible is never modified this way. It reads well as biblical Hebrew and has become standard in Hebrew NT translations.

## Other Notes

### 14:13-21 — Feeding of the Five Thousand
This is the only miracle (apart from the resurrection) recorded in all four Gospels. The triple-text comparison here is straightforward -- all three traditions agree on the details (five loaves, two fish, twelve baskets). Delitzsch's Hebrew renders the narrative in excellent wayyiqtol chain prose that reads like a natural continuation of the historical books.

### 14:27 — Christological "I am" (ego eimi / ani hu)
As noted above, this is a theologically loaded phrase. In the context of walking on water (a theophanic act -- cf. Job 9:8 "who alone stretches out the heavens and treads on the waves of the sea"), the combination of the divine sea-walking with the ani hu self-declaration makes this a passage where the Hebrew translation actually surfaces an OT christological connection more clearly than the Greek does for a Hebrew-literate reader.

### 14:33 — "truly you are the Son of God"
- **Hebrew**: aval ben-elohim attah (truly you are the son of God)
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses aval in the sense of "truly/indeed" (a post-biblical semantic development; in biblical Hebrew aval means "but/however" or, rarely, "truly" as in Gen 42:21). The Greek alethos ("truly") and Latin vere ("truly") are straightforward. The confession ben-elohim (son of God) without the article before elohim leaves the phrase slightly ambiguous between "a son of God" and "the Son of God" -- the Greek has theou uios (anarthrous, without articles) while the Latin has "Filius Dei."
