# Matthew Chapter 17 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The SBLGNT contains 27 verse slots for this chapter, but verse 21 has an empty Greek field. The Delitzsch Hebrew and the Vulgate both include content at verse 21. The SBLGNT omits Matt 17:21 entirely as a later interpolation (the verse about prayer and fasting driving out this kind of demon). This means from verse 22 onward, the Vulgate's Latin text is shifted by one verse relative to the Greek verse numbering.

Additionally, the Vulgate's Latin text for individual verses 14-20 does not correspond to the same verse numbers in the Greek and Hebrew. The Vulgate combines and redistributes content across these verses differently:

- **17:14** — The Vulgate's Latin at v.14 includes not only the approach of the man (matching Greek v.14) but also the plea about the lunatic son and the mention of fire and water (which is Greek v.15), plus the presentation to the disciples (Greek v.16), and even Jesus's reply about the faithless generation (Greek v.17). The Greek and Hebrew split this across vv.14-17.
- **17:15-20** — The subsequent Vulgate verses are each offset by content that belongs to the next Greek/Hebrew verse. The Latin at v.15 corresponds to Greek v.16; Latin v.16 corresponds to Greek v.17; Latin v.17 corresponds to Greek v.18; Latin v.18 corresponds to Greek v.19; Latin v.19 corresponds to Greek v.20.
- **17:20** — The Vulgate at v.20 contains the prayer-and-fasting logion ("Hoc autem genus non ejicitur nisi per orationem et jejunium"), which the SBLGNT omits entirely. This is the content of the "missing" verse 21.

## Content Divergences

### 17:2 — "White as light" vs. "white as snow"
- **Issue**: The Greek and Hebrew describe Jesus's garments as white "as light," while the Vulgate reads "as snow."
- **Greek**: `τὰ δὲ ἱμάτια αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο λευκὰ ὡς τὸ φῶς` ("his garments became white as the light")
- **Latin**: `vestimenta autem ejus facta sunt alba sicut nix` ("his garments became white as snow")
- **Hebrew**: `וּבְגָדָיו כָּאוֹר הִלְבִּינוּ` ("his garments became white as light")
- **Note**: The Vulgate follows a textual tradition (also found in some Greek manuscripts and the Markan parallel) that reads "snow" (nix) rather than "light" (φῶς). The SBLGNT and Delitzsch both agree on "light." This is a genuine textual variant.

### 17:12 — "Suffer" vs. "be afflicted"
- **Issue**: The Greek uses the verb μέλλει πάσχειν ("is about to suffer"), the Vulgate has "passurus est" ("is going to suffer"), but the Hebrew uses יְעֻנֶּה ("will be afflicted/tormented"), which carries a different nuance.
- **Greek**: `οὕτως καὶ ὁ υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου μέλλει πάσχειν ὑπʼ αὐτῶν`
- **Latin**: `Sic et Filius hominis passurus est ab eis`
- **Hebrew**: `וְכֵן גַּם-בֶּן-הָאָדָם יְעֻנֶּה עַל-יָדָם`
- **Note**: The Greek πάσχειν and Latin passurus both mean "to suffer" broadly. Delitzsch chose the Pual of ענה ("to be afflicted"), which echoes Isaiah 53:7 (וְהוּא נַעֲנֶה) -- a deliberate intertextual connection. This is a theologically charged translation choice.

### 17:20-21 — Omitted verse and mustard-seed logion placement
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT omits verse 21 entirely (empty Greek field). The Hebrew and Vulgate both include it. The SBLGNT places the mustard-seed faith saying at v.20 with the answer about "little faith" (ὀλιγοπιστίαν). The Vulgate distributes this differently.
- **Greek (v.20)**: `ὁ δὲ λέγει αὐτοῖς· Διὰ τὴν ὀλιγοπιστίαν ὑμῶν· ἀμὴν γὰρ λέγω ὑμῖν, ἐὰν ἔχητε πίστιν ὡς κόκκον σινάπεως...`
- **Greek (v.21)**: [empty in SBLGNT]
- **Latin (v.20)**: `Hoc autem genus non ejicitur nisi per orationem et jejunium` (the prayer-and-fasting logion)
- **Hebrew (v.21)**: `וְהַמִּין הַזֶּה לֹא יֵצֵא כִּי אִם-בִּתְפִלָּה וּבְצוֹם`
- **Note**: Matt 17:21 is absent from the earliest and best Greek manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and is considered a scribal harmonization from Mark 9:29. The SBLGNT correctly omits it. The Vulgate and Delitzsch both include it. The Hebrew text at v.21 effectively follows the Byzantine/Textus Receptus tradition.

### 17:21 — Vulgate content misalignment
- **Issue**: The Vulgate Latin text stored at verse 21 in the data actually corresponds to verse 22 content (Jesus speaking about the Son of Man being delivered). The prayer-and-fasting logion appears in the Vulgate at verse 20.
- **Latin (v.21)**: `Conversantibus autem eis in Galilæa, dixit illis Jesus : Filius hominis tradendus est in manus hominum` (This is the gathering-in-Galilee passage, corresponding to Greek v.22)
- **Note**: This confirms the one-verse offset in the Vulgate numbering from v.20 onward due to the inclusion/exclusion of the prayer-and-fasting verse.

### 17:27 — Missing Vulgate text
- **Issue**: The Vulgate field for verse 27 is empty in the data.
- **Greek**: `ἵνα δὲ μὴ σκανδαλίσωμεν αὐτούς, πορευθεὶς εἰς θάλασσαν βάλε ἄγκιστρον...`
- **Latin**: [empty]
- **Hebrew**: `וְאוּלָם לְמַעַן אֲשֶׁר-לֹא-נִהְיֶה לָהֶם לְמִכְשׁוֹל לֵךְ אֶל-הַיָּם...`
- **Note**: This is a data issue. The Vulgate does contain Matt 17:27 (the coin in the fish's mouth). The empty field likely results from the one-verse offset: the Vulgate's v.26 Latin already consumed the content that should appear at v.27 in the aligned numbering. The Vulgate equivalent is likely at the end of its v.26 entry.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 17:1 — Good biblical narrative style
- **Hebrew**: `וּמִקֵּץ שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים לָקַח לוֹ יֵשׁוּעַ`
- **Note**: The construction מִקֵּץ + time period is excellent biblical Hebrew (cf. Gen 41:1, מִקֵּץ שְׁנָתַיִם יָמִים). The use of לָקַח לוֹ ("took to himself") also echoes biblical idiom. The wayyiqtol narrative chain (וַיַּעֲלֵם) throughout the chapter is well executed.

### 17:2 — וַיִּשְׁתַּנֶּה (Hithpael of שׁנה)
- **Hebrew**: `וַיִּשְׁתַּנֶּה לְעֵינֵיהֶם`
- **Note**: The Hithpael of שׁנה ("was changed/transformed") for μετεμορφώθη is an adequate choice, but it is more characteristic of late biblical/Aramaic-influenced Hebrew (cf. Dan 3:19, אֲנוֹהִי שְׁנִי). The word appears in Daniel (Aramaic sections) more than in classical Hebrew prose. A more classical alternative might have been harder to find, so this is a reasonable compromise.

### 17:5 — Excellent echo of the bat qol tradition
- **Hebrew**: `זֶה-בְּנִי יְדִידִי אֲשֶׁר רָצִיתִי בוֹ אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן`
- **Note**: Delitzsch's rendering of the divine voice from the cloud is skillful. The term יְדִידִי ("my beloved") echoes the Song of the Beloved in Isaiah 5 and Psalm 60:7 / 108:7. The phrase אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן ("to him you shall listen") directly echoes Deuteronomy 18:15 (אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן), making the Mosaic-prophet typology explicit.

### 17:11 — Bracketed בָרִאשׁוֹנָה
- **Hebrew**: `אָכֵן אֵלִיָּהוּ יָבֹא [בָרִאשׁוֹנָה] וְהֵשִׁיב אֶת-הַכֹּל`
- **Note**: The word בָרִאשׁוֹנָה ("first") is bracketed, indicating textual uncertainty. The SBLGNT apparatus also marks this word. Some manuscripts omit πρῶτον. Delitzsch's brackets are an honest representation of the textual situation.

### 17:17 — הוֹי דּוֹר חֲסַר אֱמוּנָה וּפְתַלְתֹּל
- **Hebrew**: `הוֹי דּוֹר חֲסַר אֱמוּנָה וּפְתַלְתֹּל`
- **Note**: Excellent. The word פְתַלְתֹּל ("twisted, perverse") directly echoes Deuteronomy 32:5 (דּוֹר עִקֵּשׁ וּפְתַלְתֹּל), making the allusion to the Song of Moses unmistakable. Delitzsch chose the more emphatic biblical term over a simpler option. The interjection הוֹי is a strong prophetic "woe" (cf. Isa 1:4), well suited to render Ὦ.

### 17:20 — גַרְגַּר הַחַרְדָּל
- **Hebrew**: `כְּגַרְגַּר הַחַרְדָּל`
- **Note**: The word גַרְגַּר ("grain, berry") is post-biblical and more characteristic of Mishnaic Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would more likely use a construction with זֶרַע ("seed"), which is what the Greek κόκκον literally means ("grain/seed"). However, חַרְדָּל ("mustard") is attested in Mishnaic Hebrew but not in the Hebrew Bible, so any translation here inevitably draws on post-biblical vocabulary.

### 17:24 — גַּבָּאֵי מַחֲצִית הַשָׁקֶל
- **Hebrew**: `גַּבָּאֵי מַחֲצִית הַשָׁקֶל`
- **Note**: The term גַּבָּאֵי ("tax collectors") is Mishnaic, not biblical Hebrew. Biblical Hebrew would use a different construction, perhaps based on the root נגשׂ or a periphrastic expression. However, מַחֲצִית הַשָׁקֶל ("half-shekel") is excellent biblical Hebrew (Exod 30:13), and the identification of the Greek δίδραχμα with the temple half-shekel tax is culturally accurate and contextually correct.

### 17:27 — מַטְבֵּעַ for στατῆρα
- **Hebrew**: `תִּמְצָא בוֹ מַטְבֵּעַ`
- **Note**: The word מַטְבֵּעַ ("coin") is post-biblical (from the root טבע, "to impress/stamp"), standard in Mishnaic Hebrew but absent from the Hebrew Bible. Biblical Hebrew has no specific word for "coin" as minting postdates most biblical texts. The Greek στατήρ is a specific denomination. Delitzsch's choice is practical but inevitably non-biblical.

## Other Notes

### 17:5 — Trinitarian theophany
The Transfiguration narrative is a significant Trinitarian moment: the Son is visible in glory, the Father speaks from the cloud, and the luminous cloud itself has been traditionally associated with the Spirit's presence (cf. the shekinah cloud of Exodus). This verse would be relevant for any "red text" markup of divine speech: the Father's words "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased; listen to him" are direct speech of the First Person of the Trinity.

### 17:9-13 — Elijah typology
The dialogue about Elijah is significant for understanding the relationship between John the Baptist and the prophetic tradition. All three texts agree on the substance. The Hebrew naturally renders the scribal/rabbinic discussion context well because the terminology (סופרים, "scribes") is native.
