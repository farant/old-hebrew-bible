# Matthew Chapter 25 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 46 verses align across all three sources.

## Content Divergences

### 25:1 — "and the bride" in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "et sponsae" (and the bride) at the end of the verse, absent from both the Greek and Hebrew.
- **Greek**: ...εἰς ὑπάντησιν τοῦ νυμφίου (to meet the bridegroom)
- **Latin**: ...obviam sponso et sponsae (to meet the bridegroom and the bride)
- **Hebrew**: ...לִקְרַאת הֶחָתָן (to meet the bridegroom)
- **Note**: The addition "et sponsae" is a well-known Vulgate expansion, not supported by the critical Greek text (SBLGNT). The Hebrew follows the Greek correctly.

### 25:2 — Order of wise and foolish reversed
- **Issue**: The Greek lists the foolish first, then the wise (μωραὶ καὶ πέντε φρόνιμοι). The Hebrew reverses this order, listing the wise first. The Vulgate follows the Greek order (fatuae...prudentes).
- **Greek**: πέντε δὲ ἐξ αὐτῶν ἦσαν μωραὶ καὶ πέντε φρόνιμοι
- **Latin**: Quinque autem ex eis erant fatuae, et quinque prudentes
- **Hebrew**: חָמֵשׁ מֵהֶן חֲכָמוֹת וְחָמֵשׁ כְּסִילוֹת
- **Note**: Delitzsch reversed the order, placing the wise first. This matches some Byzantine manuscript traditions but diverges from the SBLGNT critical text. The effect is subtle but theologically notable: the Greek text foregrounds the foolish as the point of the parable.

### 25:13 — Bracketed expansion in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes a bracketed phrase at the end of the verse: [אֲשֶׁר יָבֹא בָהּ בֶּן-הָאָדָם] ("in which the Son of Man comes"). This corresponds to a textual variant found in the Textus Receptus / Byzantine tradition but absent from the SBLGNT and the Vulgate.
- **Greek**: γρηγορεῖτε οὖν, ὅτι οὐκ οἴδατε τὴν ἡμέραν οὐδὲ τὴν ὥραν (Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour)
- **Latin**: Vigilate itaque, quia nescitis diem, neque horam
- **Hebrew**: ...וְאֶת-הַשָּׁעָה [אֲשֶׁר יָבֹא בָהּ בֶּן-הָאָדָם]
- **Note**: The brackets indicate Delitzsch was aware this is a secondary reading. It comes from the Byzantine/TR tradition (matching KJV). Its absence from SBLGNT and Vulgate is significant for critical purposes.

### 25:29 — "what he seems to have" in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quod videtur habere" (what he seems to have), adding "videtur" (seems) which is not in the Greek or Hebrew.
- **Greek**: ...καὶ ὃ ἔχει ἀρθήσεται ἀπʼ αὐτοῦ (even what he has will be taken from him)
- **Latin**: ...et quod videtur habere, auferetur ab eo (and what he seems to have, will be taken from him)
- **Hebrew**: ...גַּם אֵת אֲשֶׁר-לוֹ (even that which is his)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "videtur" is an interpretive gloss, possibly influenced by Luke 8:18 (ὃ δοκεῖ ἔχειν). The Hebrew and Greek agree against the Vulgate here.

### 25:31 — "holy angels" in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds "holy" (הַקְּדֹשִׁים) to describe the angels. The Greek simply reads "all the angels" (πάντες οἱ ἄγγελοι) and the Vulgate likewise "omnes angeli."
- **Greek**: καὶ πάντες οἱ ἄγγελοι μετʼ αὐτοῦ
- **Latin**: et omnes angeli cum eo
- **Hebrew**: וְכָל-הַמַּלְאָכִים הַקְּדֹשִׁים עִמּוֹ
- **Note**: Delitzsch's addition of הַקְּדֹשִׁים follows the Byzantine/TR tradition (which includes ἅγιοι in some manuscripts, reflected in the KJV). SBLGNT does not include it. This is a genuine textual variant.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 25:1 — Lamps vs. torches
- **Hebrew**: נֵרוֹתֵיהֶן (their lamps)
- **Note**: The Greek λαμπάδας refers to torches (processional wedding torches), not oil lamps (נֵרוֹת). Delitzsch chose נֵר which in biblical Hebrew typically means a small lamp (as in Prov 13:9, 20:27). The term לַפִּיד (torch, as in Judg 7:16, 15:4) would better render the Greek λαμπάς and suit the wedding-procession context. However, the parable's logic with oil and vessels works with either term.

### 25:2 — Wise and foolish vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: חֲכָמוֹת ... כְּסִילוֹת
- **Note**: Excellent biblical vocabulary. כְּסִיל is the quintessential Proverbs/Wisdom-literature word for "fool" (Prov 1:22, 10:1, 13:20, etc.) and חָכָם is its proper counterpart. These are better choices than any modern alternatives and capture the wisdom-literature register appropriate to this parable.

### 25:14 — Modern construction "the matter resembles"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי הַדָּבָר דֹּמֶה לְאִישׁ
- **Note**: The construction הַדָּבָר דֹּמֶה is somewhat unbiblical. Biblical Hebrew would more typically use a simple כְּ comparison or the prophetic mashal form. The Greek has no explicit subject for the comparison (Ὥσπερ γὰρ ἄνθρωπος, "For just as a man..."). A more biblical rendering might begin כִּי כְאִישׁ or simply כִּי אִישׁ. This is a minor stylistic issue.

### 25:30 — "servant of Belial"
- **Hebrew**: עֶבֶד הַבְּלִיַּעַל
- **Note**: Excellent choice. The Greek ἀχρεῖον δοῦλον (useless/worthless servant) is rendered with the forceful biblical idiom בְּלִיַּעַל (1 Sam 2:12, Deut 13:14). This captures the moral weight of the Greek better than a flat translation like "worthless" would.

### 25:32 — Sheep and goats vocabulary
- **Hebrew**: הַכְּבָשִׂים ... הָעַתּוּדִים
- **Note**: Good biblical vocabulary. כֶּבֶשׂ (sheep/lamb) is standard biblical Hebrew. עַתּוּד (he-goat) occurs in biblical Hebrew (Gen 31:10, Isa 14:9, Ezek 34:17) and is appropriate here. The Greek ἐρίφων (kids/goats) is slightly different from the mature עַתּוּדִים (he-goats), but Ezek 34:17 uses עַתּוּד in exactly this kind of shepherd-judgment context, making Delitzsch's choice apt.

### 25:46 — "eternal punishment"
- **Hebrew**: לְמַעֲצֵבַת עוֹלָם
- **Note**: The word מַעֲצֶבֶת is unusual. It derives from עצב (pain, grief) but the nominal form מַעֲצֶבֶת is not a standard biblical Hebrew word. The Greek κόλασιν (punishment, chastisement) could have been rendered with a more common biblical term. The Vulgate "supplicium" (punishment) is straightforward. A more biblical choice might be עֹנֶשׁ (as in the post-biblical sense) or a phrase like יִסּוּרֵי עוֹלָם. This is one of Delitzsch's more forced coinages.

## Other Notes

### 25:31-46 — The Sheep and Goats judgment
This pericope is notable for being unique to Matthew among the Gospels. All three text traditions are substantively in agreement throughout this section, with the divergences noted above (25:31 "holy" angels) being minor textual-critical matters rather than content differences. The passage is theologically significant for its identification of service to "the least of these" with service to Christ himself.
