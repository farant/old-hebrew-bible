# Matthew Chapter 1 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 25 verses align across all three sources (Greek SBLGNT, Delitzsch Hebrew, Clementine Vulgate).

## Content Divergences

### 1:5 — Jesse/David split between verses 5 and 6
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "Jesse autem genuit David regem" (Jesse begot David the king) at the end of verse 5, while both the Greek and Hebrew place this material in verse 6. This is a content boundary difference: the Vulgate packs an extra generation into v5.
- **Greek**: v5 ends with Ἰεσσαί (Jesse); v6 begins Ἰεσσαὶ δὲ ἐγέννησεν τὸν Δαυὶδ τὸν βασιλέα
- **Latin**: v5 ends "Jesse autem genuit David regem." — David's begetting is already stated
- **Hebrew**: v5 ends with יִשָׁי (Jesse); v6 begins וְיִשַׁי הוֹלִיד אֶת-דָּוִד הַמֶּלֶךְ
- **Note**: The Greek and Hebrew agree against the Vulgate on the verse boundary here. The Vulgate anticipates David's generation one verse early.

### 1:7-8 — Asaph vs. Asa
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads Ἀσάφ (Asaph) in vv7-8, preserving what appears to be the original reading of Matthew (conflating king Asa with the psalmist Asaph). The Vulgate and Delitzsch both normalize to Asa/אָסָא, the correct OT king.
- **Greek**: Ἀσάφ (v7), Ἀσὰφ (v8) — the critical text preserves the lectio difficilior
- **Latin**: Asa (v7), Asa (v8) — harmonized to OT
- **Hebrew**: אָסָא — harmonized to OT
- **Note**: This is a well-known textual issue. The SBLGNT preserves what most text critics consider the original reading, which the Vulgate and Delitzsch have corrected to match 1 Kings 15. Delitzsch follows the Vulgate tradition here rather than the Greek critical text.

### 1:10 — Amos vs. Amon
- **Issue**: Similar to the Asaph/Asa issue. The SBLGNT reads Ἀμώς (Amos) rather than Ἀμών (Amon), again preserving an apparent conflation (this time with the prophet Amos). The Vulgate and Delitzsch normalize to Amon/אָמוֹן.
- **Greek**: Ἀμώς (the critical text preserves the variant)
- **Latin**: Amon
- **Hebrew**: אָמוֹן
- **Note**: Another lectio difficilior in the SBLGNT. Delitzsch again follows the harmonized tradition.

### 1:18 — "Jesus Christ" vs. "Christ"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT apparatus marks a variant at Ἰησοῦ (with the ⸀ sigla). Some manuscripts read only "Christou" (Christ) without "Iesou" (Jesus). The Vulgate reads "Christi" alone. Delitzsch includes the full form "Yeshua HaMashiach."
- **Greek**: Τοῦ δὲ ⸀Ἰησοῦ χριστοῦ — variant noted
- **Latin**: "Christi autem generatio" — only "Christ," no "Jesus"
- **Hebrew**: יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ — full "Jesus the Messiah"
- **Note**: The Vulgate reflects the shorter reading (Christou alone). Delitzsch follows the longer Greek reading.

### 1:25 — "firstborn son" vs. "a son"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads simply υἱόν (a son), with a variant marker. The Vulgate reads "filium suum primogenitum" (her firstborn son). Delitzsch includes אֶת-בְּכוֹרָהּ (her firstborn) in brackets, acknowledging the textual uncertainty.
- **Greek**: ἔτεκεν ⸀υἱόν — "bore a son" (critical text omits "firstborn")
- **Latin**: "peperit filium suum primogenitum" — "bore her firstborn son"
- **Hebrew**: בֵּן [אֶת-בְּכוֹרָהּ] — "a son [her firstborn]" — brackets indicate doubt
- **Note**: The word "firstborn" (πρωτότοκον) is absent from the oldest Greek manuscripts (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and is likely a scribal addition influenced by Luke 2:7. The Vulgate preserves the longer reading. Delitzsch wisely brackets the addition to signal the textual issue. This has significant theological implications for Marian doctrines.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 1:1 — Excellent biblical register
- **Hebrew**: סֵפֶר תּוֹלְדֹת יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ בֶּן-דָּוִד בֶּן-אַבְרָהָם
- **Note**: Delitzsch opens with סֵפֶר תּוֹלְדֹת, directly echoing Genesis 5:1 (סֵפֶר תּוֹלְדֹת אָדָם). This is an excellent choice that captures how Matthew's Greek Βίβλος γενέσεως deliberately echoes Genesis. The entire genealogy section uses the Hiphil הוֹלִיד with אֶת consistently, matching biblical Hebrew genealogical formulae.

### 1:18 — "huledet" for "genesis"
- **Hebrew**: וְזֶה דְבַר הֻלֶּדֶת יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses הֻלֶּדֶת (birth, begetting) for Greek γένεσις. This is a rare biblical form — the Hophal infinitive construct of ילד. It appears to be modeled on forms like those in Ezekiel 16:4. An acceptable choice, though the more common מוֹלֶדֶת or the תּוֹלְדֹת from v1 could also have served. The choice distinguishes this verse's meaning (the circumstances of birth) from v1's meaning (genealogical record).

### 1:19 — Expanded rendering
- **Hebrew**: וְלֹא אָבָה לְתִתָּהּ לְחֶרְפָּה וַיֹּאמֶר אֲשַׁלְּחֶנָּה בַּסָּתֶר
- **Note**: The Greek ἐβουλήθη (he resolved/intended) is rendered as וַיֹּאמֶר (and he said/resolved). Delitzsch splits the Greek into two clauses: "he did not want to put her to shame" and "he said he would send her away in secret." The Greek has a single clause with an aorist passive infinitive (ἀπολῦσαι — to divorce). The Hebrew rendering captures the sense well using biblical idiom, but the wayyiqtol וַיֹּאמֶר adds a speech-act nuance ("he said to himself") not explicit in the Greek. This is acceptable as biblical Hebrew narrative style.

### 1:23 — "almah" for "parthenos"
- **Hebrew**: הִנֵּה הָעַלְמָה הָרָה וְיֹלֶדֶת בֵּן
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses עַלְמָה here, matching the Hebrew of Isaiah 7:14 exactly rather than translating the Greek παρθένος (virgin) with בְּתוּלָה. This is a significant editorial choice: he quotes the original Hebrew prophet rather than back-translating from the Greek. The Greek and Latin both clearly have "virgin" (παρθένος / virgo), but Delitzsch follows the Hebrew Vorlage of the OT citation. This is defensible as a translation philosophy but worth noting for review.

### 1:24 — Good narrative Hebrew
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּיקַץ יוֹסֵף מִשְׁנָתוֹ וַיַּעַשׂ כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוָּהוּ מַלְאַךְ יְהוָֹה וַיֶּאֱסֹף אֶת-אִשְׁתּוֹ אֶל בֵּיתוֹ
- **Note**: Delitzsch adds אֶל בֵּיתוֹ (to his house), which is absent from the Greek (παρέλαβεν τὴν γυναῖκα αὐτοῦ — "he took his wife"). This is an explicating addition, though it does parallel biblical Hebrew idiom. The verb וַיֶּאֱסֹף (he gathered/took in) is a good biblical choice for παραλαμβάνω in this marital context.

## Other Notes

### 1:11 — The Jeconiah problem
The genealogy says Josiah begot Jeconiah at the time of the Babylonian exile, but historically Josiah begot Jehoiakim, who begot Jeconiah (= Jehoiachin). Matthew appears to skip Jehoiakim, which creates the well-known problem of the "fourteen generations" count. All three texts agree on this reading, so it is not a divergence between traditions — it is Matthew's own compression of the genealogy.

### 1:16 — Careful phrasing across all three traditions
All three texts carefully shift from the active "begot" pattern to a passive/relative construction at v16: Joseph is called the husband of Mary, "of whom" (ἐξ ἧς / de qua / אֲשֶׁר מִמֶּנָּה) Jesus was born. The Hebrew מִמֶּנָּה (from her) is feminine singular, correctly matching the Greek ἐξ ἧς. This careful phrasing in all three traditions preserves the virgin birth doctrine by not saying Joseph "begot" Jesus.
