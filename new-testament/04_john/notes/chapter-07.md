# John Chapter 7 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 7:53 — Beginning of the Pericope Adulterae
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes 7:53 as the beginning of the Pericope Adulterae (7:53-8:11). The SBLGNT marks this passage with double brackets, indicating it is not part of the earliest manuscripts. The Hebrew includes the verse, which is correct for following the Vulgate/Catholic canon.
- **Vulgate**: Et reversi sunt unusquisque in domum suam.
- **Greek**: [kai eporeuthē hekastos eis ton oikon autou]
- **Hebrew**: va-yelkhu ish ish le-veito.
- **Note**: All three texts are present and aligned. This verse must be included per the Vulgate tradition.

## Content Alignment

### 7:1 — "ambulabat" / "halakh...halokh ve-avor"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "ambulabat Jesus in Galilaeam" (Jesus walked in Galilee) is a simple statement. The Hebrew elaborates: "halakh Yeshua be-eretz ha-Galil halokh ve-avor" (Jesus walked in the land of the Galilee, walking and passing through), adding the infinitive absolute construction for emphasis. This is not reflected in either the Vulgate or the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: Post hæc autem ambulabat Jesus in Galilæam
- **Greek**: Kai meta tauta periepatei ho Iesous en te Galilaia
- **Hebrew**: ...halakh Yeshua be-eretz ha-Galil halokh ve-avor
- **Note**: The infinitive absolute "halokh ve-avor" is stylistically biblical (cf. Genesis 8:3) but adds semantic content not in the source texts.

### 7:8 — "non ascendo" vs "ouk/oupo anabaino"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ego autem non ascendo" (I do not go up). The SBLGNT has a textual variant: some manuscripts read "ouk" (not), others "oupo" (not yet). The Hebrew has "lo e'eleh" (I will not go up), following "ouk" and the Vulgate. This is the correct reading per the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: ego autem non ascendo ad diem festum istum
- **Greek**: ego ouk [or oupo] anabaino eis ten heorten tauten
- **Hebrew**: ani lo e'eleh el-he-chag ha-zeh
- **Note**: The Vulgate "non" is unambiguous. The Hebrew correctly follows it.

### 7:39 — "nondum erat Spiritus datus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "nondum erat Spiritus datus" (the Spirit had not yet been given). The SBLGNT has "oupo gar en pneuma" (for the Spirit was not yet). The Hebrew has "ki lo nittan ruach ha-qodesh" (for the Holy Spirit had not yet been given), adding "ha-qodesh" (holy) and the verb "nittan" (given), which aligns with the Vulgate's "datus" against the shorter Greek.
- **Vulgate**: nondum enim erat Spiritus datus
- **Greek**: oupo gar en pneuma
- **Hebrew**: ki lo nittan ruach ha-qodesh
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly adds "given" per the Vulgate. The addition of "ha-qodesh" (holy) goes slightly beyond the Vulgate's simple "Spiritus" but is theologically appropriate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 7:2 — "chag ha-Sukkot"
- **Hebrew**: va-yikrav chag ha-Yehudim chag ha-Sukkot
- **Note**: "chag ha-Sukkot" (Feast of Tabernacles/Booths) is the exact biblical Hebrew name for Sukkot (cf. Leviticus 23:34, Deuteronomy 16:13). The Greek "skenopegia" (setting up of tents) is a direct translation of the Hebrew concept. The Vulgate "Scenopegia" is a transliteration. Delitzsch's use of the Hebrew festival name is ideal.

### 7:22 — Circumcision argument
- **Hebrew**: Moshe natan lakhem ha-milah akh lo mi-Moshe hi ki im-min-ha-avot u-ve-yom ha-Shabbat tamulu khol-zakhar
- **Note**: The circumcision/Sabbath a fortiori argument is rendered in natural halakhic Hebrew. "ha-milah" (circumcision), "tamulu khol-zakhar" (you circumcise every male) -- this is the vocabulary of rabbinic discussion. The Hebrew reader would immediately recognize the legal reasoning pattern from Talmudic discourse.

### 7:37-38 — Living water proclamation
- **Hebrew**: ish ki yitzma yavo-na elai ve-yishteh...mi-bitno yinharu naharei mayim chayyim
- **Note**: "naharei mayim chayyim" (rivers of living water) echoes Ezekiel 47:1-12 and Zechariah 14:8 (both associated with Sukkot liturgy). "mi-bitno" (from his belly/innermost being) uses "beten" which in biblical Hebrew signifies the inner person (cf. Proverbs 20:27). The Sukkot setting makes this water imagery particularly resonant, as the water-drawing ceremony (simchat beit ha-sho'evah) was a central feature of the festival.

### 7:42 — Bethlehem prophecy
- **Hebrew**: ha-lo ha-katuv omer ki mi-zera David u-mi-kfar Beit-Lechem mekom David yetze ha-Mashiach
- **Note**: Direct allusion to Micah 5:1 (5:2 in English). "mi-kfar Beit-Lechem" (from the village of Bethlehem) -- "kfar" (village) matches the Vulgate's "castello" (small town/village). The Hebrew "mekom David" (place of David) is a good expansion. The ironic Johannine point is that the crowd does not realize Jesus was in fact born in Bethlehem.

### 7:46 — "me-olam lo dibber ish ka-ish ha-zeh"
- **Hebrew**: me-olam lo dibber ish ka-ish ha-zeh
- **Note**: "Never has a man spoken like this man" -- the officers' testimony. The Hebrew is beautiful in its simplicity. "me-olam" (from eternity/never) adds a stronger temporal dimension than the Greek "oudepote" (never). The Latin "Numquam sic locutus est homo, sicut hic homo" is faithfully rendered.

## Other Notes

### 7:37-39 — Sukkot water ceremony
The setting of the last great day of Sukkot (v37) is critical context. During Sukkot, priests drew water from the Pool of Siloam and poured it on the altar in a ceremony called "nisukh ha-mayim" (water libation). Jesus' proclamation about living water deliberately interrupts and fulfills this liturgical action. The Hebrew text naturally preserves this cultural context in a way translations into other languages cannot.

### 7:49 — "arurim hemah" (they are cursed)
The Pharisees' dismissal of the common people -- "ha-hamon ha-zeh asher einam yod'im et-ha-Torah arurim hemah" (this crowd who do not know the Torah, cursed are they) -- echoes the rabbinic concept of "am ha-aretz" (people of the land, i.e., the unlearned). The Hebrew vocabulary here naturally conveys the social contempt that the Greek and Latin express more neutrally.

### 7:50-52 — Nicodemus
Nicodemus' intervention uses legal language: "ha-ki tishpot Toratenu ish" (does our Torah judge a man). This is the language of halakhic procedure and shows Nicodemus operating within the Pharisaic legal framework even as he defends Jesus.
