# John Chapter 4 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 54 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 4:1 — "ha-Adon" vs "Iesus"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew reads "la-Adon" (to the Lord) where the Vulgate has "Jesus." The SBLGNT critical text reads "Iesous," though some Greek manuscripts have "Kyrios" (Lord). Delitzsch followed a variant Greek tradition.
- **Vulgate**: Ut ergo cognovit Jesus quia audierunt pharisaei
- **Greek**: Hos oun egno ho Iesous hoti ekousan hoi Pharisaioi
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi ka-asher noda la-Adon ki shame'u ha-Perushim
- **Note**: The Hebrew should read "le-Yeshua" to follow the Vulgate's "Jesus." The "Adon" reading, while reverential, departs from the Vulgate.

### 4:9 — "non enim coutuntur"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes the explanatory gloss "non enim coutuntur Judaei Samaritanis" (for Jews do not associate with Samaritans). The SBLGNT likewise includes it. The Hebrew preserves it: "ki-lo yit'arvu ha-Yehudim im-ha-Shomronim." All three agree.
- **Vulgate**: non enim coutuntur Judæi Samaritanis.
- **Greek**: ou gar sunchrontai Ioudaioi Samaritais.
- **Hebrew**: ki-lo yit'arvu ha-Yehudim im-ha-Shomronim.
- **Note**: Alignment is correct across all three.

### 4:42 — "Salvator mundi" / "ha-Mashiach"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes "ha-Mashiach" (the Messiah) in brackets before "Moshia ha-olam" (Savior of the world). The Vulgate has only "Salvator mundi" without "Christus." The SBLGNT also lacks "ho Christos" in the main text. The bracketed word suggests Delitzsch recognized it as secondary.
- **Vulgate**: hic est vere Salvator mundi.
- **Greek**: houtos estin alethos ho soter tou kosmou.
- **Hebrew**: zeh hu [ha-Mashiach] Moshia ha-olam.
- **Note**: The brackets around "ha-Mashiach" indicate it is not in the Vulgate. This editorial marking is appropriate but should be flagged for review.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 4:6 — "be'er" vs "pege/fons"
- **Hebrew**: ve-sham be'er Ya'akov
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses "be'er" (well, a dug well) consistently. The Greek uses "pege" (spring/source) in this verse, and the Vulgate uses "fons" (spring/fountain). Later in v11-12 the Greek switches to "phrear" (dug well/cistern), which matches "be'er" better. The distinction is lost in the Hebrew by using "be'er" throughout, though this is a minor point.

### 4:10 — "mayim chayyim"
- **Hebrew**: ve-natan lakh mayim chayyim
- **Note**: "mayim chayyim" (living water) is the exact biblical Hebrew idiom for flowing/spring water (cf. Genesis 26:19, Leviticus 14:5, Jeremiah 2:13). This double meaning -- literal fresh water and life-giving divine water -- mirrors the Greek "hydor zon" and Latin "aquam vivam" perfectly. Excellent translation that preserves the Johannine wordplay.

### 4:24 — "ha-Elohim ruach hu"
- **Hebrew**: ha-Elohim ruach hu ve-ha-mishtachavim lo tzerichim le-hishtachavot be-ruach u-ve-emet
- **Note**: "ha-Elohim ruach hu" (God is spirit) correctly follows Hebrew predicate order. "tzerichim" (need/must) is post-biblical/Mishnaic vocabulary; biblical Hebrew would express obligation differently (perhaps "yesh lahem" or simply the imperfect).

### 4:25 — "Kristos" transliteration
- **Hebrew**: asher yikkare lo Kristos
- **Note**: Delitzsch transliterates "Christos" as "Kristos" rather than providing a Hebrew equivalent. Since "ha-Mashiach" already appears in the same verse, this serves as a gloss explaining the Greek title to Hebrew readers. This is functional but awkward.

### 4:26 — "Ani hu" / "Ego sum"
- **Hebrew**: ani ha-medabber elayikh ani hu
- **Note**: "ani hu" (I am he) is the standard Hebrew rendering of the Johannine "ego eimi." The phrase echoes the divine self-identification in Isaiah 41:4, 43:10, 46:4. The expansion "ani ha-medabber elayikh ani hu" (I who speak to you, I am he) faithfully renders both the Greek and Vulgate.

## Other Notes

### 4:20 — Gerizim worship
The Samaritan woman's reference to "this mountain" (ba-har ha-zeh) alludes to Mount Gerizim. The Hebrew vocabulary for worship throughout this passage, "hishtachavu" (to bow down/worship), is the standard biblical Hebrew term (cf. Exodus 34:8, Psalm 95:6). This is the same root used in the Decalogue's prohibition of idol worship, giving the conversation about true worship added resonance.

### 4:46 — "regulus" / "ish me-avdei ha-melekh"
The Vulgate's "regulus" (royal official/petty king) becomes "ish me-avdei ha-melekh" (a man of the king's servants) in the Hebrew. The Greek "basilikos" means "royal official." The Hebrew rendering emphasizes his status as a servant of the king rather than his rank, which subtly shifts the characterization but is not inaccurate.
