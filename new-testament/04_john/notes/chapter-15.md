# John Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 27 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 15:2 — "tollet" vs "airei" vs "yesirenu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Omnem palmitem in me non ferentem fructum, tollet eum" (Every branch in me not bearing fruit, he will take away). The SBLGNT has "pan klema en emoi me pheron karpon airei auto" (every branch in me not bearing fruit, he takes away). The Hebrew has "kol sarig bi asher einennu oseh peri yesirenu" (every branch in me that does not bear fruit, he will remove it).
- **Vulgate**: tollet eum (will take away)
- **Greek**: airei auto (takes away / lifts up)
- **Hebrew**: yesirenu (will remove it)
- **Note**: The Greek "airei" has a double meaning: "takes away" or "lifts up." Some commentators argue the vine-dresser "lifts up" fallen branches before cutting. The Vulgate "tollet" and Hebrew "yesirenu" (from s-w-r, to remove/turn aside) both unambiguously mean removal/cutting off. Both follow the dominant interpretation.

### 15:2 — "purgabit" vs "yetaharenu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "purgabit eum" (he will purge/prune it). The SBLGNT has "kathairei auto" (he cleanses/prunes it). The Hebrew has "yetaharenu" (he will purify/cleanse it).
- **Vulgate**: purgabit eum, ut fructum plus afferat
- **Greek**: kathairei auto hina karpon pleiona phere
- **Hebrew**: yetaharenu le-harbot et-piryo
- **Note**: The Hebrew "yetaharenu" (from t-h-r, to be clean/pure) captures the ritual purification sense of "kathairei" and the Vulgate's "purgabit." There is an implicit wordplay in the Greek between "kathairei" (prunes) and "katharoi" (clean) in v3 -- the Hebrew partially preserves this with "yetaharenu" (v2) and "metoharim" (v3), both from the same root t-h-r.

### 15:16 — "posui vos" vs Hebrew "ve-hifqadti etkhem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "posui vos ut eatis" (I have appointed you that you should go). The SBLGNT has "etheka hymas hina hymeis hypagete" (I set you that you should go). The Hebrew has "ve-hifqadti etkhem lalekhet" (and I have appointed/commissioned you to go).
- **Vulgate**: posui vos ut eatis
- **Greek**: etheka hymas hina hymeis hypagete
- **Hebrew**: ve-hifqadti etkhem lalekhet
- **Note**: The Hebrew "hifqadti" (from p-q-d, to appoint/commission/visit) is richer than the simple "posui" (placed). The root p-q-d carries connotations of divine visitation and commissioning throughout the OT (cf. Numbers 1:50, where Levites are "appointed" over the tabernacle). Delitzsch's word choice elevates the apostolic commission.

### 15:25 — Psalm quotation — "odio habuerunt me gratis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Quia odio habuerunt me gratis" (Because they hated me without cause). The SBLGNT has "hoti emisisan me dorean" (because they hated me without cause). The Hebrew reads "sin'at chinnam sene'uni" (with gratuitous hatred they hated me), quoting Psalm 35:19 / 69:5.
- **Vulgate**: odio habuerunt me gratis
- **Greek**: emisisan me dorean
- **Hebrew**: sin'at chinnam sene'uni
- **Note**: The Hebrew "sin'at chinnam" (gratuitous hatred) is the exact phrase that became a central concept in rabbinic theology -- the Second Temple was destroyed because of "sin'at chinnam" (Yoma 9b). Jesus' use of this phrase is deeply ironic: the very gratuitous hatred that the tradition would later identify as the cause of the Temple's destruction is being directed at the one who is the true Temple.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 15:1 — "Anokhi ha-gefen ha-amittit"
- **Hebrew**: anokhi ha-gefen ha-amittit ve-Avi hu ha-korem
- **Note**: "ha-gefen ha-amittit" (the true vine) uses "amittit" (true/genuine), the feminine form matching "gefen" (vine, feminine). "ha-korem" (the vinedresser) is the standard Hebrew for one who tends a vineyard (from k-r-m, vineyard). The imagery connects to Isaiah 5:1-7 (the Song of the Vineyard) and Psalm 80:9-16 (the vine brought out of Egypt). Israel was the vine; now Christ declares himself the true vine. "Anokhi" again carries the divine self-revelation weight.

### 15:4-5 — "imdu bi" (abide in me)
- **Hebrew**: imdu-vi va-ani vakhem...ha-omed bi va-ani vo hu ya'aseh peri la-rov
- **Note**: Delitzsch renders "meinete en emoi" / "manete in me" as "imdu bi" (stand in me), from the root '-m-d (to stand). This is an unusual choice. The more natural Hebrew for "abide/remain" would be "shikhnu bi" (dwell in me) or "hishshar'u bi" (remain in me). The root '-m-d (standing) emphasizes stability and firmness rather than dwelling. It works in context -- standing firm in the vine -- but misses the dwelling/remaining nuance of the Greek "meno" and Latin "maneo."

### 15:6 — "ve-hayah le-va'er"
- **Hebrew**: ish asher lo ya'amod bi hushlakh ha-chutzah ka-sarig va-yivash ve-yilqetum ve-yashlikhum el-tokh ha-esh ve-hayah le-va'er
- **Note**: "ve-hayah le-va'er" (and it shall be for burning) echoes Ezekiel 15:4-6, where the wood of the vine is cast into fire as fuel. The entire verse is structured in the waw-consecutive chain typical of biblical narrative: "hushlakh...va-yivash...ve-yilqetum...ve-yashlikhum" (was cast out...and dried up...and they will gather them...and cast them). The eschatological judgment imagery is vivid.

### 15:13 — "ha-noten nafsho be'ad yedidav"
- **Hebrew**: ein ahavah rabbah me-ahavat ha-noten nafsho be'ad yedidav
- **Note**: "ha-noten nafsho be'ad yedidav" (he who gives his soul/life for his friends) uses "nefesh" (soul/life) in its fullest biblical sense. "yedidav" (his beloved friends) from the root y-d-d carries warmth and intimacy. "be'ad" (on behalf of/in place of) conveys substitutionary sacrifice. The construction echoes 2 Samuel 1:26 where David mourns Jonathan, and the language of the akedah (binding of Isaac) in Genesis 22.

### 15:15 — "lo eqra lakhem od avadim"
- **Hebrew**: lo eqra lakhem od avadim ki ha-eved einennu yode'a et-asher ya'aseh adonav ve-amarti yedidai attem
- **Note**: The transition from "avadim" (servants/slaves) to "yedidai" (my friends/beloved) marks a radical shift in status. "eved" (servant) is the term used for Moses (Deuteronomy 34:5), Joshua (Joshua 24:29), and David (2 Samuel 7:5). These were the highest-ranking individuals in Israel. Christ elevates the disciples beyond even this: from servants to friends, with full disclosure of the Father's counsel.

### 15:20 — "zikhru" for "mementote"
- **Hebrew**: zikhru et-devarai asher dibbarti alekhem
- **Note**: The Hebrew begins with "zikhru" which appears to be a variant form. The expected imperative of z-k-r would be "zikhru" (remember!). The root z-k-r is the fundamental Hebrew memory verb, carrying covenantal weight -- to remember is to actualize, not merely to recall mentally. The command to remember persecution as Christ was persecuted connects to the covenantal memory traditions of Passover.

### 15:26 — "Ruach ha-Emet ha-yotze me-et Avi"
- **Hebrew**: u-ve-vo ha-Praqlit asher eshlach lakhem me-et Avi Ruach ha-Emet ha-yotze me-et Avi hu ya'id alai
- **Note**: "ha-yotze me-et Avi" (who proceeds from the Father) uses "yotze" (goes out), the standard Hebrew for procession/emanation. This verse is central to the filioque controversy. The Hebrew follows the Greek "para tou patros ekporeuetai" in stating procession from the Father. The Spirit is both "sent" by Christ ("eshlach" -- I will send) and "proceeds from" the Father ("yotze me-et Avi"), preserving the dual-origin language that both Eastern and Western theology engage with.

## Other Notes

### 15:1-8 — Vine and Branches Allegory
The entire passage functions as a mashal (parable/allegory) with deep OT roots. The vine as Israel is established in Isaiah 5, Jeremiah 2:21, Ezekiel 15 and 19, Hosea 10:1, and Psalm 80. By declaring "anokhi ha-gefen ha-amittit" (I am the true vine), Jesus replaces national Israel with himself as the locus of fruitfulness before God. The Hebrew allows these intertextual connections to resonate in their original linguistic register.

### 15:18-25 — The World's Hatred
The forensic structure of this passage -- "if they persecuted me...if they kept my word" -- uses the conditional "im" consistently in Hebrew. The logic is qal va-chomer (light to heavy, a fortiori): if the master was hated, how much more the servants. The Hebrew legal-reasoning structure is natural to the argument.

### 15:22 — "lo hayah vahem chet"
"If I had not come and spoken to them, they would have no sin." The Hebrew "lo hayah vahem chet" (there would not be sin in them) uses the counterfactual "lulei" (if not). This raises the paradox of culpability: the revelation of truth increases responsibility. The Hebrew "chet" (sin, from ch-t-', to miss the mark) is the most common OT sin word, appropriate for the basic failure to believe.
