# John Chapter 19 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 19:14-15 — Verse boundary shift
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's v14 ends with "Ecce rex vester" (Behold your king). The crowd's response "Tolle, tolle, crucifige eum" (Away, away, crucify him) begins v15. However, the Hebrew places the crowd's cry "tul tul tzelov oto" (away, away, crucify him) at the end of v14, where the Vulgate and Greek place it in v15. The Hebrew v14 reads: "...va-yomer el-ha-yehudim hinneh malkkekhem ve-hem za'aqu tul tul tzelov oto."
- **Note**: The crowd's outcry has been pulled from v15 into v14 in the Hebrew. This means the Hebrew v15 begins with Pilate's question about crucifying their king, omitting the repeated cry. Content is preserved but redistributed across the verse boundary.

## Content Alignment

### 19:7 — "Filium Dei" / "ben-Elohim"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree: "quia Filium Dei se fecit" / "hoti huion theou heauton epoiesen" / "ki-asah atzmo ben-Elohim." No divergence. The charge that he made himself Son of God is consistent across traditions.
- **Note**: Precise alignment. The Hebrew uses the construct chain "ben-Elohim" (son of God) without the article, matching the anarthrous Greek "huion theou."

### 19:10 — Word order of "crucifigere/dimittere"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "potestatem habeo crucifigere te, et potestatem habeo dimittere te" (power to crucify you, and power to release you) -- crucifixion first, then release. The SBLGNT reads "exousian echo apolusai se kai exousian echo staurosai se" -- release first, then crucifixion. The Hebrew follows the Greek order: "yesh-le-el yadi li-tzelovkha ve-yesh-le-el yadi le-shallchekha" -- crucify first, then release. Actually the Hebrew has crucify then release, but the SBLGNT has release then crucify.
- **Vulgate**: potestatem habeo crucifigere te, et potestatem habeo dimittere te
- **Greek**: exousian echo apolusai se kai exousian echo staurosai se
- **Hebrew**: yesh-le-el yadi li-tzelovkha ve-yesh-le-el yadi le-shallchekha
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate order (crucify, then release) rather than the SBLGNT order (release, then crucify). This is correct for our purposes since we follow the Vulgate.

### 19:13 — "Lithostrotos" / "Ritzpah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in loco qui dicitur Lithostrotos, hebraice autem Gabbatha." The Hebrew renders "Lithostrotos" as "ritzpah" (pavement/floor) and gives the Aramaic "Gabbatha" as "Gabbeta." The Hebrew "ritzpah" (cf. 2 Samuel 17:28, 2 Chronicles 7:3, Ezekiel 40:17-18) is the correct Hebrew equivalent of the Greek "Lithostrotos" (stone-paved).
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "hebraice" (in Hebrew) actually refers to Aramaic, as is common in John's Gospel. The Hebrew text uses "bi-leshonam" (in their language), which avoids the confusion.

### 19:14 — "hora quasi sexta" / "ka-sha'ah ha-shishit"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "hora quasi sexta" (about the sixth hour). The Hebrew reads "ve-kha-sha'ah ha-shishit" (about the sixth hour). Both agree. The famous chronological discrepancy between John (sixth hour = noon, Jesus still before Pilate) and Mark 15:25 (third hour = 9am, already crucified) is not a textual variant but a Johannine theological choice. The Hebrew faithfully preserves John's timing.
- **Note**: No divergence between sources.

### 19:20 — Language order on the inscription
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "hebraice, graece, et latine" (Hebrew, Greek, and Latin). The SBLGNT reads "Hebraisti, Romaisti, Hellenisti" (Hebrew, Roman/Latin, Greek) -- a different order. The Hebrew reads "ivrit vi-yevanit ve-romit" (Hebrew, Greek, and Roman), following neither exactly but closest to the Vulgate's order with Greek before Latin.
- **Vulgate**: hebraice, graece, et latine
- **Greek**: Hebraisti, Romaisti, Hellenisti
- **Hebrew**: ivrit vi-yevanit ve-romit
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate order (Hebrew-Greek-Latin) rather than the SBLGNT order (Hebrew-Latin-Greek). This is correct for our purposes.

### 19:29 — "hyssopo" / "ezov"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on hyssop: "hyssopo" / "hysopo" / "ezov." The Hebrew "ezov" is the original biblical word (Exodus 12:22, Leviticus 14:4, Psalm 51:9) from which the Greek and Latin are derived. The Passover connection via Exodus 12:22 (using hyssop to apply the blood of the lamb) is unmistakable.
- **Note**: Perfect alignment. The Hebrew word is the original source of the Greek/Latin terms.

### 19:30 — "Consummatum est" / "kulah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Consummatum est" (It is finished/completed). The Greek has "Tetelestai" (It is completed/accomplished). The Hebrew renders this as "kulah" -- a single word meaning "all of it" or "it is complete." This is an unusual compression. A verbal form like "nishlamah" (it is completed) or "tam" (it is finished) might more closely match the verbal force of "Tetelestai" and "Consummatum est."
- **Vulgate**: Consummatum est
- **Greek**: Tetelestai
- **Hebrew**: kulah
- **Note**: "kulah" functions as an exclamation meaning "all is done," but it is nominal rather than verbal. The Vulgate and Greek both use a verbal form (perfect passive). The Hebrew loses some of the verbal force, though the meaning is preserved.

### 19:34 — "continuo" / no equivalent
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "continuo exivit sanguis et aqua" (immediately blood and water came out). The SBLGNT reads "exelthen euthys haima kai hydor" (immediately came out blood and water). The Hebrew reads "va-yetze dam va-mayim" (and blood and water came out), omitting any equivalent of "continuo/euthys" (immediately).
- **Vulgate**: continuo exivit sanguis et aqua
- **Hebrew**: va-yetze dam va-mayim
- **Note**: The adverb "immediately" is lost in the Hebrew. To match the Vulgate, something like "va-yetze miyad dam va-mayim" would be needed.

### 19:36 — "Os non comminuetis ex eo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate quotes "Os non comminuetis ex eo" (A bone of him you shall not break), alluding to Exodus 12:46 and Numbers 9:12 (Passover lamb) and Psalm 34:21 (the righteous one). The Hebrew "ve-etzem lo-tishberu vo" faithfully renders this, using the same root sh-b-r (break) found in the original Exodus 12:46 ("ve-etzem lo tishberu vo"). The Hebrew is quoting the actual biblical source text.
- **Note**: The Hebrew here is not a back-translation but a direct quotation of the Pentateuchal source.

### 19:37 — Zechariah 12:10 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Videbunt in quem transfixerunt" (They shall look upon him whom they pierced). The Hebrew reads "ve-habbitu elav et asher daqaru" (and they shall look upon him whom they pierced). The original Hebrew of Zechariah 12:10 reads "ve-habbitu elai et asher daqaru" (they shall look upon ME whom they pierced) -- with "elai" (upon me, first person) rather than "elav" (upon him, third person). The change from first to third person follows the Greek/Latin tradition rather than the Masoretic Hebrew of Zechariah.
- **Vulgate**: Videbunt in quem transfixerunt
- **Greek**: Opsontai eis hon exekentasan
- **Hebrew**: ve-habbitu elav et asher daqaru
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the NT quotation tradition (third person "elav") rather than inserting the Masoretic first person "elai." This is correct for a NT translation -- the evangelist is quoting Zechariah as he received it.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 19:2 — "ateret qotzim"
- **Hebrew**: va-yesargu anshei ha-tzava ateret qotzim va-yasimu al-rosho
- **Note**: "ateret qotzim" (crown of thorns) -- "atarah" (crown/wreath) is biblical Hebrew (2 Samuel 12:30, Proverbs 12:4, Song of Songs 3:11). "qotzim" (thorns) appears extensively in Scripture (Genesis 3:18, Judges 8:7, Isaiah 32:13). The juxtaposition of "atarah" (associated with royalty and honor) with "qotzim" (associated with the curse of Genesis 3:18) is theologically potent in Hebrew. The verb "va-yesargu" (they plaited/wove) from the root s-r-g is post-biblical, but there is no common biblical equivalent for plaiting.

### 19:5 — "Hinneh ha-adam"
- **Hebrew**: va-yomer aleihem Pilatos hinneh ha-adam
- **Note**: "hinneh ha-adam" (behold the man) for "Ecce homo." The demonstrative "hinneh" (behold) combined with "ha-adam" (the man/the human) resonates with the creation account (Genesis 1:26-27, 2:7) where "adam" is formed from "adamah." The definite article "ha" makes this not just "a man" but "THE man" -- the representative human being. In Hebrew the theological depth is extraordinary: the crowned-with-thorns figure is presented using the same word as the first human, recalling both the image of God and the curse of thorns from Genesis 3:18.

### 19:11 — "avon ha-masgir"
- **Hebrew**: avon ha-masgir oti elekha gadol me-avonekha
- **Note**: "avon ha-masgir" (the sin of the one who delivered me) -- "masgir" from the Hiphil of s-g-r (to hand over/deliver up) echoes the language of betrayal throughout the Hebrew Bible (cf. Obadiah 1:14, Amos 1:6). "gadol me-avonekha" (greater than your sin) is an important theological distinction: both Judas/the Sanhedrin and Pilate bear guilt, but the betrayer's is greater. The Hebrew uses the comparative construction naturally.

### 19:17 — "tzeluvo" and "meqom ha-gulggolet"
- **Hebrew**: va-yisa et-tzeluvo va-yetze el-ha-maqom ha-niqra meqom ha-gulggolet u-vi-leshonam Galgalta
- **Note**: "tzeluvo" (his cross) from the root tz-l-v is the standard Hebrew term. "meqom ha-gulggolet" (place of the skull) translates "Calvaria/Kranion" accurately -- "gulggolet" is biblical Hebrew for skull (Numbers 1:2, 18, 20, 22; Judges 9:53; 2 Kings 9:35). The Aramaic "Galgalta" is given as the local pronunciation using "u-vi-leshonam" (and in their language), which again avoids the Vulgate's misleading "hebraice."

### 19:23 — "kuttonet" and priestly garment
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-kuttonet lo-haytah tefurah ki im-ma'aseh oreg mi-piha ve-ad-qatzah
- **Note**: "kuttonet" (tunic) is the word used for the priestly garment in Exodus 28:4, 39 and for Joseph's special coat in Genesis 37:3. The description "lo-haytah tefurah ki im-ma'aseh oreg mi-piha ve-ad-qatzah" (was not sewn but woven from its top to its end) echoes the description of the High Priest's garment construction. The Vulgate's "inconsutilis, desuper contexta per totum" (without seam, woven from the top throughout) is precisely rendered. The priestly typology is unmistakable in Hebrew.

### 19:25 — "va-achot immo"
- **Hebrew**: ve-al-yad tzeluv Yeshua amdu immo va-achot immo Miryam eshet Qlofas u-Miryam ha-Magddalit
- **Note**: The list of women at the cross. The Hebrew syntax allows for either three or four women, as does the Greek and Latin. "achot immo" (his mother's sister) could be in apposition with "Miryam eshet Qlofas" (Mary wife of Clopas) making three women, or a separate person making four. The Hebrew leaves the same ambiguity as the Vulgate and Greek.

### 19:30 — "va-tetzeh rucho"
- **Hebrew**: va-yet et-rosho va-tetzeh rucho
- **Note**: "va-tetzeh rucho" (and his spirit went out) for "tradidit spiritum" (he handed over his spirit). The Vulgate's "tradidit" (handed over/delivered) implies an active, voluntary giving up of the spirit. The Hebrew "va-tetzeh" (went out) is more passive -- the spirit departed rather than being actively delivered. The Greek "paredoken to pneuma" (he handed over the spirit) matches the Vulgate's active sense. A more precise Hebrew rendering of the Vulgate would be "va-yimsor et-rucho" (he handed over his spirit), using the same m-s-r root used for Judas's betrayal, creating an intentional verbal echo between the betrayer's handing over of Christ and Christ's handing over of his own spirit.

### 19:39 — "mor va-ahalot"
- **Hebrew**: va-yave ta'arovet mor-va-ahalot ke-me'ah litrin
- **Note**: "mor" (myrrh) and "ahalot" (aloes) are both biblical Hebrew (Psalm 45:9, Proverbs 7:17, Song of Songs 4:14). The combination appears in Proverbs 7:17. The quantity "ke-me'ah litrin" (about a hundred litrai) uses the Greek weight measure "litra" transliterated into Hebrew -- there is no biblical Hebrew equivalent for this Roman/Greek unit. The Vulgate's "quasi libras centum" uses the Latin "libra."

## Other Notes

### 19:19 — INRI in Hebrew
The titulus "Yeshua ha-Natzri melekh ha-Yehudim" (Jesus of Nazareth, King of the Jews) is the Hebrew original behind the famous Latin abbreviation INRI (Iesus Nazarenus Rex Iudaeorum). The Hebrew text here is what would have actually appeared on the cross in the Hebrew/Aramaic line of the trilingual inscription. Delitzsch's rendering is plausible as the historical Hebrew text, though the original may have been in Aramaic script.

### 19:24 — Psalm 22:19 quotation
"yechallequ vegadai lahem ve-al-levushi yappilu goral" -- this is a direct Hebrew quotation of Psalm 22:19 (MT numbering). Delitzsch correctly uses the Masoretic text of the Psalm rather than back-translating from the Greek. The Hebrew quotation matches the original Psalm verbatim, which is ideal for a NT Hebrew translation.

### 19:31 — Parasceve / erev Shabbat
"ki-erev Shabbat hayah" (for it was the eve of Sabbath). The Vulgate's "parasceve" is a transliteration of the Greek "paraskeue" (preparation day), which is itself a translation of the Hebrew "erev Shabbat" (Sabbath eve). Delitzsch correctly uses the original Hebrew term rather than transliterating the Greek/Latin loanword.
