# John Chapter 16 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The Vulgate places the first part of SBLGNT 16:4b-5 into a combined verse 5. The Vulgate's v5 begins with "Haec autem vobis ab initio non dixi, quia vobiscum eram" (But these things I did not tell you from the beginning because I was with you), which in the SBLGNT belongs to the end of v4. The Vulgate then continues with "Et nunc vado..." (And now I go...) which is SBLGNT v5 proper. The Hebrew follows the SBLGNT division. No other numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 16:4 — Verse split issue (see Versification)
- **Issue**: As noted above, the Vulgate combines material. The Hebrew v4 includes "u-me-rosh lo dibbarti alekhem ka-eleh ki hayiti immakhem" (and from the beginning I did not speak to you like these because I was with you), which the Vulgate places in v5.
- **Note**: Content is identical; verse boundary differs.

### 16:13 — "docebit" vs "hodeghesei" vs "yadrikh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "docebit vos omnem veritatem" (he will teach you all truth). The SBLGNT has "hodeghesei hymas en te aletheia pase" (he will guide you in all truth). The Hebrew has "yadrikh etkhem el-ha-emet kullah" (he will guide you to all truth).
- **Vulgate**: docebit vos omnem veritatem (will teach)
- **Greek**: hodeghesei hymas en te aletheia pase (will guide in)
- **Hebrew**: yadrikh etkhem el-ha-emet kullah (will guide to)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "docebit" (will teach) is more didactic; the Greek "hodeghesei" (will guide/lead on the way) and the Hebrew "yadrikh" (will guide/direct) are more directional, evoking a journey. The Hebrew "yadrikh" from d-r-kh (way) connects to "derekh" (way/road), reinforcing the "I am the Way" theme from 14:6. The Hebrew preposition "el" (to/toward) differs from the Greek "en" (in): being guided "toward" truth vs "in" truth.

### 16:13 — "quae ventura sunt" vs "ha-otiyyot"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quae ventura sunt annuntiabit vobis" (things that are to come he will announce to you). The SBLGNT has "ta erchomena anangelei hymin" (the coming things he will announce to you). The Hebrew has "ve-ha-otiyyot yaggid lakhem" (and the signs/future things he will tell you).
- **Vulgate**: quae ventura sunt
- **Greek**: ta erchomena
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-otiyyot
- **Note**: The Hebrew "otiyyot" is unexpected. The expected word for "things to come" would be "ha-ba'ot" or "ha-atidot." "otiyyot" can mean "signs" or "letters" (of the alphabet), and in some contexts "wonders." This may be a Mishnaic Hebrew usage for "future events" or could be an unusual rendering. The Vulgate's "ventura" (things about to come) is straightforward.

### 16:16 — Bracketed clause in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds a bracketed clause at the end of v16: "[ki ani holekh el-Avi]" ([because I am going to the Father]). The Vulgate includes this: "quia vado ad Patrem." The SBLGNT textual tradition is split on whether this clause belongs in v16.
- **Vulgate**: quia vado ad Patrem
- **Greek**: [variant -- some MSS include, some omit]
- **Hebrew**: [ki ani holekh el-Avi] (bracketed)
- **Note**: The brackets in the Hebrew indicate awareness of the textual uncertainty. The Vulgate includes the clause without hesitation. The Hebrew's bracketing is editorial transparency about the variant.

### 16:23 — "Patrem" placement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "si quid petieritis Patrem in nomine meo, dabit vobis" (if you ask the Father anything in my name, he will give it to you). The SBLGNT has "an ti aitesete ton patera dosei hymin en to onomati mou" with "in my name" attached to "will give" rather than "ask." The Hebrew "kol asher tish'alu me-et Avi bi-shmi yitten lakhem" places "bi-shmi" (in my name) between "me-et Avi" (from my Father) and "yitten lakhem" (he will give you), making the attachment ambiguous.
- **Vulgate**: petieritis Patrem in nomine meo (ask the Father in my name)
- **Greek**: aitesete ton patera dosei hymin en to onomati mou (ask the Father, he will give you in my name)
- **Hebrew**: tish'alu me-et Avi bi-shmi yitten lakhem
- **Note**: The placement of "in my name" matters: does one ask in Jesus' name, or does the Father give in Jesus' name? The Vulgate attaches it to asking; the SBLGNT attaches it to giving. The Hebrew word order is ambiguous, allowing either reading.

### 16:27 — "a Deo" vs "para tou theou/patros"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quia ego a Deo exivi" (because I came forth from God). The SBLGNT has a variant: some manuscripts read "para tou theou" (from God) and others "para tou patros" (from the Father). The Hebrew has "me-et Elohim yatzati" (from God I came forth), aligning with the Vulgate's "a Deo."
- **Vulgate**: ego a Deo exivi
- **Greek**: ego para tou [theou/patros] exelthon
- **Hebrew**: me-et Elohim yatzati
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate in reading "God" rather than "Father."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 16:2 — "yedammeh la'asot avodah le-Elohim"
- **Hebrew**: kol horeg etkhem yedammeh la'asot avodah le-Elohim
- **Note**: "yedammeh la'asot avodah le-Elohim" (will imagine he performs service to God) uses "avodah" (service/worship), the same word used for Temple service. The persecutor's delusion is that murder is liturgy. "yedammeh" (will imagine/suppose) from d-m-h captures the self-deception involved. The prophetic accuracy of this verse -- realized in the persecution of Christians by Jewish authorities (Acts) and later by Roman authorities -- is rendered with precision.

### 16:7 — "Praqlit" -- third Paraclete passage
- **Hebrew**: ki im lo elekh lo yavo alekhem ha-Praqlit ve-im halakhti eshlachehu alekhem
- **Note**: The third Paraclete passage. The conditional logic is clear in Hebrew: "im lo" (if not) / "ve-im" (and if). The necessity of Christ's departure for the Spirit's coming is presented as a strict either/or. "eshlachehu" (I will send him) uses the standard commissioning verb (sh-l-ch), the same root used for the prophetic mission throughout the OT.

### 16:8 — "ve-hokhi'ach et-ha-olam"
- **Hebrew**: ve-hokhi'ach et-ha-olam al-devar ha-chet ve-ha-tzedeq ve-ha-mishpat
- **Note**: "hokhi'ach" (from y-k-ch, to reprove/convict) is a juridical term used in the Hebrew Bible for formal rebuke and legal proof (cf. Genesis 31:37, Job 9:33). The three charges -- "ha-chet" (sin), "ha-tzedeq" (righteousness), "ha-mishpat" (judgment) -- are the fundamental categories of biblical ethics and eschatology. The triad echoes Micah 6:8's "mishpat" and "chesed" framework.

### 16:20-22 — Woman in labor analogy
- **Hebrew**: ha-ishah ki taqriv laledet etzev lah ki mal'u yameha ve-acharei lidtah et-ha-yeled lo tizkhor od et-itzvonah
- **Note**: The labor analogy uses vocabulary from Genesis 3:16 ("be-etzev tel'di vanim" -- in pain you shall bear children). "etzev" (pain/sorrow) and "itzvonah" (her sorrow) are from the same root as the curse on Eve. The resolution of the curse's pain into joy at new birth creates a redemptive arc. "adam nolad la-olam" (a human is born into the world) uses "adam" generically for a human person, matching the Vulgate's "homo" and Greek "anthropos."

### 16:25 — "bi-meshalim" for "in proverbiis"
- **Hebrew**: et eleh dibbarti alekhem bi-meshalim
- **Note**: "meshalim" (parables/proverbs) from m-sh-l is the term used for the Proverbs of Solomon and for prophetic allegories (cf. Ezekiel 17:2). The Vulgate's "proverbiis" and Greek "paroimiais" are both rendered with the single Hebrew term that covers parables, allegories, and wise sayings. The contrast "bi-meshalim" (in parables) vs "barur" (clearly) in the continuation marks the shift from enigmatic to plain teaching.

### 16:28 — Chiastic structure
- **Hebrew**: me-et ha-Av yatzati va-avo la-olam ashuvah asur min-ha-olam ve-elekh el-Avi
- **Note**: The Hebrew preserves the chiastic structure of this verse: A) from the Father I came forth, B) and entered the world, B') I will depart from the world, A') and go to the Father. The verbs form a neat pattern: yatzati (I went out) / va-avo (and I came) / asur (I will depart) / ve-elekh (and I will go). This is the most concise summary of the incarnation and ascension in the Gospel.

### 16:33 — "ani nitzachti et-ha-olam"
- **Hebrew**: et eleh dibbarti alekhem le-ma'an yihyeh lakhem shalom bi tzarah lakhem ba-olam akh ya'ametz levavkhem ani nitzachti et-ha-olam
- **Note**: "ani nitzachti et-ha-olam" (I have conquered the world) uses "nitzachti" from n-tz-ch (to conquer/prevail/endure). This root also appears in the Psalms in the superscription "la-menatze'ach" (to the chief musician/conductor), connecting victory with worship. "ya'ametz levavkhem" (let your heart be strong) echoes the Deuteronomic formula of courage (Deuteronomy 31:6, Joshua 1:6). The verse ends the Farewell Discourse with a declaration of accomplished victory in the prophetic perfect -- the battle is already won.

## Other Notes

### 16:8-11 — Triple "Conviction" Structure
The Spirit's work of conviction is presented in a tightly structured triad. Each element is introduced with "al" (concerning/on account of) and explained with "ki" (because). The Hebrew legal terminology throughout -- "hokhi'ach" (convict), "chet" (sin), "tzedeq" (righteousness), "mishpat" (judgment), "niddon" (is judged) -- frames the Spirit's work as a cosmic trial. The "sar ha-olam" (prince of this world) stands condemned, the unbelieving world is convicted, and the departed Christ is vindicated.

### 16:32 — "u-nefotztem ish le-veito"
"And you will be scattered, each to his own house." The Hebrew "nefotztem" (from p-w-tz, to scatter) is the same verb used for the scattering of Israel among the nations (Ezekiel 11:17, 20:34). The disciples' flight fulfills the shepherd-struck-flock-scattered pattern of Zechariah 13:7. Yet the verse immediately follows with assurance: "ve-eineni le-vaddi ki Avi immadi" (and I am not alone because my Father is with me).
