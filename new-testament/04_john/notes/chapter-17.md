# John Chapter 17 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 26 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 17:1 — "clarifica" vs "pa'er"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate consistently uses "clarifica/clarificavi" (clarify/glorify) throughout chapter 17 for the Greek "doxason/edoxasa." The Hebrew uses "pa'er" (adorn/glorify) rather than the more common "kabbeid" (honor/glorify from k-b-d). This creates a consistent but distinctive translation register.
- **Vulgate**: clarifica Filium tuum, ut Filius tuus clarificet te
- **Greek**: doxason sou ton huion, hina ho huios doxase se
- **Hebrew**: pa'er et-Binkha le-ma'an yefa'erkha gam-Bnekha
- **Note**: The Hebrew "pa'er" from p-'-r (to beautify/glorify) appears in Isaiah 60:7, 13; 61:3 in the context of God glorifying his Temple and his people. It carries connotations of beauty and splendor rather than the weightiness of "kavod." The Vulgate's "clarifica" (make bright/clear) maps well to "pa'er." This word choice is consistent throughout the chapter.

### 17:3 — "solum Deum verum" vs Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ut cognoscant te, solum Deum verum" (that they may know you, the only true God). The SBLGNT has "hina ginoskosi se ton monon alethinon theon" (that they may know you the only true God). The Hebrew has "la-da'at otkha ki attah ha-Elohim levaddekha" (to know you that you are God alone).
- **Vulgate**: solum Deum verum
- **Greek**: ton monon alethinon theon
- **Hebrew**: ki attah ha-Elohim levaddekha
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits "verum/alethinon" (true) and restructures: instead of "the only true God" it reads "you are God alone." The sense is similar but the Hebrew drops the qualifier "true" and instead emphasizes exclusivity with "levaddekha" (you alone). The Vulgate's "verum" (true) distinguishes the true God from false gods; the Hebrew's "levaddekha" (alone) makes a monotheistic statement. To follow the Vulgate precisely, the Hebrew might read "ha-Elohim ha-emet ha-echad."

### 17:11 — "in nomine tuo, quos dedisti mihi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "serva eos in nomine tuo, quos dedisti mihi" (keep them in your name, whom you have given me). The SBLGNT has "tereson autous en to onomati sou ho dedokas moi" (keep them in your name which you have given me). The relative pronoun's antecedent is debated: does "which you have given me" refer to the "name" (Greek) or to "those whom you have given" (Vulgate)?
- **Vulgate**: quos dedisti mihi (whom you have given me -- referring to the persons)
- **Greek**: ho dedokas moi (which you have given me -- referring to the name)
- **Hebrew**: et asher natatta li (that which you have given me)
- **Note**: The Hebrew "et asher natatta li" is ambiguous, like the Greek. The Vulgate resolves the ambiguity by making "those given" refer to the disciples. The Greek reading (the name given) is theologically significant: the Father's name has been entrusted to the Son. Both readings are orthodox.

### 17:12 — "filius perditionis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "nisi filius perditionis" (except the son of perdition). The SBLGNT has "ei me ho huios tes apoleias" (except the son of destruction). The Hebrew has "zulati ben-ha-avaddon" (except the son of destruction/Abaddon).
- **Vulgate**: filius perditionis
- **Greek**: ho huios tes apoleias
- **Hebrew**: ben-ha-avaddon
- **Note**: The Hebrew "Avaddon" is the name of the angel/place of destruction in Job 26:6, 28:22, 31:12, Proverbs 15:11, and Revelation 9:11. By using the proper noun "Avaddon" rather than the common noun "avdan" (destruction), Delitzsch intensifies the title, connecting Judas to the personified abyss of the OT.

### 17:15 — "a malo" vs "min-ha-ra"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ut serves eos a malo" (that you keep them from evil). The SBLGNT has "hina tereses autous ek tou ponerou" (that you keep them from the evil one). The Hebrew has "she-titzrem min-ha-ra" (that you guard them from the evil/evil one).
- **Vulgate**: a malo
- **Greek**: ek tou ponerou
- **Hebrew**: min-ha-ra
- **Note**: The ambiguity is the same in all three: "evil" (abstract) or "the evil one" (Satan)? The Hebrew "ha-ra" (the evil) with the definite article could go either way. In the Lord's Prayer context (Matthew 6:13), the same ambiguity exists. The Catholic tradition generally reads "a malo" as abstract evil, but patristic commentators are divided.

### 17:19 — "sanctifico meipsum" / "maqdish et-nafshi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ego sanctifico meipsum" (I sanctify myself). The SBLGNT has "ego hagiazo emauton" (I sanctify myself). The Hebrew has "ani maqdish et-nafshi" (I sanctify/consecrate my soul/self).
- **Vulgate**: ego sanctifico meipsum
- **Greek**: ego hagiazo emauton
- **Hebrew**: ani maqdish et-nafshi ba'adam
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "ba'adam" (for them/on their behalf) and uses "nafshi" (my soul) rather than a reflexive "myself." "maqdish et-nafshi" (I consecrate my soul) recalls the priestly consecration language of Exodus 28-29. The self-consecration is simultaneously priestly (setting apart for sacred service) and sacrificial (offering one's life). "nafshi" connects to the sacrificial soul-offering of Isaiah 53:10 ("im tasim asham nafsho").

### 17:21 — "ut omnes unum sint"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "ut omnes unum sint" (that all may be one) is a central ecumenical text. The Hebrew "le-ma'an yihyu khulam echad" (so that they all may be one) uses "echad" for "unum/hen."
- **Vulgate**: ut omnes unum sint, sicut tu Pater in me, et ego in te
- **Greek**: hina pantes hen osin, kathos su, pater, en emoi kago en soi
- **Hebrew**: le-ma'an yihyu khulam echad ka-asher attah Avi bi attah va-ani vakh
- **Note**: The Hebrew "echad" (one), as in 10:30, is the Shema word. The prayer for unity of all believers modeled on the unity of Father and Son is rendered with the most theologically loaded unity-word in Hebrew. The triple nesting -- the Father in the Son, the Son in the Father, the believers in both -- is clearly expressed.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 17:1 — "va-yissa einav ha-shamayemah"
- **Hebrew**: et eleh dibber Yeshua va-yissa einav ha-shamayemah va-yomar
- **Note**: "va-yissa einav ha-shamayemah" (and he lifted his eyes heavenward) is a classic biblical prayer posture (cf. Genesis 18:2, Psalm 121:1, Daniel 4:31). The directive -ah suffix on "shamayim" (heaven) creates "shamayemah" (heavenward), a biblical Hebrew formation. The waw-consecutive narrative "va-yissa...va-yomar" (and he lifted...and he said) sets the prayer in the historical-narrative tense, presenting it as an event, not a composition.

### 17:5 — "ba-khavod asher hayah li imkha terem heyot ha-olam"
- **Hebrew**: ve-attah fa'areni attah Avi imkha ba-khavod asher hayah li imkha terem heyot ha-olam
- **Note**: "terem heyot ha-olam" (before the world existed) expresses the pre-existence of Christ. "terem" (before, not yet) is the biblical Hebrew term for temporal priority (cf. Genesis 2:5, "terem yitzmach" -- before it sprouted). The pre-existent glory "asher hayah li imkha" (which was mine with you) asserts that the Son possessed glory with the Father before creation. This is the clearest Johannine pre-existence statement, and the Hebrew renders it unambiguously.

### 17:6 — "et shimkha hoda'ti li-vnei ha-adam"
- **Hebrew**: et shimkha hoda'ti li-vnei ha-adam asher natatta li mi-tokh ha-olam
- **Note**: "et shimkha hoda'ti" (your name I have made known) uses the hiphil of y-d-' (to know), meaning "to cause to know/reveal." "shimkha" (your name) in Hebrew theology is not a mere label but the revealed character of God. The manifestation of the divine Name is the mission of the Son. "li-vnei ha-adam" (to the sons of man/humanity) is more general than the Greek "tois anthropois" -- it evokes the Hebrew "benei adam" that covers all humanity.

### 17:11 — "Avi ha-Qadosh"
- **Hebrew**: Avi ha-Qadosh netzor otam be-shimkha
- **Note**: "Avi ha-Qadosh" (my Holy Father) is the only time this address appears in Scripture. The Hebrew "Qadosh" (Holy) is the defining attribute of God in Isaiah's vision (Isaiah 6:3, "Qadosh, Qadosh, Qadosh"). Addressing the Father as "Holy" before a petition for protection sets the sanctity of the divine name as the ground of the disciples' safety. "netzor" (guard/preserve) from n-tz-r is a watching/vigilance word used for guarding the law (Proverbs 3:1) and guarding vineyards (Song of Songs 1:6).

### 17:17 — "Qaddesh otam ba-amittekha Devarkha emet"
- **Hebrew**: qaddesh otam ba-amittekha Devarkha emet
- **Note**: "qaddesh" (sanctify/consecrate) is the imperative from q-d-sh, the central holiness root of the Hebrew Bible. "ba-amittekha" (in your truth) uses the noun form of emet (truth/faithfulness). The second clause "Devarkha emet" (your Word is truth) is terse and declarative. The identification of God's word with truth echoes Psalm 119:142 ("Toratkha emet" -- your Torah is truth) and 2 Samuel 7:28 ("devarekha yihyu emet" -- your words shall be truth). The substitution of "Devarkha" (your Word) for "Torah" in the Psalm 119 pattern is theologically significant.

### 17:24 — "lifnei mosdot olam"
- **Hebrew**: ki ahavtani lifnei mosdot olam
- **Note**: "lifnei mosdot olam" (before the foundations of the world) renders "ante constitutionem mundi" / "pro kataboles kosmou." The Hebrew "mosdot" (foundations) from y-s-d (to found/establish) connects to creation language in Job 38:4 ("be-yosdi aretz" -- when I founded the earth) and Psalm 104:5. The Father's love for the Son is pre-creational, establishing the eternal relational life of the Trinity before any created thing existed.

## Other Notes

### 17:1-26 — The High Priestly Prayer
The entire chapter is the High Priestly Prayer (sacerdotal prayer), the longest recorded prayer of Jesus. Its structure in Hebrew reads naturally as a prayer text: the address "Avi" (my Father) recurs as a refrain (vv1, 5, 11, 21, 24, 25), creating a six-fold invocation. The prayer moves from the Son's own glorification (vv1-5) to prayer for the present disciples (vv6-19) to prayer for all future believers (vv20-26). The Hebrew sacrificial and priestly vocabulary throughout -- "maqdish" (consecrating), "qaddesh" (sanctify), "shmor" (guard) -- reinforces the priestly character of the prayer.

### 17:22-23 — Unity as Witness
The climactic prayer for unity "le-ma'an yihyu mushalamim le-echad" (that they may be perfected into one) uses "mushalamim" (completed/perfected) from sh-l-m, the root of "shalom" (peace) and "shalem" (whole/complete). The unity of believers is not mere agreement but a completeness and wholeness that mirrors the divine unity. This unity is evangelistic: "u-le-ma'an yeda ha-olam" (and so that the world may know). The Hebrew makes the connection between internal Christian unity and external missionary effectiveness linguistically explicit through the sh-l-m root.

### 17:25 — "Avi ha-Tzaddiq"
The final address shifts from "Avi ha-Qadosh" (v11, Holy Father) to "Avi ha-Tzaddiq" (Righteous Father). "Tzaddiq" (righteous) is the attribute of God that governs judgment and covenant fidelity. The prayer closes by grounding the disciples' security not only in God's holiness (separation/consecration) but in his righteousness (covenantal faithfulness). The pairing of "qadosh" and "tzaddiq" as divine attributes echoes Isaiah's theology where God is both "Qedosh Yisra'el" (Holy One of Israel) and "Tzaddiq" (Righteous).
