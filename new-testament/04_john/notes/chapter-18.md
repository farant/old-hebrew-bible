# John Chapter 18 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 40 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 18:5 — "Ego sum" / "Ani hu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Ego sum" (I am) without the name "Jesus" as subject of the reply. The SBLGNT adds "Iesous" as the subject of "legei" (he says). The Hebrew includes "Yeshua" as subject: "va-yomer aleihem Yeshua ani hu." This follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate, which has the more austere "Dicit eis Jesus: Ego sum" where Jesus is the subject of the saying verb, not repeated redundantly.
- **Vulgate**: Dicit eis Jesus: Ego sum.
- **Greek**: legei autois: Ego eimi.
- **Note**: Minor. Both include the name Jesus as subject of the verb of speaking. The alignment is acceptable; the difference is negligible.

### 18:14 — "mori" vs "yovad"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "unum hominem mori pro populo" (one man to die for the people). The Hebrew uses "ish-echad yovad be'ad kol-ha-am" (one man should perish for all the people). The verb "yovad" (perish) from the root a-b-d is slightly different in register from "mori" (die) -- "perish" carries connotations of destruction rather than simple death. The Greek "apothanein" (to die) matches the Vulgate's "mori."
- **Vulgate**: unum hominem mori pro populo
- **Greek**: hena anthropon apothanein hyper tou laou
- **Hebrew**: ish-echad yovad be'ad kol-ha-am
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "kol" (all) before "ha-am" (the people), matching the reading of 11:50. The Vulgate here does not have "all" (it simply reads "pro populo"). The word "kol" should be evaluated against the Vulgate reading.

### 18:15-16 — "alius discipulus" vs "talmid acher"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate in v15 reads "alius discipulus" (another disciple) while the SBLGNT reads "allos mathetes" with a variant adding the article. In v16, the Vulgate reads "discipulus alius" and the SBLGNT has "ho mathetes ho allos" (the other disciple) with the definite article. The Hebrew in v15 uses "talmid acher" (another disciple, indefinite) and in v16 "ha-talmid ha-acher" (the other disciple, definite). This correctly mirrors the shift from indefinite to definite across the two verses, following the Greek more closely. The Vulgate lacks articles, but the Hebrew rendering is defensible.
- **Note**: No correction needed. The Hebrew handles the definite/indefinite distinction naturally.

### 18:20 — "palam" vs "be-oznei kol-ha-olam"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Ego palam locutus sum mundo" (I have spoken openly to the world). The Hebrew renders this as "anokhi dibarti be-oznei kol-ha-olam" (I have spoken in the ears of the whole world). The phrase "be-oznei" (in the ears of) is an idiomatic expansion not present in the Latin "palam" (openly) or the Greek "parresia" (with boldness/openly). It adds a concrete Hebrew idiom where the original is abstract.
- **Vulgate**: Ego palam locutus sum mundo
- **Greek**: Ego parresia lelaleka to kosmo
- **Hebrew**: anokhi dibarti be-oznei kol-ha-olam
- **Note**: The Hebrew idiom "be-oznei" is well-attested biblically (Exodus 24:7, Deuteronomy 31:11, 1 Samuel 11:4) and conveys the sense of public speech. While it introduces a bodily metaphor absent from the Latin, it is an acceptable Hebrew locution for the concept.

### 18:24 — "ba-aziqqim" (in chains)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "misit eum Annas ligatum" (Annas sent him bound). The Greek has "dedemenon" (having been bound). The Hebrew adds "ba-aziqqim" (in chains/fetters), specifying the means of binding. Neither the Vulgate nor the Greek specifies chains -- they simply say "bound."
- **Vulgate**: misit eum Annas ligatum ad Caipham
- **Greek**: apesteilen oun auton ho Hannas dedemenon pros Kaiaphan
- **Hebrew**: va-yeshallachehu Chanan asur ba-aziqqim el-Qayyafa
- **Note**: "ba-aziqqim" is a Delitzsch addition that makes the narrative more concrete but goes beyond the source texts. The Hebrew "asur" (bound) alone would suffice to match the Vulgate.

### 18:28 — "bet ha-mishpat" for "praetorium"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate and Greek both use the Latin loanword "praetorium" (Roman governor's headquarters). The Hebrew translates this as "bet ha-mishpat" (house of judgment), which is a semantic translation rather than a transliteration. This is consistent throughout John 18-19.
- **Vulgate**: in praetorium
- **Hebrew**: el-bet ha-mishpat
- **Note**: "bet ha-mishpat" is a reasonable Hebrew calque. A transliteration like "praetorium" would be foreign to biblical Hebrew style. Delitzsch's choice prioritizes naturalness over precision.

### 18:36 — "Regnum meum"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on the threefold repetition: "my kingdom is not of this world... if my kingdom were of this world... but now my kingdom is not from here." The Hebrew "malkhuti einennah min-ha-olam ha-zeh" faithfully renders each occurrence. No content divergence.
- **Note**: Precise alignment across all three traditions.

### 18:40 — "latro" vs "shoded"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Erat autem Barabbas latro" (Barabbas was a robber). The Greek uses "lestes" (bandit/revolutionary). The Hebrew uses "shoded" (robber/plunderer). All three agree in substance, though "lestes" in first-century context often implies an insurrectionary bandit.
- **Note**: "shoded" is adequate. "paritz" (violent man) might better capture the connotation of "latro/lestes" as used by Josephus for revolutionary bandits, but "shoded" is not wrong.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 18:1 — "nachal Qidron"
- **Hebrew**: va-yetze ha-chutzah im-talmidav el-ever le-nachal Qidron
- **Note**: "nachal Qidron" (Kidron brook/valley) is the exact biblical Hebrew name found in 2 Samuel 15:23 and 1 Kings 2:37, where David crosses it fleeing Absalom. The Vulgate's "torrentem Cedron" and the Greek "cheimmarrou tou Kedron" are both renderings of this Hebrew place name. Delitzsch correctly uses the original biblical toponym. The David/Jesus typological parallel (both crossing Kidron in times of betrayal) is preserved naturally through the Hebrew.

### 18:5-6 — "Ani hu" for "Ego sum"
- **Hebrew**: ani hu (I am he)
- **Note**: The "ego eimi" / "Ego sum" declaration in the garden arrest is theologically charged -- it echoes the divine name revelation. The Hebrew "ani hu" directly parallels the self-identification of God in Isaiah 41:4, 43:10, 43:13, 46:4, 48:12 (ani hu). This makes the divine resonance even more explicit in Hebrew than in Greek or Latin, since "ani hu" is the actual phrase YHWH uses of himself in the prophets. The falling to the ground (v6) becomes a natural response to hearing the divine self-identification.

### 18:10 — "va-yeqatzetz et-ozno"
- **Hebrew**: va-yeqatzetz et-ozno ha-yemanit
- **Note**: "va-yeqatzetz" (and he cut off) from the root q-tz-tz is a strong verb meaning to cut off/sever. The Vulgate "abscidit" and Greek "apekopsen" both mean "cut off." The Hebrew verb is appropriate, though the root q-tz-tz typically applies to cutting branches or horns in biblical usage (Deuteronomy 25:12 uses "ve-qatzotah" for cutting off a hand). "Malkhus" is rendered "Malkos" -- a reasonable Hebrew form.

### 18:11 — "nedanah" (sheath)
- **Hebrew**: hashev charbbekha el-nedanah
- **Note**: "nedanah" (its sheath) is from the root n-d-n, found in 1 Chronicles 21:27 ("va-yashev charbo el-nedanah"). This is excellent use of a rare but authentic biblical Hebrew word. The Vulgate's "vaginam" and Greek "theken" are precisely rendered.

### 18:13 — "shimesh bi-khunnah gedolah"
- **Hebrew**: asher shimesh bi-khunnah gedolah ba-shanah ha-hi
- **Note**: "shimesh bi-khunnah gedolah" (served in the high priesthood) is Delitzsch's rendering of "erat pontifex anni illius." The phrase uses the Mishnaic verb "shimesh" (to serve/minister) rather than a biblical verb. A more biblical construction might use "kohen gadol" directly. However, the phrasing captures the Vulgate's temporal limitation "anni illius" (of that year), which John emphasizes.

### 18:22 — "al-ha-lechi" (on the cheek)
- **Hebrew**: va-yakh... et-Yeshua al-ha-lechi
- **Note**: "al-ha-lechi" (on the cheek) for "alapam" (a slap). The word "lechi" (jaw/cheek) is biblical Hebrew (Judges 15:15-17, 1 Kings 22:24, Micah 4:14). The striking on the cheek of the servant of God echoes Isaiah 50:6 ("lecheya le-mortehem," my cheeks to those who plucked) and Micah 4:14 (5:1 in English: "be-shevet yakku al ha-lechi et-shofet Yisrael"). The resonance with the suffering servant and smitten judge of Israel is powerful.

### 18:37 — "le-ha'id la-emet"
- **Hebrew**: la-zot noladeti ve-la-zot ba'ti va-olam le-ha'id la-emet
- **Note**: "le-ha'id la-emet" (to testify to the truth) -- the verb "ha'id" (to testify/bear witness) from the root 'ayin-vav-dalet is deeply biblical, connected to the covenant witness tradition (Deuteronomy 4:26, 30:19, 31:28). The claim "la-zot noladeti" (for this I was born) uses the Niphal of y-l-d, implying a divine passive -- born for a purpose. The Hebrew makes the incarnational theology concrete.

### 18:38 — "mah hi ha-emet"
- **Hebrew**: mah hi ha-emet
- **Note**: Pilate's question rendered as "mah hi ha-emet" (what is the truth?) rather than "mah emet" (what is truth?) -- the definite article "ha" (the) makes this a question about a specific truth rather than an abstract philosophical inquiry. The Vulgate's "Quid est veritas?" and Greek "Ti estin aletheia?" are both without the article. The Hebrew article slightly shifts the meaning.

## Other Notes

### 18:28 — Passover chronology
The statement that the Jewish leaders did not enter the praetorium "ut non contaminarentur, sed ut manducarent Pascha" (lest they be contaminated, but that they might eat the Passover) is one of the key chronological indicators in John's Passion narrative. The Hebrew "le-ma'an asher lo-yittam'u ki im-yokhlu et-ha-pasach" faithfully preserves this -- Jesus is being tried while the Passover meal has not yet been eaten. The Hebrew "pasach" is the original word behind both the Greek "pascha" and Latin "Pascha."

### 18:31 — "ein-lanu reshut le-hamit ish"
"We have no authority to put any man to death." The Hebrew "reshut" (permission/authority) is Mishnaic rather than biblical. The biblical equivalent might be "mishpat mavet" (judgment of death) or a construction with "yad" (hand/power). However, "reshut" effectively conveys the jurisdictional concept -- the Sanhedrin's capital authority had been removed by Roman administration.

### 18:36 — "malkhuti einennah mi-poh"
The final clause "nunc autem regnum meum non est hinc" is rendered "aval attah malkhuti einennah mi-poh" (but now my kingdom is not from here). The word "mi-poh" (from here) is a precise rendering of "hinc" and "enteuthen." The shift from "min-ha-olam ha-zeh" (from this world) in the first two occurrences to "mi-poh" (from here) in the third matches the Vulgate's own shift from "de hoc mundo" to "hinc" and the Greek's shift from "ek tou kosmou toutou" to "enteuthen."
