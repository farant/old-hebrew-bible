# John Chapter 21 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 25 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 21:5 — "Pueri" vs "Banai"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Pueri" (children/boys) as Jesus' form of address. The Greek has "Paidia" (children/little ones). The Hebrew uses "banai" (my sons), adding the possessive suffix. The Vulgate and Greek do not include the possessive.
- **Vulgate**: Pueri, numquid pulmentarium habetis?
- **Greek**: Paidia, me ti prosphagion echete?
- **Hebrew**: banai ha-yesh lakhem davar le-ekhol
- **Note**: "banai" (my sons) is warmer than the Vulgate's "Pueri." A strict Vulgate rendering would use "yeladim" or "ne'arim" without the possessive. However, "banai" is a natural Hebrew address from a teacher to his disciples.

### 21:5 — "pulmentarium" vs "davar le-ekhol"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "numquid pulmentarium habetis?" (Do you have any food to eat with bread?). "Pulmentarium" specifically means a relish or side dish eaten with bread. The Greek "prosphagion" similarly means something eaten alongside bread, typically fish. The Hebrew renders this generally as "davar le-ekhol" (something to eat), losing the specificity.
- **Vulgate**: pulmentarium
- **Greek**: prosphagion
- **Hebrew**: davar le-ekhol
- **Note**: The Hebrew generalizes. Biblical Hebrew lacks a precise equivalent for "pulmentarium/prosphagion." "liftan" (a Rabbinic term for relish eaten with bread) would be closer but is not biblical. The generalization is acceptable.

### 21:9 — "prunas" vs "gachalei-esh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "prunas positas, et piscem superpositum, et panem" (coals laid, and a fish placed on them, and bread). The Greek has "anthrakian keimenen kai opsarion epikeimenon kai arton" (a charcoal fire laid and a fish placed on it and bread). The Hebrew reads "gachalei-esh arukhim ve-dagim aleihem ve-lechem le-ekhol" (arranged coals of fire and fish upon them and bread to eat). The Hebrew has "dagim" (fish, plural) where the Vulgate has "piscem" (fish, singular) and the Greek has "opsarion" (fish, singular).
- **Vulgate**: piscem superpositum (fish, singular)
- **Greek**: opsarion epikeimenon (fish, singular)
- **Hebrew**: dagim (fish, plural)
- **Note**: The Hebrew pluralizes the fish. To follow the Vulgate, "dag" (singular) should be used. The Hebrew also adds "le-ekhol" (to eat) after "lechem" (bread), which is not in the Vulgate or Greek.

### 21:15-17 — "Joannis" vs "ben-Yonah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Simon Joannis" (Simon son of John) in all three exchanges. The SBLGNT also reads "Simon Ioannou" (Simon of John). The Hebrew uses "Shimon ben-Yonah" (Simon son of Jonah) in all three instances. This follows a variant reading associated with Matthew 16:17 ("Simon Bar-Jona") rather than the Vulgate's "Joannis."
- **Vulgate**: Simon Joannis (all three times)
- **Greek**: Simon Ioannou (all three times)
- **Hebrew**: Shimon ben-Yonah (all three times)
- **Note**: The Hebrew should read "Shimon ben-Yochanan" (Simon son of John) to match the Vulgate. This was also flagged in the Chapter 1 notes at 1:42. The harmonization with Matthew 16:17's "Bar-Jona" is understandable but does not follow the Vulgate of John.

### 21:15-17 — "diligis/amas" and "agapas/phileis" distinction
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses two different verbs: "diligis" (vv15, 16) and "amas" (v17). The Greek uses "agapas" (vv15, 16) and "phileis" (v17). The Hebrew uses "ha-te'ehav oti" (do you love me?) uniformly in all three exchanges, using the single root a-h-v without distinction.
- **Vulgate**: diligis me (v15), diligis me (v16), amas me (v17)
- **Greek**: agapas me (v15), agapas me (v16), phileis me (v17)
- **Hebrew**: ha-te'ehav oti (v15), ha-te'ehav oti (v16), ha-te'ehav oti (v17)
- **Note**: Hebrew does not easily distinguish between "agapao" and "phileo." The single root a-h-v covers both. The Vulgate's shift from "diligis" to "amas" in the third question (which causes Peter's grief) is lost in the Hebrew. This is a genuine limitation of the Hebrew language for this passage. The verb "chasaq" (to desire, cling to) or "chafetz" (to delight in) could theoretically differentiate the third question, but these would sound unnatural.

### 21:15-17 — "agnos/oves" and "tela'ai/tzo'ni"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses "agnos meos" (my lambs, vv15, 16) and "oves meas" (my sheep, v17). The SBLGNT uses "arnia" (lambs, v15), "probata" (sheep, v16), and "probata" (sheep, v17). The Hebrew uses "tela'ai" (my lambs, v15), "tzo'ni" (my flock/sheep, v16), and "tzo'ni" (my flock/sheep, v17).
- **Vulgate**: agnos meos (v15), agnos meos (v16), oves meas (v17)
- **Greek**: arnia mou (v15), probata mou (v16), probata mou (v17)
- **Hebrew**: tela'ai (v15), tzo'ni (v16), tzo'ni (v17)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek pattern (lambs, sheep, sheep) rather than the Vulgate pattern (lambs, lambs, sheep). To follow the Vulgate, v16 should use "tela'ai" (lambs) rather than "tzo'ni" (sheep).

### 21:15-17 — "Pasce" verbs
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses "Pasce" (feed/tend) all three times. The SBLGNT alternates: "Boske" (feed, v15), "Poimaine" (shepherd/tend, v16), "Boske" (feed, v17). The Hebrew alternates: "re'eh" (shepherd/pasture, v15), "nehhag" (lead/guide, v16), "re'eh" (shepherd/pasture, v17).
- **Vulgate**: Pasce (v15), Pasce (v16), Pasce (v17)
- **Greek**: Boske (v15), Poimaine (v16), Boske (v17)
- **Hebrew**: re'eh (v15), nehhag (v16), re'eh (v17)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek pattern of alternating verbs rather than the Vulgate's uniform "Pasce." To follow the Vulgate, a single verb should be used throughout -- "re'eh" (shepherd) in all three would work, matching the uniform "Pasce."

### 21:18 — "cingebas te" / "chagarta atzmekha"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on the substance of the prophecy about Peter's old age: you will stretch out your hands, another will gird you and carry you where you do not wish. The Hebrew "u-farasta kappekha" (you will spread out your hands) accurately renders the Vulgate's "extendes manus tuas." The "spreading of hands" is widely understood as a reference to crucifixion.
- **Note**: Full alignment. No divergence.

### 21:20 — "super pectus ejus" / "al-libbo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "recubuit in coena super pectus ejus" (reclined at supper on his breast). The Hebrew reads "nafal al-libbo ba-se'udah" (fell upon his heart at the meal). "libbo" (his heart) replaces "pectus" (breast/chest). The Greek has "epi to stethos autou" (on his chest).
- **Vulgate**: super pectus ejus
- **Greek**: epi to stethos autou
- **Hebrew**: al-libbo
- **Note**: The Hebrew "lev" (heart) is used where "chazeh" (breast/chest) would be more anatomically precise. However, "nafal al-libbo" creates a deeper intimacy -- leaning on the heart rather than merely the chest. This is a Delitzsch interpretive choice rather than a strict translation.

### 21:22 — "Sic eum volo manere" / "retzioni she-yisha'er"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Sic eum volo manere donec veniam" (Thus I wish him to remain until I come). The Greek has "Ean auton thelo menein heos erchomai" (If I wish him to remain until I come). There is a significant difference: the Vulgate's "Sic" (thus/so) is declarative, while the Greek "Ean" (if) is conditional. The Hebrew uses "im-retzoni she-yisha'er" (if my desire is that he remain), following the Greek conditional rather than the Vulgate's declarative.
- **Vulgate**: Sic eum volo manere donec veniam (declarative)
- **Greek**: Ean auton thelo menein heos erchomai (conditional)
- **Hebrew**: im-retzoni she-yisha'er ad-bo'i (conditional)
- **Note**: To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should use a declarative construction: "ken ani rotzeh she-yisha'er" (thus I wish him to remain) rather than the conditional "im" (if).

### 21:25 — "nec ipsum arbitror mundum" / "adameh she-gam-ha-olam kullo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses first person singular "arbitror" (I think/I suppose). The SBLGNT also uses first person "oimai" (I think). The Hebrew uses "adammeh" (I suppose), correctly preserving the first person. This is the one moment where the narrator speaks in the first person singular, identifying himself as the author.
- **Note**: Precise alignment. The Hebrew "adammeh" from the root d-m-h (to think/suppose/imagine) is post-biblical but appropriate.

### 21:25 — "[amen]"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew text ends with "[amen]" in brackets. The Vulgate does not include "Amen" at the end of John's Gospel (some manuscripts do). The SBLGNT does not include it either. The bracketed "amen" appears to be a liturgical addition by Delitzsch or a later editor.
- **Note**: The brackets suggest the translator recognized this as non-original. It should be omitted to follow the Vulgate strictly.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 21:1 — "yam Tivaryah"
- **Hebrew**: va-yosef Yeshua le-hera'ot el-talmidav al-yam Tivaryah
- **Note**: "yam Tivaryah" (Sea of Tiberias) uses the standard Hebrew place name. "le-hera'ot" (to appear/show himself) is the Niphal infinitive of r-'-h, used for divine appearances (theophanies) throughout the Hebrew Bible (Genesis 12:7, 17:1, 18:1, Exodus 3:2). The choice of this verb for the post-resurrection appearances implicitly places them in the theophanic tradition.

### 21:6 — "ha-mikhmoret" for "rete"
- **Hebrew**: hashliku ha-mikhmoret mi-yamin la-oniyyah
- **Note**: "mikhmoret" (dragnet/seine net) from the root k-m-r is found in Isaiah 19:8 and Habakkuk 1:15-16. It refers to a large fishing net, appropriate for the commercial fishing operation described. The Vulgate's "rete" and Greek "diktyon" are generic terms for net. "mikhmoret" is more specific than either and is excellent biblical Hebrew.

### 21:7 — "va-yitnafel el-ha-yam"
- **Hebrew**: va-yachgor et-me'ilo ki eirom hayah va-yitnafel el-ha-yam
- **Note**: "va-yitnafel" (he threw himself) from the Hithpael of n-p-l is dramatic and effective. The reflexive form of "fall" conveys Peter's impetuous leap into the water. This verb form appears in Ruth 3:18. "eirom" (naked) in biblical Hebrew can mean either fully naked or merely stripped to an undergarment (cf. 1 Samuel 19:24, Isaiah 20:2-4). The context suggests Peter was working in minimal clothing and dressed before jumping.

### 21:9 — "gachalei-esh"
- **Hebrew**: gachalei-esh arukhim
- **Note**: "gachalei-esh" (coals of fire) connects verbally to 18:18, where Peter denied Christ while warming himself at "esh gechalim" (a charcoal fire). The repetition creates an inclusio: Peter denied Christ at a charcoal fire and is restored at a charcoal fire. The Hebrew makes this verbal connection clearer than the Latin "prunas" (coals), since the same Hebrew word "gechalim/gachalim" appears in both scenes.

### 21:12 — "boru-lachem"
- **Hebrew**: bo'u veru-lachem
- **Note**: "beru-lachem" means "break bread" or "eat a meal." The invitation echoes the language of communion meals. The Vulgate's "prandete" (have breakfast/eat the midday meal) is more mundane -- it denotes a specific meal (breakfast/lunch). The Hebrew "beru-lachem" (literally "break for yourselves bread") has sacramental overtones that the Latin does not carry as strongly.

### 21:15-17 — Pastoral commission language
- **Hebrew**: re'eh et-tela'ai (v15), nehhag et-tzo'ni (v16), re'eh et-tzo'ni (v17)
- **Note**: "re'eh" (shepherd/pasture) is the verb from which "ro'eh" (shepherd) derives. It is the activity of David (1 Samuel 16:11, 17:15), of God (Psalm 23:1 "YHWH ro'i"), and of the prophesied messianic shepherd (Ezekiel 34:23). "nehhag" (lead/guide) in v16 adds the dimension of governance and direction (cf. Exodus 3:1 where Moses "nahag" the flock, and 2 Samuel 6:3 where the Ark is led). Peter is being invested with both nurturing (re'eh) and governing (nehhag) authority over the flock -- the dual papal commission of pastoral care and jurisdictional leadership.

### 21:18 — "u-farasta kappekha"
- **Hebrew**: u-farasta kappekha ve-acher yachagorkha u-nesa'akha el-asher lo techpatz
- **Note**: "parasta kappekha" (you will spread out your hands) uses the same language as prayer postures (Exodus 9:29, 33; 1 Kings 8:22, 38) and crucifixion. The double meaning is preserved in Hebrew: the gesture of prayer becomes the posture of martyrdom. "kappekha" (your palms/hands) specifically refers to the open palms, appropriate for crucifixion where the hands are spread open and nailed.

### 21:24 — "zeh hu ha-talmid"
- **Hebrew**: zeh hu ha-talmid ha-me'id al-elleh va-asher katav kol-zot ve-yada'nu ki eduto emet
- **Note**: "ve-yada'nu" (and we know) -- the shift to first person plural "we" indicates a community of witnesses vouching for the Beloved Disciple's testimony. "eduto emet" (his testimony is truth) uses "emet" absolutely -- not merely "truthful" (ne'emanah) but "truth" itself. This echoes the prologue where "chesed ve-emet" came through Jesus Christ (1:17). The truth of the witness participates in the Truth of Christ.

## Other Notes

### 21:1-14 — Miraculous catch and Eucharistic overtones
The sequence of failed fishing, obedience to the Lord's word, miraculous catch, and shared meal of bread and fish has strong Eucharistic resonance. The Hebrew "va-yiqqach et-ha-lechem va-yitten lahem ve-et-ha-dagim" (he took the bread and gave to them, and the fish) in v13 echoes the multiplication narrative of John 6:11. The vocabulary of taking, giving, and distributing is common to both passages.

### 21:15-17 — Triple restoration
The three questions to Peter correspond to the three denials in chapter 18. The Hebrew creates a structural parallel: Peter denied three times at the "gechalim" (charcoal fire, 18:18), and is restored three times at the "gachalei-esh" (coals of fire, 21:9). The Hebrew verbal echo between "va-yekachesh" (he denied, 18:25, 27) and "ha-te'ehav oti" (do you love me, 21:15-17) creates a healing antithesis: where denial spoke, love now confesses.

### 21:25 — Johannine hyperbole
The final verse's claim that the world itself could not contain the books is the most striking hyperbole in the New Testament. The Hebrew "adammeh she-gam-ha-olam kullo lo yakhi et-ha-sefarim asher yikkatvu" (I suppose that even the whole world could not contain the books that would be written) uses "yakhi" (could contain/hold) from the root k-v-l (or y-k-l in this form), appropriately conveying the physical impossibility. The verse functions as an open ending, suggesting the Gospel narrative is a selection from an inexhaustible reality.
