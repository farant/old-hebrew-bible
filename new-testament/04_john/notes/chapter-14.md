# John Chapter 14 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 31 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 14:2 — "si quo minus" reading
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "si quo minus dixissem vobis: quia vado parare vobis locum" (if it were not so, I would have told you; because I go to prepare a place for you). The SBLGNT has "ei de me, eipon an hymin hoti poreuomai hetoimasai topon hymin" (if not, I would have told you that I go to prepare a place for you). The Hebrew has "ve-im lo khen hayiti omer lakhem hineni holekh le-hakhin lakhem maqom" (and if it were not so, I would say to you: behold I go to prepare a place for you).
- **Vulgate**: si quo minus dixissem vobis
- **Greek**: ei de me, eipon an hymin
- **Hebrew**: ve-im lo khen hayiti omer lakhem
- **Note**: All three versions agree on the conditional sense, but the Hebrew changes the tense from past counterfactual ("would have told") to a present hypothetical ("I would be saying"). The difference is subtle but the Vulgate's pluperfect counterfactual is more precise.

### 14:7 — "Si cognovissetis me" — conditional tense
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Si cognovissetis me, et Patrem meum utique cognovissetis" (If you had known me, you would certainly have known my Father also). This is a past contrary-to-fact conditional. The SBLGNT has "ei egnokeite me, kai ton patera mou an edeite" which is similar. The Hebrew has "lu yeda'tem oti gam et-Avi yeda'tem" (if you had known me, you would have known my Father also), correctly using "lu" (if, counterfactual).
- **Vulgate**: Si cognovissetis me, et Patrem meum utique cognovissetis
- **Hebrew**: lu yeda'tem oti gam et-Avi yeda'tem
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly uses "lu" for the counterfactual conditional. However, the second half -- "u-me'attah yeda'tem oto ve-gam re'item oto" (and from now you know him and have also seen him) -- correctly shifts from counterfactual to indicative, matching the Vulgate's "et amodo cognoscetis eum, et vidistis eum."

### 14:11 — Vulgate verse split
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's v11 reads "Non creditis quia ego in Patre, et Pater in me est?" which corresponds to the first part of SBLGNT v11. But the Vulgate's continuation ("alioquin propter opera ipsa credite") appears at the beginning of v12's Latin text. The SBLGNT keeps both "believe me" and "believe on account of the works" together in v11, then begins v12 with "amen amen." The Hebrew follows the SBLGNT division.
- **Vulgate v11**: Non creditis quia ego in Patre, et Pater in me est?
- **Vulgate v12 beginning**: alioquin propter opera ipsa credite. Amen, amen dico vobis...
- **Greek v11**: pisteuete moi...ei de me, dia ta erga auta pisteuete.
- **Hebrew v11**: ha'aminu li...ve-im lo ha'aminu li biglal ha-ma'asim
- **Note**: The content is preserved; only the verse boundary placement differs between Vulgate and Greek/Hebrew.

### 14:13 — "Patrem" in Vulgate vs direct petition
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quodcumque petieritis Patrem in nomine meo" (whatever you ask the Father in my name). The SBLGNT reads "ho ti an aitesete en to onomati mou touto poieso" (whatever you ask in my name, this I will do) -- notably, the Greek omits "the Father." The Hebrew "ve-khol asher tish'alu bi-shmi e'esenu" (and whatever you ask in my name I will do it) follows the Greek, omitting "the Father."
- **Vulgate**: quodcumque petieritis Patrem in nomine meo
- **Greek**: ho ti an aitesete en to onomati mou
- **Hebrew**: ve-khol asher tish'alu bi-shmi
- **Note**: The Vulgate includes "Patrem" (the Father) as the recipient of the petition; the Greek/Hebrew present the request as directed to Christ himself. This is theologically significant for the question of prayer to Christ vs prayer to the Father through Christ. To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should add "me-et Avi" or "me-Avi."

### 14:16 — "Paraclitum" / "Praqlit"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate retains the Greek loanword "Paraclitum" (Paraclete/Advocate). The Hebrew uses "Praqlit," a transliteration into Hebrew of the same Greek-origin term, which entered Rabbinic Hebrew.
- **Vulgate**: alium Paraclitum
- **Greek**: allon parakleton
- **Hebrew**: Praqlit acher
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the Rabbinic Hebrew borrowing "Praqlit" (cf. Mishnah Avot 4:11), which is the same word already present in Jewish literature as a legal advocate. "acher" (another) follows "alium/allon" -- implying Jesus himself is the first Paraclete.

### 14:26 — "quaecumque dixero vobis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quaecumque dixero vobis" (whatever I shall have said to you), using the future perfect. The SBLGNT has "panta ha eipon hymin" (all things which I said to you), past tense. The Hebrew "kol asher higgadti lakhem" (all that I told you) follows the Greek's past tense.
- **Vulgate**: quaecumque dixero vobis (future perfect)
- **Greek**: panta ha eipon hymin (aorist/past)
- **Hebrew**: kol asher higgadti lakhem (past)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's future perfect extends the scope of the Spirit's reminding to include future teachings. The Greek/Hebrew limit it to what has already been said. The Vulgate reading may reflect the ongoing nature of revelation in the Church's tradition.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 14:1 — "Al-yibahel levavkhem"
- **Hebrew**: al-yibahel levavkhem ha'aminu ve-Elohim ve-gam bi ha'aminu
- **Note**: "al-yibahel levavkhem" (let not your heart be troubled) uses "b-h-l" (to be terrified/dismayed), which is stronger than the Vulgate's "turbetur" (be troubled). This verb appears in Psalm 6:3, 4 and Psalm 30:8, contexts of distress before God. "levavkhem" uses "levav" (the deep heart) rather than "lev" (heart), adding depth to the emotional register.

### 14:2 — "medorot rabbim"
- **Hebrew**: be-veit Avi medorot rabbim
- **Note**: "medorot" (dwelling places/abodes) for "mansiones" (Vulgate) / "monai" (Greek). The word "mador" (dwelling) is rare in biblical Hebrew -- it appears in contexts of God's dwelling. The more common "mishkanot" (tabernacles) might have evoked the wilderness tabernacle imagery more strongly, but "medorot" emphasizes permanent residence, matching the Vulgate's "mansiones" (permanent dwellings, not temporary lodgings).

### 14:6 — "Anokhi ha-Derekh ve-ha-Emet ve-ha-Chayyim"
- **Hebrew**: anokhi ha-Derekh ve-ha-Emet ve-ha-Chayyim lo yavo ish el-ha-Av bilti al-yadi
- **Note**: One of the most important ego-eimi declarations. "Anokhi" (I) carries the weight of divine self-revelation (cf. "Anokhi YHVH Elohekha" in the Decalogue). "ha-Derekh" (the Way) recalls the "derekh YHVH" (way of the LORD) throughout the OT. "ha-Emet" (the Truth) is the divine attribute par excellence. "ha-Chayyim" (the Life) connects to "Elohim chayyim" (the living God). "bilti al-yadi" (except through me, lit. except by my hand) uses a vivid concrete metaphor -- not merely "through me" but "by my hand."

### 14:9 — "zeh kammah yamim"
- **Hebrew**: zeh kammah yamim anokhi ittkhem
- **Note**: "zeh kammah yamim" (this how-many days) for "tanto tempore" (so long a time) / "tosoutoi chronoi" (so much time). The Hebrew is somewhat weak here -- the Vulgate's "tanto tempore" conveys exasperated emphasis (so long a time!) that "zeh kammah yamim" (these how-many days) does not quite capture. A more emphatic rendering might be "kol ha-yamim ha-rabbim ha-eleh" (all these many days).

### 14:23 — "ve-nasim me'onatenu etzlo"
- **Hebrew**: ve-navoah elav ve-nasim me'onatenu etzlo
- **Note**: "ve-nasim me'onatenu etzlo" (and we will set our dwelling-place with him) renders "mansionem apud eum faciemus" (we will make our abode with him). The plural "me'onatenu" (our dwelling-places) is noteworthy -- the Father and Son together ("ve-navoah" -- and we will come, first person plural) take up residence. "me'onah" (dwelling/habitation) is used for God's dwelling in Deuteronomy 33:27 ("me'onah Elohei qedem" -- the eternal God is your dwelling place). This creates a reciprocal dwelling: God dwells in us, we dwell in God.

### 14:27 — "Shalom annichan lakhem"
- **Hebrew**: shalom annichan lakhem et-shelomi etten lakhem
- **Note**: "shalom annichan lakhem" (peace I leave to you) -- the word "annichan" appears to be a variant or error for "aniach" (I leave/rest). The distinction between "shalom" (the general greeting and blessing of peace) and "shelomi" (my peace) is well rendered -- Christ's peace is qualitatively different from the world's peace.

## Other Notes

### 14:16-17 — The Paraclete Discourse
The first Paraclete passage introduces the Spirit of Truth. The Hebrew "Ruach ha-Emet" (Spirit of Truth) is found in the Dead Sea Scrolls (1QS 3:18-19), where it refers to the spirit that guides the "sons of light." Delitzsch's rendering connects the Johannine concept to its Jewish theological background. The Spirit "shokhen ittkhem" (dwells with you) and "yihyeh be-qirbkhem" (will be in your midst/interior) -- the transition from "with" to "in" marks the Pentecostal shift from external presence to internal indwelling.

### 14:28 — "Avi gadol mimmenni"
"My Father is greater than I" -- "Avi gadol mimmenni." This verse has been central to Trinitarian and Arian controversies. The Hebrew "gadol" (great/greater) does not specify the mode of greatness -- it could be relational (as the Catholic tradition teaches: the Father is greater in authority as unbegotten source) rather than essential (which would be the Arian reading). The Hebrew preserves the ambiguity of the Greek "meizon."

### 14:30 — "sar ha-olam ha-zeh"
Second occurrence of "prince of this world" (cf. 12:31). "u-vi ein lo khol" (and in me he has nothing) -- the Hebrew "ein lo khol" (he has nothing at all) is emphatic. Christ's complete sinlessness means the devil has no foothold, no claim, no leverage. The forensic language ("ein lo" -- he has no right/claim) recalls legal proceedings where a creditor's claim is denied.

### 14:31 — "Qumu ve-nelkhah mi-zeh"
"Arise, let us go from here" -- the same command structure as in Gethsemane (Mark 14:42). In the Hebrew, "qumu" (arise, masculine plural imperative) and "nelkhah" (let us go, cohortative) echo military and prophetic commands. The departure from the upper room moves the narrative toward the Passion.
