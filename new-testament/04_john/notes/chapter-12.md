# John Chapter 12 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The Vulgate merges the content of SBLGNT 12:24 and 12:25 into a single verse at 12:25. The Vulgate's 12:24 ends mid-sentence ("mortuum fuerit,") with the continuation ("ipsum solum manet...") appearing in v25 along with the verse about loving/hating one's soul. The Hebrew and Greek maintain these as two separate verses. No other numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 12:4 — "Judas Iscariotes" identification
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "unus ex discipulis ejus, Judas Iscariotes, qui erat eum traditurus" (one of his disciples, Judas Iscariot, who was about to betray him). The SBLGNT reads "Ioudas ho Iskariotes heis ton matheton autou, ho mellon auton paradidonai." The Hebrew has "Yehudah ben-Shim'on ish Qeriyyot" (Judah son of Simon, man of Kerioth), adding the patronymic "ben-Shim'on" (son of Simon) which appears in the Vulgate of 13:2 but not here.
- **Vulgate**: Judas Iscariotes
- **Greek**: Ioudas ho Iskariotes
- **Hebrew**: Yehudah ben-Shim'on ish Qeriyyot
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds the patronymic from a parallel passage. Not a discrepancy per se, but the Vulgate here does not include "son of Simon."

### 12:7 — "Sinite illam" — preservation of ointment
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Sinite illam ut in diem sepulturae meae servet illud" (Let her alone, that she may keep it for the day of my burial). The Greek has "aphes auten, hina eis ten hemeran tou entaphiasmou mou terese auto." The Hebrew renders as "hanneichah-lah le-yom qevurati tzafnah zo't" (leave her alone; for the day of my burial she has stored this).
- **Vulgate**: ut in diem sepulturae meae servet illud
- **Greek**: hina eis ten hemeran tou entaphiasmou mou terese auto
- **Hebrew**: le-yom qevurati tzafnah zo't
- **Note**: The Hebrew "tzafnah" (she has stored/hidden) shifts the tense from a purpose clause (let her keep it for...) to a past indicative (she has stored this for...). The Vulgate's "servet" (subjunctive) preserves the purpose clause.

### 12:11 — "abibant ex Judaeis" vs Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "multi propter illum abibant ex Judaeis" (many on account of him were going away from the Jews). The SBLGNT reads "polloi di auton hupegon ton Ioudaion kai episteuon" (many of the Jews went away because of him and believed). The Hebrew "ba'u shammah rabbim min-ha-Yehudim" (many of the Jews came there) is significantly different -- it says they "came there" rather than "went away from the Jews."
- **Vulgate**: multi propter illum abibant ex Judaeis
- **Greek**: polloi di auton hupegon ton Ioudaion
- **Hebrew**: bi-glalo ba'u shammah rabbim min-ha-Yehudim
- **Note**: The Hebrew misconstrues the direction. The Vulgate and Greek indicate that Jews were departing from (i.e., leaving) the Jewish leadership because of Lazarus; the Hebrew says they "came there." This is a meaningful translation error.

### 12:20 — "Gentiles" in Vulgate vs "Yevanim" in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quidam gentiles" (certain gentiles/Greeks). The SBLGNT has "Hellenes tines" (certain Greeks). The Hebrew has "anashim Yevanim" (Greek men).
- **Vulgate**: quidam gentiles
- **Greek**: Hellenes tines
- **Hebrew**: anashim Yevanim
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "gentiles" is broader (non-Jews in general); the Greek and Hebrew specifically say "Greeks." These may have been Greek-speaking God-fearers (proselytes attending the feast). The Hebrew follows the Greek specificity rather than the Vulgate's generalization.

### 12:32 — "omnia" vs "pantas"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "omnia traham ad meipsum" (I will draw all things to myself). The SBLGNT has "pantas helkyso pros emauton" (I will draw all people to myself). "omnia" (all things, neuter) vs "pantas" (all people, masculine). The Hebrew has "emshokh kullam elai" (I will draw them all to me), using "kullam" which is ambiguous but leans toward persons.
- **Vulgate**: omnia traham ad meipsum
- **Greek**: pantas helkyso pros emauton
- **Hebrew**: emshokh kullam elai
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "omnia" (all things) encompasses the entire created order being drawn to Christ on the cross; the Greek/Hebrew limit to "all persons." The Vulgate reading has broader cosmic implications.

### 12:40 — Isaiah quotation (Isaiah 6:10)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Excaecavit oculos eorum, et induravit cor eorum" (He has blinded their eyes and hardened their hearts). The SBLGNT has "Tetyphloken auton tous ophthalmous kai eporosen auton ten kardian." The Hebrew has "hesha eineihem ve-hishmin levavam" (he has blinded their eyes and fattened their heart), using "hishmin" (made fat) rather than "hardened."
- **Vulgate**: induravit cor eorum
- **Greek**: eporosen auton ten kardian
- **Hebrew**: ve-hishmin levavam
- **Note**: The Hebrew "hishmin" (made fat) follows the original Isaiah 6:10 Hebrew "hashmen lev" rather than the Vulgate's "induravit" (hardened) or the Greek "eporosen" (hardened/petrified). This is a case where Delitzsch returns to the OT Hebrew source for the quotation, which is legitimate but creates a slight divergence from the Vulgate.

### 12:49 — Repeated phrase in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew has a seeming duplication: "et asher omar ve-et asher omar ve-et asher adabber" (that which I say and that which I say and that which I speak). The Vulgate has "quid dicam et quid loquar" (what I should say and what I should speak). The Greek has "ti eipo kai ti laleso" (what I should say and what I should speak).
- **Vulgate**: quid dicam et quid loquar
- **Greek**: ti eipo kai ti laleso
- **Hebrew**: et asher omar ve-et asher omar ve-et asher adabber
- **Note**: The Hebrew appears to have a scribal duplication. "et asher omar" appears twice before "ve-et asher adabber." This should likely read "et asher omar ve-et asher adabber" (what I should say and what I should speak), matching the Vulgate's two-part structure.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 12:3 — "mirqachat nerd zakh ve-yaqar me'od"
- **Hebrew**: va-tiqqach Miryam mirqachat nerd zakh ve-yaqar me'od litra achat mishqalah
- **Note**: "mirqachat nerd zakh" (pure nard ointment) renders the Greek "myrou nardou pistikes polytimou" and Vulgate "unguenti nardi pistici pretiosi" well. "zakh" (pure) for "pistikes/pistici" is a reasonable interpretation (the Greek word's exact meaning is debated -- genuine, liquid, or pistachio-derived). "litra achat mishqalah" (one litra its weight) is a transliteration of the Roman weight measure.

### 12:13 — "Hosha-na barukh ha-ba be-shem YHVH"
- **Hebrew**: va-yari'u lemor Hosha-na barukh ha-ba be-shem YHVH melekh Yisra'el
- **Note**: The Hebrew returns to the original Psalm 118:25-26: "Hosha-na" (save now) and "barukh ha-ba be-shem YHVH" (blessed is he who comes in the name of the LORD). This is the actual Hebrew source text being quoted, not a back-translation. The addition "melekh Yisra'el" (King of Israel) corresponds to the Vulgate's "rex Israel." The use of the Tetragrammaton is noteworthy.

### 12:15 — Zechariah 9:9 quotation
- **Hebrew**: al-tir'i bat-Tziyyon hinneh malkekh yavo lakh rokhev al-ayir ben-atonot
- **Note**: This quotes Zechariah 9:9 directly in its original Hebrew. "bat-Tziyyon" (daughter of Zion), "rokhev al-ayir" (riding on a donkey) -- the Hebrew naturally returns to the source text. However, the Vulgate has "sedens super pullum asinae" (sitting on the foal of a she-ass) while the Hebrew "ben-atonot" (son of she-asses) follows the OT Hebrew text more closely than the Vulgate.

### 12:24 — Grain of wheat parable
- **Hebrew**: amen amen ani omer lakhem im lo-tippol ha-chittah el-tokh ha-adamah u-metah tishshar le-vaddah ve-ka-asher metah ta'aseh peri harbeh
- **Note**: "ha-chittah" (the wheat grain) for "granum frumenti" / "kokkos tou sitou" is precise. The agricultural metaphor is rendered in natural Hebrew terms. "u-metah" (and has died) uses the standard biblical death verb. "peri harbeh" (much fruit) is the standard Hebrew for fruitfulness (cf. Genesis 1:28).

### 12:27 — "Hatzileni Avi"
- **Hebrew**: attah nivhalah nafshi u-mah omar hatzileni Avi min-ha-sha'ah ha-zo't akh al-ken higga'ti la-sha'ah ha-zo't
- **Note**: "nivhalah nafshi" (my soul is troubled/terrified) echoes Psalm 6:4 ("ve-nafshi nivhalah me'od"). "hatzileni" (deliver me) is the standard Hebrew cry for deliverance found throughout the Psalms. The entire verse reads like a psalm of lament, which is exactly the right register for Jesus' anguished prayer.

### 12:31 — "sar ha-olam ha-zeh"
- **Hebrew**: attah ha-olam ha-zeh niddon attah yushlakh sar ha-olam ha-zeh chutzah
- **Note**: "sar ha-olam ha-zeh" (prince of this world) for "princeps hujus mundi" / "ho archon tou kosmou toutou" uses the Hebrew term "sar" (prince/chief), which is the same title used for angelic and demonic rulers in Daniel 10:13, 20-21. The connection to Daniel's spiritual warfare vocabulary is apt.

## Other Notes

### 12:38 — Double Isaiah quotation
Verses 38 and 40 both quote Isaiah (53:1 and 6:10 respectively). The Hebrew naturally returns to the original OT Hebrew for both quotations, which is a strength of the Delitzsch translation. The first quotation "YHVH mi he'emin li-shemu'atenu u-zeroa YHVH al-mi niglatah" is nearly verbatim Isaiah 53:1.

### 12:41 — "When he saw his glory"
The narrator's comment "ki-r'oto et-tif'arto va-yinnave alav" (when he saw his glory and prophesied about him) identifies the glory Isaiah saw in Isaiah 6 as Christ's glory. The Hebrew "tif'eret" (beauty/glory) is a distinct word from "kavod" (the glory of the Shekinah), which is an interesting choice. The Vulgate has "gloriam ejus" and the Greek "doxan autou" -- "kavod" would be more natural here for the theophanic glory of Isaiah 6.

### 12:44-50 — Jesus' public proclamation
This final public discourse is essentially a summary of Johannine theology. The Hebrew handles the key concepts consistently: "ha-ma'amin bi" (he who believes in me), "ha-shole'ach oti" (he who sent me), "le-hoshia et-ha-olam" (to save the world). The forensic/juridic language in v48 -- "yadin oto ba-yom ha-acharon" (will judge him on the last day) -- uses the standard Hebrew legal verb "din" (to judge).
