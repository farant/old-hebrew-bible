# John Chapter 13 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 38 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 13:2 — "coena facta" vs "bi-se'udat ha-erev"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "coena facta" (supper being made/served). The SBLGNT has "deipnou ginomenou" (during supper, present tense -- supper was taking place). The Hebrew has "bi-se'udat ha-erev" (at the evening meal), which aligns with both.
- **Vulgate**: Et coena facta, cum diabolus jam misisset in cor ut traderet eum Judas
- **Greek**: kai deipnou ginomenou, tou diabolou ede beblekotos eis ten kardian
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi bi-se'udat ha-erev ve-ha-Satan natan be-lev Yehudah
- **Note**: The key difference: the Vulgate says the devil "had [already] put into the heart" (misisset, pluperfect) that Judas should betray him. The Hebrew "natan be-lev" (placed in the heart of) is a simple past, not a pluperfect. Both adequately convey the devil's initiative preceding the supper events.

### 13:10 — "nisi ut pedes lavet" vs SBLGNT textual variant
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Qui lotus est, non indiget nisi ut pedes lavet" (He who is bathed needs only to wash his feet). Some SBLGNT manuscripts omit "ei me tous podas" (except the feet), reading simply "ou chreian echei nipsasthai" (has no need to wash). The Hebrew "ein lo lirchotz od ki im et-raglav" (he has no need to wash again except his feet) follows the longer reading that includes the feet, aligning with the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: non indiget nisi ut pedes lavet
- **Greek**: ouk echei chreian [ei me] tous podas nipsasthai
- **Hebrew**: ein-lo lirchotz od ki im-et-raglav
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate here, retaining the "except the feet" clause that some Greek manuscripts omit.

### 13:18 — Psalm 41:10 quotation — "mecum panem" vs "lachmi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Qui manducat mecum panem" (He who eats bread with me). The SBLGNT has "ho trogon mou ton arton" (he who eats my bread). The Hebrew has "okhel lachmi" (he who eats my bread), following the SBLGNT and the Hebrew OT source (Psalm 41:10: "okhel lachmi") rather than the Vulgate's "with me."
- **Vulgate**: Qui manducat mecum panem
- **Greek**: ho trogon mou ton arton
- **Hebrew**: okhel lachmi
- **Note**: "my bread" (Hebrew/Greek) vs "bread with me" (Vulgate) -- a small but meaningful distinction. The Hebrew correctly follows the original Psalm 41:10 text.

### 13:24 — Vulgate "dixit ei" vs Greek "pythesthai"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Innuit ergo huic Simon Petrus, et dixit ei: Quis est, de quo dicit?" (Simon Peter therefore beckoned to him and said to him: Who is it of whom he speaks?). The SBLGNT has Peter only gesturing for the beloved disciple to ask ("neuei...pythesthai" -- he nods to him to inquire). The Hebrew follows the Greek: "va-yirmoz-lo Shim'on Petros lidrosh" (Simon Peter signaled to him to inquire).
- **Vulgate**: et dixit ei: Quis est, de quo dicit?
- **Greek**: neuei...pythesthai tis an eie peri hou legei
- **Hebrew**: va-yirmoz-lo...lidrosh mi-hu zeh asher dibber alav
- **Note**: The Vulgate has Peter speaking the question aloud; the Greek/Hebrew have him silently gesturing. The Hebrew follows the Greek.

### 13:32 — "Si Deus clarificatus est in eo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes the conditional clause "Si Deus clarificatus est in eo" (If God is glorified in him). Some SBLGNT manuscripts omit this clause. The Hebrew includes it: "hen ha-Elohim nitpa'ar bo" (behold, God is glorified in him), though it uses "hen" (behold) rather than a conditional.
- **Vulgate**: Si Deus clarificatus est in eo
- **Greek**: [ei ho theos edoxasthe en auto]
- **Hebrew**: hen ha-Elohim nitpa'ar bo
- **Note**: The Hebrew retains the clause (matching the Vulgate) but transforms the conditional "si/ei" (if) into the assertive "hen" (behold/indeed). The theological sense changes slightly: the Vulgate presents a conditional-then-consequent; the Hebrew presents a declarative affirmation.

### 13:34 — Typographical error in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Hebrew contains "teh'vu ish et-achiv" but the word "ish" appears misspelled earlier in the verse: "asher te'ehevu itsh et-achiv" -- "itsh" is a corrupted form. This appears to be a typographical error for "ish" (man/each one).
- **Note**: The word should read "ish" (each one) consistently. The Hebrew text as stored appears to contain a typo.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 13:1 — "la-avor min-ha-olam ha-zeh el-Aviv"
- **Hebrew**: ke-she-yada Yeshua ki va'ah sha'ato la-avor min-ha-olam ha-zeh el-Aviv
- **Note**: "la-avor min-ha-olam ha-zeh el-Aviv" (to pass from this world to his Father) uses "la-avor" (to pass/cross over), which recalls the Passover (Pesach, from p-s-ch, to pass over). The chapter opens with the Passover setting, and the verb "avor" (pass) creates an implicit wordplay. "ka-asher ahav et bechirav" (as he loved his chosen ones) renders the Vulgate's "suos" (his own) as "bechirav" (his chosen ones), which adds a predestinarian nuance.

### 13:5 — "kiyyor" for "pelvim"
- **Hebrew**: va-yatzaq mayim ba-kiyyor
- **Note**: "kiyyor" (basin/laver) is the exact term used for the bronze laver in the Tabernacle (Exodus 30:18, 38:8). Delitzsch's choice creates a direct typological connection: the priestly laver of the Temple cult is now the basin of Jesus' servant ministry. This is one of the most theologically resonant word choices in the chapter.

### 13:14 — "ha-Moreh ve-ha-Adon"
- **Hebrew**: lakhen im-ani ha-Moreh ve-ha-Adon rachatzti et-raglekhem gam-attem chayyavim lirchotz ish et-raglei achiv
- **Note**: "ha-Moreh ve-ha-Adon" (the Teacher and the Lord) for "Dominus et Magister" places the titles in reversed order from the Vulgate. The Vulgate has "Dominus et Magister" (Lord and Master); the Hebrew has "Teacher and Lord." The Greek also has "ho kyrios kai ho didaskalos" (the Lord and the Teacher). The Hebrew reverses the order. Minor, but the Vulgate places "Dominus" first for emphasis.

### 13:27 — "acharei bil'o ba ha-Satan el-qirbo"
- **Hebrew**: ve-acharei vil'o ba ha-Satan el-qirbo
- **Note**: "acharei vil'o" (after his swallowing/eating it) uses the vivid verb "bala" (to swallow), which is stronger than the Vulgate's "accepisset" (had received) or the Greek "meta to psomion" (after the morsel). "ba ha-Satan el-qirbo" (Satan entered into his midst/innards) is visceral. "qirbo" (his innards/interior) is more physical than the Vulgate's simple "introivit in eum."

### 13:30 — "va-laylah hayah"
- **Hebrew**: ve-hu be-qachto et-perusat ha-lechem miher latze't ha-chutzah ve-laylah hayah
- **Note**: "ve-laylah hayah" (and it was night) ends the verse with one of the most theologically weighted statements in John's Gospel. The Hebrew captures the narrative abruptness perfectly. The simple clause mirrors the Vulgate's "Erat autem nox" and Greek "en de nyx." The darkness is both literal and symbolic -- Judas exits into the night that represents his spiritual state.

### 13:34 — "mitzvah chadashah"
- **Hebrew**: mitzvah chadashah ani noten lakhem
- **Note**: "mitzvah chadashah" (a new commandment) is the technical Hebrew term for a commandment/precept (from tz-v-h, to command). This echoes the language of Sinai and the Torah. The "newness" of this commandment within the framework of the 613 mitzvot is striking -- it is not abolishing the law but adding a new precept with the force of divine legislation.

## Other Notes

### 13:23 — "ha-meisev al-cheiq Yeshua"
"One of his disciples, whom Jesus loved, leaning on Jesus' bosom." The Hebrew "meisev al-cheiq Yeshua" (reclining at the bosom of Jesus) uses "cheiq" (bosom/lap), the same word used in the patriarchal "bosom of Abraham" tradition (cf. Luke 16:22). The beloved disciple's position mirrors the intimacy described in John 1:18 where the Son is "in the bosom of the Father."

### 13:33 — "Banai"
Jesus addresses the disciples as "Banai" (my sons/my children). The Vulgate has "Filioli" (little children) and the Greek "teknia" (little children). The Hebrew "banai" lacks the diminutive force of the Latin and Greek -- it means "my sons" rather than "my little children." A more precise rendering might use a diminutive form, but biblical Hebrew lacks robust diminutive suffixes.

### 13:38 — Rooster's Crow
"be-terem yiqra ha-tarnegol tekachesh bi shalosh pe'amim" (before the rooster crows you will deny me three times). "tarnegol" (rooster) is the standard Mishnaic Hebrew word; biblical Hebrew does not have a common word for rooster (the term in Job 38:36 "sekhvi" is disputed). Delitzsch uses the later Hebrew vocabulary here appropriately.
