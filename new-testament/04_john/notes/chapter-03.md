# John Chapter 3 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 36 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 3:5 — "ex aqua et Spiritu Sancto"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ex aqua, et Spiritu Sancto" (from water and the Holy Spirit), explicitly naming the Spirit as "Sancto." The Greek has simply "ex hydatos kai pneumatos" (from water and spirit) without "hagiou." The Hebrew has "min-ha-mayim ve-ha-ruach" (from the water and the spirit), without "ha-qodesh."
- **Vulgate**: nisi quis renatus fuerit ex aqua, et Spiritu Sancto
- **Greek**: ean me tis gennethe ex hydatos kai pneumatos
- **Hebrew**: im-lo yivvaled ish min-ha-mayim ve-ha-ruach
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek in omitting "Holy" (qodesh). To align with the Vulgate, it should read "min-ha-mayim ve-ruach ha-qodesh."

### 3:13 — "qui est in caelo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "qui est in caelo" (who is in heaven) at the end of the verse. The SBLGNT omits this phrase. The Hebrew includes it: "asher hu ba-shamayim" (who is in heaven), correctly following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Filius hominis, qui est in cælo.
- **Greek**: ho huios tou anthropou. [no "ho on en to ourano"]
- **Hebrew**: ben-ha-adam asher hu ba-shamayim.
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly preserves the Vulgate's longer reading. This is theologically significant as it asserts Christ's simultaneous presence in heaven and earth.

### 3:15 — "non pereat, sed"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "non pereat, sed" (should not perish, but) in 3:15. The SBLGNT omits these words in v15 (they appear in v16 instead). The Hebrew includes the longer text "le-ma'an asher lo yovad" (so that he shall not perish), matching the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: ut omnis qui credit in ipsum, non pereat, sed habeat vitam aeternam.
- **Greek**: hina pas ho pisteuon en auto eche zoen aionion.
- **Hebrew**: le-ma'an asher lo yovad kol-ha-ma'amin bo ki im-yichyeh chayyei olam.
- **Note**: The Hebrew aligns with the Vulgate's fuller reading.

### 3:25 — "cum Judaeis" vs "meta Ioudaiou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "cum Judaeis" (with Jews, plural). The SBLGNT reads "meta Ioudaiou" (with a Jew, singular). The Hebrew has "u-vein Yehudi" (with a Jew, singular), following the Greek against the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Facta est autem quæstio ex discipulis Joannis cum Judæis de purificatione.
- **Greek**: meta Ioudaiou peri katharismou.
- **Hebrew**: u-vein Yehudi al-devar ha-taharah.
- **Note**: The Hebrew should follow the Vulgate's plural "Judæis" and read "u-vein Yehudim" (with Jews).

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:3 — "yivvaled mi-le-ma'lah" for "renatus fuerit denuo/anothen"
- **Hebrew**: im-lo yivvaled ish mi-le-ma'lah
- **Note**: "mi-le-ma'lah" (from above) captures one meaning of Greek "anothen" (from above/again). The Vulgate has "denuo" (again/anew). The Hebrew opts for the spatial/divine meaning ("from above") rather than the temporal ("again"), which is actually the Johannine theological intention, though it loses the ambiguity that drives Nicodemus' misunderstanding in v4. A dual rendering is difficult in any language.

### 3:8 — "ha-ruach" for both "wind" and "spirit"
- **Hebrew**: ha-ruach ba-asher yachpotz sham hu noshev
- **Note**: Excellent preservation of the double meaning. Hebrew "ruach" means both "wind" and "spirit" (just as Greek "pneuma" does), so the wordplay is naturally preserved. "noshev" (blows) is good biblical Hebrew for wind (cf. Isaiah 40:7). The Vulgate uses "Spiritus" which also has this dual capacity in context.

### 3:14 — "higbi'ah Moshe et ha-nachash ba-midbar"
- **Hebrew**: ve-ka-asher higbi'ah Moshe et ha-nachash ba-midbar ken tzarikh ben-ha-adam le-hinnase
- **Note**: Direct allusion to Numbers 21:8-9. "higbi'ah" (lifted up) and "nachash" (serpent) are exactly the vocabulary of the Numbers passage. "le-hinnase" (to be lifted up) from the root n-s-a is a good biblical term. Note that "tzarikh" (needs/must) is a post-biblical/Mishnaic word; biblical Hebrew would use "yesh le-" or "nachon le-" or simply convey necessity through the imperfect tense.

### 3:16 — "ki ahavah rabbah ahav ha-Elohim"
- **Hebrew**: ki ahavah rabbah ahav ha-Elohim et ha-olam
- **Note**: "ahavah rabbah ahav" (a great love He loved) uses the cognate accusative construction, which is beautifully biblical (cf. "simchah gedolah" patterns). "et beno et yechido" (his son, his only one) echoes Genesis 22:2 "et binekha et yechidekha" (your son, your only one), creating a profound Akedah (binding of Isaac) typology that is theologically masterful.

### 3:29 — "re'a he-chatan"
- **Hebrew**: asher-lo ha-kallah hu he-chatan ve-re'a he-chatan
- **Note**: "re'a he-chatan" (friend of the bridegroom) is a good rendering. The term "shoshbin" would be the technical Mishnaic term for best man/groomsman. Delitzsch's "re'a" (friend) is more biblical (cf. Judges 14:20, "mera'ehu" for Samson's companion). "samoach yismach" (rejoicing he shall rejoice) is an excellent cognate accusative, very biblical in style (cf. Isaiah 61:10).

## Other Notes

### 3:14 — Numbers 21 typology
The bronze serpent typology is one of the most significant OT connections in John's Gospel. The Hebrew perfectly preserves the verbal echoes to Numbers 21:8-9 through its vocabulary choices.

### 3:16-21 — Red text boundaries
This section raises the question of where Jesus' direct speech ends and the evangelist's commentary begins. Traditionally, Jesus' words are considered to continue through v21, though some scholars end the discourse at v15. For red text marking purposes, the Vulgate tradition treats 3:16-21 as Jesus' words.

### 3:29 — Bridegroom imagery
The Hebrew "chatan/kallah" (bridegroom/bride) vocabulary echoes Song of Songs and the prophetic marriage metaphor (Hosea, Jeremiah, Isaiah). This is important for the Johannine ecclesiology.
