# John Chapter 20 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 31 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 20:1 — "Una sabbati" / "be-echad ba-Shabbat"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Una autem sabbati" (on the first day of the week). The Hebrew has "be-echad ba-Shabbat" (on the first of the Sabbath/week). Both correctly convey the first day of the week, using the Semitic idiom where "sabbat" can mean "week" (cf. Leviticus 23:15-16). No divergence.
- **Note**: The Hebrew preserves the original Semitic idiom that underlies both the Greek "mia ton sabbaton" and the Vulgate's calque "una sabbati."

### 20:16 — "Rabboni"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Rabboni (quod dicitur Magister)" -- keeping the Aramaic and providing a Latin gloss. The SBLGNT adds "Hebraisti" (in Hebrew) before "Rabbouni." The Hebrew has "Rabbuni hu moreh" (Rabbuni, meaning teacher). The Hebrew drops the language marker ("Hebraisti" in the Greek) since the text is already in Hebrew/Aramaic. This is appropriate.
- **Vulgate**: Rabboni (quod dicitur Magister)
- **Greek**: Hebraisti Rabbouni (ho legetai Didaskale)
- **Hebrew**: Rabbuni hu moreh
- **Note**: The Hebrew retains the Aramaic intensified form "Rabbuni" (my great master) rather than normalizing to Hebrew "Rabbi." This preserves the emotional intimacy of Mary's address.

### 20:17 — "Noli me tangere" / "al-tigg'i vi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Noli me tangere" (Do not touch me). The Greek has "Me mou haptou" (Do not cling to me / Stop holding on to me) -- the present imperative with "me" suggests cessation of an ongoing action. The Hebrew "al-tigg'i vi" (do not touch me) uses the Qal imperfect with "al," matching the Vulgate's simple prohibition.
- **Vulgate**: Noli me tangere
- **Greek**: Me mou haptou
- **Hebrew**: al-tigg'i vi
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate's "do not touch" rather than the Greek's more nuanced "stop clinging to." Both are defensible, but for Vulgate priority this is correct.

### 20:17 — "Ascendo ad Patrem meum" / "ani oleh"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on the content of Jesus' commission to Mary: "I ascend to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God." The Hebrew "ani oleh el-avi va-avikhem ve-el-Elohai ve-Eloheikhem" is precise. The verb "oleh" (ascending, present participle) conveys the imminence of the ascension.
- **Note**: No divergence. The fourfold "my Father/your Father/my God/your God" is faithfully preserved.

### 20:19 — "propter metum Judaeorum" / "mi-yir'at ha-Yehudim"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree: the disciples gathered behind closed doors "for fear of the Jews." The Hebrew "mi-yir'at ha-Yehudim" uses "yir'ah" (fear/awe), the standard biblical Hebrew term. No content divergence.
- **Note**: Alignment is precise.

### 20:22 — "insufflavit" / "va-yippach"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "insufflavit" (he breathed upon/into them). The Greek has "enephysesen" (he breathed on). The Hebrew "va-yippach bahem" (and he breathed into them) uses the same root n-p-ch found in Genesis 2:7: "va-yippach be-appav nishmat chayyim" (and he breathed into his nostrils the breath of life). All three sources agree.
- **Vulgate**: insufflavit
- **Greek**: enephysesen
- **Hebrew**: va-yippach bahem
- **Note**: The Hebrew makes the Genesis 2:7 connection explicit through the identical verb form. This is the new creation: as God breathed life into Adam, the risen Christ breathes the Holy Spirit into the apostles. The Hebrew of John 20:22 and Genesis 2:7 share the same verb, making the typological link unmistakable.

### 20:23 — "remiseritis...retinueritis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quorum remiseritis peccata, remittuntur eis: et quorum retinueritis, retenta sunt" (whose sins you shall forgive, they are forgiven them; and whose sins you shall retain, they are retained). The Hebrew uses "tislekhu le-chatotam ve-nislach lahem... ta'ashimu ye'eshamu" (you forgive their sins, they are forgiven... you hold guilty, they are held guilty). The verb pair s-l-ch (forgive) and a-sh-m (hold guilty/declare guilty) is strong.
- **Note**: "salach" (forgive) in the Hebrew Bible is used almost exclusively of divine forgiveness (Numbers 14:19-20, 1 Kings 8:30, Psalm 103:3). Its application here to the apostles underscores the extraordinary nature of the authority being conferred. "asham" (to be guilty/to declare guilty) from the root a-sh-m is a legal/cultic term. The Hebrew makes the priestly dimension of apostolic authority vivid.

### 20:28 — "Dominus meus et Deus meus" / "Adoni ve-Elohai"
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on Thomas's confession: "My Lord and my God." The Hebrew "Adoni ve-Elohai" is precise.
- **Vulgate**: Dominus meus et Deus meus
- **Greek**: Ho kyrios mou kai ho theos mou
- **Hebrew**: Adoni ve-Elohai
- **Note**: The confession uses "Adoni" (my Lord, with the first person suffix) and "Elohai" (my God). The direct address to Jesus as "Elohai" (my God) is the most explicit declaration of divinity in the Gospels. In Hebrew, "Elohai" is the form used in prayer to God (Psalm 22:2 "Eli, Eli" / Psalm 63:2 "Elohim Eli attah"). Thomas is addressing Jesus with the same word used to address YHWH in the Psalms.

### 20:29 — "Thoma" in Vulgate, absent in SBLGNT
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Quia vidisti me, Thoma, credidisti" (Because you have seen me, Thomas, you have believed). The SBLGNT does not include the vocative "Thomas." The Hebrew omits the name: "ya'an ra'ita oti he'emanta" (because you saw me you believed), following the Greek omission.
- **Vulgate**: Quia vidisti me, Thoma, credidisti
- **Greek**: Hoti heorakas me pepisteukas
- **Hebrew**: ya'an ra'ita oti he'emanta
- **Note**: To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should include "Toma" as a vocative: "ya'an ra'ita oti Toma he'emanta."

### 20:31 — "Christus Filius Dei" / "ha-Mashiach ben-Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quia Jesus est Christus Filius Dei" (that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God). The Hebrew has "ki Yeshua hu ha-Mashiach ben-Elohim" (that Jesus is the Messiah, Son of God). All three agree.
- **Note**: The purpose statement of the Gospel is faithfully rendered. "ha-Mashiach" (the Anointed One) is the Hebrew original behind "Christus/Christos."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 20:1 — "li-fenot ha-boqer be-od choshekh"
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi be-echad ba-Shabbat li-fenot ha-boqer be-od choshekh
- **Note**: "li-fenot ha-boqer be-od choshekh" (toward the turning of the morning while it was still dark) beautifully renders the Vulgate's "mane, cum adhuc tenebrae essent." The temporal construction "be-od choshekh" (while still dark) echoes 1 Samuel 9:26 ("ka-alot ha-shachar," at the rising of dawn). This is elegant biblical Hebrew prose.

### 20:7 — "ha-sudar" and the burial details
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-sudar asher haytah al-rosho einennah munnachat etzel ha-takhrikhin ki im-mequppelet le-vaddah be-maqom echad
- **Note**: "sudar" (face cloth/napkin) is a loanword from Latin "sudarium" that entered Mishnaic Hebrew. The Vulgate uses the same Latin word "sudarium." The description of it being "mequppelet le-vaddah" (folded apart by itself) is precise Hebrew. "takhrikhin" (burial wrappings/shrouds) is the standard Rabbinic Hebrew term for burial linens. The detail suggests orderly departure rather than grave robbery.

### 20:11-12 — "va-tashqef el-tokh ha-qaver"
- **Hebrew**: va-tashqef el-tokh ha-qaver... va-tere shnei mal'akhim levushei levanim yoshvim
- **Note**: "va-tashqef" (she peered/looked down) from the Hiphil of sh-q-f is the same verb used of YHWH looking down from heaven (Psalm 14:2, 102:20) and of the woman at the window in Judges 5:28 and 2 Samuel 6:16. The verb carries connotations of looking down from above into a lower space, perfectly suited to peering into a rock-hewn tomb. "mal'akhim levushei levanim" (angels clothed in white) uses excellent construct-chain biblical Hebrew.

### 20:15 — "shomer ha-gan" for "hortulanus"
- **Hebrew**: ve-hi chashavatuhu le-shomer ha-gan
- **Note**: "shomer ha-gan" (keeper/guardian of the garden) for "hortulanus" (gardener). The phrase "shomer ha-gan" evokes the original gardener of Eden -- Adam was placed in the garden "le-ovdah u-le-shomrah" (to work it and to keep it, Genesis 2:15). The risen Christ in the garden, mistaken for the gardener, thus carries an Adamic resonance in the Hebrew that the Latin "hortulanus" does not convey as strongly.

### 20:17 — "al-tigg'i vi" (Noli me tangere)
- **Hebrew**: al-tigg'i vi ki od lo aliti el-avi
- **Note**: The root n-g-' (to touch) in biblical Hebrew often carries the sense of sacred contact -- touching holy things (Exodus 19:12-13, touching the mountain; Leviticus 5:2-3, touching unclean things; 2 Samuel 6:6-7, Uzzah touching the Ark). The prohibition "al-tigg'i" places the risen body of Christ in the category of sacred objects that may not be casually touched. The Hebrew word choice adds a dimension of holiness that the Latin "tangere" and Greek "haptou" carry less explicitly.

### 20:22 — "va-yippach bahem"
- **Hebrew**: va-yippach bahem va-yomer aleihem qechu lakhem et-ruach ha-qodesh
- **Note**: As noted in Content Alignment above, "va-yippach" (he breathed) is the Genesis 2:7 verb. The addition of "lakhem" (for yourselves) in "qechu lakhem" (receive for yourselves) is a Delitzsch addition not present in the Vulgate's simple "Accipite" or Greek "Labete." It adds reflexive emphasis. "ruach ha-qodesh" (the Holy Spirit) uses the standard Hebrew phrase found in Psalm 51:13 and Isaiah 63:10-11.

### 20:25 — "reshem ha-masmerot"
- **Hebrew**: im-lo er'eh ve-yadav et-reshem ha-masmerot
- **Note**: "reshem ha-masmerot" (the mark/imprint of the nails) -- "reshem" (mark/imprint) is post-biblical Hebrew. The Greek "typon" (type/impression) and Vulgate "fixuram" (the marks of the fastening) both convey the visible wound pattern. "masmerot" (nails) is biblical Hebrew (2 Chronicles 3:9, Ecclesiastes 12:11, 1 Chronicles 22:3, Isaiah 41:7). The word carries weight: it is the same term used for the nails of the Temple construction.

### 20:27 — "mechusar emunah" / "ma'amin"
- **Hebrew**: ve-al-tehi mechusar emunah ki im-ma'amin
- **Note**: "mechusar emunah" (lacking faith) for "incredulus" (unbelieving) is an elegant Hebrew construction. The root ch-s-r (to lack) combined with "emunah" (faith/faithfulness) creates a compound meaning "faith-deficient." The contrast with "ma'amin" (believing, from the root a-m-n) is sharp. "emunah" is the word used for Abraham's faith in Genesis 15:6 ("ve-he'emin ba-YHWH") and for God's own faithfulness. Thomas is being asked to share in Abrahamic faith.

## Other Notes

### 20:19 — "shalom lakhem"
The risen Christ's greeting "shalom lakhem" (peace to you) is the standard Hebrew greeting, but in this context it echoes Judges 6:23 (the angel of YHWH saying "shalom lekha" to Gideon) and the messianic promise of Isaiah 9:5 ("sar-shalom," prince of peace). The threefold "shalom" in this chapter (vv19, 21, 26) has eucharistic and liturgical resonance -- it becomes the "Pax" of the Mass.

### 20:22-23 — Apostolic authority
The sequence of breathing the Spirit and conferring the power to forgive/retain sins is the Johannine institution of sacramental authority. In Hebrew, the connection between "ruach" (spirit/breath/wind) and "va-yippach" (he breathed) is natural -- the Spirit IS the breath of God. The Hebrew makes the sacramental commission more tangible: the very breath of the risen Christ, carrying "ruach ha-qodesh," empowers the apostles to pronounce divine "selichah" (forgiveness, from s-l-ch).

### 20:28 — The climactic confession
Thomas's "Adoni ve-Elohai" (my Lord and my God) is the theological climax of the Fourth Gospel, answering the prologue's "ve-Elohim hayah ha-davar" (and God was the Word, 1:1). The inclusion is made: the Word who was God (1:1) is now confessed as God by a disciple who has seen and touched the wounds. The entire Gospel moves from the abstract theological claim of the prologue to the concrete personal confession of a doubter. The Hebrew carries this arc with particular force since "Elohim" in 1:1 and "Elohai" in 20:28 share the same root.
