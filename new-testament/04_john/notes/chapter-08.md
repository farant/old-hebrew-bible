# John Chapter 8 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 8:1-11 — Pericope Adulterae (canonical per Vulgate)
- **Issue**: The Pericope Adulterae (7:53-8:11) is absent from the earliest Greek manuscripts and is double-bracketed in the SBLGNT. However, it is present in the Vulgate and is part of the Catholic canon. The Hebrew includes all verses, which is correct for our purposes.
- **Vulgate**: Full text present, 8:1-11 continuous.
- **Greek**: Double-bracketed in SBLGNT, included in many later manuscripts.
- **Hebrew**: Full text present.
- **Note**: All three sources contain the text. The passage must be retained per the Vulgate tradition. The Council of Trent affirmed the canonicity of the entire Vulgate text.

### 8:9 — "et a conscientia convicti" expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's "Audientes autem unus post unum exibant, incipientes a senioribus" (hearing this, they went out one by one, beginning from the elders). The Greek expanded text adds "kai hypo tes syneideseos elenchomenoi" (convicted by their own conscience). The Hebrew includes the conscience element: "ve-libbam noqfam" (and their heart smote them), which follows the expanded Greek but goes beyond the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Audientes autem unus post unum exibant
- **Greek**: hoi de, akousantes, kai hypo tes syneideseos elenchomenoi, exerchonto
- **Hebrew**: hem shame'u ve-libbam noqfam va-yetz'u echad echad
- **Note**: "libbam noqfam" is not in the Vulgate. The Hebrew follows a fuller Greek tradition here.

## Content Alignment

### 8:25 — "Principium, qui et loquor vobis"
- **Issue**: This is one of the most disputed translation points in all of John. The Vulgate reads "Principium, qui et loquor vobis" (The Beginning, who also speaks to you). The Greek "Ten archen hoti kai lalo hymin" is notoriously difficult. The Hebrew has "asher gam me-rosh dibbarti lakhem" (that which also from the beginning I spoke to you), taking it as a temporal statement rather than the Vulgate's self-identification as "the Beginning."
- **Vulgate**: Principium, qui et loquor vobis.
- **Greek**: Ten archen hoti kai lalo hymin
- **Hebrew**: asher gam me-rosh dibbarti lakhem
- **Note**: The Vulgate reading is Christologically significant -- Christ identifies himself as "the Beginning" (cf. Revelation 1:8, 21:6). The Hebrew reading loses this self-identification. To follow the Vulgate, a rendering like "ha-Reshit, ha-medabber gam aleikhem" would be needed.

### 8:38 — "patrem vestrum" / "avikhem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quae vidistis apud patrem vestrum" (what you have seen with your father), implying the devil as their father (made explicit in v44). The SBLGNT has a textual variant: some manuscripts read "tou patros" (the father), others "tou patros hymon" (your father). The Hebrew has "avikhem" (your father), aligning with the Vulgate.
- **Note**: Alignment is correct. The possessive is theologically important as it sets up the paternity dispute of vv39-44.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 8:6 — "va-yetav be-etzba'o al-ha-qarka"
- **Hebrew**: va-yitkofef Yeshua le-mattah va-yetav be-etzba'o al-ha-qarka
- **Note**: "va-yetav" (and he traced/drew marks) from the root t-v (to make a mark) is an interesting choice. The Vulgate's "digito scribebat in terra" (wrote with his finger on the ground) uses "scribebat" (was writing). The Greek "kategraphen" (was writing down). "va-yetav" is less specific than "va-yikhtov" (and he wrote) -- it suggests marking or tracing rather than composing text. This may preserve an ambiguity about what exactly Jesus wrote.

### 8:32 — "ve-ha-emet totzi'akhem le-cherut"
- **Hebrew**: vi-da'tem et ha-emet ve-ha-emet totzi'akhem le-cherut
- **Note**: "ha-emet totzi'akhem le-cherut" (the truth will bring you out to freedom) uses Exodus vocabulary. "totzi'akhem" (will bring you out) from the root y-tz-a is the verb of the Exodus (cf. Exodus 6:6-7). "cherut" (freedom/liberty) echoes the Passover Haggadah: "avadim hayinu...bnei chorin" (we were slaves...now free). The Johannine truth-as-liberation theme gains profound resonance through this Exodus vocabulary.

### 8:44 — "avi ha-shaqer"
- **Hebrew**: atem me-et avikhem ha-Satan...ki meshakker hu va-avi ha-shaqer
- **Note**: "ha-Satan" (the Adversary) for "diabolus" is the standard Hebrew term. "avi ha-shaqer" (father of the lie/falsehood) is a powerful Hebrew construction. "rotze'ach hayah me-rosh" (he was a murderer from the beginning) -- "rotze'ach" is the exact term from the Sixth Commandment (Exodus 20:13). The Hebrew connects the devil's nature directly to the Decalogue violation.

### 8:56 — "Avraham avikhem sas lir'ot et-yomi"
- **Hebrew**: Avraham avikhem sas lir'ot et-yomi va-yar va-yismach
- **Note**: "sas" (rejoiced) and "va-yismach" (and he was glad) create a doublet of joy. The claim that Abraham saw Christ's day connects to rabbinic traditions about Abraham's prophetic vision. "va-yar va-yismach" (he saw and rejoiced) echoes the biblical pattern of seeing God's works and responding with joy (cf. Psalm 97:1).

### 8:58 — "be-terem heyot Avraham ani hayiti"
- **Hebrew**: be-terem heyot Avraham ani hayiti
- **Note**: This is the climactic "ego eimi" of John 8. The Greek "ego eimi" (I am) echoes the divine name from Exodus 3:14. The Vulgate "ego sum" preserves this. The Hebrew "ani hayiti" (I was/I have been) unfortunately adds a past-tense verb, weakening the timeless present that "ego eimi" conveys. A rendering of "ani hu" or even "ehyeh" (cf. Exodus 3:14) would better capture the divine self-identification. "ani hayiti" makes it sound like mere temporal pre-existence rather than eternal divine being.

## Other Notes

### 8:1-11 — Pericope Adulterae: Catholic canonical status
This passage was affirmed as canonical by the Council of Trent (Session IV, 1546). While textual criticism notes its absence from early Greek manuscripts, its presence in the Vulgate and its long liturgical use in the Western Church establish it firmly within the Catholic biblical canon. The Hebrew translation is faithful to the narrative.

### 8:12 — "Ani or ha-olam"
The second great "I am" statement -- "I am the light of the world." Following immediately after the Sukkot context of chapter 7, this may allude to the great lampstands lit in the Temple Court of the Women during Sukkot. The Hebrew "or ha-olam" (light of the world) resonates with Isaiah 42:6, 49:6 (light to the nations).

### 8:58 — Stoning response
The immediate attempt to stone Jesus (v59) confirms that his audience understood "ego eimi" as a divine claim. Under the Torah (Leviticus 24:16), blasphemy was punishable by stoning. The Hebrew narrative "az yarimu avanim lirgom oto" (then they lifted up stones to stone him) uses the precise legal vocabulary of judicial stoning.
