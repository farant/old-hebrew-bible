# John Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 41 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 9:4 — "Me oportet" vs "hemas dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Me oportet operari" (I must work). The SBLGNT has "hemas dei ergazesthai" (we must work), using the first person plural. The Hebrew has "alai la'asot" (upon me to do), following the Vulgate's singular "Me" rather than the Greek plural "hemas."
- **Vulgate**: Me oportet operari opera ejus qui misit me
- **Greek**: hemas dei ergazesthai ta erga tou pempsantos me
- **Hebrew**: alai la'asot ma'asei shol'chi
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate's singular reading. The variant is significant: the plural "we must work" includes the disciples in Christ's mission; the singular emphasizes Christ's unique vocation.

### 9:35 — "Filium Dei" vs "huion tou anthropou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Tu credis in Filium Dei?" (Do you believe in the Son of God?). The SBLGNT reads "Su pisteueis eis ton huion tou anthropou?" (Do you believe in the Son of Man?). The Hebrew has "ha-ta'amin be-Ven ha-Adam" (Do you believe in the Son of Man?), following the SBLGNT against the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: Tu credis in Filium Dei?
- **Greek**: Su pisteueis eis ton huion tou anthropou?
- **Hebrew**: ha-ta'amin be-Ven ha-Adam?
- **Note**: This is a significant textual variant with Christological implications. "Son of God" (Vulgate) makes an explicit divine claim; "Son of Man" (SBLGNT/Hebrew) is the apocalyptic title from Daniel 7:13. To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should read "ha-ta'amin be-Ven ha-Elohim."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:1 — "ivver mi-yom hivvaldo"
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi be-ovro va-yar ish ve-hu ivver mi-yom hivvaldo
- **Note**: "ivver mi-yom hivvaldo" (blind from the day of his birth) is natural biblical Hebrew. The Vulgate's "caecum a nativitate" and Greek "tuphlon ek genetes" are both accurately rendered. The construction "va-yehi be-ovro" (and it was as he passed by) uses the classic waw-consecutive narrative opening found throughout biblical prose.

### 9:6 — "va-yaroq al-ha-aretz va-ya'as tit"
- **Hebrew**: va-yaroq al-ha-aretz va-ya'as tit min-ha-roq va-yimrach et-ha-tit al-einei ha-ivver
- **Note**: "va-yaroq" (and he spat) is direct biblical Hebrew (cf. Numbers 12:14, Deuteronomy 25:9). "tit" (clay/mud) is the standard Hebrew for moldable earth (cf. Isaiah 41:25, Nahum 3:14). The detail of making clay from spittle and applying it to the eyes is rendered concretely and naturally. The connection to Genesis 2:7 (God forming man from the earth) is implicit in the vocabulary.

### 9:7 — "Brekhat ha-Shiloach hu shaluach"
- **Hebrew**: lekh u-rechatz bi-vrekhat ha-Shiloach hu shaluach
- **Note**: The Hebrew naturally explains the etymology: "ha-Shiloach" (Siloam) is glossed as "hu shaluach" (that is, Sent). This mirrors the Greek "ho hermeunetai Apestalmenos" and Latin "quod interpretatur Missus." In Hebrew, the wordplay is organic -- "Shiloach" and "shaluach" share the root sh-l-ch (to send), making the etymological connection immediately transparent in a way it cannot be in Greek or Latin.

### 9:22 — "le-naddot" for "aposynagogos"
- **Hebrew**: ki ha-Yehudim kevar no'atzu le-naddot et kol asher yodeh ki hu ha-Mashiach
- **Note**: "le-naddot" (to excommunicate/ban) is the rabbinic term for the lesser form of excommunication (nidduy). The Greek "aposynagogos" (put out of the synagogue) and Latin "extra synagogam" are technical terms for this social-religious penalty. The Hebrew uses the precise halakhic vocabulary that would have been understood in a Jewish legal context. The three levels of Jewish excommunication were nidduy (reproof), cherem (ban), and shamta (permanent expulsion).

### 9:24 — "ten kavod le-Elohim"
- **Hebrew**: ten kavod le-Elohim anachnu yada'nu ki ha-ish ha-zeh chote hu
- **Note**: "ten kavod le-Elohim" (give glory to God) is a juridical oath formula from Joshua 7:19, where Joshua adjures Achan before confession. The Pharisees use this formula to pressure the healed man to denounce Jesus. The Hebrew perfectly preserves this biblical-legal allusion that is present in the Greek "Dos doxan to Theo" and Latin "Da gloriam Deo."

### 9:34 — "be-chata'im noladta kullakh"
- **Hebrew**: hen ba-chata'im noladta kullakh ve-attah telammadenu
- **Note**: "ba-chata'im noladta kullakh" (in sins you were born entirely) -- the accusation links his blindness to sin, the very assumption Jesus rejected in v3. "kullakh" (all of you/entirely) emphasizes total sinfulness. The Hebrew is sharp and contemptuous, matching the tone of the Greek and Latin.

## Other Notes

### 9:5 — "or ha-olam ani"
The third occurrence of the light-of-the-world theme (cf. 1:4-9, 8:12). The Hebrew "be-odeni ba-olam or ha-olam ani" (while I am in the world, I am the light of the world) is a more compressed "ego eimi" statement. The qualification "while I am in the world" gives it a temporal dimension absent from 8:12.

### 9:39-41 — Judgment paradox
The chapter concludes with a profound reversal: those who were blind now see; those who claim to see are blind. The Hebrew "le-ma'an yir'u ha-ivrim ve-ha-ro'im yihyu le-ivrim" (so that the blind may see and the seeing may become blind) uses clean antithetical parallelism, a characteristic feature of Hebrew wisdom and prophetic literature (cf. Isaiah 6:9-10, which is the conceptual background).

### 9:38 — Worship of Jesus
"va-yishtachu lo" (and he worshipped him) -- the healed man's response uses the same verb of worship (hishtachavah) that is used for worship of God throughout the Hebrew Bible. This is significant for Johannine Christology: the man's progression from calling Jesus "a man" (v11) to "prophet" (v17) to worshipping him (v38) traces a journey to faith in Christ's divinity.
