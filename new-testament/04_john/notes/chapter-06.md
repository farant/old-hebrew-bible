# John Chapter 6 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 6:52-58 — Verse boundary shifts (Eucharistic discourse)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's verse numbering for the Eucharistic discourse section diverges from the SBLGNT. In the Vulgate, 6:52 contains "Si quis manducaverit ex hoc pane, vivet in aeternum: et panis quem ego dabo, caro mea est pro mundi vita." In the SBLGNT, this content is the second half of v51, while v52 begins with the Jews' dispute. The Hebrew follows the Greek/Protestant versification, placing the dispute at v52.
- **Note**: This is a persistent numbering offset through vv52-58. The content is the same; only the verse boundaries shift. Readers comparing the Vulgate directly with the Hebrew will find content displaced by approximately one verse in this section.

### 6:59-67 — Continued offset
- **Issue**: The Vulgate assigns the synagogue location note to 6:60, the disciples' complaint to 6:61, the ascension saying to 6:62, and so on. The Hebrew (following SBLGNT) places these at 6:59, 6:60, 6:61 respectively. The offset from the Eucharistic section propagates through the rest of the chapter.
- **Note**: The Hebrew of v59 = Vulgate v60 content. The Hebrew of v60 = Vulgate v61 content. And so on through the end of the chapter.

### 6:70-72 — Extra verse
- **Issue**: The Hebrew has 72 verses; the Vulgate has 72 verses. However, the Hebrew's verse 72 has empty Greek and Hebrew fields -- it appears to be a ghost entry. The Vulgate verse 72 contains "Dicebat autem Judam Simonis Iscariotem: hic enim erat traditurus eum, cum esset unus ex duodecim." This content appears in the Hebrew's v71.
- **Note**: The empty v72 entry in the data should be disregarded or removed.

## Content Alignment

### 6:11 — Distribution through disciples
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "distribuit discumbentibus" (distributed to those reclining), directly from Jesus to the crowd. The Hebrew adds the intermediary step: "va-yitten le-talmidav ve-ha-talmidim natnu la-mesubbim" (and he gave to his disciples, and the disciples gave to those reclining). This follows the Synoptic accounts (Matthew 14:19, Mark 6:41) rather than the Vulgate of John.
- **Vulgate**: distribuit discumbentibus
- **Greek**: diedoken tois anakeimenois
- **Hebrew**: va-yitten le-talmidav ve-ha-talmidim natnu la-mesubbim
- **Note**: The Hebrew inserts material not present in either the Vulgate or the SBLGNT for this verse. It should follow the Vulgate's simpler reading.

### 6:23 — "eucharistesantos tou kyriou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "gratias agente Domino" (the Lord giving thanks). The Greek has "eucharistesantos tou kyriou" (the Lord having given thanks). The Hebrew renders "be-virkat ha-Adon" (with the blessing of the Lord). All three agree on the Eucharistic overtone.
- **Note**: "birkat" (blessing) for "gratias agente" (giving thanks) shifts from thanksgiving to blessing. Both are liturgically valid but the Hebrew introduces a slight semantic difference.

### 6:69 — "Christus Filius Dei" vs "Qedosh Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "tu es Christus Filius Dei" (you are the Christ, the Son of God). The SBLGNT reads "su ei ho hagios tou theou" (you are the Holy One of God). The Hebrew has "ki-Qedosh Elohim attah" (for the Holy One of God you are), following the SBLGNT against the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: quia tu es Christus Filius Dei.
- **Greek**: su ei ho hagios tou theou.
- **Hebrew**: ki-Qedosh Elohim attah.
- **Note**: This is a significant textual variant. The Vulgate's "Christus Filius Dei" is the fuller confession. To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should read "ki attah ha-Mashiach Ben ha-Elohim."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:31 — "lechem min ha-shamayim natan-lamo le-ekhol"
- **Hebrew**: lechem min ha-shamayim natan-lamo le-ekhol
- **Note**: This is a direct quotation of Psalm 78:24 (also Exodus 16:4, Nehemiah 9:15). The Hebrew correctly uses the biblical wording. "natan-lamo" (he gave to them) with the archaic dative suffix "-lamo" is excellent elevated biblical Hebrew.

### 6:35 — "Anokhi hu lechem ha-chayyim"
- **Hebrew**: Anokhi hu lechem ha-chayyim
- **Note**: The first of the great "I am" declarations in John. "Anokhi hu" (I am he) uses the emphatic first-person pronoun "anokhi" rather than simple "ani," echoing divine self-revelation language (cf. Exodus 20:2 "Anokhi YHWH Eloheikha"). This is theologically astute.

### 6:45 — Isaiah 54:13 quotation
- **Hebrew**: ve-khol-banayikh limmudei YHWH
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the Tetragrammaton here where the Greek has "theou" (of God) and the Vulgate "Dei." The quotation is from Isaiah 54:13, which in the MT reads "ve-khol-banayikh limmudei YHWH." Delitzsch correctly goes to the Hebrew source text for the OT quotation rather than back-translating from the Greek. This is good practice for a Hebrew translation.

### 6:51 — "besari" for "caro mea"
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-lechem asher ettenennu hu vesari asher etten be'ad chayyei ha-olam
- **Note**: "besari" (my flesh) is the critical Eucharistic term. The Hebrew "basar" carries strong biblical connotations: the creation of woman from Adam's "basar" (Genesis 2:23), the covenant phrase "kol basar" (all flesh), and the incarnational "ve-ha-Davar hayah basar" (John 1:14). The Eucharistic discourse gains profound depth in Hebrew through these resonances.

### 6:63 — "ha-ruach hu ha-noten chayyim"
- **Hebrew**: ha-ruach hu ha-noten chayyim ve-ha-basar ein-bo mo'il
- **Note**: "ha-ruach hu ha-noten chayyim" (the spirit is the one who gives life) and "ha-basar ein-bo mo'il" (the flesh has no profit in it). "mo'il" (useful/profitable) from the root y-'-l is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Isaiah 44:9-10, Jeremiah 2:11). The spirit/flesh contrast here resonates with the Hebraic prophetic tradition.

## Other Notes

### 6:14-15 — Prophet and King
The crowd's reaction -- identifying Jesus as "the Prophet" (ha-navi ha-ba la-olam) and wanting to make him king -- links to Deuteronomy 18:15 (the prophet like Moses). The Hebrew perfectly echoes this Mosaic expectation. The feeding miracle, set near Passover (v4), deliberately parallels Moses and the manna.

### 6:53-56 — Eucharistic realism
The Eucharistic discourse is the most sacramentally explicit passage in John. The Hebrew vocabulary -- "basar" (flesh), "dam" (blood), "okhel" (food), "shikkuy" (drink) -- is concrete and unambiguous. The Latin "caro mea vere est cibus" (my flesh truly is food) becomes "ki vesari be-emet hu okhel" (for my flesh is truly food). The realism of the Vulgate is preserved.
