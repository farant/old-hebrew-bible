# John Chapter 11 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The Vulgate combines 11:56-57 into a single verse in some editions, with 11:56 containing material that corresponds to both SBLGNT vv56-57. In this text, v57 has an empty Latin field, indicating the Vulgate joined the content of vv56-57 into v56. The SBLGNT and Hebrew maintain two separate verses. No other numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 11:2 — "unxit Dominum unguento" vs "aleipsasa ton kyrion myro"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "unguento" (with ointment) where the Hebrew has "be-mirqachat" (with ointment/perfume), aligning well. However, the Hebrew omits the verb for anointing: "asher et-ha-Adon be-mirqachat" is missing the verbal action. The Greek has "he aleipsasa ton kyrion myro" (she who anointed the Lord with ointment). The Hebrew appears to have dropped the main verb.
- **Vulgate**: Maria autem erat quae unxit Dominum unguento
- **Greek**: Maria he aleipsasa ton kyrion myro
- **Hebrew**: hi Miryam asher et-ha-Adon be-mirqachat
- **Note**: The Hebrew is defective here -- it lacks a verb corresponding to "unxit" / "aleipsasa." The sentence reads awkwardly as "she is Miriam who the Lord with ointment." A verb such as "mashchah" or "sakha" is needed.

### 11:5 — "Mariam" in Vulgate vs omission in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Diligebat autem Jesus Martham, et sororem ejus Mariam, et Lazarum," explicitly naming Mary. The SBLGNT omits Mary's name: "ten Marthan kai ten adelphen autes kai ton Lazaron." The Hebrew follows the Greek: "et Marta ve-et achotah ve-et La'zar," omitting Mary's name.
- **Vulgate**: sororem ejus Mariam
- **Greek**: ten adelphen autes
- **Hebrew**: ve-et achotah
- **Note**: Minor difference. To follow the Vulgate strictly, the Hebrew should include "ve-et Miryam achotah" (and Miriam her sister).

### 11:27 — "Filium Dei vivi" vs "huion tou theou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "tu es Christus, Filius Dei vivi" (you are the Christ, the Son of the living God). This includes "vivi" (living), which is not in the SBLGNT "su ei ho christos ho huios tou theou." The Hebrew "attah ha-Mashiach Ben-ha-Elohim ha-ba la-olam" follows the Greek, omitting "living" and including "who comes into the world."
- **Vulgate**: Filius Dei vivi, qui in hunc mundum venisti
- **Greek**: ho christos ho huios tou theou ho eis ton kosmon erchomenos
- **Hebrew**: ha-Mashiach Ben-ha-Elohim ha-ba la-olam
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Dei vivi" (living God) recalls Peter's confession in Matthew 16:16. To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should add "ha-chai" (the living) after "ha-Elohim." Both Vulgate and Greek/Hebrew include the coming-into-the-world clause.

### 11:29 — "cito" in Vulgate vs compression in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate says "surgit cito" (rises quickly). The Greek has "egerthhe tachy" (she arose quickly). The Hebrew has "va-taqom va-tavo elav" (she arose and came to him), omitting the adverb "quickly."
- **Vulgate**: surgit cito, et venit ad eum
- **Greek**: egerthhe tachy kai ercheto pros auton
- **Hebrew**: hi sham'ah va-taqom va-tavo elav
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits "quickly" (maher/meherah). Minor, but "cito" carries narrative urgency.

### 11:33 — "infremuit spiritu" rendering
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "infremuit spiritu, et turbavit seipsum" (he groaned in spirit and troubled himself). The Greek has "enebrimesato to pneumati kai etaraxen heauton." The Hebrew renders this as "va-tiz'om rucho va-yehi mar'id" (his spirit raged and he was trembling).
- **Vulgate**: infremuit spiritu, et turbavit seipsum
- **Greek**: enebrimesato to pneumati kai etaraxen heauton
- **Hebrew**: va-tiz'om rucho va-yehi mar'id
- **Note**: The Hebrew "va-tiz'om" (from z-'-m, to be indignant/enraged) captures the intensity of "infremuit" well. "mar'id" (trembling/shaking) for "turbavit seipsum" shifts from a reflexive self-disturbance to a passive trembling. The Vulgate's reflexive "seipsum" suggests a deliberate act of the will; the Hebrew makes it appear involuntary.

### 11:41 — Vulgate "ubi positus fuerat mortuus" vs Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Tulerunt ergo lapidem" without additional qualification. The SBLGNT simply reads "eran oun ton lithon." The Hebrew adds "asher ha-met husam sham" (where the dead man was placed there), a clarifying gloss not present in the Vulgate or the main SBLGNT text.
- **Vulgate**: Tulerunt ergo lapidem
- **Greek**: eran oun ton lithon
- **Hebrew**: va-yis'u et-ha-even asher ha-met husam sham
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds a clarifying clause. This may derive from a variant reading, but it is not in the Vulgate.

### 11:45 — "ad Mariam, et Martham" in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "qui venerant ad Mariam, et Martham" (who had come to Mary and Martha). The SBLGNT has only "pros ten Mariam" (to Mary). The Hebrew has "asher ba'u el-Miryam" (who came to Miriam), following the Greek with only Mary.
- **Vulgate**: qui venerant ad Mariam, et Martham
- **Greek**: hoi elthontes pros ten Mariam
- **Hebrew**: asher ba'u el-Miryam
- **Note**: To follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should add "ve-el Marta" (and to Martha).

### 11:56-57 — Combined Vulgate verse
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's v56 contains the material for both SBLGNT vv56 and 57: it includes both the people's speculation in the temple and the chief priests' order to report Jesus' location. The Hebrew text has v57 with its own content but v57 has an empty Latin field.
- **Note**: The versification difference is well-known. The content is identical; only the verse boundary placement differs.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 11:1 — "La'zar mi-Beit-Hini"
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi ish choleh ve-hu La'zar mi-Beit-Hini kefar Miryam u-Marta achotah
- **Note**: "La'zar" is the Hebrew form of Lazarus (from El'azar, "God has helped"). "Beit-Hini" for Bethania is a reasonable Hebrew transliteration. The waw-consecutive "va-yehi" (and it was) is the classic biblical narrative opening. "choleh" (sick) for the Vulgate's "languens" and Greek "asthenon" is appropriate.

### 11:23 — Defective Hebrew initial letter
- **Hebrew**: The text begins with "nun-final + yod" (ןַיֹּאמֶר) rather than the expected waw-yod (וַיֹּאמֶר). This appears to be a typographical error in the source data -- a nun-sofit where a waw should be.
- **Note**: This should read "va-yomer" (וַיֹּאמֶר) not "nan-yomer" (ןַיֹּאמֶר).

### 11:25 — "Anokhi ha-tequmah ve-ha-chayyim"
- **Hebrew**: anokhi ha-tequmah ve-ha-chayyim ha-ma'amin bi yichyeh gam ki yamut
- **Note**: "ha-tequmah" (the resurrection/arising) for "resurrectio" / "anastasis" is effective. The root q-w-m (to rise/stand up) is the standard Hebrew for resurrection and connects to passages like Daniel 12:2. "anokhi" rather than "ani" lends the divine "I am" weight, echoing Exodus 3:14. This is one of the seven great "ego eimi" statements and the Hebrew rendering is strong.

### 11:35 — "va-yevk Yeshua"
- **Hebrew**: va-yevk Yeshua
- **Note**: The shortest verse in the Bible. The Hebrew "va-yevk" (and he wept) is a perfect waw-consecutive using the same verb form found when Abraham wept for Sarah (Genesis 23:2) and when Joseph wept over his brothers (Genesis 45:2). The verb carries deep biblical resonance for grief in the face of death.

### 11:39 — "kevar ba'ash"
- **Hebrew**: Adonai hinneh kevar ba'ash ki arba'ah yamim lo
- **Note**: "kevar ba'ash" (he already stinks) is vivid and direct, matching the Vulgate's blunt "jam foetet" and Greek "ede ozei." Martha's objection is rendered with appropriate physical frankness. "arba'ah yamim lo" (four days to him) captures the time indicator naturally.

### 11:43 — "La'zar qum tze"
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi ke-khalloto le-dabber va-yiqra be-qol gadol La'zar qum tze
- **Note**: The command "La'zar qum tze" (Lazarus, arise, come out) uses two imperatives. "qum" (arise) is the same command used throughout the prophetic literature for raising up. "tze" (go out/come forth) matches the Vulgate's "veni foras" and Greek "deuro exo." The Hebrew captures the commanding force of the cry with elegant brevity.

### 11:47 — "Sanhedrin"
- **Hebrew**: az yaqhilu rashei ha-kohanim ve-ha-Perushim et-Sanhedrin
- **Note**: Delitzsch uses the original Hebrew/Aramaic term "Sanhedrin" rather than translating it (as the Vulgate does with "concilium" and the Greek with "synedrion"). This is appropriate since the institution is natively Semitic. "yaqhilu" (they assembled) from q-h-l is a strong biblical Hebrew verb for gathering an assembly (cf. Numbers 20:10).

## Other Notes

### 11:4 — "Ben-ha-Elohim" (Son of God)
The Vulgate's "ut glorificetur Filius Dei per eam" is rendered as "le-ma'an yikkaved-bah Ben-ha-Elohim." The use of the niphal "yikkaved" (will be glorified) from k-b-d captures the Vulgate's "glorificetur" well. This verse establishes the theological purpose of Lazarus's death: the glorification of the Son of God.

### 11:49-52 — Caiaphas's Unwitting Prophecy
The narrator's theological commentary is well handled in Hebrew. "va-yinnave" (and he prophesied, v51) uses the standard Hebrew prophetic verb from n-b-'. The scope of the prophecy -- dying not only "be'ad ha-am" (for the people) but also "le-qabbetz et-benei ha-Elohim ha-mefuzzarim" (to gather the scattered children of God, v52) -- uses vocabulary from the ingathering prophecies of Isaiah and Ezekiel. "mefuzzarim" (scattered) echoes Ezekiel 34:12.

### 11:54 — "Ir Efrayim"
The city of Ephraim (Latin "Ephrem," Greek "Ephraim") near the wilderness is rendered as "Ir Efrayim," using the biblical tribal name. Its location near the desert connects to the wilderness retreat motif found in the lives of Moses, Elijah, and David.
