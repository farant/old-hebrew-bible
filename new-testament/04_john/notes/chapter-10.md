# John Chapter 10 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 42 verses align across all three sources. No numbering discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 10:8 — "Omnes quotquot venerunt" vs "pantes hosoi elthon pro emou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Omnes quotquot venerunt" (all who came). The SBLGNT includes "pro emou" (before me) in some manuscripts. The Hebrew has "kol asher ba'u le-fanai" (all who came before me), including "le-fanai" (before me), following the SBLGNT tradition that includes "pro emou."
- **Vulgate**: Omnes quotquot venerunt, fures sunt, et latrones
- **Greek**: pantes hosoi elthon [pro emou] kleptai eisin kai lestai
- **Hebrew**: kol asher ba'u le-fanai gannavim hemah u-faritzim
- **Note**: The Vulgate omits "pro emou" (before me). To strictly follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should omit "le-fanai." The inclusion of "before me" has been controversial patristically, as it seems to condemn the prophets.

### 10:26 — "sicut dixi vobis" / "ka-asher amarti lakhem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads simply "quia non estis ex ovibus meis" (because you are not of my sheep). The SBLGNT adds "kathos eipon hymin" (as I told you). The Hebrew includes "ka-asher amarti lakhem" (as I said to you), following the Greek addition.
- **Vulgate**: sed vos non creditis, quia non estis ex ovibus meis.
- **Greek**: alla hymeis ou pisteuete, hoti ouk este ek ton probaton ton emon [kathos eipon hymin].
- **Hebrew**: ...ki lo mi-tzo'ni attem ka-asher amarti lakhem.
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds a clause not in the Vulgate. To follow the Vulgate, "ka-asher amarti lakhem" should be omitted.

### 10:29 — "Pater meus quod dedit mihi"
- **Issue**: This verse has a significant textual variant. The Vulgate reads "Pater meus quod dedit mihi, majus omnibus est" (My Father, what he has given me, is greater than all). The SBLGNT reads "ho pater mou ho dedoken moi panton meizon estin" (My Father who has given them to me is greater than all). The Hebrew has "ha-Av asher netanan li gadol hu al-kol" (the Father who gave them to me is greater than all), aligning with the Greek reading where the Father himself is "greater than all."
- **Vulgate**: Pater meus quod dedit mihi, majus omnibus est
- **Greek**: ho pater mou ho dedoken moi panton meizon estin
- **Hebrew**: ha-Av asher netanan li gadol hu al-kol
- **Note**: Both readings are orthodox. The Vulgate emphasizes the gift (what the Father gave = greater than all); the Hebrew/Greek emphasize the Father himself (the Father is greater than all). The Hebrew follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate here.

### 10:41-42 — Verse boundary
- **Issue**: The Vulgate splits the content differently than the SBLGNT across vv41-42. The Vulgate's v41 includes only the first clause ("Joannes quidem signum fecit nullum"), while v42 contains both the second clause about John's truthful testimony and the statement about many believing. The SBLGNT includes John's truthful testimony in v41 and places only the believing statement in v42. The Hebrew follows the SBLGNT division.
- **Note**: No content is lost; only the verse boundary placement differs.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 10:1 — "mikla ha-tzon" for "aulen ton probaton"
- **Hebrew**: ish asher lo-yavo derekh ha-sha'ar el-mikla ha-tzon ki im-ya'aleh be-derekh acher gannav hu u-faritz
- **Note**: "mikla ha-tzon" (sheepfold/enclosure of the flock) is excellent biblical Hebrew. "mikla" (enclosure/pen) appears in Habakkuk 3:17 and Psalm 50:9. "gannav" (thief) and "faritz" (robber/violent one) -- the Hebrew distinguishes between stealth theft and violent robbery, matching the Greek "kleptes...lestes" and Latin "fur...latro."

### 10:7 — "Ani hu petach ha-tzon"
- **Hebrew**: amen amen ani omer lakhem ani hu petach ha-tzon
- **Note**: "ani hu petach ha-tzon" (I am the door of the sheep). The word "petach" (opening/entrance) is the standard Hebrew for a doorway (cf. Genesis 4:7, Exodus 12:22). The Vulgate "ostium" and Greek "thyra" both mean door/gate. This "I am" declaration uses "ani hu" consistently with the other ego-eimi statements.

### 10:11 — "ha-ro'eh ha-tov"
- **Hebrew**: Anokhi hu ha-ro'eh ha-tov ha-ro'eh ha-tov yitten et-nafsho be'ad tzo'no
- **Note**: "ha-ro'eh ha-tov" (the good shepherd) echoes Ezekiel 34, where God promises to shepherd Israel himself after the failure of Israel's shepherds. "yitten et-nafsho" (gives his life/soul) uses "nefesh" in its fullest biblical sense: life, soul, self. The phrase "be'ad tzo'no" (for his flock) uses "be'ad" which denotes substitutionary action (on behalf of, in place of). The entire verse resonates with Psalm 23 and the messianic shepherd prophecies.

### 10:16 — "eder echad ve-ro'eh echad"
- **Hebrew**: ve-hayah eder echad ve-ro'eh echad
- **Note**: "eder echad ve-ro'eh echad" (one flock and one shepherd). The Vulgate has "unum ovile et unus pastor" (one fold and one shepherd). The Greek distinguishes "poimen" (flock) from "aule" (fold/enclosure), and the Hebrew "eder" (flock/herd) correctly follows the Greek "poimne" rather than the enclosure concept. The Vulgate's "ovile" (sheepfold) emphasizes the Church as a single institution; the Hebrew/Greek "flock" emphasizes the people under one shepherd. The Vulgate reading has historically been more significant in ecclesiology.

### 10:22 — "Chanukkah"
- **Hebrew**: va-yehi Chanukkah bi-Yerushalayim ve-ha-et stav
- **Note**: "Chanukkah" is the Hebrew name for the Feast of Dedication, commemorating the Maccabean rededication of the Temple (1 Maccabees 4:59, 2 Maccabees 10:5). The Greek "Enkainia" and Latin "Encaenia" are translations of this Hebrew/Aramaic term. Delitzsch uses the original Hebrew festival name, which is ideal. "stav" (winter) for "hiems" is post-biblical Hebrew (the biblical term would be "choref").

### 10:30 — "Ani ve-Avi echad anachnu"
- **Hebrew**: ani ve-Avi echad anachnu
- **Note**: "I and my Father are one" -- one of the most theologically dense verses in the Gospel. The Hebrew "echad" (one) is the same word used in the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4): "YHWH Eloheinu YHWH echad." The Greek "hen" (one thing, neuter) specifies unity of essence rather than personal identity. The Hebrew "echad" naturally carries the same range: it can mean a composite unity (as in "yom echad" -- one day composed of evening and morning, Genesis 1:5) or absolute unity. The verse's position after the Chanukkah/Temple setting adds the dimension of divine presence.

### 10:34 — "Elohim attem" (Psalm 82:6)
- **Hebrew**: ha-lo khatuv be-Toratkhem ani amarti Elohim attem
- **Note**: Direct quotation of Psalm 82:6. The Hebrew here is the actual biblical text, not a back-translation. Jesus' argument from the lesser to the greater (a fortiori / qal va-chomer) -- if Scripture calls human judges "elohim," how much more the one sanctified and sent by the Father -- is rendered in its native Hebrew legal-reasoning form.

## Other Notes

### 10:10 — "chayyim u-melo sifqam"
"I came that they may have life, and have it abundantly." The Hebrew "chayyim u-melo sifqam" (life and the fullness of their sufficiency) for "vitam... et abundantius" is an unusual rendering. "sifqam" from the root s-p-q (sufficiency, enough) is Mishnaic rather than biblical. A more biblical rendering might use "chayyim la-sova" (life to satiety) or "chayyim ve-yoter" (life and more).

### 10:22-39 — Chanukkah discourse
The Chanukkah setting provides the backdrop for Jesus' most explicit claims to divinity (vv30, 38). Chanukkah celebrates the re-consecration of the Temple; Jesus claims to be the one "whom the Father sanctified (qiddesho) and sent into the world" (v36). The Hebrew "qiddesh" (sanctified/consecrated) is the same root as "chanukkah" (dedication/consecration), creating a verbal link between the festival and Christ's mission.

### 10:33 — Blasphemy charge
"al-gaddafkha et-Elohim" (because you blaspheme God) and "va-ta'as atzmekha Elohim" (and you make yourself God). This is the most explicit accusation of claiming divinity in the Gospel. The Hebrew starkly states the charge: a human being (adam) making himself God (Elohim). The gravity of this in Hebrew, where Elohim is the word for the Creator in Genesis 1:1, cannot be overstated.
