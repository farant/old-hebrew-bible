# Mark Chapter 16 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 16:9-20 — The Longer Ending of Mark
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT encloses 16:9-20 in double brackets (⟦ ⟧), indicating this passage is considered a later addition by the editors. It is absent from Codex Sinaiticus and Codex Vaticanus. However, the Vulgate includes these verses without any notation, and they are canonical in the Catholic tradition (affirmed by the Council of Trent).
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. All 20 verses (16:1-20) are included and canonical. The Hebrew text from Delitzsch also includes all 20 verses. No verses should be removed.

All 20 verses align across all three sources (Greek has text for 9-20 in brackets; Vulgate and Hebrew include them without qualification).

## Content Alignment

### 16:8 — Shorter ending absent from Vulgate
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT Greek includes a "shorter ending" after verse 8 (beginning with "Panta de ta parengelmena..."), enclosed in brackets. Neither the Vulgate nor the Hebrew includes this shorter ending.
- **Vulgate**: Proceeds directly from verse 8 to verse 9
- **Hebrew**: Proceeds directly from verse 8 to verse 9
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The shorter ending is not part of the Catholic canon. No action needed.

### 16:3 — "Ab ostio monumenti" (from the door of the monument)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ab ostio monumenti" (from the door of the tomb). The SBLGNT reads "ek tes thyras tou mnemeiou" (from the door of the tomb). The Hebrew reads מֵעַל פֶּתַח הַקָּבֶר (from upon the opening of the tomb).
- **Vulgate**: *Quis revolvet nobis lapidem ab ostio monumenti?*
- **Hebrew**: מִי יָגֶל-לָנוּ אֶת-הָאֶבֶן מֵעַל פֶּתַח הַקָּבֶר
- **Note**: Hebrew is adequate. The preposition מֵעַל (from upon) accurately conveys the stone sitting over/against the tomb entrance. The verb גָּלַל (to roll) is the standard Hebrew term for rolling a stone (cf. Genesis 29:3, 29:10; Joshua 10:18).

### 16:9 — "Prima sabbati" (first of the week)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "mane prima sabbati" (morning on the first of the week). The Hebrew reads בְּאֶחָד בַּשַּׁבָּת (on the first of the week). Both match well.
- **Vulgate**: *Surgens autem mane prima sabbati*
- **Hebrew**: כַּאֲשֶׁר קָם מִן-הַמֵּתִים בְּאֶחָד בַּשַּׁבָּת
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly renders the temporal expression. No change needed.

### 16:14 — "Novissime" (lastly/finally)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Novissime" (lastly). The Greek reads "Hysteron" (later/afterward). The Hebrew reads וּבָאַחֲרֹנָה (and at last/finally).
- **Vulgate**: *Novissime recumbentibus illis undecim apparuit*
- **Hebrew**: וּבָאַחֲרֹנָה נִרְאָה לְעַשְׁתֵּי הֶעָשָׂר
- **Note**: Hebrew correctly renders the finality of this last appearance. The numeral עַשְׁתֵּי הֶעָשָׂר (eleven) is the correct biblical Hebrew form (cf. Deuteronomy 1:2). Good.

### 16:16 — "Condemnabitur" (will be condemned)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "condemnabitur" (will be condemned). The Greek reads "katakrithesetai" (will be condemned/judged). The Hebrew reads יֶאְשָׁם (will be found guilty/bear guilt).
- **Vulgate**: *qui vero non crediderit, condemnabitur*
- **Hebrew**: וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא-יַאֲמִין יֶאְשָׁם
- **Note**: The Hebrew root א-שׁ-מ carries the sense of bearing guilt or being found guilty (Leviticus 5:2-5, Hosea 14:1). It captures the juridical meaning of "condemnabitur" with an OT sacrificial overtone, since אָשָׁם also means "guilt offering." Effective word choice.

### 16:19 — "Dominus quidem Jesus" (the Lord Jesus indeed)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate names "Dominus quidem Jesus" (the Lord Jesus indeed). The Greek has "ho men oun kyrios Iesous" (the Lord Jesus). The Hebrew reads הָאָדוֹן (the Lord) without "Jesus."
- **Vulgate**: *Et Dominus quidem Jesus*
- **Greek**: *ho men oun kyrios Iesous*
- **Hebrew**: הָאָדוֹן
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits the name "Jesus." It should be corrected to: הָאָדוֹן יֵשׁוּעַ to follow the Vulgate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 16:1 — סַמִּים (spices/aromatics)
- **Hebrew**: וַתִּקְנֶינָה... סַמִּים לָלֶכֶת וְלָסוּךְ אֹתוֹ בָּהֶם
- **Note**: The word סַמִּים (spices) is the biblical term for aromatic substances (Exodus 30:34, Song of Songs 4:14-16). It is the same word used for the incense ingredients of the Tabernacle. The verb סוּךְ (to anoint/smear) appears in Ruth 3:3 and 2 Samuel 12:20 for anointing the body. Both terms are well-chosen biblical vocabulary for burial preparation.

### 16:5 — בָּחוּר (young man)
- **Hebrew**: וַתִּרְאֶינָה בָּחוּר אֶחָד ישֵּׁב מִיָּמִין
- **Note**: The word בָּחוּר (young man) is standard biblical Hebrew (Judges 14:10, Ruth 3:10, Isaiah 62:5). It matches the Vulgate "juvenem" and the Greek "neaniskon." However, the Hebrew verb appears to have a vowel irregularity: ישֵׁב should be יֹשֵׁב (sitting).

### 16:15 — לְכָל-הַבְּרִיאָה (to all creation)
- **Hebrew**: לְכוּ אֶל-כָּל-הָעוֹלָם וְקִרְאוּ אֶת-הַבְּשׂוֹרָה לְכָל-הַבְּרִיאָה
- **Note**: The word הַבְּרִיאָה (the creation) for "omni creaturae" uses the Hebrew nominal form from the root ב-ר-א (to create), the verb of Genesis 1:1. This gives the Great Commission a cosmic scope anchored in the Genesis creation vocabulary. The word הָעוֹלָם (the world) for "mundum universum" is standard post-biblical Hebrew but well understood.

### 16:19 — וַיֵּשֶׁב לִימִין הָאֱלֹהִים (and he sat at the right hand of God)
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּנָּשֵׂא הַשָּׁמָיְמָה וַיֵּשֶׁב לִימִין הָאֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: The verb נָשָׂא (was lifted up) in the niphal is used for Elijah's ascent in 2 Kings 2:11. The phrase לִימִין (at the right hand) echoes Psalm 110:1 (שֵׁב לִימִינִי), the most quoted OT text in the NT. The Ascension language draws naturally on the OT theophanic vocabulary.

## Other Notes

### 16:9-20 — Canonicity and the Longer Ending
The Longer Ending of Mark (16:9-20) is absent from the two oldest Greek majuscules (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) and is enclosed in brackets in the SBLGNT. However, it is present in the Vulgate, was used liturgically throughout the Western Church, and was affirmed as canonical at the Council of Trent. The Hebrew of Delitzsch includes these verses. For this project, following the Vulgate, all twelve verses are authoritative and must be retained.

### 16:17-18 — Signs accompanying believers
The charismata listed (exorcism, tongues, serpent-handling, poison immunity, healing) are given in Jesus' direct speech. The Hebrew vocabulary is appropriate: שֵׁדִים (demons) is the standard Hebrew/Aramaic term for evil spirits (cf. Deuteronomy 32:17, Psalm 106:37); לְשֹׁנוֹת חֲדָשׁוֹת (new tongues) is a literal rendering. The passage has no parallel elsewhere in the Gospels, making it distinctive to the Longer Ending.

### 16:20 — Final verse and "Amen"
The Hebrew concludes with אָמֵן, which matches liturgical manuscripts of the Vulgate tradition. The SBLGNT Greek text also ends with "semeion" (signs) within the bracketed ending. The Hebrew אָמֵן serves as both a textual conclusion and a liturgical affirmation, fitting for a Gospel ending read in worship.
