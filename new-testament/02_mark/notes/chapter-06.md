# Mark Chapter 6 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 56 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 6:3 — "Joseph" (Vulgate) vs "Joses" (Greek/Hebrew)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Joseph" as the name of Jesus' brother. The SBLGNT reads "Iosetos" (Joses). The Hebrew reads יוֹסֵי (Yosei/Joses), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *frater Jacobi, et Joseph, et Judae, et Simonis*
- **Greek**: *Iakobou kai Iosetos kai Iouda kai Simonos*
- **Hebrew**: וַאֲחִי יַעֲקֹב וְיוֹסֵי וִיהוּדָה וְשִׁמְעוֹן
- **Note**: The Vulgate form "Joseph" is distinct from "Joses." The Hebrew should read וְיוֹסֵף to match the Vulgate.

### 6:11 — Bracketed Sodom/Gomorrah addition
- **Issue**: The Hebrew contains a lengthy bracketed addition: [אָמֵן אֲנִי אֹמֵר לָכֶם לִסְדֹם וְלַעֲמֹרָה יֵקַל בְּיוֹם הַדִּין מִן-הָעִיר הַהִיא] ("Amen I say to you, it shall be more tolerable for Sodom and Gomorrah in the day of judgment than for that city"). This is absent from both the Vulgate and the SBLGNT for this verse.
- **Vulgate**: *excutite pulverem de pedibus vestris in testimonium illis* (ends here)
- **Hebrew**: adds the Sodom/Gomorrah saying in brackets
- **Note**: This addition is harmonized from Matthew 10:15. The Vulgate does not include it in Mark 6:11. The bracketed text should be removed.

### 6:20 — "multa faciebat" (did many things) vs "aporein" (was perplexed)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "multa faciebat" (he did many things), meaning Herod acted on John's teaching. The SBLGNT reads "polla eporei" (he was much perplexed). These represent different readings: the Vulgate follows a variant (epoiei = did) while the critical text reads (eporei = was perplexed). The Hebrew reads וְהַרְבֵּה עָשָׂה (and he did many things), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *et audito eo multa faciebat* (having heard him, he did many things)
- **Greek**: *akousas autou polla eporei* (having heard him, he was much perplexed)
- **Hebrew**: וְהַרְבֵּה עָשָׂה (and he did many things)
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate reading here. No change needed.

### 6:22 — "filia ipsius Herodiadis" (daughter of Herodias herself)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "filia ipsius Herodiadis" (the daughter of Herodias herself). The SBLGNT reads "tes thygatros autou Herodiados" (his daughter Herodias) with apparatus marks, reflecting the famous textual variant where some manuscripts make the girl Herod's own daughter named Herodias. The Hebrew reads בַת-הוֹרוֹדְיָה (daughter of Herodias), following the Vulgate sense.
- **Vulgate**: *filia ipsius Herodiadis* (daughter of Herodias herself)
- **Hebrew**: בַת-הוֹרוֹדְיָה (daughter of Herodias)
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate reading.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:4 — אֵין הַנָּבִיא נִקְלֶה כִּי אִם... (A prophet is not dishonored except...)
- **Hebrew**: אֵין הַנָּבִיא נִקְלֶה כִּי אִם-בְּאַרְצוֹ וּבֵין קְרוֹבָיו וּבְבֵיתוֹ
- **Note**: Strong proverbial Hebrew. The passive participle נִקְלֶה (dishonored/despised) with the exceptive כִּי אִם construction is good biblical syntax. The term נִקְלֶה appears in 1 Samuel 18:23 and Isaiah 16:14. The Vulgate's "sine honore" (without honor) is rendered as the more active "dishonored" in Hebrew, which works well.

### 6:14 — הוֹרְדוֹס for Herod
- **Hebrew**: הַמֶּלֶךְ הוֹרְדוֹס
- **Note**: Delitzsch consistently transliterates Herod as הוֹרְדוֹס throughout. The title הַמֶּלֶךְ (the king) matches the Vulgate "rex Herodes" and the Greek "basileus Herodes." The Vulgate's terminology is followed.

### 6:34 — כַּצֹּאן אֲשֶׁר אֵין-לָהֶם רֹעֶה (like sheep without a shepherd)
- **Hebrew**: כַּצֹּאן אֲשֶׁר אֵין-לָהֶם רֹעֶה
- **Note**: Direct echo of Numbers 27:17 and 1 Kings 22:17 (Micaiah's prophecy). The Masoretic text of Numbers 27:17 reads כַּצֹּאן אֲשֶׁר אֵין-לָהֶם רֹעֶה. Delitzsch reproduces the OT phrase verbatim. Excellent.

### 6:48 — הָאַשְׁמֹרֶת הָרְבִיעִית (the fourth watch)
- **Hebrew**: כְּעֵת הָאַשְׁמֹרֶת הָרְבִיעִית
- **Note**: The term אַשְׁמֹרֶת (watch of the night) is good biblical Hebrew (Exodus 14:24, Judges 7:19, Lamentations 2:19). However, the OT knows only three watches, while the Roman system used by Mark has four. Delitzsch correctly uses the biblical term but applies it to the Roman system, as required by the text.

### 6:50 — אֲנִי הוּא (I am he / ego sum)
- **Hebrew**: חִזְקוּ כִּי-אֲנִי הוּא אַל-תִּירָאוּ
- **Note**: The phrase אֲנִי הוּא carries enormous theological weight in Hebrew, echoing the divine self-identification in Isaiah 41:4, 43:10, 43:25, 46:4, 48:12. What could be read as simple self-identification ("It's me") in Greek/Latin resonates in Hebrew as a divine name formula. Delitzsch preserves this ambiguity faithfully.

## Other Notes

### 6:34 — Numbers 27:17 / Ezekiel 34
The shepherd-sheep imagery connects to Moses' prayer in Numbers 27:17 (requesting a successor so Israel not be "like sheep without a shepherd") and Ezekiel 34 (God's indictment of Israel's false shepherds and promise to shepherd his flock himself). Jesus acting as the shepherd who teaches and feeds fulfills Ezekiel 34:15: "I myself will be the shepherd of my sheep."

### 6:41-44 — Feeding of the five thousand (2 Kings 4:42-44)
The multiplication of loaves echoes Elisha's feeding miracle in 2 Kings 4:42-44. Elisha fed 100 with 20 barley loaves; Jesus feeds 5,000 with 5 loaves. The Hebrew vocabulary (וַיִּפְרֹס, loaves, baskets) anchors the narrative in the OT prophetic tradition. The surplus of 12 baskets (שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר סַלִּים) suggests the twelve tribes.

### 6:48-50 — Walking on the sea (Job 9:8, Psalm 77:20)
God alone "treads on the waves of the sea" (Job 9:8: דּוֹרֵךְ עַל-בָּמֳתֵי יָם). The walking-on-water episode, combined with the "I am he" declaration, constitutes a theophany in the fullest OT sense.
