# Mark Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 15:28 — Verse present in Vulgate, absent from SBLGNT
- **Issue**: Mark 15:28 is present in the Vulgate and in the Delitzsch Hebrew but has no Greek text in the SBLGNT (the greek field is empty). The verse quotes Isaiah 53:12.
- **Vulgate**: *Et impleta est Scriptura, quae dicit: Et cum iniquis reputatus est.*
- **Greek**: (absent from SBLGNT main text)
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּמָּלֵא הַכָּתוּב הָאֹמֵר וְאֶת-פּשְׁעִים נִמְנָה
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. This verse is part of the Catholic canon. The Hebrew correctly includes it. The verse is attested in Codex Alexandrinus, many Byzantine manuscripts, and the Old Latin tradition.

All other 46 verses (1-27, 29-47) align across all three sources.

## Content Alignment

### 15:29 — "Templum Dei" (Temple of God) vs "the Temple"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "templum Dei" (temple of God). The SBLGNT reads "ton naon" (the temple) without "tou theou." The Hebrew reads הַהֵיכָל (the Temple) without "of God."
- **Vulgate**: *Vah! qui destruis templum Dei*
- **Hebrew**: הֶאָח אַתָּה הַהוֹרֵס אֶת-הַהֵיכָל
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate in omitting "of God." It should be corrected to: הַהוֹרֵס אֶת-הֵיכַל הָאֱלֹהִים.

### 15:34 — Eloi/Eli and the cry of dereliction
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Eloi, eloi, lamma sabacthani" and interprets it as "Deus meus, Deus meus, ut quid dereliquisti me." The Greek has "Eloi eloi lema sabachthani." The Hebrew presents the Aramaic as אֱלָהִי אֱלָהִי לְמָה שְׁבַקְתַּנִי and then provides the Hebrew interpretation: אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי.
- **Vulgate**: *Eloi, eloi, lamma sabacthani... Deus meus, Deus meus, ut quid dereliquisti me?*
- **Hebrew**: Aramaic: אֱלָהִי אֱלָהִי לְמָה שְׁבַקְתַּנִי / Hebrew: אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי
- **Note**: The Hebrew interpretation is Psalm 22:2 (22:1 in English) quoted verbatim from the Masoretic text. Delitzsch presents both the Aramaic form and the original Hebrew of the Psalm. This is an excellent handling of the multilingual text. The Vulgate uses the Aramaic form; the Hebrew correctly provides both.

### 15:39 — "Quia sic clamans expirasset" (because crying out thus he expired)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "sic clamans" (thus crying out) as the reason the centurion was moved. The SBLGNT reads "houtos exepneusen" (thus he expired). The Hebrew reads כִּי בְּזַעֲקוֹ כָכָה יָצְאָה נַפְשׁוֹ (because crying out thus his soul departed).
- **Vulgate**: *quia sic clamans expirasset*
- **Hebrew**: כִּי בְּזַעֲקוֹ כָכָה יָצְאָה נַפְשׁוֹ
- **Note**: Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate in specifying the manner of death (crying out). No change needed.

### 15:43 — "Nobilis decurio" (noble councillor)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "nobilis decurio" (a noble councillor/senator). The Greek reads "euschemon bouleutes" (a respected councillor). The Hebrew reads יוֹעֵץ נִכְבָּד (an honored adviser).
- **Vulgate**: *nobilis decurio*
- **Hebrew**: יוֹעֵץ נִכְבָּד
- **Note**: The Hebrew יוֹעֵץ (adviser/counselor) is appropriate for "decurio" (a member of the council). The adjective נִכְבָּד (honored) matches "nobilis." Adequate rendering.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 15:17 — אַרְגָּמָן (purple)
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּלְבִּישֻׁהוּ אַרְגָּמָן
- **Note**: The term אַרְגָּמָן is the biblical Hebrew word for purple/crimson (Exodus 25:4, Judges 8:26, Proverbs 31:22). It is associated with royalty and wealth in the OT. The mockery of Jesus with a purple robe is well served by this word, which evokes the Tabernacle hangings and kingly garments.

### 15:22 — גָּלְגָּלְתָּא (Golgotha)
- **Hebrew**: גָּלְגָּלְתָּא הַמָּקוֹם הוּא מְקוֹם הַגֻּלְגֹּלֶת
- **Note**: Delitzsch provides the Aramaic transliteration גָּלְגָּלְתָּא followed by the Hebrew explanation מְקוֹם הַגֻּלְגֹּלֶת (place of the skull). The Hebrew גֻּלְגֹּלֶת appears in Judges 9:53 and 2 Kings 9:35. This dual-language approach mirrors the original text's translation practice.

### 15:28 — וְאֶת-פּשְׁעִים נִמְנָה (and with transgressors he was numbered)
- **Hebrew**: וְאֶת-פּשְׁעִים נִמְנָה
- **Note**: This is Isaiah 53:12 quoted nearly verbatim from the Masoretic text. The original reads: וְאֶת-פֹּשְׁעִים נִמְנָה. Delitzsch's Hebrew has a minor spelling variant (פּשְׁעִים vs. פֹּשְׁעִים), but the quote is recognizable and strong. The connection between the Suffering Servant and the crucifixion is made explicit.

### 15:34 — אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי (My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?)
- **Hebrew**: אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי
- **Note**: Verbatim from Psalm 22:2 (MT). This is the most important OT quotation in the Passion narrative. Delitzsch correctly presents both the Aramaic spoken form and the Hebrew Psalm source. The Hebrew form is flawless.

### 15:38 — פָרֹכֶת הַהֵיכָל (the veil of the Temple)
- **Hebrew**: וּפָרֹכֶת הַהֵיכָל נִקְרְעָה לִשְׁנַיִם קְרָעִים מִלְּמַעְלָה לְמָטָּה
- **Note**: The word פָרֹכֶת is the exact term for the Tabernacle/Temple curtain (Exodus 26:31-33, 2 Chronicles 3:14). It refers to the veil separating the Holy Place from the Holy of Holies. The verb נִקְרְעָה (was torn) is powerful; the same root ק-ר-ע is used for the rending of garments in mourning (cf. 14:63 where the high priest tears his garments). The parallel between the priest tearing his clothing and God tearing the Temple veil is striking in the Hebrew.

## Other Notes

### 15:34 — Aramaic vs Hebrew in the cry
The Vulgate's "Eloi" is the Aramaic form. Matthew 27:46 has "Eli" (Hebrew). The bystanders in 15:35 think Jesus calls Elijah (אֵלִיָּהוּ), which only works as a mishearing if the Aramaic "Eloi" is spoken. The Hebrew text handles this well by presenting the Aramaic first and then the Hebrew Psalm interpretation, preserving the phonetic confusion that drives the Elijah misunderstanding.

### 15:42-46 — Burial chronology
The Vulgate's "parasceve, quod est ante sabbatum" is rendered as עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת (Sabbath eve), which is the standard Jewish term for Friday. Delitzsch naturally uses the Hebrew calendrical vocabulary. The term is attested in later Hebrew literature and would have been immediately understood in a Jewish context.
