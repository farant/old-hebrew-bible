# Mark Chapter 12 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 44 verses align across sources. No numbering mismatches or absent verses.

## Content Alignment

### 12:4 — "Stoned" (lapidaverunt) — bracketed Hebrew addition
- **Issue**: The Hebrew text for 12:4 includes a bracketed phrase: [סָקְלוּ בָאֲבָנִים וּ] (they stoned him with stones and...) before מָחֲצוּ רֹאשׁוֹ. Neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT mentions stoning. The Vulgate reads "illum in capite vulneraverunt" (wounded him in the head). The Greek uses *ekephaliosan* (struck on the head).
- **Vulgate**: *et illum in capite vulneraverunt, et contumeliis affecerunt.*
- **Greek**: *kakeinon ekephalaiosan kai etimasan*
- **Hebrew**: [סָקְלוּ בָאֲבָנִים וּ] מָחֲצוּ רֹאשׁוֹ (bracketed stoning + struck his head)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The bracketed stoning addition should be removed, as Delitzsch himself flagged it as uncertain and it has no basis in the Vulgate or SBLGNT.

### 12:23 — "Cum resurrexerint" (when they shall rise)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate and Hebrew both include "when they shall rise" (cum resurrexerint / כְּשֶׁיָּקוּמוּ), while the SBLGNT omits the phrase (the critical text marks *hotan anastasin* as a later addition).
- **Vulgate**: *In resurrectione ergo cum resurrexerint*
- **Greek**: *en te anastasei* (in the resurrection — no "when they rise")
- **Hebrew**: בִּתְחִיַּת הַמֵּתִים כְּשֶׁיָּקוּמוּ (in the resurrection of the dead, when they rise)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly includes the clause. No change needed.

### 12:30 — "Hoc est primum mandatum" (This is the first commandment)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate appends "Hoc est primum mandatum" (This is the first commandment) at the end of the Shema quotation. The SBLGNT does not include this phrase — it belongs to verse 29 in the Greek. The Hebrew places it at the end of verse 30: זֹאת הִיא הַמִּצְוָה הָרִאשֹׁנָה.
- **Vulgate**: *...et ex tota virtute tua. Hoc est primum mandatum.*
- **Greek**: Not in v.30
- **Hebrew**: זֹאת הִיא הַמִּצְוָה הָרִאשֹׁנָה
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly appends this summary clause at the end of the love commandment. No change needed.

### 12:33 — Strength and soul order
- **Issue**: The Vulgate lists four elements in 12:33: "ex toto corde, et ex toto intellectu, et ex tota anima, et ex tota fortitudine" (heart, understanding, soul, strength). The Hebrew lists: בְּכָל-לֵבָב וּבְכָל-מֵדָע וּבְכָל-נֶפֶשׁ וּבְכָל-מְאֹד (heart, knowledge, soul, might). The SBLGNT has only three: heart, understanding, strength (omitting soul).
- **Vulgate**: heart, understanding, soul, strength (four)
- **Greek**: heart, understanding, strength (three)
- **Hebrew**: heart, knowledge, soul, might (four)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly includes all four elements, matching the Vulgate. No change needed.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 12:1 — Vineyard parable with Isaiah 5 language
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ אֶחָד נָטַע כֶּרֶם וַיַּעַשׂ גָּדֵר סָבִיב וַיַּחְצֹב יֶקֶב וַיִּבֶן מִגְדָּל
- **Note**: Outstanding. The vineyard description closely echoes Isaiah 5:1-2 (the Song of the Vineyard): כֶּרֶם ... וַיְעַזְּקֵהוּ ... וַיַּחְצְּבֵהוּ ... וַיִּבֶן מִגְדָּל. The verbs נָטַע (planted), יַּחְצֹב (dug/hewn), and יִּבֶן (built), plus the nouns גָּדֵר (wall), יֶקֶב (winepress), and מִגְדָּל (tower) are drawn from the Isaiah passage. A Hebrew reader would immediately recognize the prophetic allusion.

### 12:10-11 — Psalm 118:22-23 verbatim
- **Hebrew**: אֶבֶן מָאֲסוּ הַבּוֹנִים הָיְתָה לְרֹאשׁ פִּנָּה מֵאֵת יְהוֹה הָיְתָה זֹּאת הִיא נִפְלָאת בְּעֵינֵינוּ
- **Note**: This is virtually a word-for-word quotation of Psalm 118:22-23 from the Masoretic text. Delitzsch correctly reproduces the Psalm source rather than back-translating from the Greek. The result is that a Hebrew reader encounters the same stone/cornerstone passage that would be familiar from liturgical use of the Hallel.

### 12:26 — Exodus 3:6 at the burning bush
- **Hebrew**: אָנֹכִי אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם וֵאלֹהֵי יִצְחָק וֵאלֹהֵי יַעֲקֹב
- **Note**: Closely follows the Masoretic text of Exodus 3:6. The use of אָנֹכִי (I) rather than אֲנִי matches the Exodus original. The three-fold repetition of אֱלֹהֵי with the patriarchal names is preserved exactly. Excellent OT fidelity.

### 12:29-30 — Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-5)
- **Hebrew**: שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל יְהוֹה אֱלֹהֵינוּ יְהוֹה אֶחָד וְאָהַבְתָּ אֵת יְהוֹה אֱלֹהֶיךָ בְּכָל-לְבָבְךָ וּבְכָל-נַפְשְׁךָ וּבְכָל-מַדָּעֲךָ וּבְכָל-מְאֹדֶךָ
- **Note**: The Shema is rendered in its Deuteronomic form. The first line (שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל) matches Deuteronomy 6:4 exactly. The love commandment tracks Deuteronomy 6:5 closely, with the addition of מַדָּעֲךָ (your knowledge/mind) to account for the Greek *dianoia* / Latin *mente* — a fourth element not in the MT of Deuteronomy. This expansion is necessary because the NT text adds "mind" to the Deuteronomic triad of heart, soul, and strength.

### 12:36 — Psalm 110:1 (David's Lord)
- **Hebrew**: נְאֻם יְהוֹה לַאֲדֹנִי שֵׁב לִימִינִי עַד-אָשִׁית אֹיְבֶיךָ הֲדֹם לְרַגְלֶיךָ
- **Note**: Excellent. This is a near-verbatim quotation of Psalm 110:1 from the Masoretic text. The word נְאֻם (oracle/utterance of) is the prophetic formula marker. The theological force of the argument — David calls the Messiah "my Lord" (אֲדֹנִי), so how can the Messiah be merely David's son? — is far more vivid in Hebrew where the distinction between יְהוֹה (the LORD) and אֲדֹנִי (my lord/master) is immediately apparent, whereas in Greek both are rendered *kyrios*.

### 12:31 — Leviticus 19:18
- **Hebrew**: וְאָהַבְתָּ לְרֵעֲךָ כָּמוֹךָ
- **Note**: Verbatim quotation of Leviticus 19:18. No adaptation needed. The Hebrew reader encounters the Torah source text directly.

## Other Notes

### 12:26 — God of the living (Trinitarian/Christological)
Jesus's argument for the resurrection from the burning bush passage (Exodus 3:6) is a Christological reading of Torah: the God who speaks to Moses declares himself the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — who are therefore alive, not dead. The Hebrew rendering allows the reader to see this argument in its original scriptural language, strengthening the exegetical force.

### 12:29-31 — The Great Commandment as Torah synthesis
The combination of Deuteronomy 6:4-5 (love of God) and Leviticus 19:18 (love of neighbor) as a summary of all commandments is presented in the Hebrew with both source texts in their original Masoretic form. This makes the passage function as a kind of Torah anthology — the reader hears Moses directly through the voice of Christ.

### 12:35-37 — The "Lord's Lord" argument
This pericope depends entirely on the Hebrew text of Psalm 110:1 for its force. The YHWH/Adonai distinction — "The LORD said to my Lord" — is the crux of the argument, and it only works fully in Hebrew (or Aramaic). In Greek, both become *kyrios*, requiring explanation. The Hebrew NT restores the original clarity of the argument.
