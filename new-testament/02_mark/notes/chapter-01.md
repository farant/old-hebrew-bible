# Mark Chapter 1 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 45 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 1:1 — "Son of God" (Filii Dei)
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT text ends at "Christou" with an apparatus mark, reflecting the text-critical debate over whether "Son of God" (huiou theou) is original. The Vulgate and the Hebrew both include "Son of God."
- **Vulgate**: *Initium Evangelii Jesu Christi, Filii Dei.*
- **Greek**: Ends at *christou* with no *huiou theou* in the main text line
- **Hebrew**: תְּחִלַּת בְּשׂוֹרַת יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ בֶּן-הָאֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly includes "Son of God." The phrase is attested in Sinaiticus, Vaticanus, and the vast majority of manuscripts. Its absence in a few witnesses is likely scribal oversight.

### 1:2 — "In Isaiah the prophet" vs "In the prophets"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate and Greek both read "in Isaiah the prophet" (in Isaia propheta / en to Esaia to prophete). Delitzsch's Hebrew reads בַּנְּבִיאִים ("in the prophets," plural), following the Byzantine/TR reading, not the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *Sicut scriptum est in Isaia propheta*
- **Greek**: *en to Esaia to prophete* (in Isaiah the prophet)
- **Hebrew**: בַּנְּבִיאִים (in the prophets)
- **Note**: The Hebrew diverges from both the Vulgate and SBLGNT. Since the quotation that follows actually combines Malachi 3:1 and Isaiah 40:3, the plural "prophets" avoids an apparent difficulty, but the Vulgate reading "in Isaiah the prophet" must be followed. The Hebrew should read בְּיְשַׁעְיָהוּ הַנָּבִיא.

### 1:13 — "Forty days and forty nights"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "et quadraginta noctibus" (and forty nights), harmonizing with Matthew 4:2. Neither the Greek nor the Hebrew mentions nights.
- **Vulgate**: *quadraginta diebus, et quadraginta noctibus*
- **Greek**: *tesserakonta hemeras* (forty days only)
- **Hebrew**: אַרְבָּעִים יוֹם (forty days only)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew should add וְאַרְבָּעִים לָיְלָה to match "and forty nights."

### 1:14 — "Evangelium regni Dei" (Gospel of the kingdom of God)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Evangelium regni Dei" (gospel of the kingdom of God). The Greek reads "euangelion tou theou" (gospel of God) without "regni." The Hebrew follows the Vulgate with בְּשׂוֹרַת מַלְכוּת הָאֱלֹהִים (gospel of the kingdom of God).
- **Vulgate**: *Evangelium regni Dei*
- **Greek**: *to euangelion tou theou* (the gospel of God)
- **Hebrew**: בְּשׂוֹרַת מַלְכוּת הָאֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly aligns with the Vulgate here, adding "kingdom" (מַלְכוּת). No change needed.

### 1:41 — "Moved with compassion" vs "Angry"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT reads *orgistheis* (being angry), a lectio difficilior found in Codex Bezae. The Vulgate reads *misertus ejus* (having compassion on him). The Hebrew follows the Vulgate with וַיְרַחֵם (and he had compassion).
- **Vulgate**: *Jesus autem misertus ejus*
- **Greek**: *orgistheis* (being angry) -- SBLGNT follows the difficult reading
- **Hebrew**: וַיְרַחֵם עָלָיו יֵשׁוּעַ (Jesus had compassion on him)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew is correct. The "angry" reading is a peculiarity of the critical text and is not followed by any Catholic tradition.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 1:3 — Excellent Isaiah 40:3 allusion
- **Hebrew**: קוֹל קוֹרֵא בַמִּדְבָּר פַּנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ יְהוָֹה יַשְּׁרוּ מְסִלּוֹתָיו
- **Note**: Excellent. Delitzsch closely follows the Masoretic text of Isaiah 40:3, using פַּנּוּ דֶּרֶךְ and מְסִלּוֹתָיו. The only adaptation is replacing the Isaianic אֱלֹהֵינוּ with the pronoun suffix to fit the NT context. Strong biblical Hebrew.

### 1:4 — טְבִילַת הַתְּשׁוּבָה (baptism of repentance)
- **Hebrew**: טְבִילַת הַתְּשׁוּבָה לִסְלִיחַת חֲטָאִים
- **Note**: The noun טְבִילָה (immersion) is post-biblical/Mishnaic. Biblical Hebrew would not have a specific noun for this rite. However, there is no clean biblical alternative, and the root ט-ב-ל is used in the OT (2 Kings 5:14 for Naaman). Acceptable as a necessary coinage.

### 1:7 — קָטֹנְתִּי (I am too small)
- **Hebrew**: אֲשֶׁר קָטֹנְתִּי מִלִּכְרֹעַ (I am too small to bow down)
- **Note**: Excellent biblical phrasing. Echoes Genesis 32:11 (Jacob's prayer): קָטֹנְתִּי מִכֹּל הַחֲסָדִים. The Greek uses *hikanos* (worthy/sufficient), and translating with קָטֹנְתִּי rather than a mechanical "I am not worthy" is an inspired choice that gives the passage deep OT resonance.

### 1:10 — הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהִנֵּה נִפְתְּחוּ (the heavens, and behold, they were opened)
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּרְא הַשָּׁמַיִם וְהִנֵּה נִפְתְּחוּ
- **Note**: The Greek/Vulgate use *schizomenous/apertos* (being torn/opened). The Hebrew uses נִפְתְּחוּ (were opened), matching Matthew's version rather than Mark's more vivid "torn" (schizomenous). Mark's σχιζομένους echoes the tearing of the veil in 15:38 and is theologically significant. Consider נִקְרְעוּ (from the root ק-ר-ע, to tear) to preserve Mark's distinctive language.

### 1:22 — תּוֹרָתוֹ for "teaching"
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּשְׁתּוֹמֲמוּ עַל-תּוֹרָתוֹ
- **Note**: Using תּוֹרָה for "teaching/doctrine" is semantically possible but carries heavy theological weight in Hebrew (it is THE Torah). The Greek is *didache* and the Latin *doctrina*. A less loaded term like מוּסָרוֹ or לִמּוּדוֹ might avoid the implication that Jesus was teaching Torah in the technical sense, though the word choice may be deliberately provocative.

## Other Notes

### 1:2-3 — Composite OT quotation
Mark 1:2 quotes Malachi 3:1 (הִנְנִי שֹׁלֵחַ מַלְאָכִי) and 1:3 quotes Isaiah 40:3 (קוֹל קוֹרֵא בַמִּדְבָּר). The Hebrew of both closely tracks the Masoretic originals, which is fitting since these are direct OT citations. The attribution to Isaiah alone (in the Vulgate) follows the ancient practice of attributing composite quotations to the more prominent prophet.

### 1:11 — Voice from heaven (Trinitarian)
The Father's voice declares "Tu es Filius meus dilectus" / אַתָּה בְּנִי יְדִידִי. This is the first Person of the Trinity speaking, identifying the second Person, while the Spirit descends (1:10). Mark's baptism narrative is the most explicit Trinitarian theophany in the Synoptics.
