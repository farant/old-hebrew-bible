# Mark Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification

The Vulgate includes verses 9:44 and 9:46, which repeat the refrain "ubi vermis eorum non moritur, et ignis non extinguitur" (where their worm does not die and the fire is not quenched). These verses are absent from the SBLGNT (the Greek field is empty for both). The Delitzsch Hebrew includes them with identical text: אֲשֶׁר-שָׁם תּוֹלַעְתָּם לֹא תָמוּת וְאִשָּׁם לֹא תִכְבֶּה. Follow the Vulgate: these verses are retained.

Note: The Vulgate numbering is offset from the SBLGNT in the early part of the chapter. The Vulgate places 8:39-9:1 differently than the Greek, such that Vulgate 9:1 = SBLGNT 9:2 (the Transfiguration), and SBLGNT 9:1 (the "some standing here" saying) belongs to the end of chapter 8 in the Vulgate. The data file maps Vulgate Latin for 9:1 ("Et post dies sex assumit Jesus Petrum...") to SBLGNT 9:1, which is actually Greek 9:2. This one-verse offset runs through approximately 9:1 and resolves. The Hebrew tracks with the Greek numbering throughout.

## Content Alignment

### 9:1 — Verse boundary offset (Transfiguration)
- **Issue**: The data shows a mismatch. The Greek field for verse 1 contains the "some standing here shall not taste death" saying (Greek 9:1 / Vulgate 8:39). But the Latin field for the same record contains the Transfiguration opening ("Et post dies sex assumit Jesus Petrum..."), which is Vulgate 9:2. This is a versification mapping issue in the data, not a textual problem.
- **Vulgate**: 9:1 = "Et post dies sex assumit Jesus Petrum..."
- **Greek**: 9:1 = The "taste death" saying (which the Vulgate places at end of ch. 8)
- **Note**: The content of all three traditions is present; only the verse numbering differs. No action required beyond awareness.

### 9:29 — "In prayer and fasting"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in oratione et jejunio" (in prayer and fasting). The SBLGNT reads only "en proseuche" (in prayer), omitting fasting. The Hebrew includes both: בִּתְפִלָּה וּבְצוֹם.
- **Vulgate**: *Hoc genus in nullo potest exire, nisi in oratione et jejunio.*
- **Greek**: *en proseuche* (in prayer only)
- **Hebrew**: בִּתְפִלָּה וּבְצוֹם (in prayer and in fasting)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly includes fasting. The critical text omits "and fasting" as a suspected ascetic interpolation, but the Vulgate (and the broad manuscript tradition) retains it.

### 9:49 — "Et omnis victima sale salietur"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds a second clause: "et omnis victima sale salietur" (and every sacrifice shall be salted with salt). The SBLGNT has only the first clause, "pas gar pyri halisthesetai" (for everyone shall be salted with fire). The Hebrew includes both clauses: כִּי כָל-אִישׁ בָּאֵשׁ יָמְלָח וְכָל-קָרְבָּן בַּמֶּלַח יָמְלָח.
- **Vulgate**: *Omnis enim igne salietur, et omnis victima sale salietur.*
- **Greek**: First clause only
- **Hebrew**: Both clauses present
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew correctly includes the sacrificial allusion (Leviticus 2:13). No change needed.

### 9:50 — Absent Vulgate Latin
- **Issue**: The Latin field for 9:50 is empty in the data file ("latin": ""), though the verse exists in the Clementine Vulgate. The Greek and Hebrew are both present and correspond to Vulgate 9:50 ("Bonum est sal...").
- **Note**: This is a data gap. The Vulgate text should be: "Bonum est sal : quod si sal insulsum fuerit, in quo illud condietis ? Habete in vobis sal, et pacem habete inter vos." The Hebrew and Greek are correct.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:3 — כַּשָּׁלֶג (like snow)
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּהְיוּ לְבָנִים מְאֹד כַּשָּׁלֶג
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "velut nix" (like snow) and the Greek has no snow simile (only "lian" = exceedingly). The Hebrew follows the Vulgate's snow comparison. Good alignment with the Vulgate tradition.

### 9:5 — סֻכּוֹת (tabernacles/booths)
- **Hebrew**: נַעֲשֶׂה-נָּא שָׁלֹשׁ סֻכּוֹת
- **Note**: Excellent. The Greek *skenas* and Latin *tabernacula* are rendered with the precise biblical Hebrew term סֻכּוֹת, evoking the Feast of Tabernacles (Leviticus 23:42). This is exactly the right word and carries rich OT resonance.

### 9:7 — בְּנִי יְדִידִי (my beloved son)
- **Hebrew**: זֶה בְּנִי יְדִידִי אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן
- **Note**: Strong biblical Hebrew. The Father's voice at the Transfiguration echoes the Baptism (1:11). The word יְדִידִי for "beloved" (Latin *carissimus*, Greek *agapetos*) has excellent OT precedent (cf. Psalm 127:2, Jedidiah in 2 Samuel 12:25). The command אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן deliberately echoes Deuteronomy 18:15 (the prophet like Moses: אֵלָיו תִּשְׁמָעוּן).

### 9:24 — אֲנִי מַאֲמִין אֲדֹנִי עֲזָר-נָא לְחֶסְרוֹן אֱמוּנָתִי
- **Hebrew**: I believe, my Lord; help the lack of my faith
- **Note**: The Vulgate adds "Domine" (Lord), absent from the SBLGNT Greek. The Hebrew includes אֲדֹנִי, correctly following the Vulgate. The phrase חֶסְרוֹן אֱמוּנָתִי (lack of my faith) is a clean calque of the Greek *apistia* / Latin *incredulitas*.

### 9:42 — פֶלַח-רֶכֶב (millstone)
- **Hebrew**: שֶׁיִּתָּלֶה פֶלַח-רֶכֶב עַל-צַוָּארוֹ
- **Note**: The compound פֶלַח-רֶכֶב (literally "piece of the upper millstone") is drawn from Judges 9:53 (the death of Abimelech). Excellent biblical vocabulary. The Greek *mylos onikos* (donkey-millstone) is a large commercial stone, and the Hebrew פֶלַח-רֶכֶב captures the same sense.

### 9:48 — Isaiah 66:24 allusion
- **Hebrew**: אֲשֶׁר-שָׁם תּוֹלַעְתָּם לֹא תָמוּת וְאִשָּׁם לֹא תִכְבֶּה
- **Note**: This closely follows the Masoretic text of Isaiah 66:24: כִּי תוֹלַעְתָּם לֹא תָמוּת וְאִשָּׁם לֹא תִכְבֶּה. Delitzsch appropriately reuses the prophetic source text. The only minor difference is the prefixed אֲשֶׁר-שָׁם (where/there) to adapt it from a standalone prophecy to a relative clause.

## Other Notes

### 9:7 — Trinitarian theophany
The Transfiguration is a Trinitarian revelation: the Father speaks from the cloud (9:7), the Son is transfigured (9:2-3), and the cloud (שְׁכִינָה / divine presence) overshadows them. The Father's declaration "This is my beloved Son; hear him" identifies the second Person and commands obedience to him. The Hebrew renders the Father's voice with strong Deuteronomic language.

### 9:44, 9:46 — Vulgate-only refrain verses
These verses, repeating the Isaiah 66:24 refrain after the hand (9:43) and foot (9:45) warnings respectively, are absent from critical Greek editions but present in the Vulgate and the received text tradition. They create a solemn threefold repetition (9:44, 9:46, 9:48) that reinforces the eschatological warning. The Hebrew faithfully reproduces all three occurrences with identical text.
