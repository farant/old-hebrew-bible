# Mark Chapter 5 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 43 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 5:1 — Gerasenes vs Gadarenes
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Gerasenorum," the Greek (SBLGNT) reads "Gerasenon" (with apparatus mark), and the Hebrew reads הַגַּדְרִיִּם (the Gadarenes). The three-way geographic variant (Gerasa, Gadara, Gergesa) is one of the most famous textual problems in the Synoptics.
- **Vulgate**: *in regionem Gerasenorum* (Gerasenes)
- **Greek**: *Gerasenon* (Gerasenes)
- **Hebrew**: הַגַּדְרִיִּם (Gadarenes)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Byzantine/TR reading (Gadarenes), not the Vulgate (Gerasenes). Since we follow the Vulgate, the Hebrew should read הַגְּרָסִיִּם or a similar transliteration of Gerasa.

### 5:19 — "Dominus" (the Lord) vs "Jesus"
- **Issue**: Both the Vulgate and Greek 5:19 say "the Lord" (Dominus / kyrios) did great things for him. The Hebrew correctly uses יְהוָֹה. But in 5:20, all three sources switch to "Jesus" for the man's preaching. This Lord/Jesus interchange is theologically significant.
- **Vulgate 5:19**: *quanta tibi Dominus fecerit* (what the Lord has done for you)
- **Hebrew 5:19**: אֶת-הַגְּדֹלוֹת אֲשֶׁר-עָשָׂה לְךָ יְהוָֹה (the great things YHWH did for you)
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly renders "Lord" as YHWH. Jesus tells the man to proclaim what "the Lord" (YHWH) did, but the man goes and tells what "Jesus" did (5:20). This implicit identification of Jesus with YHWH is preserved in the Hebrew.

### 5:41 — Talitha cumi
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Talitha cumi" and glosses it "Puella (tibi dico), surge." The Greek glosses it "To korasion, soi lego, egeire." The Hebrew renders the Aramaic as טַלְיְתָא קוּמִי and glosses it יַלְדָּה אֲנִי אֹמֵר לָךְ קוּמִי נָא.
- **Vulgate**: *Talitha cumi, quod est interpretatum: Puella (tibi dico), surge*
- **Hebrew**: טַלְיְתָא קוּמִי פֵּרוּשׁוֹ יַלְדָּה אֲנִי אֹמֵר לָךְ קוּמִי נָא
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds נָא ("please/I pray") which is not in the Vulgate or Greek. The Aramaic original is preserved. The added נָא is a Delitzsch flourish that softens the command, though the Vulgate's tone is simply imperative.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 5:7 — מַה-לִי וָלָךְ (What is between me and you?)
- **Hebrew**: מַה-לִי וָלָךְ יֵשׁוּעַ בֶּן-אֵל עֶלְיוֹן
- **Note**: Excellent. The formula מַה-לִי וָלָךְ is biblical, appearing in Judges 11:12, 2 Samuel 16:10, 1 Kings 17:18, and 2 Kings 3:13. It is the standard OT expression of protest or disassociation. Delitzsch uses genuine biblical idiom rather than a mechanical translation.

### 5:9 — לִגְיוֹן (Legion)
- **Hebrew**: לִגְיוֹן שְׁמִי כִּי-רַבִּים אֲנָחְנוּ
- **Note**: The Latin loanword "Legio" is transliterated as לִגְיוֹן, which is the standard Talmudic/Midrashic form. There is no biblical Hebrew equivalent. The choice is appropriate and matches rabbinic Hebrew convention.

### 5:23 — אָנָּא בוֹא-נָא (Please, come please)
- **Hebrew**: אָנָּא בוֹא-נָא וְשִׂים יָדֶיךָ עָלֶיהָ
- **Note**: The double supplication particle (אָנָּא...נָא) is a biblical pattern for urgent, reverent pleading. Compare Exodus 32:31-32 (Moses), 2 Kings 20:3 (Hezekiah). Good biblical Hebrew capturing the emotional weight of a father's desperation.

### 5:34 — בִּתִּי אֱמוּנָתֵךְ הוֹשִׁיעָה לָּךְ (Daughter, your faith has saved you)
- **Hebrew**: בִּתִּי אֱמוּנָתֵךְ הוֹשִׁיעָה לָּךְ לְכִי לְשָׁלוֹם וְחָיִית מִנִּגְעֵךְ
- **Note**: The root י-שׁ-ע (save/deliver) correctly captures the double meaning of the Greek "sesoken" (healed/saved) and the Latin "salvam fecit." The phrase לְכִי לְשָׁלוֹם echoes David's dismissal formula (1 Samuel 1:17, 20:42; 2 Samuel 15:9). Strong biblical Hebrew with appropriate register for divine pronouncement.

## Other Notes

### 5:19-20 — Lord/Jesus identification
Jesus says "tell what the Lord (YHWH) did," and the man tells what "Jesus" did. This is one of Mark's most significant implicit Christological statements. The Hebrew preserves this by using יְהוָֹה in v.19 and יֵשׁוּעַ in v.20, making the identification even more striking in a Semitic context.

### 5:25-34 — Levitical purity context
The woman with a flow of blood (זוֹב דָּמֶיהָ) evokes Leviticus 15:25-30. The Hebrew term זוֹב is the exact Levitical term for bodily discharge causing ritual impurity. Delitzsch's use of this word connects the healing directly to the Torah purity system, emphasizing that Jesus' touch reverses impurity rather than contracting it.
