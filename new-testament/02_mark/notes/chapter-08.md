# Mark Chapter 8 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 8:38-39 — Vulgate includes 9:1 as 8:39
- **Issue**: Mark 8:39 in our data has Vulgate text ("Et dicebat illis: Amen dico vobis, quia sunt quidam de hic stantibus, qui non gustabunt mortem donec videant regnum Dei veniens in virtute") but empty Greek and Hebrew fields. This verse corresponds to Mark 9:1 in modern critical editions and in the Greek/Hebrew traditions. The Vulgate numbers it as 8:39.
- **Vulgate 8:39**: *Et dicebat illis: Amen dico vobis, quia sunt quidam de hic stantibus, qui non gustabunt mortem donec videant regnum Dei veniens in virtute.*
- **Greek 8:39**: (empty -- this content appears as 9:1 in SBLGNT)
- **Hebrew 8:39**: (empty -- this content appears as 9:1 in Delitzsch)
- **Note**: This is a genuine versification mismatch between the Vulgate and Greek/Hebrew traditions. The Vulgate places this saying at the end of chapter 8, while the Greek tradition places it at the beginning of chapter 9. The Hebrew text for this verse needs to be supplied from the 9:1 data (or composed). Since we follow the Vulgate, 8:39 must be populated.

## Content Alignment

### 8:17 — "caecatum" (blinded) vs "peporomenen" (hardened)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "adhuc caecatum habetis cor vestrum" (is your heart still blinded). The Greek reads "peporomenen echete ten kardian hymon" (is your heart hardened). The Hebrew reads וְלִבְּכֶם עוֹדֶנּוּ קָשֶׁה (and your heart is still hard), using קָשֶׁה (hard) rather than עִוֵּר (blind).
- **Vulgate**: *caecatum* (blinded)
- **Greek**: *peporomenen* (hardened/petrified)
- **Hebrew**: קָשֶׁה (hard)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek sense (hardened) rather than the Vulgate (blinded). Since we follow the Vulgate, consider עוֹדֶנּוּ עִוֵּר or a similar form reflecting blindness rather than hardness. However, the Vulgate "caecatum" (blinded) applied to a heart is metaphorical, and "hardened" is a reasonable Hebraic equivalent (cf. Exodus 7:3, Pharaoh's hardened heart). This is a judgment call.

### 8:26 — "si in vicum introieris, nemini dixeris" (expanded command)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Vade in domum tuam: et si in vicum introieris, nemini dixeris" (Go to your house; and if you enter the village, tell no one). The Greek reads simply "Mede eis ten komen eiselthes" (Do not even enter the village). The Hebrew has a bracketed addition: [וְאַל-תְּדַבֵּר לְאִישׁ בַּכְּפָר] ("and do not speak to anyone in the village").
- **Vulgate**: *Vade in domum tuam: et si in vicum introieris, nemini dixeris*
- **Greek**: *Mede eis ten komen eiselthes* (Do not enter the village)
- **Hebrew**: אַל-תָּבֹא אֶל-תּוֹךְ הַכְּפָר [וְאַל-תְּדַבֵּר לְאִישׁ בַּכְּפָר]
- **Note**: The bracketed Hebrew addition matches the Vulgate's "nemini dixeris." Since the Vulgate includes this command, the brackets should be removed. However, the Hebrew's primary command ("do not enter the village") follows the Greek, while the Vulgate restructures it as a conditional ("if you enter...tell no one"). The Hebrew phrasing should be adjusted to match the Vulgate's conditional structure.

### 8:29 — "Tu es Christus" (You are the Christ)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Tu es Christus" (You are the Christ), without "the Son of the living God" (which Matthew 16:16 includes). The Greek matches: "Su ei ho christos." The Hebrew reads אַתָּה הוּא הַמָּשִׁיחַ (You are the Messiah). All three align.
- **Note**: No divergence. The Hebrew correctly limits the confession to "the Messiah/Christ" without harmonizing with Matthew's fuller form. The pronoun הוּא adds emphasis ("You -- he is -- the Messiah").

### 8:38 — "in gloria Patris sui cum angelis sanctis" (Trinitarian)
- **Issue**: All three sources agree on the eschatological Son of Man coming "in the glory of his Father with the holy angels." The Hebrew reads בִּכְבוֹד אָבִיו עִם-הַמַּלְאָכִים הַקְּדוֹשִׁים.
- **Note**: No divergence. This is an important Trinitarian verse: the Son of Man (the Son) comes in the glory of the Father, accompanied by holy angels (ministering spirits). The Hebrew preserves the relational language.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 8:2 — נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמַי (My compassion is stirred)
- **Hebrew**: נִכְמְרוּ רַחֲמַי עַל-הָעָם
- **Note**: Outstanding. The root כ-מ-ר (to grow warm, be stirred) with רַחֲמִים (compassion) is a striking biblical phrase from Genesis 43:30 (Joseph's compassion for Benjamin) and 1 Kings 3:26 (the true mother's compassion). The Greek "splanchnizomai" (to be moved in the bowels/gut) and the Vulgate "Misereor" (I have compassion) are rendered with this deeply resonant OT phrase. It places Jesus' compassion in the lineage of Joseph and Solomon.

### 8:12 — אִם-יִנָּתֵן אוֹת לַדּוֹר הַזֶּה (If a sign be given to this generation)
- **Hebrew**: אָמֵן אֹמֵר אֲנִי לָכֶם אִם-יִנָּתֵן אוֹת לַדּוֹר הַזֶּה
- **Note**: The אִם construction here functions as a Hebrew oath formula: "If [X happens, may I be cursed]" -- the apodosis is left unsaid for emphasis. This is the standard OT pattern for solemn oaths (cf. Psalm 95:11, 2 Samuel 3:35, Hebrew 3:11). The Greek "ei dothesetai" uses the same Semitic oath pattern. Delitzsch perfectly captures the Hebrew idiom behind the Greek.

### 8:18 — Jeremiah 5:21 / Ezekiel 12:2 echo
- **Hebrew**: עֵינַיִם לָכֶם וְלֹא תִרְאוּ וְאָזְנַיִם לָכֶם וְלֹא תִשְׁמְעוּ
- **Note**: Direct echo of Jeremiah 5:21 (עֵינַיִם לָהֶם וְלֹא יִרְאוּ אָזְנַיִם לָהֶם וְלֹא יִשְׁמָעוּ) and Ezekiel 12:2 (עֵינַיִם לָהֶם לִרְאוֹת וְלֹא רָאוּ אָזְנַיִם לָהֶם לִשְׁמֹעַ וְלֹא שָׁמֵעוּ). Delitzsch adjusts from third person to second person for direct address while preserving the prophetic phrasing. Excellent.

### 8:33 — סוּר מֵעַל פָּנַי הַשָׂטָן (Get behind me, Satan)
- **Hebrew**: סוּר מֵעַל פָּנַי הַשָׂטָן
- **Note**: The Vulgate reads "Vade retro me Satana" (Go behind me, Satan). The Hebrew סוּר מֵעַל פָּנַי literally means "depart from before my face" rather than "go behind me." This subtly changes the meaning: the Vulgate/Greek implies "get back into your place behind me" (a disciple's position), while the Hebrew implies "get away from me." The Vulgate sense should be preserved; consider לֵךְ אַחֲרַי or סוּר לְאָחוֹרַי.

### 8:34 — צְלוּבוֹ (his cross)
- **Hebrew**: וְיִשָׂא אֶת-צְלוּבוֹ
- **Note**: The word צְלוּב (cross) is post-biblical, derived from the root צ-ל-ב (to crucify), which does not appear in the Hebrew Bible. There is no biblical Hebrew word for "cross." Delitzsch's coinage is functional and follows the standard Hebrew NT convention. The root may be connected to the Aramaic צְלַב.

## Other Notes

### 8:22-26 — Two-stage healing (unique to Mark)
The healing of the blind man at Bethsaida is the only healing in the Gospels that occurs in stages. The man first sees "men like trees walking" (אֶרְאֶה אֶת-בְּנֵי הָאָדָם כִּי מִתְהַלְּכִים כְּאִילָנוֹת אֲנִי רֹאֶה), then sees clearly. This gradual restoration is unique to Mark and structurally parallels the disciples' own gradual understanding, positioned as it is between the "blind" disciples of 8:17-21 and Peter's partial confession in 8:29.

### 8:27-30 — Peter's confession at Caesarea Philippi
This is the hinge of Mark's Gospel. The Hebrew הַמָּשִׁיחַ (the Messiah/Anointed One) translates the Greek "ho christos" and the Latin "Christus." In the Hebrew context, this title carries its full OT weight: the anointed Davidic king (Psalm 2:2, 1 Samuel 2:10, Daniel 9:25-26). The immediately following passion prediction (8:31) redefines what "Messiah" means, against Peter's expectations.

### 8:31 — First passion prediction
The Hebrew מִקְצֵה שְׁלשֶׁת יָמִים קוֹם יָקוּם (at the end of three days he shall surely rise) uses the infinitive absolute construction (קוֹם יָקוּם) for emphasis. The Vulgate reads "post tres dies resurgere." The cognate accusative intensifier is a Delitzsch addition that gives the resurrection prediction emphatic force in biblical Hebrew.

### 8:38-39 — Vulgate chapter boundary
The Vulgate tradition places the "some standing here shall not taste death" saying in chapter 8, connecting it to the passion/glory discourse. Modern editions begin chapter 9 with this verse. For consistency with the Vulgate, our text must include 8:39 with appropriate Hebrew and Greek content sourced from 9:1 in those traditions.
