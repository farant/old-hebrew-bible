# Mark Chapter 2 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 28 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 2:16 — "Eats and drinks" (manducat et bibit)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "manducat et bibit Magister vester" (your Master eats and drinks). The Greek mentions only eating (*esthiei*). The Hebrew follows the Vulgate with אֹכֵל וְשֹׁתֶה (eats and drinks).
- **Vulgate**: *Quare cum publicanis et peccatoribus manducat et bibit Magister vester?*
- **Greek**: *hoti meta ton telonon kai hamartolon esthiei* (eats — no mention of drinking)
- **Hebrew**: אֹכֵל וְשֹׁתֶה הוּא (he eats and drinks)
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate by including "and drinks." No change needed.

### 2:17 — "To repentance" (ad poenitentiam) — bracketed in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate does not include "ad poenitentiam" (to repentance) here, and neither does the SBLGNT Greek. However, the Hebrew adds [לִתְשׁוּבָה] in brackets. This addition comes from the parallel in Luke 5:32 and some Byzantine manuscripts.
- **Vulgate**: *non enim veni vocare justos, sed peccatores.*
- **Greek**: *ouk elthon kalesai dikaious alla hamartoulous.*
- **Hebrew**: כִּי אִם-הַחַטָּאִים [לִתְשׁוּבָה]
- **Note**: Since neither the Vulgate nor SBLGNT includes "to repentance" here, the brackets in Delitzsch are appropriate. The word should be removed or retained in brackets to show it is not part of the Markan text. Follow the Vulgate — omit it from the main text.

### 2:23 — "Dominus" (the Lord) in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "Dominus" as the subject: "cum Dominus sabbatis ambularet" (when the Lord walked on the Sabbath). The Greek has simply *auton* (him). The Hebrew does not include "the Lord" either.
- **Vulgate**: *cum Dominus sabbatis ambularet per sata*
- **Greek**: *auton en tois sabbasin paraporeuesthai* (him on the Sabbath passing through)
- **Hebrew**: כְעָבְרוֹ בַּשַּׁבָּת (as he passed on the Sabbath)
- **Note**: Follow the Vulgate. The Hebrew should add a subject referencing "the Lord" — perhaps הָאָדוֹן or use the name explicitly.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 2:4 — Idiomatic narrative construction
- **Hebrew**: וַיָּסִירוּ אֶת-הַגָּג בַּמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר הָיָה שָׁם וַיַּחְתְּרוּ חֲתִירָה
- **Note**: Excellent use of the cognate accusative חֲתִירָה from the root ח-ת-ר (to dig through). This construction (verb + cognate noun) is classic biblical Hebrew intensification. The verb חָתַר appears in Ezekiel 8:8 and 12:7 for digging through walls.

### 2:21 — Parable of the patch
- **Hebrew**: אֵין-אָדָם תֹּפֵר טְלַאי בַד חָדָשׁ עַל-שִׂמְלָה בָלָה
- **Note**: Good biblical vocabulary. שִׂמְלָה (garment) is well-attested biblical Hebrew (Gen 9:23, Ex 22:8). The word טְלַאי (patch) is found in Joshua 9:5. However, תֹּפֵר (sew) in the active participle with אֵין-אָדָם is somewhat awkward. Biblical usage would more naturally use a yiqtol form: לֹא-יִתְפֹּר אִישׁ.

### 2:22 — Wine and wineskins
- **Hebrew**: וְאֵין אָדָם נֹתֵן יַיִן חָדָשׁ בְּנֹאדוֹת בָּלִים
- **Note**: Excellent. נֹאדוֹת (wineskins) is proper biblical Hebrew (Joshua 9:4, 13; Judges 4:19). The entire construction flows naturally in biblical idiom.

### 2:26 — "Sub Abiathar principe sacerdotum"
- **Hebrew**: בִּימֵי אֶבְיָתָר הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל
- **Note**: The Hebrew renders "sub Abiathar" (under Abiathar) as בִּימֵי (in the days of), which is a reasonable interpretation of the Greek *epi Abiathar archiiereos*. The phrase הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל for "high priest" is standard biblical Hebrew (Lev 21:10; 2 Kings 12:11).

### 2:27 — Sabbath for man
- **Hebrew**: הַשַּׁבָּת נִתְּנָה בַּעֲבוּר הָאָדָם וְלֹא הָאָדָם בַּעֲבוּר הַשַּׁבָּת
- **Note**: Clean biblical style using the niphal נִתְּנָה (was given). The preposition בַּעֲבוּר (for the sake of) is well-attested in Genesis 12:13, 20:11, etc. Reads naturally as biblical Hebrew.

## Other Notes

### 2:10 — Son of Man's authority to forgive sins
This is the first explicit "Son of Man" (בֶּן-הָאָדָם) claim in Mark, coupled with the divine prerogative of forgiving sins. The scribes' objection in 2:7 ("Who can forgive sins but God alone?") sets up Jesus' implicit claim to divine authority. The Hebrew preserves the theological force well.

### 2:26 — Historical difficulty with Abiathar
Both the Vulgate ("sub Abiathar principe sacerdotum") and Hebrew (בִּימֵי אֶבְיָתָר) name Abiathar as high priest, but 1 Samuel 21 identifies Ahimelech as the priest who gave David the showbread. Abiathar was Ahimelech's son. This is a well-known harmonization question; the Vulgate reading is authoritative for our purposes.
