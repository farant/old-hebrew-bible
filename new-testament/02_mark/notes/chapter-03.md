# Mark Chapter 3 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 35 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 3:5 — "Blindness" vs "Hardness" of heart
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "super caecitate cordis eorum" (over the blindness of their hearts). The Greek has *porosis* (hardness/callousness). The Hebrew has קְשִׁי לְבָבָם (hardness of their hearts), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *contristatus super caecitate cordis eorum*
- **Greek**: *sullupoumenos epi te porosei tes kardias auton* (grieved at the hardness of their hearts)
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּתְעַצֵּב עַל-קְשִׁי לְבָבָם (he was grieved at the hardness of their hearts)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek "hardness" rather than the Vulgate "blindness." To follow the Vulgate, consider עִוְרוֹן לִבָּם (blindness of their heart). However, this is a minor point; קְשִׁי לְבָבָם is a powerful biblical phrase (Deut 9:27) and theologically unobjectionable.

### 3:11 — "Tu es Filius Dei" (You are the Son of God)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate splits 3:11-12 differently from the Greek. In the Vulgate, 3:12 begins with "Tu es Filius Dei" (You are the Son of God) — the words of the unclean spirits — followed by "Et vehementer comminabatur eis." In the SBLGNT, "Su ei ho huios tou theou" is at the end of verse 11.
- **Vulgate**: 3:11 ends at *dicentes*; 3:12 begins *Tu es Filius Dei. Et vehementer...*
- **Greek**: 3:11 includes *Su ei ho huios tou theou*; 3:12 begins with the silencing command
- **Hebrew**: Verse 11 includes אַתָּה הוּא בֶּן-אֱלֹהִים, following the Greek versification
- **Note**: Minor verse-boundary difference. The content is identical; only the verse break differs. The Hebrew follows the Greek verse boundary rather than the Vulgate's.

### 3:15 — "Curandi infirmitates" (healing infirmities)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "curandi infirmitates et" (healing infirmities and), so the full text reads "gave them power of healing infirmities and casting out demons." The Greek has only *echein exousian ekballein ta daimonia* (to have authority to cast out demons). The Hebrew adds לִרְפֹּא אֶת-הַתַּחֲלֻאִים (to heal the sick), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: *dedit illis potestatem curandi infirmitates et ejiciendi daemonia*
- **Greek**: *echein exousian ekballein ta daimonia* (to have authority to cast out demons)
- **Hebrew**: וְהָיָה לָהֶם הַשָּׁלְטָן לִרְפֹּא אֶת-הַתַּחֲלֻאִים וּלְגָרֵשׁ אֶת-הַשֵּׁדִים
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate in including healing of infirmities. No change needed.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:17 — Boanerges translation
- **Hebrew**: בְּנֵי-רְגוֹשׂ הוּא בְּנֵי-רָעַם
- **Note**: Delitzsch provides a double rendering: בְּנֵי-רְגוֹשׂ as a transliteration of the Aramaic, then בְּנֵי-רָעַם as the Hebrew gloss ("sons of thunder"). The word רַעַם (thunder) is proper biblical Hebrew (Job 26:14; Ps 77:19). The transliteration רְגוֹשׂ is an attempt to represent the Aramaic *r-g-sh* root (agitation, tumult), found in Daniel 6:7, 12, 16 in Aramaic. A reasonable approach.

### 3:21 — יָצָא מִדַּעְתּוֹ (he has gone out of his mind)
- **Hebrew**: כִּי אָמְרוּ יָצָא מִדַּעְתּוֹ
- **Note**: The expression יָצָא מִדַּעְתּוֹ is post-biblical/Rabbinic Hebrew. In biblical Hebrew, madness is expressed differently — cf. 1 Samuel 21:14 (David feigns madness: וַיְשַׁנּוֹ אֶת-טַעְמוֹ) or Ecclesiastes 2:12. The construction "went out of his mind" is natural in later Hebrew but unattested in the biblical corpus.

### 3:22 — בַּעַל-זְבוּל (Beelzebul)
- **Hebrew**: בַּעַל-זְבוּל בּוֹ
- **Note**: The name בַּעַל-זְבוּל could mean "lord of the dwelling" or is a corruption/polemic of בַּעַל-זְבוּב (lord of flies, 2 Kings 1:2). Delitzsch uses the -זְבוּל form reflecting the Greek *Beelzeboul*. The construction with בּוֹ (in him) is idiomatic Hebrew for possession.

### 3:27 — Binding the strong man
- **Hebrew**: Not specifically flagged, but the parable vocabulary (binding/plundering) uses proper biblical terms. The root א-ס-ר (to bind) and the imagery connect to Isaiah 49:24-25 (taking spoil from the mighty).

### 3:29 — "Eternal sin" / "aeterna delicti"
- **Hebrew**: כִּי-עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָׂא לָנֶצַח
- **Note**: Excellent biblical construction. The phrase עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָׂא (he shall bear his iniquity) is classic priestly/legal language from Leviticus (Lev 5:1, 17; 7:18; 17:16; 19:8). Adding לָנֶצַח (forever) powerfully conveys the eternal nature of this sin using pure biblical vocabulary.

## Other Notes

### 3:28-29 — Blasphemy against the Holy Spirit
The Hebrew renders this passage with strong OT legal terminology. The contrast between סְלִיחָה (forgiveness, used in priestly/liturgical contexts) and עֲוֹנוֹ יִשָׂא (bearing iniquity, Levitical penalty formula) gives the passage a legal-covenantal gravity that suits its seriousness.

### 3:14 — "The Twelve" as a Twelve-Tribe echo
The appointment of שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אִישׁ (twelve men) deliberately echoes the twelve tribes of Israel. The Hebrew makes this connection more natural than the Greek, since "twelve men" (שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר אִישׁ) is the standard OT formula for tribal representatives (Josh 3:12; 4:2).
