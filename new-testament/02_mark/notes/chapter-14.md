# Mark Chapter 14 — Translation Notes

## Versification
All 72 verses align across all three sources. No numbering mismatches.

## Content Alignment

### 14:22 — "Sumite" (Take) — Hebrew adds "eat"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Sumite, hoc est corpus meum" (Take, this is my body). The SBLGNT reads "Labete" (Take). The Hebrew reads קְחוּ אִכְלוּ (Take, eat). The addition of "eat" (אִכְלוּ) follows the Matthean parallel (Matthew 26:26, "Accipite, et comedite") rather than Mark's Vulgate text.
- **Vulgate**: *Sumite, hoc est corpus meum*
- **Greek**: *Labete, touto estin to soma mou*
- **Hebrew**: קְחוּ אִכְלוּ זֶה הוּא גּוּפִי
- **Note**: The word אִכְלוּ (eat) should be removed to follow the Vulgate of Mark. It is a harmonization with Matthew.

### 14:24 — "Novi testamenti" (new testament/covenant)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "novi testamenti" (of the new covenant). The SBLGNT reads only "tes diathekes" (of the covenant) without "kaines" (new). The Hebrew includes "new": דַּם-הַבְּרִית הָחֲדָשָׁה (blood of the new covenant).
- **Vulgate**: *Hic est sanguis meus novi testamenti*
- **Hebrew**: זֶה דָמִי דַּם-הַבְּרִית הָחֲדָשָׁה
- **Note**: Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate in including "new." The expression echoes Jeremiah 31:31 (בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה). No change needed.

### 14:27 — "In nocte ista" (in this night)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in me in nocte ista" (in me in this night). The SBLGNT does not include "in this night" (en te nukti taute). The Hebrew includes it: בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה (in this night).
- **Vulgate**: *Omnes scandalizabimini in me in nocte ista*
- **Hebrew**: אַתֶּם כֻּלְּכֶם תִּכָּשְׁלוּ בִי בַּלַּיְלָה הַזֶּה
- **Note**: Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate against the SBLGNT. No change needed.

### 14:41 — "Sufficit" (It is enough)
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Sufficit" rendering the enigmatic Greek "apechei." The Hebrew renders this as רַב-לִי ("it is enough for me" / "I have enough"). The Vulgate "Sufficit" is impersonal, while the Hebrew adds the first-person suffix לִי.
- **Vulgate**: *Sufficit*
- **Greek**: *apechei* (it is enough / the account is settled)
- **Hebrew**: רַב-לִי
- **Note**: The first-person suffix changes the sense slightly. The Vulgate's impersonal "Sufficit" could be rendered as רַב or דַּי without the suffix. Minor nuance difference.

### 14:45 — "Ave Rabbi" vs "Rabbi Rabbi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Ave Rabbi" (Hail, Rabbi). The Greek reads "Rabbi" once. The Hebrew doubles it: רַבִּי רַבִּי (Rabbi, Rabbi).
- **Vulgate**: *Ave Rabbi*
- **Greek**: *Rabbi*
- **Hebrew**: רַבִּי רַבִּי
- **Note**: The doubling in the Hebrew follows no known Vulgate reading. The Hebrew should read שָׁלוֹם רַבִּי or simply render the greeting plus title to match "Ave Rabbi."

### 14:62 — "Ego sum" and "Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "videbitis Filium hominis sedentem a dextris virtutis Dei" (seated at the right hand of the power of God). The SBLGNT reads "tes dynameos" (of the power) without "tou theou" (of God). The Hebrew reads לִימִין הַגְּבוּרָה (at the right hand of the Power) without "of God."
- **Vulgate**: *a dextris virtutis Dei*
- **Hebrew**: לִימִין הַגְּבוּרָה
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits "of God" (הָאֱלֹהִים), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate. It should be added: לִימִין גְּבוּרַת הָאֱלֹהִים or לִימִין הַגְּבוּרָה שֶׁל אֱלֹהִים.

### 14:68 — "Et gallus cantavit"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "et gallus cantavit" (and the cock crowed) at the end of verse 68. The SBLGNT includes "kai alektor ephonesen" in the apparatus. The Hebrew includes it: וַיִּקְרָא הַתַּרְנְגֹל.
- **Vulgate**: *et gallus cantavit*
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּקְרָא הַתַּרְנְגֹל
- **Note**: Hebrew correctly includes the first cock crow, matching the Vulgate. This detail is important for Mark's "twice" motif in 14:30 and 14:72.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 14:1 — חַג הַפֶּסַח וְהַמַּצּוֹת (Feast of Passover and Unleavened Bread)
- **Hebrew**: וַיְהִי עוֹד יוֹמַיִם וְחַג הַפֶּסַח וְהַמַּצּוֹת בָּא
- **Note**: Good biblical Hebrew. The combined designation echoes the usage in 2 Chronicles 35:17 and Ezra 6:22. The wayyiqtol narrative opening וַיְהִי is excellent Hebraic storytelling style.

### 14:3 — נֵרְדְּ זַךְ וְיָקָר (pure and precious nard)
- **Hebrew**: פַּךְ-מִרְקַחַת נֵרְדְּ זַךְ וְיָקָר מְאֹד
- **Note**: The word נֵרְדְּ (nard/spikenard) is taken from Song of Songs 1:12 and 4:13-14, where it appears as a precious aromatic. The term פַּךְ (flask) echoes the anointing flask used by Samuel (1 Samuel 10:1, 1 Samuel 16:1). The Hebrew vocabulary evokes both royal anointing and the Song of Songs bridal imagery.

### 14:36 — אַבָּא אָבִי (Abba, Father)
- **Hebrew**: אַבָּא אָבִי כֹּל תּוּכַל
- **Note**: The Aramaic אַבָּא is preserved, followed by the Hebrew אָבִי (my Father) as the translation. This mirrors the Greek "Abba ho pater" and the Vulgate "Abba pater." The retention of both the Aramaic intimate address and the Hebrew translation is effective.

### 14:34 — נַפְשִׁי מָרָה-לִי עַד-מָוֶת (My soul is bitter unto death)
- **Hebrew**: נַפְשִׁי מָרָה-לִי עַד-מָוֶת
- **Note**: The phrase echoes Judges 18:25 and 1 Samuel 22:2 (מָרֵי נֶפֶשׁ) and Job 3:20 (לְמָרֵי נָפֶשׁ). The construction is idiomatic biblical Hebrew for deep anguish. The Vulgate's "Tristis est anima mea usque ad mortem" (My soul is sorrowful unto death) is well captured, though מָרָה carries a stronger sense of bitterness than sadness.

### 14:72 — וַיָּשֶׂם עַל-לִבּוֹ וַיֵּבְְֲ (and he laid it on his heart and wept)
- **Hebrew**: וַיָּשֶׂם עַל-לִבּוֹ וַיֵּבְְֲ
- **Note**: The phrase וַיָּשֶׂם עַל-לִבּוֹ (he placed upon his heart / he took it to heart) renders the Greek "epibalon" and the Vulgate "coepit." The Hebrew text appears to be corrupted at the final word, which should be וַיֵּבְךְּ (and he wept). The consonantal text is damaged.

## Other Notes

### 14:22-24 — Words of Institution
The Eucharistic words are theologically central. The Hebrew גּוּפִי (my body) is the standard Delitzsch rendering of "corpus" / "soma." For the cup, the Hebrew echoes Exodus 24:8 (דַּם הַבְּרִית) and Jeremiah 31:31 (בְּרִית חֲדָשָׁה), combining the Mosaic covenant-blood formula with Jeremiah's new covenant prophecy. The liturgical density of the Hebrew is very high.

### 14:32 — Gethsemane (גַּת-שְׁמָנֵי)
The Hebrew גַּת-שְׁמָנֵי (oil-press) is the etymological rendering of the place name. The Vulgate uses the transliteration "Gethsemani." Delitzsch provides the Hebrew meaning, connecting the location to the olive cultivation of the Mount of Olives. The word גַּת (winepress/oil-press) appears frequently in the OT (Judges 6:11, Nehemiah 13:15).
