# Acts Chapter 15 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 15:34 — Vulgate-only verse
- **Issue**: Acts 15:34 is present in the Vulgate but absent from the SBLGNT. The Hebrew text includes it in brackets.
- **Vulgate**: "Visum est autem Silae ibi remanere : Judas autem solus abiit Jerusalem."
- **Greek**: Empty in SBLGNT.
- **Hebrew**: Bracketed text only about Silas remaining; omits the second clause about Judas departing alone to Jerusalem.
- **Note**: The Vulgate has two clauses: (1) Silas decided to remain, and (2) Judas departed alone to Jerusalem. The Hebrew only renders the first clause in brackets. The full Vulgate reading should be represented. This verse is attested in some Greek manuscripts (D, various minuscules) and is part of the received Catholic text.

### 15:41 — Additional content in Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "praecipiens custodire praecepta Apostolorum et seniorum" (commanding them to keep the precepts of the Apostles and elders), which is absent from both the SBLGNT and the Hebrew.
- **Vulgate**: "Perambulabat autem Syriam et Ciliciam, confirmans ecclesias : praecipiens custodire praecepta Apostolorum et seniorum."
- **Greek/Hebrew**: Both end at "strengthening the churches" without the additional clause.
- **Note**: The Hebrew should include this Vulgate-plus content about commanding observance of the apostolic decrees.

## Content Alignment

### 15:11 — "Domini Jesu Christi" vs. shorter Greek
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "per gratiam Domini Jesu Christi" (through the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ). The Greek has only "tou kyriou Iesou" (the Lord Jesus), without "Christou." The Hebrew follows the fuller reading with "Yeshua haMashiach Adoneinu" (Jesus the Messiah our Lord).
- **Vulgate**: "per gratiam Domini Jesu Christi credimus salvari"
- **Greek**: "dia tes charitos tou kyriou Iesou"
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the fuller Vulgate reading here. Good alignment.

### 15:17-18 — OT quotation from Amos 9:11-12 (LXX form)
- **Issue**: This quotation follows the Septuagint form of Amos 9:11-12, which differs from the Masoretic Hebrew text. The MT reads "that they may possess the remnant of Edom" while the LXX/Acts reads "that the remnant of men may seek the Lord." The Hebrew translation uses YHWH for kyrios.
- **Vulgate**: "ut requirant ceteri hominum Dominum"
- **Note**: Significant Septuagint connection. The Hebrew correctly uses the LXX reading ("that the remnant of men may seek the Lord") rather than the MT reading of Amos. The divergence stems from the LXX reading 'adam (men) for 'edom and darash (seek) for yarash (possess).

### 15:20 — Apostolic Decree: order of prohibitions
- **Issue**: The Vulgate lists: (1) contaminationibus simulacrorum, (2) fornicatione, (3) suffocatis, (4) sanguine. The Greek lists: (1) idols, (2) porneia, (3) strangled, (4) blood. The Hebrew follows a slightly different order matching the Greek rather than the Vulgate's fornicatione-before-suffocatis order.
- **Note**: Minor ordering difference. The Hebrew matches the Greek order. The Vulgate order places fornication second; the Hebrew and Greek place it last among the four.

### 15:24 — Bracketed Vulgate-plus content
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes a bracketed phrase absent from SBLGNT: "saying to you to be circumcised and keep the Torah." The Vulgate similarly lacks this specific wording but is closer with "quibus non mandavimus."
- **Vulgate**: "turbaverunt vos verbis, evertentes animas vestras, quibus non mandavimus"
- **Greek**: Includes "ois ou diesteilametha" (whom we did not command) but not the circumcision/Torah clause.
- **Note**: The bracketed Hebrew phrase reflects the Western text tradition (Codex D). Since the Vulgate does not include this specific expansion, the brackets are appropriate, but this content should be marked as non-Vulgate.

### 15:40 — "gratiae Dei" vs. "gratiae Domini"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "traditus gratiae Dei" (entrusted to the grace of God). The Greek reads "te chariti tou kyriou" (to the grace of the Lord). The Hebrew reads "chesed YHWH" (the grace of the LORD).
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "Dei" (God); the Greek has "kyriou" (Lord). The Hebrew uses YHWH which could map to either. Vulgate reading preferred.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 15:7 — "mikulanu" (from all of us)
- **Hebrew**: "bי bachar haElohim mikulanu" (God chose from all of us)
- **Note**: The Vulgate "in nobis elegit" and Greek "en hymin exelexato" both mean "chose among you/us." Delitzsch's "mikulanu" is a reasonable rendering but the preposition min (from) rather than be (among/in) slightly shifts the sense.

### 15:16 — Excellent biblical Hebrew for Amos quotation
- **Hebrew**: "acharei-khen ashuv va'aqim et-sukkat David hanofelet vaharisoteha aqim uvenitiha"
- **Note**: This is excellent biblical Hebrew, closely echoing the vocabulary of Amos 9:11 in the Masoretic text (sukkath David hanofelet). The word "harisoteha" (its ruins) mirrors the prophetic register well. Strong translation.

### 15:22 — "vayyitav be'einei" (and it was good in the eyes of)
- **Hebrew**: "vayyitav be'einei hashlichim"
- **Note**: This is a fine biblical idiom (cf. Gen 41:37, Deut 1:23). Accurately renders the Vulgate "placuit" and Greek "edoxe" in classical biblical style.

### 15:28 — "ki tov lifnei Ruach haQodesh"
- **Hebrew**: "ki tov lifnei Ruach haQodesh ulefaneinu"
- **Note**: "Visum est Spiritui Sancto et nobis" — the Hebrew "it was good before the Holy Spirit and before us" is a suitable biblical construction. The phrase "tov lifnei" mirrors the Hebrew Bible's way of expressing divine approval (cf. Neh 2:5).

## Other Notes

### 15:16-18 — Septuagint connection: Amos 9:11-12
This is one of the most theologically significant OT quotations in Acts. James cites the LXX form of Amos, which speaks of Gentiles seeking the Lord, to justify the inclusion of Gentiles without full Torah observance. The Hebrew translation appropriately follows the LXX reading here, creating a notable case where the NT Hebrew text of an OT quotation diverges from the Masoretic Hebrew of the source passage.

### 15:20, 29 — The Apostolic Decree and Leviticus 17-18
The four prohibitions (idolatrous pollution, fornication, strangled meat, blood) correspond to the requirements placed on resident aliens (gerim) in Leviticus 17-18. This connects the Council's decision to Torah categories for righteous Gentiles living among Israel.
