# Acts Chapter 6 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 15 verses align with Vulgate numbering.

## Content Alignment

### 6:1 — "Graecorum" vs "haYehudim haYevanim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "murmur Graecorum adversus Hebraeos" (murmuring of the Greeks against the Hebrews). The Greek has "ton Helleniston pros tous Hebraious" (of the Hellenists against the Hebrews). The Hebrew renders "haYehudim haYevanim" (the Greek Jews), which is an interpretive gloss — "Hellenists" in context means Greek-speaking Jews, and the Hebrew makes this explicit by adding "Yehudim" (Jews).
- **Vulgate**: "Graecorum" (of the Greeks)
- **Hebrew**: "haYehudim haYevanim" (the Greek Jews)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Graecorum" is less specific than the Hebrew's interpretation. The Hebrew clarifies but adds a word not in the source.

### 6:1 — "ministerio quotidiano" vs "aruchatam"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "despicerentur in ministerio quotidiano viduae eorum" (their widows were neglected in the daily ministry/service). The Greek has "en te diakonia te kathemerine" (in the daily service). The Hebrew renders the neglect as "he'elimu eineihem me'almenoteihem levilti-tet lahen yom yom et-aruchatan" (they hid their eyes from their widows, not giving them daily their meal).
- **Vulgate**: "despicerentur in ministerio quotidiano"
- **Hebrew**: "hid their eyes from their widows, not giving them daily their meal"
- **Note**: The Hebrew significantly expands and interprets. "He'elimu eineihem" (they hid their eyes) is an idiomatic biblical expression (Isaiah 1:15, Leviticus 20:4) but adds detail. "Aruchah" (meal/portion) specifies the nature of the "ministerium" as food distribution, which is likely correct contextually but goes beyond the Vulgate text.

### 6:3 — "Spiritu Sancto et sapientia" vs "Ruach haQodesh vechokmah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "plenos Spiritu Sancto et sapientia" (full of the Holy Spirit and wisdom). The Greek has "pleres pneumatos kai sophias" — some manuscripts read "pneumatos hagiou" (Holy Spirit), others just "pneumatos" (spirit). The Hebrew has "umele'im Ruach haQodesh vechokmah" (and full of the Holy Spirit and wisdom).
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate. Both specify the Holy Spirit.

### 6:7 — "verbum Domini" vs "devar ha'Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "verbum Domini" (the word of the Lord). The Greek has "ho logos tou theou" (the word of God). The Hebrew has "devar ha'Elohim" (the word of God), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "verbum Domini"
- **Hebrew**: "devar ha'Elohim" (the word of God)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Domini" (of the Lord) differs from the Greek's "tou theou" (of God). The Hebrew follows the Greek. To follow the Vulgate, "devar YHVH" or "devar ha'Adon" would be needed.

### 6:8 — "gratia et fortitudine" vs "chesed ugvurah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "plenus gratia et fortitudine" (full of grace and strength). The Greek has "pleres charitos kai dynameos" (full of grace and power). The Hebrew renders "male chesed ugvurah" (full of grace and might).
- **Note**: Good alignment. "Chesed" for "gratia" and "gvurah" for "fortitudine" are both strong biblical choices.

### 6:13 — "locum sanctum hunc" vs "meqom haQodesh hazeh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "adversus locum sanctum, et legem" (against the holy place, and the law). The Greek has "kata tou topou tou hagiou toutou kai tou nomou" (against this holy place and the law). The Hebrew adds "hazeh" (this) for the holy place, following the Greek demonstrative rather than the Vulgate which lacks "hunc" (this) for "locum sanctum."
- **Vulgate**: "locum sanctum" (without "this") then adds "hunc" only in some editions
- **Note**: Minor. The demonstrative is present in the Greek and most Vulgate manuscripts.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 6:2 — "neshamesh et-hashulchanot" for "ministrare mensis"
- **Hebrew**: "veneshammesh et-hashulchanot" (and we should serve the tables)
- **Note**: Literal and accurate. "Shimmesh" (serve/minister) with "shulchanot" (tables) directly mirrors "ministrare mensis." The Twelve's point — that serving tables would detract from the ministry of the Word — comes through clearly.

### 6:5 — Proper names transliteration
- **Hebrew**: "Istephanos" (אסטפנוס), "Filippos" (פילפוס), "Prochoros" (פרוכורוס), "Niqanor" (ניקנור), "Timon" (טימון), "Parmenas" (פרמנס), "Niqlas" (ניקלס)
- **Note**: All seven names are transliterated into Hebrew characters. "Istephanos" for Stephen uses the Hebrew convention of prefixing an aleph to Greek names beginning with "St-." "Niqlas ger Antiochya" (Nicolas, a proselyte of Antioch) accurately renders the Vulgate's "Nicolaum advenam Antiochenum."

### 6:9 — "Libertinon" rendering
- **Hebrew**: "haLibertinim" (הליברטינים)
- **Note**: The Latin loanword "Libertini" (freedmen) is simply transliterated into Hebrew. This is appropriate since the term has no Hebrew equivalent. The Vulgate's "Libertinorum" is a Latin name for a specific synagogue group.

### 6:15 — "kifnei mal'akh Elohim" for "faciem angeli"
- **Hebrew**: "vayir'u vehineh panav kifnei mal'akh Elohim" (and they saw, and behold his face was like the face of an angel of God)
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "Elohim" (of God) to "angel," which is absent from both the Vulgate ("tamquam faciem angeli") and the Greek ("hosei prosopon angelou"). However, the biblical idiom "mal'akh Elohim" (angel of God) is extremely common (Genesis 21:17, 31:11, Judges 6:20, etc.), so this is a natural Hebrew expansion.

## Other Notes

### 6:5 — The Seven
The selection of the Seven marks an important organizational development. All seven have Greek names, suggesting they were chosen from the Hellenist community to address the Hellenists' complaint. The Hebrew correctly identifies Nicolas as a "ger" (proselyte/convert), matching the Vulgate's "advenam" (convert/foreigner).

### 6:13-14 — Charges against Stephen
The false witnesses' charges echo the charges against Jesus (Matthew 26:61, Mark 14:58). The Hebrew's "yittoz et-hamaqom hazeh" (he will tear down this place) for "destruet locum istum" is strong and accurate, using the same root (n-t-tz) used for the destruction of altars and high places in the Old Testament (Judges 6:30-32, 2 Kings 23:12).
