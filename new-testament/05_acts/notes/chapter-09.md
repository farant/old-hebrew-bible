# Acts Chapter 9 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 9:5-6 — Major Vulgate expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate contains substantial additional material in 9:5-6 not found in the SBLGNT. The Vulgate 9:5 adds "durum est tibi contra stimulum calcitrare" (it is hard for you to kick against the goads), and 9:6 begins "Et tremens ac stupens dixit: Domine, quid me vis facere?" (And trembling and astonished, he said: Lord, what do you want me to do?). The SBLGNT has neither clause here (they appear in Paul's later retellings, Acts 22:10 and 26:14).
- **Vulgate**: 9:5 includes the goads saying; 9:6 begins with Saul's trembling question before the command to go to the city.
- **Greek**: 9:5 ends after "I am Jesus whom you persecute"; 9:6 immediately says "but rise and enter the city."
- **Hebrew**: The Hebrew includes both additions in brackets, indicating awareness of the textual issue.
- **Note**: The Vulgate reading harmonizes the three accounts of Paul's conversion (chs. 9, 22, 26). From the Vulgate-authoritative perspective, these clauses are canonical. The Hebrew bracketing should be removed to match the Vulgate text.

### 9:6-7 — Verse boundary shift
- **Issue**: The Vulgate places "Surge, et ingredere civitatem, et ibi dicetur tibi quid te oporteat facere" (Rise, and enter the city, and there it will be told you what you must do) at the beginning of the content that the SBLGNT/Hebrew assign to verse 7 (with the companions standing speechless). The Vulgate verse 6 ends with the question "Domine, quid me vis facere?" and the command to rise belongs to the Lord's response.
- **Vulgate**: 9:7 begins "Viri autem illi qui comitabantur..." and includes the command to enter the city.
- **Greek/Hebrew**: The command to enter the city is in 9:6; verse 7 is about the speechless companions.
- **Note**: The content aligns but verse boundaries differ slightly. The Vulgate verse 7 is longer because it includes the city command plus the companions.

## Content Alignment

### 9:5 — "Durum est tibi contra stimulum calcitrare"
- **Issue**: This proverbial expression about kicking against the goads is present in the Vulgate at 9:5 but absent from SBLGNT here. It appears in Acts 26:14 in both traditions.
- **Vulgate**: "durum est tibi contra stimulum calcitrare"
- **Hebrew**: קָשֶׁה לְךָ לִבְעֹט בַּדָּרְבֹנוֹת (in brackets)
- **Note**: The Hebrew rendering is good biblical Hebrew. דָּרְבֹנוֹת (goads) occurs in Eccl 12:11. Following the Vulgate, this should be unbracketed.

### 9:29 — "Gentibus" vs. "Hellenists"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Loquebatur quoque gentibus" (he spoke also to the Gentiles), while the SBLGNT has Ἑλληνιστάς (Hellenists, i.e. Greek-speaking Jews). The Hebrew follows the Greek with הַיְּהוּדִים הַיְּוָנִים (Greek Jews).
- **Vulgate**: "gentibus" — the Gentiles/nations
- **Greek**: Ἑλληνιστάς — Hellenists (Greek-speaking Jews)
- **Hebrew**: הַיְּהוּדִים הַיְּוָנִים — the Greek Jews
- **Note**: This is a meaningful difference. The Vulgate suggests Paul was already preaching to Gentiles at this early stage, which the Greek text does not support. The Hebrew follows the Greek reading here rather than the Vulgate.

### 9:31 — Singular vs. plural "churches"
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT has the singular ἡ ἐκκλησία (the church), while the Vulgate has "Ecclesia quidem" (singular). The Hebrew uses the plural הַקְּהִלּוֹת (the congregations).
- **Vulgate**: "Ecclesia" — singular, the Church as one entity
- **Hebrew**: הַקְּהִלּוֹת — plural, the congregations
- **Note**: The Vulgate and the best Greek text agree on the singular, emphasizing the unity of the Church. The Hebrew plural diverges from both.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 9:1 — Strong biblical opening
- **Hebrew**: וְשָׁאוּל עוֹדֶנּוּ יְפֵחַ זַעַם וָרֶצַח
- **Note**: יְפֵחַ (breathing out) is vivid and appropriate. The pairing of זַעַם וָרֶצַח (fury and murder) is a strong biblical hendiadys. Cf. the Greek ἐμπνέων ἀπειλῆς καὶ φόνου.

### 9:4 — Direct speech formula
- **Hebrew**: שָׁאוּל שָׁאוּל לָמָּה תִרְדְּפֵנִי
- **Note**: The doubled name is correctly preserved. The Hebrew lamah + imperfect construction is natural biblical syntax for accusatory questions (cf. Gen 12:18, Exod 5:22).

### 9:15 — "Vessel of election"
- **Hebrew**: כְלִי חֵפֶץ הוּא לִי
- **Note**: כְלִי חֵפֶץ (vessel of desire/delight) is a reasonable rendering but not identical to the Vulgate's "vas electionis" (vessel of election/choosing). The Greek σκεῦος ἐκλογῆς maps better to כְּלִי בְחִירָה. However, כְלִי חֵפֶץ echoes Jer 48:38 and Hos 8:8 and has a good biblical feel.

### 9:18 — "Scales fell from his eyes"
- **Hebrew**: נָפְלוּ מֵעַל עֵינָיו כְּמוֹ קַשְׂקַשִּׂים
- **Note**: קַשְׂקַשִּׂים (scales) is the exact word used for fish scales in Lev 11:9-10. The imagery is striking and the Hebrew word choice is precise.

### 9:36 — Tabitha/Dorcas translation note
- **Hebrew**: טָבִיתָא תַּרְגּוּמוֹ צְבִיָּה
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly translates Tabitha as צְבִיָּה (gazelle/Dorcas), preserving both the Aramaic name and its Hebrew equivalent. The word תַּרְגּוּמוֹ (its translation) is post-biblical Aramaic/Mishnaic usage, however. Biblical Hebrew would use פֵּרוּשׁוֹ or a circumlocution.

## Other Notes

### 9:4 — Christophany on the Damascus Road
- **Note**: This is the first of three accounts of Paul's conversion in Acts (cf. 22:6-16, 26:12-18). The Lord's words "I am Jesus whom you persecute" constitute a direct divine speech identifying the risen Christ with the persecuted Church. This is red-text material (words of the Lord).

### 9:15 — OT "chosen vessel" typology
- **Note**: The concept of a person as God's chosen vessel echoes Jeremiah's call narrative (Jer 1:5) and the Servant Songs of Isaiah. The phrase "to carry my name before Gentiles and kings and sons of Israel" echoes Isa 49:6 (light to the nations).

### 9:34 — "Dominus Jesus Christus"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "sanat te Dominus Jesus Christus" (the Lord Jesus Christ heals you). The Greek has simply "Jesus Christ." The Hebrew follows the Greek with יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
- **Note**: The Vulgate's addition of "Dominus" is theologically significant, explicitly identifying Jesus as Lord in a healing formula. The Hebrew should add הָאָדוֹן to match.
