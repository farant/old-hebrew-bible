# Acts Chapter 27 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 44 verses align with the Vulgate. No versification discrepancies.

### 27:5 -- "Lystram" vs. "Mura"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "venimus Lystram, quae est Lyciae" (we came to Lystra, which is in Lycia). This is a well-known Vulgate variant. The Greek has Myra tes Lukias (Myra of Lycia), which is the correct geographical location. The Hebrew has Mura asher be-Luqya ("Myra which is in Lycia"), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "Lystram, quae est Lyciae"
- **Hebrew**: Mura asher be-Luqya
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Lystram" is almost certainly a scribal corruption of "Myram" (Myra). Lystra is an inland city in Lycaonia (not Lycia) and makes no geographical sense in a coastal voyage. Most modern Vulgate editions note this as an error. The Hebrew's Mura is geographically correct and follows the Greek. This is a case where the Hebrew is arguably preferable to a strict Vulgate following.

## Content Alignment

### 27:9 -- "Jejunium" / "ha-tzom"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "jejunium jam praeteriisset" (the fast had already passed). The Greek has ten nesteian (the fast). Both refer to Yom Kippur (the Day of Atonement fast, 10 Tishri). The Hebrew renders this as ha-tzom ("the fast"), which is the standard biblical term for the Day of Atonement fast (cf. Isa 58:5, Lev 16:29 innui nefesh).
- **Vulgate**: "jejunium jam praeteriisset"
- **Hebrew**: ha-tzom kvar avar
- **Note**: The Hebrew ha-tzom is precise. A Jewish reader would immediately understand this as Yom Kippur. After Yom Kippur (roughly October), Mediterranean sailing became dangerous. No correction needed.

### 27:14 -- "Euroaquilo" vs. "Eurikleidon"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "Euroaquilo" (a northeast wind, compound of Greek euros + Latin aquilo). The Greek SBLGNT has Eurakulon. The Hebrew transliterates as Auriqlidon, which follows the Greek rather than the Vulgate's Latin-hybrid form.
- **Vulgate**: "Euroaquilo"
- **Hebrew**: Auriqlidon
- **Note**: Both forms refer to the same fierce northeast gale. The transliteration difference is minor. The Hebrew transliteration follows the Greek tradition rather than the Latin.

### 27:37 -- Passenger count

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "ducentae septuaginta sex" (276). The Greek has diakosiai hebdomekonta hex (276). The Hebrew has matayim ve-shiv'im va-shesh (276). All three agree.
- **Vulgate**: 276
- **Hebrew**: 276
- **Note**: All traditions agree on the number. Some manuscripts have 76 or "about 76," but the Vulgate's 276 is followed by the Hebrew.

### 27:43-44 -- Split between verses

- **Issue**: The Vulgate's 27:43 contains the instruction for swimmers to go first and then mentions "the rest" in 27:44. The Hebrew compresses 27:43b-44a into 27:43, placing the instruction about planks and ship-pieces together with the swimmers: ve-ha-nish'arim elleh al qrashim ve-elleh al shivrei ha-oniyyah ("and the rest, these on planks and these on pieces of the ship"). Then 27:44 becomes simply va-yehi khen va-yimmaltu khullam el ha-yabashah ("and so it was, and they all escaped to land").
- **Vulgate**: 27:44 -- "et ceteros, alios in tabulis ferebant, quosdam super ea quae de navi erant"
- **Hebrew**: the planks/ship-pieces content is in 27:43
- **Note**: The content is preserved but redistributed. Not a material issue.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 27:20 -- "Va-tikkaret mimmenu kol tiqvah"

- **Hebrew**: va-tikkaret mimmenu kol tiqvah -- "all hope was cut off from us"
- **Note**: The verb nikrat ("was cut off") with tiqvah ("hope") is a powerful biblical idiom. The construction echoes Ezek 37:11 (avdah tiqvatenu, nigzarnu lanu -- "our hope is lost, we are cut off"), though using a different verb. The Vulgate's "ablata erat spes omnis" (all hope was taken away) is well rendered.

### 27:22-26 -- Paul's exhortation at sea

- **Hebrew**: hizqu ve-imtzu ("be strong and courageous") in both 27:22 and 27:25
- **Note**: This is the language of Josh 1:6, 7, 9 (hazaq ve-ematz). The echo of Joshua's divine commission during a perilous passage is powerful and theologically apt -- the crossing of the sea to a new land. The Vulgate's "bono animo estote" (be of good spirit) is more prosaic; the Hebrew's evocation of the conquest narrative is an enrichment.

### 27:29 -- "Va-yikhsefu le-or ha-yom"

- **Hebrew**: va-yikhsefu le-or ha-yom -- "and they longed for the light of day"
- **Note**: The verb kasaf ("to long/pine for") is rare and poetic biblical Hebrew (cf. Gen 31:30, Ps 84:3, Job 14:15). The Vulgate's "optabant diem fieri" (they wished for day to come) is rendered with this beautiful literary verb. Excellent choice.

### 27:34 -- "Lo yippol mi-sa'arat rosho artzah"

- **Hebrew**: ki ish mikkhem lo yippol mi-sa'arat rosho artzah -- "not a hair of your head shall fall to the ground"
- **Note**: This is a direct quotation of the biblical idiom found in 1 Sam 14:45 and 2 Sam 14:11 (im yippol mi-sa'arat rosho artzah). The Vulgate's "nullius vestrum capillus de capite peribit" matches perfectly. This is one of the strongest biblical echo points in the chapter.

### 27:41 -- "Mi-she'on ha-gallim"

- **Hebrew**: va-ahoreiha nishberu mi-she'on ha-gallim -- "her stern was broken by the roaring of the waves"
- **Note**: The word she'on ("roaring/tumult") is excellent biblical Hebrew for the sound of violent waters (cf. Ps 65:8, Isa 17:12 -- she'on ammim, "tumult of peoples," compared to the roaring of seas). The Vulgate's "a vi maris" (by the force of the sea) is rendered with greater auditory vividness.

## Other Notes

### 27:23-24 -- Angelic speech

The angel's words to Paul in 27:24 are direct divine communication: "al tira Polos od amod ta'amod lifnei Qeisar ve-attah hinneh natan lekha ha-Elohim et kol ha-holkhim ittekha ba-oniyyah." The phrase amod ta'amod (cognate absolute "you shall surely stand") intensifies the promise beyond the Vulgate's simple "Caesari te oportet assistere." This is the language of prophetic assurance.

### 27:35 -- Eucharistic echo

Paul's action of taking bread, giving thanks, breaking it, and eating (laqah et ha-lehem, va-yodeh l'Elohim, va-yivtza, va-yahel le-ekhol) deliberately echoes the Last Supper narrative and Eucharistic language. The Hebrew sequence mirrors the Vulgate's "sumens panem, gratias egit Deo... cum fregisset, coepit manducare" precisely.
