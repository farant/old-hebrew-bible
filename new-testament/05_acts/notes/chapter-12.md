# Acts Chapter 12 — Translation Notes

## Versification

No significant versification differences between the Vulgate and the Greek/Hebrew in this chapter.

## Content Alignment

### 12:1 — "Ecclesia" vs. Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "de ecclesia" (from the church). The Hebrew renders this as מִן-הַקָּהָל (from the assembly/congregation).
- **Vulgate**: "ecclesia" — the Church
- **Hebrew**: הַקָּהָל — the assembly
- **Note**: In 12:5, the Hebrew uses הַקְּהִילָּה for "ecclesia." The inconsistency between הַקָּהָל (12:1) and הַקְּהִילָּה (12:5) for the same Latin/Greek term is notable. Both are defensible, but consistency would be preferable.

### 12:7 — "Latere Petri" / "percussoque latere"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "percussoque latere Petri" (and striking the side of Peter). The Hebrew has וַיִּסְפֹּק עַל-יֶרֶךְ פֶּטְרוֹס (and he struck the thigh/hip of Peter).
- **Vulgate**: "latere" — side/flank
- **Greek**: τὴν πλευρὰν — the side
- **Hebrew**: יֶרֶךְ — thigh/hip
- **Note**: The Hebrew shifts the body part from side (latus/πλευρά) to thigh (יֶרֶךְ). While the angel striking Peter's side to wake him is the standard reading, יֶרֶךְ has strong biblical associations with oaths and patriarchal narratives (Gen 24:2, 47:29). A closer rendering would be צֵלָע or צַד (side).

### 12:11 — "Dominus" vs. "Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "misit Dominus angelum suum" (the Lord sent his angel). The Greek has ἐξαπέστειλεν ὁ κύριος (the Lord sent forth). The Hebrew has הָאֱלֹהִים שָׁלַח (God sent).
- **Vulgate**: "Dominus" — the Lord
- **Hebrew**: הָאֱלֹהִים — God
- **Note**: The Hebrew substitutes "God" for "Lord." Throughout Acts, the Delitzsch translation sometimes renders κύριος/Dominus as הָאֱלֹהִים rather than הָאָדוֹן or יְהוָֹה, which can obscure the christological dimension when "Dominus" refers specifically to the Lord Jesus. Here it likely refers to God the Father, so the substitution is less problematic, but Vulgate fidelity would prefer הָאָדוֹן.

### 12:24 — "Verbum Domini" vs. "Verbum Dei"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Verbum autem Domini" (the word of the Lord). The SBLGNT has ὁ λόγος τοῦ θεοῦ (the word of God). The Hebrew follows the Greek with דְבַר אֱלֹהִים (word of God).
- **Vulgate**: "Domini" — of the Lord
- **Greek**: τοῦ θεοῦ — of God
- **Hebrew**: אֱלֹהִים — of God
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek against the Vulgate. To match the Vulgate, it should read דְּבַר-יְהוָֹה or דְּבַר הָאָדוֹן.

### 12:25 — Direction of travel
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "reversi sunt ab Jerosolymis" (they returned from Jerusalem). The Greek has a textual variant: some manuscripts read εἰς Ἰερουσαλήμ (to Jerusalem), others ἐξ Ἰερουσαλήμ (from Jerusalem). The SBLGNT prints εἰς (to). The Hebrew has מִירוּשָׁלָיִם (from Jerusalem), following the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "ab Jerosolymis" — from Jerusalem
- **Greek (SBLGNT)**: εἰς Ἰερουσαλήμ — to Jerusalem
- **Hebrew**: מִירוּשָׁלָיִם — from Jerusalem
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate reading against the SBLGNT. The context (they had been sent to Jerusalem with famine relief in 11:30) means "returned from Jerusalem" is the natural reading.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 12:4 — Military guard detail
- **Hebrew**: לְאַרְבַּע מַחְלְקוֹת שֶׁל-אַרְבָּעָה אַנְשֵׁי צָבָא
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "quatuor quaternionibus militum" (four quaternions of soldiers = four squads of four) is clearly rendered. מַחְלְקוֹת (divisions/shifts) is a good military term from biblical Hebrew (1 Chr 27:1-15). The phrase שֶׁל is post-biblical (Mishnaic), however; classical biblical Hebrew would use a construct chain.

### 12:6 — Peter sleeping in chains
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּישַׁן פֶּטְרוֹס בֵּין-שְׁנֵי אַנְשֵׁי צָבָא וְהוּא אָסוּר בִּשְׁנַיִם זִקִּים
- **Note**: Vivid biblical narrative style. The word זִקִּים (chains/fetters) appears in Isa 45:14, Nah 3:10, and Ps 149:8. The construction is natural.

### 12:8 — Angel's instructions
- **Hebrew**: חֲגֹר מָתְנֶיךָ וְהַנְעֵל אֶת-רַגְלֶיךָ...עֲטֵה מְעִילְךָ וְלֵךְ אַחֲרָי
- **Note**: Excellent biblical diction. חֲגֹר מָתְנֶיךָ (gird your loins) echoes the Passover command (Exod 12:11) and Elijah narratives (1 Kgs 18:46, 2 Kgs 4:29). עֲטֵה (wrap/put on) for putting on a garment is classical (Isa 61:10). The imperatives create a fast-paced sequence appropriate for the urgent liberation scene.

### 12:15 — "Angelus ejus est"
- **Hebrew**: מַלְאָכוֹ הוּא
- **Note**: The belief that Peter's guardian angel appeared is succinctly rendered. The concept of a personal angel (מַלְאָכוֹ = his angel) reflects the Jewish belief in guardian angels attested in Matt 18:10 and early Jewish literature.

### 12:23 — Death of Herod
- **Hebrew**: וַיַּכֵּהוּ מַלְאַךְ-יְהוָֹה פִּתְאֹם...וַיֹּאכְלֻהוּ תוֹלָעִים וַיָּמֹת
- **Note**: Strong biblical narrative. The phrase מַלְאַךְ-יְהוָֹה (angel of the LORD) striking someone echoes 2 Sam 24:16-17 and 2 Kgs 19:35 (the angel striking 185,000 Assyrians). The Hebrew adds פִּתְאֹם (suddenly), which corresponds to the Vulgate's "confestim" (immediately). תוֹלָעִים (worms) is the correct biblical term (Isa 14:11, Job 25:6).

## Other Notes

### 12:3 — Chronological note: Feast of Unleavened Bread
- **Note**: The Hebrew חַג-הַמַּצּוֹת is the correct OT term for the Feast of Unleavened Bread (Exod 23:15, 34:18). The Vulgate's "dies Azymorum" preserves the Greek ἄζυμα. The narrative anchoring to Passover/Unleavened Bread connects Herod's persecution typologically to the Exodus.

### 12:17 — James the brother of the Lord
- **Note**: The instruction "Nuntiate Jacobo et fratribus" (report to James and the brothers) indicates that James (the brother of the Lord) is now the leader of the Jerusalem church after the death of James son of Zebedee (12:2). The Hebrew correctly renders this as הַגִּידוּ אֶת-זֹאת לְיַעֲקֹב וְלָאַחִים.
