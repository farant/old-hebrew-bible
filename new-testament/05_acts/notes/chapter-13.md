# Acts Chapter 13 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 13:32-33 — Verse division and content split
- **Issue**: In the SBLGNT, 13:32 ends with "the promise made to the fathers" and 13:33 begins with "that God has fulfilled this..." The Hebrew combines the fulfillment statement and the introduction of Jesus into verse 32, making verse 33 begin directly with the Psalm citation.
- **Vulgate**: 13:32 is the announcement; 13:33 is the fulfillment and Psalm quote.
- **Hebrew**: Verse 32 absorbs some of verse 33's content (כִּי אֹתָהּ קִיֵּם הָאֱלֹהִים לְבָנֵינוּ בַּהֲקִימוֹ אֶת-יֵשׁוּעַ).
- **Note**: Content is preserved but the verse boundary differs. The Psalm quotation in both follows correctly.

### 13:38-39 — Verse division
- **Issue**: The Greek divides 13:38 (announcement of forgiveness) and 13:39 (justification by faith) as two short verses. The Hebrew places the justification clause into verse 39 along with the comparison to the law of Moses: וְכָל-הַמַּאֲמִין יִצְדַּק בּוֹ לְכֹל אֲשֶׁר לֹא-יְכָלְתֶּם לְהִצְטַדֵּק מִמֶּנּוּ בְּתוֹרַת משֶׁה.
- **Vulgate**: 13:38 includes both the announcement and the mention of the law of Moses; 13:39 is the brief "in hoc omnis qui credit, justificatur."
- **Hebrew**: Content is rearranged so that verse 38 is shorter and verse 39 longer.
- **Note**: The verse boundary shift does not lose content, but tracking individual verse references requires awareness of this difference.

## Content Alignment

### 13:1 — Name order: "Saulum et Barnabam"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in quibus Barnabas, et Simon...et Saulus" listing Barnabas first and Saul last. The Greek similarly lists Barnabas first, Saul last. The Hebrew follows the same order. But in 13:2, the Vulgate has "Segregate mihi Saulum et Barnabam" (Saul and Barnabas), reversing the order of the Greek (τὸν Βαρναβᾶν καὶ Σαῦλον = Barnabas and Saul).
- **Vulgate**: "Saulum et Barnabam" — Saul listed first
- **Greek**: Βαρναβᾶν καὶ Σαῦλον — Barnabas first
- **Hebrew**: בַּר-נַבָּא וְאֶת-שָׁאוּל — Barnabas first (follows Greek)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek name order against the Vulgate. The Vulgate's reversal placing Saul first may anticipate his emerging prominence. For Vulgate fidelity, the order should be reversed to שָׁאוּל וְאֶת-בַּר-נַבָּא.

### 13:23 — "Salvatorem" vs. "Redeemer"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "salvatorem Jesum" (savior Jesus). The Greek has σωτῆρα Ἰησοῦν (savior Jesus). The Hebrew renders this as גּוֹאֵל (redeemer).
- **Vulgate**: "salvatorem" — savior
- **Hebrew**: גּוֹאֵל — redeemer/kinsman-redeemer
- **Note**: גּוֹאֵל (go'el) is a rich OT term denoting the kinsman-redeemer (Ruth 3:9, 4:1-6) and God as Israel's redeemer (Isa 41:14, 43:14, 44:6). While theologically resonant, the standard Hebrew for "savior" is מוֹשִׁיעַ (cf. Isa 43:3, 45:15, 49:26). The גּוֹאֵל choice imports kinship-redemption theology not explicit in the Latin or Greek.

### 13:33 — Psalm 2 quotation
- **Issue**: All three traditions quote Ps 2:7 correctly. The Hebrew בְּנִי אַתָּה אֲנִי הַיּוֹם יְלִדְתִּיךָ matches the Masoretic Hebrew of Psalm 2:7 precisely.
- **Note**: This is divine speech — God the Father declaring the Son. Red-text material. The alignment across all traditions is exact.

### 13:34 — Isaiah 55:3 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate quotes "dabo vobis sancta David fidelia" (I will give you the holy faithful things of David). The Hebrew renders this as אֶתֵּן לָכֶם חַסְדֵי דָוִד הַנֶּאֱמָנִים.
- **Vulgate**: "sancta David fidelia" — the holy things of David, faithful
- **Hebrew**: חַסְדֵי דָוִד הַנֶּאֱמָנִים — the faithful mercies/kindnesses of David
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Masoretic text of Isa 55:3 (חַסְדֵי דָוִד הַנֶּאֱמָנִים) rather than the Vulgate's "sancta...fidelia." The LXX ὅσια Δαυίδ τὰ πιστά corresponds more to "holy things" (sancta) than to "kindnesses" (חַסְדֵי). However, since the Hebrew OT source naturally governs OT citations in the Hebrew NT, this is an expected and acceptable alignment.

### 13:35 — Psalm 16:10 quotation
- **Hebrew**: לֹא-תִתֵּן חֲסִידְךָ לִרְאוֹת שָׁחַת
- **Note**: This exactly matches the Masoretic Ps 16:10. The Vulgate's "non dabis sanctum tuum videre corruptionem" corresponds. The key christological term חֲסִידְךָ (your holy one) is precisely right. This is divine speech quoted by Paul — red-text material.

### 13:47 — Isaiah 49:6 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Posui te in lucem gentium, ut sis in salutem usque ad extremum terrae." The Hebrew renders this as נְתַתִּיךָ לְאוֹר גּוֹיִם לִהְיוֹת יְשׁוּעָתִי עַד-קְצֵה הָאָרֶץ.
- **Note**: This closely follows the Masoretic Isa 49:6. This is God's speech to the Servant, quoted by Paul as applying to the apostolic mission. Red-text material (OT divine speech applied christologically).

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 13:1 — "Collactaneus"
- **Hebrew**: אֲשֶׁר גֻּדַּל עִם-הוֹרְדוֹס
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "collactaneus" (foster-brother, one nursed together with) is rendered as אֲשֶׁר גֻּדַּל עִם (who was raised with). The Hebrew captures the sense accurately. The root g-d-l (to grow/raise) is standard biblical usage for upbringing.

### 13:10 — Paul's denunciation of Elymas
- **Hebrew**: אַתָּה הַמָּלֵא כָל-מִרְמָה וְכָל-עַוְלָה בֶּן-בְּלִיַּעַל וְשׂוֹנֵא כָל-צֶדֶק
- **Note**: Powerful Hebrew rhetoric. בֶּן-בְּלִיַּעַל (son of Belial) is a stronger rendering than the Vulgate's "fili diaboli" (son of the devil), since it roots the insult in OT diction (Deut 13:14, Judg 19:22, 1 Sam 2:12). The Hebrew phrase carries both the meaning of worthlessness and association with evil.

### 13:11 — "Caligo et tenebrae"
- **Hebrew**: אֲפֵלָה וַחֲשֵׁכָה
- **Note**: The pairing אֲפֵלָה וַחֲשֵׁכָה (darkness and gloom) echoes Joel 2:2 and Zeph 1:15 (יוֹם חֹשֶׁךְ וַאֲפֵלָה). The reversed order from the Vulgate's "caligo et tenebrae" is stylistically natural in Hebrew.

### 13:17 — Exodus language
- **Hebrew**: וּבִזְרוֹעַ רָמָה הוֹצִיאָם מִשָּׁם
- **Note**: The phrase בִּזְרוֹעַ רָמָה (with an uplifted arm) directly echoes Exod 6:6 and Deut 5:15. This is precisely the OT idiom for the Exodus deliverance — excellent register.

### 13:22 — "Virum secundum cor meum"
- **Hebrew**: אִישׁ כִּלְבָבִי
- **Note**: The Hebrew אִישׁ כִּלְבָבִי (a man after my heart) is the standard OT phrasing from 1 Sam 13:14. This is divine speech about David and is red-text material. The Hebrew matches the OT source exactly.

### 13:36 — "Dormivit"
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּגְוַע וַיֵּאָסֵף אֶל-אֲבוֹתָיו
- **Note**: The Vulgate's single "dormivit" (he slept) is expanded to the double formula וַיִּגְוַע וַיֵּאָסֵף אֶל-אֲבוֹתָיו (he expired and was gathered to his fathers). This is the patriarchal death formula from Genesis (Gen 25:8, 35:29, 49:33). While the expansion goes beyond the Latin, the biblical register is excellent and thematically appropriate for a summary of David's life.

## Other Notes

### 13:2 — Holy Spirit speaking
- **Note**: The Holy Spirit's words "Segregate mihi Saulum et Barnabam in opus ad quod assumpsi eos" are direct divine speech. Red-text material. The Hebrew הַבְדִּילוּ לִי (set apart for me) echoes the priestly consecration language of Leviticus.

### 13:9 — Name transition: Saul to Paul
- **Note**: This is the pivotal moment where "Saulus, qui et Paulus" appears. From this point forward in Acts, the apostle is consistently called Paul (פּוֹלוֹס in Hebrew). The double naming formula marks the transition to the Gentile mission.

### 13:14-41 — Paul's Antioch Pisidian sermon
- **Note**: This is Paul's first major sermon in Acts and the longest recorded in his missionary journeys. It recapitulates salvation history from the Exodus through David to Jesus. The Hebrew translation maintains good biblical narrative register throughout, with appropriate OT echoes at each stage of the history.
