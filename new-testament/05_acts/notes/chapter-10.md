# Acts Chapter 10 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 10:6 — Vulgate expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate 10:6 ends with "hic dicet tibi quid te oporteat facere" (he will tell you what you must do). The SBLGNT omits this clause entirely. The Hebrew includes it: הוּא יֹאמַר לְךָ אֶת-אֲשֶׁר עָלֶיךָ לַעֲשׂוֹת.
- **Vulgate**: Includes the clause about Peter telling Cornelius what he must do.
- **Greek**: Ends after "whose house is by the sea" with no further clause.
- **Hebrew**: Includes the additional clause, following the Vulgate tradition here.
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly preserves the Vulgate reading. No action needed.

### 10:30 — Cornelius fasting
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "orans eram hora nona" (I was praying at the ninth hour), while the Greek has "ἤμην τὴν ἐνάτην προσευχόμενος" with a variant about fasting. The Hebrew adds צָם (fasting): הָיִיתִי צָם עַד-הַשָּׁעָה הַזֹּאת.
- **Vulgate**: Does not mention fasting at this point in all manuscripts, though some Vulgate recensions include "jejunans."
- **Greek**: SBLGNT similarly lacks explicit fasting, though the Western text adds νηστεύων.
- **Hebrew**: Includes fasting explicitly.
- **Note**: The Clementine Vulgate text does not include "jejunans" in 10:30. The Hebrew addition of צָם goes beyond the authoritative text.

### 10:32 — Vulgate expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate 10:32 ends with no additional clause beyond the location description. The Hebrew adds a bracketed clause: [אֲשֶׁר בְּבוֹאוֹ יְדַבֶּר-לָךְ] (who when he comes will speak to you). The brackets indicate awareness that this is a textual addition.
- **Vulgate**: Does not include a clause about Peter speaking to Cornelius here (that clause appeared in 10:6).
- **Greek**: No corresponding clause in SBLGNT.
- **Hebrew**: Includes the clause in brackets.
- **Note**: The brackets appropriately mark this as secondary. The clause should be removed to match the Vulgate, which already includes this material in 10:6.

## Content Alignment

### 10:14 — "Commune et immundum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "commune et immundum" (common and unclean). The Greek has κοινὸν καὶ ἀκάθαρτον. The Hebrew renders this as כָּל-פִּגּוּל וְטָמֵא.
- **Vulgate**: "commune" (common/profane) paired with "immundum" (unclean)
- **Hebrew**: פִּגּוּל (abomination, specifically sacrificial defilement) paired with טָמֵא (unclean)
- **Note**: The Hebrew פִּגּוּל is a strong choice. In Leviticus (7:18, 19:7), it refers specifically to sacrificial meat rendered unfit. The Vulgate's "commune" is more neutral, meaning ritually common/profane rather than specifically abominable. A closer Hebrew rendering would be חֹל (profane/common), which is in fact used in 10:28 (חֹל אוֹ-טָמֵא). The inconsistency between 10:14 (פִּגּוּל) and 10:28 (חֹל) for the same Vulgate term is notable.

### 10:21 — Bracketed clause
- **Issue**: The Hebrew 10:21 includes a bracketed phrase [הַנִּשְׁלָחִים אֵלָיו מֵאֵת קָרְנֵילִיּוֹס] (who were sent to him from Cornelius). Neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT include this qualifier here.
- **Vulgate**: "Descendens autem Petrus ad viros" — simply "to the men."
- **Hebrew**: Adds a gloss identifying which men.
- **Note**: The bracketed gloss is an explanatory interpolation. It should be removed for Vulgate fidelity.

### 10:48 — "In nomine Domini Jesu Christi" vs. Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in nomine Domini Jesu Christi" (in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ). The Greek has ἐν τῷ ὀνόματι Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ (in the name of Jesus Christ, without "Lord"). The Hebrew reads בְּשֵׁם הָאָדוֹן (in the name of the Lord), omitting "Jesus Christ" entirely.
- **Vulgate**: "in nomine Domini Jesu Christi" — full title
- **Greek**: "in the name of Jesus Christ" — no "Lord"
- **Hebrew**: בְּשֵׁם הָאָדוֹן — "in the name of the Lord" only
- **Note**: The Hebrew diverges from both traditions. It should read בְּשֵׁם הָאָדוֹן יֵשׁוּעַ הַמָּשִׁיחַ to match the Vulgate.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 10:2 — Cornelius description
- **Hebrew**: חָסִיד וִירֵא אֱלֹהִים
- **Note**: The Vulgate "religiosus, ac timens Deum" is rendered well. חָסִיד captures "religiosus" with a strong biblical resonance (cf. Pss 4:4, 12:2). The pairing with ירא אלהים is natural biblical diction for a God-fearer.

### 10:10 — "Mentis excessus"
- **Hebrew**: נָפְלָה עָלָיו תַּרְדֵּמָה
- **Note**: The Vulgate "mentis excessus" (ecstasy of mind) and the Greek ἔκστασις both denote a trance or ecstatic state. The Hebrew תַּרְדֵּמָה (deep sleep) is the word used for Adam's sleep in Gen 2:21 and Abraham's in Gen 15:12. While it captures the supernatural quality, תַּרְדֵּמָה suggests unconscious sleep rather than visionary trance. The word חִזָּיוֹן or a construct with רוּח might better render the ecstatic dimension.

### 10:34 — "Non est personarum acceptor Deus"
- **Hebrew**: הָאֱלֹהִים אֵינֶנּוּ נֹשֵׂא פָנִים
- **Note**: Excellent. נֹשֵׂא פָנִים (lifter of faces, i.e., one who shows partiality) directly echoes Deut 10:17, where God is described as לֹא-יִשָּׂא פָנִים. This is precisely the OT concept behind the Vulgate's "personarum acceptor."

### 10:38 — "Benefaciendo"
- **Hebrew**: עֹשֵׂה חֶסֶד
- **Note**: The Vulgate "benefaciendo" (doing good) is rendered as עֹשֵׂה חֶסֶד (doing covenant-kindness). This is theologically richer than the Latin, importing the covenantal dimension of חֶסֶד. A more neutral rendering would be מֵיטִיב (doing good), but the choice is defensible and has strong biblical resonance.

### 10:44 — "Cecidit Spiritus Sanctus"
- **Hebrew**: רוּחַ הַקֹּדֶשׁ צָלְחָה
- **Note**: The Vulgate "cecidit" (fell upon) and Greek ἐπέπεσε both use the image of falling. The Hebrew צָלְחָה (rushed upon) echoes Judg 14:6, 14:19, 15:14, and 1 Sam 10:6, 10:10, where the Spirit of the Lord "rushes upon" individuals. This is a strong, idiomatic biblical choice that captures the sudden, powerful nature of the event, even though it does not literally mean "fell."

## Other Notes

### 10:9-16 — Peter's vision and dietary law
- **Note**: Peter's vision of clean and unclean animals is the pivotal moment opening the door to Gentile inclusion. The divine voice "Quod Deus purificavit, tu commune ne dixeris" (10:15) constitutes direct divine speech and is red-text material. The Hebrew rendering אֶת-אֲשֶׁר טִהַר הָאֱלֹהִים אַתָּה אַל-תְּטַמְּאֶנּוּ is strong, though note it renders "commune ne dixeris" (do not call common) as אַל-תְּטַמְּאֶנּוּ (do not defile it), which shifts the sense from speech about status to an act of defilement.

### 10:13 — Divine command
- **Note**: The voice commanding "Surge, Petre: occide, et manduca" is divine speech (red-text). The Hebrew קוּם פֶּטְרוֹס טְבַח וֶאֱכֹל uses the biblical imperative forms well.

### 10:20 — Spirit speaking
- **Note**: The Spirit's words in 10:19-20 ("Ecce viri tres quaerunt te. Surge itaque...quia ego misi illos") constitute divine speech of the Holy Spirit. This is red-text material. The Hebrew אָנֹכִי שְׁלַחְתִּים (I sent them) uses the emphatic first-person pronoun, appropriate for divine speech.
