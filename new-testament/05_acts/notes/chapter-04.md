# Acts Chapter 4 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 37 verses align with Vulgate numbering.

## Content Alignment

### 4:8 — "audite" absent from Greek, present in Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Principes populi, et seniores, audite" (Rulers of the people and elders, hear). The Greek has only "Archontes tou laou kai presbyteroi" (Rulers of the people and elders) with no "hear" command. The Hebrew adds "shim'uni" (hear me), aligning with the Vulgate's "audite."
- **Vulgate**: "Principes populi, et seniores, audite"
- **Hebrew**: "shim'uni rashei ha'am veziknei Yisrael" (hear me, heads of the people and elders of Israel)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Vulgate in including the imperative. However, it adds "of Israel" (Yisrael), which is absent from both the Vulgate and the Greek.

### 4:10 — "Domini nostri" Vulgate expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in nomine Domini nostri Jesu Christi Nazareni" (in the name of our Lord Jesus Christ the Nazarene). The Greek has simply "en to onomati Iesou Christou tou Nazoraios" (in the name of Jesus Christ the Nazarene), without "Domini nostri" (our Lord). The Hebrew omits "our Lord," following the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: "in nomine Domini nostri Jesu Christi Nazareni"
- **Hebrew**: "beshem Yeshua haMashiach haNatzri" (in the name of Jesus the Messiah the Nazarene)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Domini nostri" is a significant addition reflecting developed Christological language. The Hebrew should include "Adonenu" to follow the Vulgate.

### 4:25 — Disordered syntax and Psalm 2 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "qui Spiritu Sancto per os patris nostri David pueri tui dixisti" (who by the Holy Spirit through the mouth of our father David your servant said). The Hebrew renders this with brackets: "asher amarta befi avdekha David [avinu beRuach haQodesh]" (who said through the mouth of your servant David [our father, by the Holy Spirit]), placing the bracketed material differently.
- **Vulgate**: "Spiritu Sancto per os patris nostri David pueri tui"
- **Note**: The brackets in the Hebrew indicate textual uncertainty. The Greek text of this verse is notoriously difficult syntactically. The Hebrew arrangement is reasonable.

### 4:27 — "sanctum puerum tuum" vs "avdekha haQadosh"
- **Issue**: Same pattern as 3:13. The Vulgate reads "sanctum puerum tuum" (your holy child/son). The Hebrew has "Yeshua avdekha haQadosh" (Jesus your holy servant).
- **Vulgate**: "sanctum puerum tuum Jesum, quem unxisti"
- **Hebrew**: "Yeshua avdekha haQadosh asher mashachta" (Jesus your holy servant whom you anointed)
- **Note**: Again, the Vulgate's "puer" carries filial connotations in context. The Hebrew's "eved" (servant) follows the Greek "pais." See note at 3:13.

### 4:27 — "in civitate ista" word order
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Convenerunt enim vere in civitate ista" (they truly gathered in this city). The Hebrew has brackets around "ba'ir hazot" (in this city), suggesting textual awareness.
- **Note**: The brackets appropriately flag this reading, which appears in some manuscripts but not all.

### 4:30 — "filii tui" vs "avdekha"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "per nomen sancti filii tui Jesu" (through the name of your holy Son Jesus). The Greek has "paidos sou" (your servant/child). The Hebrew has "avdekha haQadosh" (your holy servant).
- **Vulgate**: "sancti filii tui Jesu"
- **Hebrew**: "Yeshua avdekha haQadosh"
- **Note**: Fourth occurrence of this pattern (3:13, 3:26, 4:27, 4:30). The Vulgate consistently reads "filius" (son) where the Hebrew consistently reads "eved" (servant). This is a systematic divergence from the Vulgate.

### 4:33 — "Jesu Christi Domini nostri" expansion
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "resurrectionis Jesu Christi Domini nostri" (of the resurrection of Jesus Christ our Lord). The Greek has "tes anastaseos tou kyriou Iesou" (of the resurrection of the Lord Jesus). The Hebrew has "tequmat ha'Adon Yeshua" (the resurrection of the Lord Jesus), following the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: "Jesu Christi Domini nostri"
- **Hebrew**: "ha'Adon Yeshua" (the Lord Jesus)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's expanded form "Jesus Christ our Lord" should be reflected.

### 4:36 — "Filius consolationis" vs "ben-hanechamah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate interprets Barnabas as "Filius consolationis" (Son of consolation). The Greek has "huios parakleseos" (son of encouragement/exhortation). The Hebrew renders "ben-hanechamah" (son of comfort/consolation).
- **Vulgate**: "Filius consolationis"
- **Hebrew**: "ben-hanechamah"
- **Note**: Good alignment. "Nechamah" (comfort/consolation) matches the Vulgate's "consolationis" well. The Greek "paraklesis" can mean either encouragement or consolation; the Vulgate and Hebrew both choose the consolation sense.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 4:11 — Psalm 118:22 quotation
- **Hebrew**: "vehu ha'even asher me'astem atem habonim vattehi lerosh pinnah"
- **Note**: Closely follows MT Psalm 118:22. Excellent rendering. The verb forms align with the biblical psalm text. The Vulgate's "lapis qui reprobatus est" (the stone which was rejected) matches "ha'even asher me'astem."

### 4:12 — "vehayeshu'ah" for "salus"
- **Hebrew**: "ve'ein hayeshu'ah be'acher" (and there is no salvation in another)
- **Note**: "Yeshu'ah" (salvation) is the obvious and best choice for "salus." The phonetic resonance with the name "Yeshua" (Jesus) is striking and theologically pointed, though likely coincidental to the translation.

### 4:24 — "Adonai" for "Domine/Despota"
- **Hebrew**: "Adonai atah ha'El" (Lord, you are God)
- **Note**: The Greek uses "Despota" (Sovereign Master), not the usual "Kyrios." The Vulgate simply has "Domine" (Lord). The Hebrew's "Adonai" is appropriate, though "Ribon" (sovereign/master) would more precisely capture "Despota."

### 4:32 — "lenachalat kullam" for "omnia communia"
- **Hebrew**: "ki hakol hayah lahem lenachalat kullam" (for everything was for them as an inheritance of all)
- **Note**: "Nachalat kullam" (inheritance of all) is an interpretive expansion of the Vulgate's simple "omnia communia" (all things in common). The concept of shared "nachalah" (inheritance/possession) adds a biblical-covenantal nuance not present in the Latin. A more literal rendering might be "hakol hayah lahem beshtaf" or "meshutefet."

## Other Notes

### 4:25-26 — Psalm 2 quotation
The quotation of Psalm 2:1-2 follows the MT closely: "lammah ragshu goyim ule'ummim yehgu riq" (why do nations rage and peoples plot vanity). The Vulgate's "fremuerunt... meditati sunt inania" aligns well. The application to Herod and Pilate in v.27 makes the Christological typology explicit.

### 4:32-37 — Community of goods
This section describes the early communal life. The Vulgate's "cor unum et anima una" (one heart and one soul) is rendered "lev echad venefesh achat" — a direct and elegant Hebrew rendering that echoes biblical expressions of unity (cf. 1 Chronicles 12:38).
