# Acts Chapter 19 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 19:40-41 — Verse split between Vulgate and SBLGNT
- **Issue**: The SBLGNT 19:40 contains material that the Vulgate splits across 19:40 and 19:41. The Vulgate 19:40 ends with "concursus istius," and 19:41 adds "Et cum haec dixisset, dimisit ecclesiam" (And when he had said these things, he dismissed the assembly). The SBLGNT includes the dismissal clause at the end of 19:40 itself: "kai tauta eipon apelysen ten ekklesian."
- **Greek 19:41**: Empty in SBLGNT (no verse).
- **Hebrew 19:40**: Ends at "hamehuma hazot" (this tumult), omitting the dismissal.
- **Hebrew 19:41**: "vayehi ka'asher killah ledabber kaddevarim ha'eleh vayeshallach et-haqahal" (when he finished speaking these words, he dismissed the assembly).
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly provides a separate 19:41, aligning with the Vulgate versification. However, the Vulgate Latin field in the data is empty for 19:41 — the Vulgate text should read: "Et cum haec dixisset, dimisit ecclesiam."

## Content Alignment

### 19:4 — "hoc est, in Jesum" vs. "vehu haMashiach Yeshua"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "hoc est, in Jesum" (that is, in Jesus) identifies the one coming after John as Jesus. The Greek similarly has "tout' estin eis ton Iesoun." The Hebrew renders "vehu haMashiach Yeshua" (and he is the Messiah, Jesus), adding "haMashiach" (the Messiah/Christ) which is absent from both the Vulgate and Greek.
- **Vulgate**: "in eum qui venturus esset post ipsum ut crederent, hoc est, in Jesum"
- **Hebrew**: "she-ya'aminu ba'asher yavo acharav vehu haMashiach Yeshua"
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds the title "haMashiach" (Messiah) which is not present in the Vulgate or SBLGNT. This is a theological expansion.

### 19:9 — "viam Domini" vs. "ten hodon"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "maledicentes viam Domini" (cursing the way of the Lord). The Greek reads "kakologountes ten hodon" (speaking evil of the Way). The Hebrew reads "derekh hahu" (that way), following the Greek's bare reference without "Domini."
- **Vulgate**: "viam Domini"
- **Hebrew**: "haderekh hahu" (that way)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek by omitting "the Lord." For Vulgate alignment, "derekh YHWH" or "derekh haAdon" would be needed.

### 19:10 — "verbum Domini" vs. "devar Yeshua haAdon"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "verbum Domini" (the word of the Lord). The Greek reads "ton logon tou kyriou" (the word of the Lord). The Hebrew reads "devar Yeshua haAdon" (the word of Jesus the Lord), adding the name "Yeshua" which is not present in either the Vulgate or Greek.
- **Vulgate**: "audirent verbum Domini"
- **Hebrew**: "devar Yeshua haAdon"
- **Note**: The Hebrew expands "the Lord" into "Jesus the Lord," an interpretive addition. The Vulgate simply has "Domini."

### 19:16 — "amborum" vs. "kulam"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "dominatus amborum" (mastered both of them) says the demoniac overpowered two of them. The Greek "amphoteron" also means "both." The Hebrew uses "vayyigbar aleihem vayyikhbeshem" (he overpowered them and subdued them) without specifying a number, thus implying all of them.
- **Vulgate**: "dominatus amborum"
- **Greek**: "katakyrieusas amphoteron" (mastering both)
- **Note**: This is a textual variant. The SBLGNT reads "amphoteron" (both), suggesting only two were present for this particular encounter, while the Hebrew implies all seven sons from 19:14. The Vulgate's "amborum" (both) should be followed.

### 19:19 — "quinquaginta millium" vs. "chamesh ribbo dinarim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "denariorum quinquaginta millium" (fifty thousand denarii) matches the Greek "arguriou myriadas pente" (five myriads of silver). The Hebrew renders "chamesh ribbo dinarim" (five myriads of dinars), using "dinarim" where the Vulgate specifies "denariorum."
- **Note**: Adequate rendering. The Hebrew "dinarim" is a recognized loanword equivalent to denarii. The Greek is ambiguous (just "silver pieces"), while the Vulgate specifies denarii. The Hebrew's "dinarim" appropriately follows the Vulgate.

### 19:20 — "verbum Dei" vs. "devar YHWH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "verbum Dei" (the word of God). The Greek reads "tou kyriou ho logos" (the word of the Lord). The Hebrew reads "devar YHWH" (the word of the LORD).
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "Dei" (God); the Greek has "kyriou" (Lord). The Hebrew uses YHWH, which maps to the Greek "kyriou" rather than to the Vulgate "Dei." Minor alignment issue.

### 19:27 — "Diana" / "Artemis" rendering
- **Issue**: The Vulgate consistently uses "Diana" (the Roman name) while the Greek uses "Artemis" (the Greek name). The Hebrew transliterates the Greek form: "Artemis." Additionally, the Hebrew renders "theas" (goddess) as "hammelekhet hagedolah" (the great queen), an unusual choice.
- **Vulgate**: "magnae Dianae templum"
- **Hebrew**: "heikhal Artemis hammelekhet hagedolah"
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses the Greek name rather than the Vulgate's Roman name. This is a minor point since both refer to the same deity. The translation of "goddess" as "queen" (malkah) rather than a more literal term avoids using "elohah" (goddess) for a pagan deity, which may be a deliberate theological choice. Cf. Jer 7:18, 44:17 where "melekhet hashamayim" (queen of heaven) is used for a pagan goddess.

### 19:35 — "Jovisque prolis" vs. "tsalmah hayyored min-hashamayim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "Jovisque prolis" (and the offspring of Jupiter) refers to the image that fell from the sky. The Greek "tou diopetous" means "fallen from Zeus/heaven." The Hebrew renders "tsalmah hayyored min-hashamayim" (her image that descended from heaven), avoiding any mention of Jupiter/Zeus.
- **Vulgate**: "cultricem esse magnae Dianae, Jovisque prolis"
- **Hebrew**: "uletsalmah hayyored min-hashamayim"
- **Note**: The Hebrew paraphrases rather than preserving the reference to Jupiter. The Vulgate's "Jovisque prolis" explicitly names Jupiter as the divine origin. The Hebrew simply says "descended from heaven." While the sense is preserved, the specific pagan theological reference is softened.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 19:9 — "beit hamidrash asher leTurnus" (the study house of Tyrannus)
- **Hebrew**: "beit hamidrash asher leTurnus"
- **Note**: Rendering "schola" (Vulgate) / "schole" (Greek) as "beit hamidrash" (house of study) is an excellent choice that uses the well-known rabbinic institution as an equivalent for the Greek philosophical school. This creates a natural cultural bridge for Hebrew readers.

### 19:13 — "nevonei lachash" (skilled in whispering/incantation)
- **Hebrew**: "nevonei lachash vesovavim ba'aretz"
- **Note**: The term "nevonei lachash" (skilled in incantation) for "exorcists" is a creative coinage. "Lachash" means "whispering/enchantment" (Isa 3:3 "uchkhom charashim unvon lachash" — wise enchanter and skilled in incantation). The phrase "sovavim ba'aretz" (going about in the land) captures "circumeuntes" (going around) well. Strong biblical Hebrew.

### 19:15 — "et-Yeshua ani makkir ve'et-Polos ani yodea" (Jesus I know and Paul I recognize)
- **Hebrew**: "et-Yeshua ani makkir ve'et-Polos ani yodea"
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses two different verbs for "know" — "makkir" (recognize/acknowledge) for Jesus and "yodea" (know about) for Paul. This mirrors the Greek distinction between "ginosko" (know intimately) and "epistamai" (know of/am acquainted with). The Vulgate similarly distinguishes "novi" and "scio." Excellent precision.

### 19:28-34 — Crowd chant
- **Hebrew**: "gedolah Artemis shel ha'Efesim"
- **Note**: The crowd chant "gedolah Artemis shel ha'Efesim" (Great is Artemis of the Ephesians) is simple and effective. The use of "shel" (of, belonging to) rather than a construct state is slightly Mishnaic in register but works for a chanted slogan.

### 19:35 — "vayyahas sofer ha'ir" (the scribe of the city quieted)
- **Hebrew**: "vayyahas sofer ha'ir et-ha'am"
- **Note**: "Sofer ha'ir" (scribe of the city) for "grammateus" / Vulgate "scriba" is a precise rendering. The verb "yahas" (hiphil of husah, to quiet) is good biblical Hebrew for restoring calm (cf. Neh 8:11 "vehaLeviyyim machshim et-kol-ha'am").

## Other Notes

### 19:11-12 — Contact relics (sudaria et semicinctia)
The passage about Paul's handkerchiefs and aprons healing the sick is significant for Catholic theology of relics. The Hebrew "sudarim vachagorot" transliterates "sudaria" and uses "chagorot" (belts/girdles) for "semicinctia" (work aprons). The Vulgate's "sudaria et semicinctia" is adequately preserved.

### 19:23-40 — The Ephesus Riot
This extended narrative is one of the most vivid scenes in Acts. The Hebrew maintains good narrative pace with waw-consecutive forms throughout. The speech of the town clerk (19:35-40) is rendered in appropriately formal register, befitting an official address.
