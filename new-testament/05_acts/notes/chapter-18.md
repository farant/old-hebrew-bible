# Acts Chapter 18 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 18:21 — Vulgate-plus content
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes a clause absent from the SBLGNT: "Oportet me diem festum sequentem facere Jerosolymis" (I must keep the coming feast in Jerusalem). The Greek simply has "I will return to you, God willing." The Hebrew includes this additional material: "chayyav ani lachog et-hechag habba biYerushalayim" (I am obligated to celebrate the coming feast in Jerusalem).
- **Vulgate**: "sed valefaciens, et dicens : Iterum revertar ad vos, Deo volente : profectus est ab Epheso."
- **Hebrew**: Includes the feast obligation clause matching the Vulgate tradition.
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate's longer reading. This clause appears in the Western/Byzantine text tradition and is part of the received Catholic text. Good alignment.

## Content Alignment

### 18:4 — "interponens nomen Domini Jesu" — Vulgate plus
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "interponens nomen Domini Jesu" (introducing the name of the Lord Jesus) between the synagogue preaching and the persuasion. This clause is absent from the SBLGNT and from the Hebrew.
- **Vulgate**: "Et disputabat in synagoga per omne sabbatum, interponens nomen Domini Jesu : suadebatque Judaeis et Graecis."
- **Greek/Hebrew**: Both lack the clause about introducing the name of the Lord Jesus.
- **Note**: The Hebrew should include this Vulgate-plus content. It is a significant omission since it names the content of Paul's synagogue preaching.

### 18:7 — "Titi Justi" — name variation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Titi Justi" (Titus Justus), giving both names. The SBLGNT has "Titiou Ioustou" in some manuscripts but the main reading is "Ioustou" (Justus alone). The Hebrew has only "Yustos" (Justus), omitting "Titus."
- **Vulgate**: "nomine Titi Justi"
- **Hebrew**: "ushemo Yustos" (and his name is Justus)
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits the praenomen "Titus." For Vulgate alignment, both names should be present.

### 18:17 — "omnes Graeci" vs. "omnes"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "apprehendentes autem omnes Sosthenem" (all seizing Sosthenes), without specifying who "all" were. The SBLGNT has "pantes" (all) without ethnic specification. The Hebrew reads "kol-haYevanim" (all the Greeks), adding an ethnic identifier not present in the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "Apprehendentes autem omnes Sosthenem"
- **Hebrew**: "vayyochezu khol-haYevanim" (all the Greeks seized)
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "haYevanim" (the Greeks) which follows the Byzantine/Western reading (D, some minuscules). The Vulgate simply says "omnes" (all). Since we follow the Vulgate, the ethnic identification is an addition.

### 18:26 — "viam Domini" vs. "hodon tou theou"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "viam Domini" (the way of the Lord). The Greek reads "ten hodon tou theou" (the way of God). The Hebrew reads "derekh haElohim" (the way of God), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "diligentius exposuerunt ei viam Domini"
- **Hebrew**: "derekh haElohim" (the way of God)
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek "theou" (God) rather than the Vulgate "Domini" (Lord). For Vulgate alignment, this should be "derekh haAdon" or "derekh YHWH."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 18:6 — "dimkhem beroshekhem" (your blood on your heads)
- **Hebrew**: "dimkhem beroshekhem ve'anokhi naqi"
- **Note**: This is excellent biblical Hebrew, directly echoing 2 Sam 1:16 ("damkha al-roshekha") and Josh 2:19 ("damav berosho"). The self-declaration of innocence "anokhi naqi" mirrors the biblical idiom well. Paul's words deliberately echo OT blood-guilt formulas.

### 18:9-10 — Vision of the Lord
- **Hebrew**: "al-tira ki im-dabber ve'al-techesheh ki-immekha anokhi"
- **Note**: The language echoes multiple prophetic commissioning formulas. "Al-tira" (do not fear) is the standard divine encouragement (Gen 15:1, Isa 41:10). "Ki-immekha anokhi" (I am with you) mirrors Jer 1:8 and Isa 41:10 precisely. The Vulgate "Noli timere... ego sum tecum" is well rendered in prophetic Hebrew register.

### 18:12 — "sar medinat Akayya" (ruler of the province of Achaia)
- **Hebrew**: "sar medinat Akayya"
- **Note**: The Vulgate "proconsule Achaiae" is rendered with "sar medinah" (provincial ruler), which is a good biblical-era administrative term. Compare Esth 1:3, 3:12 where "medinah" designates a province. The choice of "sar" over a transliteration preserves biblical Hebrew style.

### 18:18 — "ki neder alav" (for a vow was upon him)
- **Hebrew**: "vaygallach et-rosho beQankhrai ki neder alav"
- **Note**: Good biblical vocabulary. "Neder" is the standard biblical term for a vow (Num 6:2, 30:3). The shaving of the head connects directly to Nazirite vow language in Numbers 6:18. The Vulgate "habebat enim votum" is well matched.

### 18:24 — "ish devarim vegadol bamiqra" (a man of words and great in Scripture)
- **Hebrew**: "ish devarim vegadol bamiqra"
- **Note**: "Ish devarim" literally means "a man of words/matters" — a somewhat underspecified rendering of the Vulgate "vir eloquens" (eloquent man) and Greek "aner logios" (learned/eloquent man). "Gadol bamiqra" (great in Scripture) effectively renders "potens in scripturis." The phrase "ish devarim" could also recall Moses's self-description in Exod 4:10 "lo ish devarim anokhi" (I am not a man of words), creating an interesting intertextual resonance.

## Other Notes

### 18:2 — The Edict of Claudius
The expulsion of Jews from Rome under Claudius (ca. 49 CE) is attested by Suetonius. The Hebrew renders Claudius as "Qlodiyos," a reasonable transliteration. All three texts agree on the historical details.

### 18:12-17 — The Gallio Inscription
The encounter with Gallio is one of the most important chronological anchors for Pauline chronology, datable to ca. 51-52 CE from the Delphi inscription. The Hebrew preserves Gallio's dismissive stance well. The phrase "lo shat libbo gam-lazot" (he did not set his heart even to this) in 18:17 is an excellent biblical idiom for indifference.
