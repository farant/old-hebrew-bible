# Acts Chapter 20 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 38 verses align between the Vulgate, SBLGNT, and Hebrew text.

## Content Alignment

### 20:4 — "Sopater Pyrrhi" — patronymic
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "Sopater Pyrrhi Beroeensis" includes the patronymic "Pyrrhi" (son of Pyrrhus). The Greek similarly has "Sopatros Pyrrou Beroiaios." The Hebrew includes it in brackets: "Sopatros [ben-Puros] haBro'i," indicating uncertainty.
- **Vulgate**: "Sopater Pyrrhi Beroeensis"
- **Hebrew**: "Sopatros [ben-Puros] haBro'i"
- **Note**: The Hebrew brackets suggest the patronymic is a textual variant, but the Vulgate includes it without reservation. For Vulgate alignment, the brackets should be removed.

### 20:15 — "Trogyllio" stop — Vulgate plus
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes "vanalen biTrogulion" (and we lodged at Trogyllium), which corresponds to a reading present in some manuscripts and reflected in the Vulgate tradition. The SBLGNT omits any mention of Trogyllium.
- **Vulgate**: "et sequenti die venimus Miletum" (and the following day we came to Miletus) — the Clementine Vulgate text does not explicitly mention Trogyllium in the main text.
- **Hebrew**: Includes the Trogyllium stop.
- **Note**: The Trogyllium reference appears in Western/Byzantine manuscripts. The Hebrew includes it, possibly following the TR/Byzantine tradition. The Clementine Vulgate's reading should be checked carefully; some Vulgate manuscripts include "manentes Trogylio" while the standard Clementine does not prominently feature it.

### 20:21 — "Jesum Christum" vs. "Yeshua haMashiach"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in Dominum nostrum Jesum Christum" (in our Lord Jesus Christ). The SBLGNT reads "eis ton kyrion hemon Iesoun" (in our Lord Jesus), omitting "Christon." The Hebrew reads "ba'Adoneinu Yeshua haMashiach" (in our Lord Jesus the Messiah).
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly includes "haMashiach" (Christ), matching the Vulgate's fuller reading.

### 20:25 — "regnum Dei" vs. "malkhut YHWH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "praedicans regnum Dei" (preaching the kingdom of God). The Greek reads "keryssontes ten basileian" (preaching the kingdom), without "tou theou" in some manuscripts. The Hebrew reads "malkhut YHWH" (the kingdom of the LORD).
- **Vulgate**: "regnum Dei" (kingdom of God)
- **Hebrew**: "malkhut YHWH" (kingdom of the LORD)
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses YHWH where the Vulgate has "Dei" (God). This is a translation choice that maps "God" to the Tetragrammaton. While not incorrect within Hebrew convention (God and YHWH are identified), it introduces the divine name where the source text has the common noun.

### 20:28 — "ecclesiam Dei" — critical textual issue
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ecclesiam Dei" (the church of God). The SBLGNT reads "ten ekklesian tou theou" (the church of God), though some manuscripts have "tou kyriou" (of the Lord). The Hebrew reads "adat haAdon" (the congregation of the Lord), following "kyriou" rather than "theou."
- **Vulgate**: "ecclesiam Dei" (church of God)
- **Hebrew**: "adat haAdon" (congregation of the Lord)
- **Note**: This is a significant divergence. The Vulgate explicitly reads "Dei" (God), which is theologically important because the verse continues "quam acquisivit sanguine suo" (which He acquired with His own blood). "Church of God, which He purchased with His own blood" has strong Christological implications (God's own blood). The Hebrew "adat haAdon" (congregation of the Lord) softens this. For Vulgate alignment, "adat haElohim" should be used.

### 20:28 — "episcopos" rendering
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "posuit episcopos" (placed you as bishops/overseers) uses the term that becomes the title of the Catholic episcopate. The Greek "etheto episkopous" (appointed as overseers). The Hebrew renders "lifqidim" (as overseers/appointees), from the root p-q-d.
- **Note**: "Paqid" (overseer) is an adequate biblical Hebrew equivalent. Cf. Num 31:14, 2 Kgs 11:15 where "paqid" denotes an appointed overseer. The term preserves the functional meaning without the later ecclesiastical connotation.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 20:7 — "be'echad bashabbat" (on the first of the sabbath/week)
- **Hebrew**: "be'echad bashabbat ka'asher ne'esfu hattalmidim livtsoa hallechem"
- **Note**: "Be'echad bashabbat" follows the biblical idiom for the first day of the week (cf. the Greek "te mia ton sabbaton"). "Livtsoa hallechem" (to break the bread) is good biblical Hebrew for the Eucharistic action, echoing Lam 4:4 and Isa 58:7.

### 20:9-10 — The Eutychus episode
- **Hebrew**: "vayyippol mehamador hashlishi lemattah vayyisa'uhu vehu met... ki nishmatov bo"
- **Note**: The phrasing "nishmatov bo" (his breath/soul is in him) directly echoes the Elijah/Elisha resuscitation narratives. Compare 1 Kgs 17:21-22 and 2 Kgs 4:34-35. Paul's action of "falling upon him" (vayyighar alav vaychabbequhu) mirrors Elisha's revival of the Shunammite's son. Excellent intertextual resonance.

### 20:24 — "merutsati" (my race/course)
- **Hebrew**: "mikkallot besimchah et-merutsati ve'et hasheirut"
- **Note**: "Merutsah" (race/running) for the Vulgate "cursum meum" and Greek "ton dromon mou" is good. The word appears in later Hebrew but the concept of the race/course of life is well attested. The addition of "besimchah" (with joy) renders a nuance present in some Greek manuscripts ("meta charas") but absent from the Vulgate "dummodo consummem cursum meum." This is a textual variant issue.

### 20:29 — "ze'evim azzim" (fierce wolves)
- **Hebrew**: "yavo'u betokakhem ze'evim azzim"
- **Note**: The Vulgate "lupi rapaces" (ravenous wolves) and Greek "lykoi bareis" (heavy/savage wolves). The Hebrew "ze'evim azzim" (fierce wolves) is excellent, echoing Hab 1:8 "ze'evei erev" (wolves of the evening) and Ezek 22:27 "ze'evim torfei taref" (wolves tearing prey). The adjective "azzim" (fierce/strong) is apt.

### 20:35 — "tov asher-titten mishshetiqqach" (it is better to give than to receive)
- **Hebrew**: "tov asher-titten mishshetiqqach"
- **Note**: This agraphon (unrecorded saying of Jesus) is rendered in clean, proverbial Hebrew that would fit naturally among the Proverbs. The comparative construction with "min" is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Prov 16:32 "tov erech appayim migibbor" — better is patience than a warrior).

### 20:37-38 — Farewell scene
- **Hebrew**: "vayyivku khulam bekhi gadol vayyiplu al-tsavvarei Polos vayyenashqu lo"
- **Note**: Beautiful biblical narrative. "Vayyiplu al-tsavvarei" (and they fell on his neck) echoes Gen 33:4 (Esau and Jacob) and Gen 46:29 (Joseph and Jacob). The emotional farewell scene is rendered in the finest biblical prose register.

## Other Notes

### 20:7 — "una sabbati" and the Lord's Day
The mention of gathering "on the first day of the week" to "break bread" is one of the earliest references to Sunday Christian worship. The Hebrew "be'echad bashabbat" follows the Jewish convention of counting days from the Sabbath. The Vulgate "una sabbati" preserves the Semitic idiom.

### 20:17-38 — Paul's Miletus Farewell
This is Paul's only speech to a Christian audience in Acts. The Hebrew renders the speech in elevated prophetic register, appropriate for its tone of testament and warning. The pastoral language (shepherds, wolves, flock) in 20:28-30 uses OT prophetic vocabulary (Ezek 34, Jer 23) to powerful effect.
