# Acts Chapter 3 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 26 verses align with Vulgate numbering.

## Content Alignment

### 3:1 — "orationis nonam" vs "tefilat haminchah"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ad horam orationis nonam" (the ninth hour of prayer). The Hebrew renders "litefilat haminchah basha'ah hateshi'it" (for the Minchah prayer at the ninth hour), inserting the rabbinic term "Minchah" which is absent from both the Vulgate and the Greek.
- **Vulgate**: "ad horam orationis nonam"
- **Note**: The addition of "Minchah" is a culturally accurate gloss — the ninth hour (3 PM) is indeed the Minchah prayer service. However, since the Vulgate does not specify which prayer, this is an interpretive addition by Delitzsch.

### 3:6 — "surge, et ambula" vs "qum hithalekh"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "surge, et ambula" (rise and walk), two commands. The Hebrew renders "qum hithalekh" (rise, walk about), which accurately reflects both the Vulgate's two imperatives. However, the Vulgate has "Nazareni" while the Greek has "Nazoraios." The Hebrew uses "haNatzri" following the standard Hebrew form.
- **Vulgate**: "in nomine Jesu Christi Nazareni surge, et ambula"
- **Note**: Good alignment overall. The Hebrew follows the Vulgate closely here.

### 3:12 — "potestate" vs "chasidutenu"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quasi nostra virtute aut potestate" (as if by our own power or authority). The Greek has "dynamei e eusebeia" (power or piety/godliness). The Hebrew renders "becochenu uvachasidutenu" (by our strength and by our piety), following the Greek "eusebeia" (piety) rather than the Vulgate's "potestate" (authority/power).
- **Vulgate**: "virtute aut potestate"
- **Greek**: "dynamei e eusebeia"
- **Hebrew**: "becochenu uvachasidutenu" (our strength and our piety)
- **Note**: The Vulgate diverges from the Greek here. "Potestate" (authority) differs from "eusebeia" (piety/godliness). The Hebrew follows the Greek. To follow the Vulgate, something like "besholtonenu" or "besiltanenu" would be needed for "potestate."

### 3:13 — "filium suum" vs "avdo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "glorificavit filium suum Jesum" (glorified his Son Jesus). The Greek has "ton paida autou" (his servant/child). The Hebrew renders "et-avdo Yeshua" (his servant Jesus), following the Greek "pais" in the sense of "servant" rather than the Vulgate's "filium" (son).
- **Vulgate**: "filium suum Jesum"
- **Hebrew**: "avdo Yeshua" (his servant Jesus)
- **Note**: This is theologically significant. The Vulgate consistently renders "pais" as "filius" (son) in this context (also 3:26, 4:27, 4:30), making an explicit Christological claim. The Hebrew's "eved" (servant) follows the Greek ambiguity. To follow the Vulgate, "beno" (his son) would be needed.

### 3:16 — "fides, quae per eum est" vs "emunatenu bo"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "fides, quae per eum est" (the faith which is through him). The Hebrew renders "emunatenu bo" (our faith in him), adding the first-person plural possessive "our" which is absent from the Vulgate and Greek. The Greek has "he pistis he di' autou" (the faith which is through him), not "our faith."
- **Vulgate**: "fides, quae per eum est"
- **Hebrew**: "emunatenu bo" (our faith in him)
- **Note**: The Hebrew's "our" is an interpretive addition, and "in him" is less precise than "through him" (di' autou / per eum).

### 3:20 — "a conspectu Domini" vs "millifnei YHVH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "a conspectu Domini" (from the face/presence of the Lord). The Greek has "apo prosopou tou kyriou" (from the face of the Lord). The Hebrew uses "millifnei YHVH" with the Tetragrammaton, which is a specific interpretive choice — identifying "the Lord" as YHVH.
- **Vulgate**: "a conspectu Domini"
- **Note**: Consistent with the Hebrew NT's general practice of using YHVH for "Kyrios/Dominus" in divine contexts. Appropriate here.

### 3:22 — "Dominus Deus vester" vs "YHVH Eloheikhem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Dominus Deus vester" (the Lord your God), quoting Deuteronomy 18:15. The Greek has "Kyrios ho theos." The Hebrew renders "YHVH Eloheikhem," directly citing the MT form. However, the Hebrew also adds "el-avotenu" (to our fathers), which the Vulgate does not have — the Vulgate says Moses told "the children of Israel."
- **Vulgate**: "Moyses quidem dixit: Quoniam prophetam suscitabit vobis Dominus Deus vester"
- **Hebrew**: "hen Moshe amar el-avotenu" (behold Moses said to our fathers)
- **Note**: The Vulgate says Moses spoke directly ("dixit"), and the addressees are "you" (Israel). The Hebrew changes this to "our fathers," which is an interpretive shift.

### 3:26 — "filium suum" again
- **Issue**: Same as 3:13. The Vulgate reads "suscitans filium suum" (raising up his Son). The Hebrew has "et-avdo Yeshua" (his servant Jesus).
- **Vulgate**: "filium suum"
- **Hebrew**: "avdo" (his servant)
- **Note**: See note at 3:13. The Vulgate's "filium" should be reflected consistently.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 3:2 — Proper gate name
- **Hebrew**: "sha'ar hamehuddar" (the splendid/adorned gate)
- **Note**: Good rendering of "Speciosa" (Beautiful). "Mehuddar" is not common in biblical Hebrew but is a suitable translation. The Vulgate's "Speciosa" and the Greek "Horaia" (Beautiful) are well served.

### 3:7 — Medical terminology
- **Hebrew**: "raglav veqarsullav" (his feet and his ankles)
- **Note**: "Qarsullav" (ankles) renders the Vulgate's "plantae" (soles of feet) and the Greek "sphydra" (ankles). The Hebrew follows the Greek anatomical term more closely than the Vulgate's "bases... et plantae" (bases and soles).

### 3:19 — "hinachamu veshuvu" for "poenitentiam... convertimini"
- **Hebrew**: "hinachamu veshuvu" (be comforted and return)
- **Note**: "Hinachamu" from root n-ch-m can mean both "repent" and "be comforted." In the Niphal, the repentance sense is present but secondary. The Vulgate's "poenitemini" (repent) and "convertimini" (be converted/turn) are two distinct actions. The Hebrew captures both adequately, though "nacham" in the Niphal is somewhat ambiguous.

### 3:21 — "letiqunam" for "restitutionis"
- **Hebrew**: "ad-yemei shuv kol-hadevarim letiqunam" (until the days of the return of all things to their restoration)
- **Note**: "Tiqun" (restoration/repair) is an excellent choice for "restitutionis" (restoration). The word carries deep resonance in later Jewish thought (tikkun olam) and adequately conveys the eschatological sense.

### 3:25 — "benei haneviim uvenei haberit"
- **Hebrew**: "atem benei haneviim uvenei haberit" (you are sons of the prophets and sons of the covenant)
- **Note**: Faithful and elegant rendering of "filii prophetarum, et testamenti." "Benei haberit" (sons of the covenant) is an excellent Hebrew idiom. The Vulgate's "testamenti" (of the covenant/testament) is well captured.

## Other Notes

### 3:13-15 — Petrine Christological titles
Three titles appear in rapid succession: "his servant/son" (avdo/filium), "the Holy and Just One" (haQadosh vehaTzaddiq / sanctum et justum), and "the Author of Life" (sar hachayyim / auctorem vitae). The Vulgate's "filium" for "pais" is a significant Christological reading that elevates the title from "servant" to "Son" throughout this chapter. The Hebrew consistently uses "eved" (servant), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.

### 3:22-23 — Deuteronomy 18:15-19 quotation
The quotation of Moses' prophecy follows the MT closely. The Vulgate's "exterminabitur de plebe" matches the Hebrew "venichretah me'ammeha" (shall be cut off from her people), echoing the standard MT formula for covenantal excommunication (karet).
