# Acts Chapter 14 — Translation Notes

## Versification

### 14:6-7 — Content distribution
- **Issue**: In the SBLGNT, 14:6 describes the flight to Lystra, Derbe, and surrounding region, while 14:7 is a brief summary: "and there they were evangelizing." The Vulgate 14:7 begins differently, starting the story of the lame man at Lystra ("Et quidam vir Lystris infirmus pedibus sedebat..."), which corresponds to SBLGNT 14:8. The Hebrew 14:7 follows the Greek with the short evangelizing summary (וַיְבַשְּׂרוּ-שָׁם הַבְּשׂוֹרָה), and 14:8 begins the lame man narrative.
- **Vulgate**: 14:7 = beginning of the lame man episode (content of Greek 14:8)
- **Greek/Hebrew**: 14:7 = brief evangelizing note; 14:8 = lame man episode
- **Note**: This is a significant verse-boundary shift. The Vulgate's 14:7 includes the healing setup, while the Hebrew's 14:7 is just a transitional half-verse. All content from 14:7 through 14:10 is shifted by approximately one verse between the two systems. Cross-referencing between Vulgate and Hebrew verse numbers requires care here.

### 14:9-10 — Vulgate verse content shift
- **Issue**: Continuing from the above, the Vulgate's 14:9 ("dixit magna voce: Surge...") corresponds to the SBLGNT's 14:10. The Vulgate's 14:10 ("Turbae autem cum vidissent...") corresponds to the SBLGNT's 14:11.
- **Vulgate**: 14:9 = Paul's healing command; 14:10 = crowd reaction in Lycaonian
- **Greek/Hebrew**: 14:10 = Paul's healing command; 14:11 = crowd reaction
- **Note**: The one-verse shift continues through the entire Lycaonian episode. The Hebrew follows the Greek versification consistently.

### 14:13-18 — Continued Vulgate shift
- **Issue**: The Vulgate verse numbers continue to run approximately one verse ahead of the Greek/Hebrew through the Jupiter-Mercury episode and Paul's speech. Vulgate 14:14 ("Quod ubi audierunt Apostoli...") includes material that spans Greek 14:14-15.
- **Note**: The misalignment resolves by the end of the episode. Verse-level references in this chapter must always specify which system is being used.

## Content Alignment

### 14:12 — "Jovem" and "Mercurium"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate uses the Roman divine names "Jovem" (Jupiter) and "Mercurium" (Mercury). The Greek has Δία (Zeus) and Ἑρμῆν (Hermes). The Hebrew transliterates the Roman names: יֻפִּיטֶר and הֶרְמִיס.
- **Vulgate**: Jupiter and Mercury (Roman names)
- **Greek**: Zeus and Hermes (Greek names)
- **Hebrew**: יֻפִּיטֶר and הֶרְמִיס — Jupiter (Roman) and Hermes (Greek)
- **Note**: The Hebrew inconsistently uses the Roman name for Jupiter but the Greek name for Hermes. For Vulgate fidelity, both should use the Latin forms: יֻפִּיטֶר and מֶרְקוּרְיוּס.

### 14:15 — "Mortales sumus" vs. Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "et nos mortales sumus, similes vobis homines" (we too are mortal, men like you). The Greek has ὁμοιοπαθεῖς ἐσμεν ὑμῖν ἄνθρωποι (we are men of like nature with you). The Hebrew renders this as גַּם-אֲנַחְנוּ בְנֵי-אָדָם חַלָּשִׁים כְּמוֹכֶם (we too are weak sons of Adam like you).
- **Vulgate**: "mortales" — mortal
- **Hebrew**: חַלָּשִׁים — weak
- **Note**: The Hebrew חַלָּשִׁים (weak) does not quite capture "mortales" (mortal/subject to death). A more precise rendering would be בְּנֵי תְמוּתָה (sons of mortality) or simply בְּנֵי-אָדָם כְּמוֹתְכֶם (sons of Adam like you), since אָדָם inherently carries mortality.

### 14:17 — "Corda nostra" vs. "corda vestra"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "implens cibo et laetitia corda nostra" (filling our hearts with food and gladness) — first person plural. The Greek has τὰς καρδίας ὑμῶν (your hearts) — second person plural. The Hebrew has לִבּוֹתֵינוּ (our hearts), following the Vulgate tradition.
- **Vulgate**: "corda nostra" — our hearts (first person)
- **Greek**: τὰς καρδίας ὑμῶν — your hearts (second person)
- **Hebrew**: לִבּוֹתֵינוּ — our hearts (first person)
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate reading against the Greek. The first-person form emphasizes Paul's solidarity with his pagan audience as fellow recipients of common grace.

### 14:22 — "Per multas tribulationes"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quoniam per multas tribulationes oportet nos intrare in regnum Dei" (through many tribulations we must enter the kingdom of God). The Hebrew renders this as כִּי רַק-בְּצָרוֹת רַבּוֹת בּוֹא נָבוֹא (for only through many tribulations shall we surely enter).
- **Vulgate**: "oportet nos intrare" — it is necessary for us to enter
- **Hebrew**: בּוֹא נָבוֹא — we shall surely enter (infinitive absolute construction)
- **Note**: The Hebrew infinitive absolute construction (בּוֹא נָבוֹא) adds an emphasis not present in the Latin. The construction is classic biblical Hebrew (cf. Gen 43:7), expressing certainty. The addition of רַק (only) also strengthens the exclusivity of suffering as the path. These intensifications go beyond the Latin but create powerful biblical Hebrew.

### 14:23 — "Presbyteros" — Ordination of elders
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "cum constituissent illis per singulas ecclesias presbyteros" (when they had appointed for them elders in each church). The Greek has χειροτονήσαντες (having appointed by stretching out hands, i.e. ordaining). The Hebrew reads וַיִּבְחֲרוּ לָהֶם זְקֵנִים (and they chose for them elders).
- **Vulgate**: "constituissent" — they had established/appointed
- **Greek**: χειροτονήσαντες — having ordained by hand-raising
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּבְחֲרוּ — they chose/selected
- **Note**: The Hebrew בחר (to choose) shifts the sense toward democratic selection rather than apostolic appointment. The Vulgate's "constituissent" implies authoritative establishment. A closer rendering would be וַיַּעֲמִידוּ (and they appointed/stood up) or וַיִּמְנוּ (and they appointed).

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 14:3 — "Fiducialiter agentes in Domino"
- **Hebrew**: וַיְלַמֵּדוּ וּמָעֻזָּם יְהוָֹה
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "fiducialiter agentes in Domino" (acting boldly in the Lord) is rendered as "they taught and the LORD was their fortress." The word מָעֻזָּם (their fortress) from עֹז echoes Ps 27:1 (יְהוָה מָעוֹז חַיַּי) and Nah 1:7. This is a creative theological rendering — the boldness comes from the Lord being their stronghold. While not a literal translation, it captures the Vulgate's point through biblical imagery.

### 14:10 — Healing command
- **Hebrew**: עֲמֹד הָכֵן עַל-רַגְלֶיךָ
- **Note**: The Vulgate "Surge super pedes tuos rectus" (rise upright on your feet) is rendered as עֲמֹד הָכֵן (stand upright/ready). The word הָכֵן (prepared, ready, upright) echoes the niphal of כון, used for being established/firm (cf. Ps 93:2). The command is terse and powerful, fitting biblical miracle narratives.

### 14:11 — Lycaonian language
- **Hebrew**: בְּלָשׁוֹן לוּקוֹנִית
- **Note**: The simple note that the crowd spoke "in the Lycaonian tongue" is rendered cleanly. The Vulgate "lycaonice" and the Greek Λυκαονιστί are both adverbs; the Hebrew uses a prepositional phrase instead.

### 14:13 — "Ante civitatem"
- **Hebrew**: לִפְנֵי הָעִיר
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "Jovis qui erat ante civitatem" (of Jupiter whose [temple] was before the city) describes the location of the temple. The Hebrew לִפְנֵי הָעִיר is a straightforward spatial rendering. The construct בֵּית-יֻפִּיטֶר (house/temple of Jupiter) helpfully makes explicit what the Latin implies.

### 14:19 — Stoning of Paul
- **Hebrew**: וַיִּרְגְּמוּ אֶת-פּוֹלוֹס בָּאֲבָנִים וַיִּסְחָבֻהוּ חוּצָה לָעִיר כִּי חֲשָׁבֻהוּ לְמֵת
- **Note**: The verb רגם (to stone) is the standard biblical term for judicial/mob stoning (Lev 20:2, 24:14, Num 15:35). The addition of בָּאֲבָנִים (with stones) is pleonastic but occurs in biblical Hebrew (cf. Josh 7:25). וַיִּסְחָבֻהוּ (and they dragged him) uses the vivid verb סחב, which conveys violent dragging. The narrative quality is strong.

## Other Notes

### 14:15 — Natural theology speech
- **Note**: Paul's speech at Lystra (14:15-17) is his only recorded address to a purely pagan audience with no Jewish or God-fearer framework. The appeal to God as creator ("qui fecit caelum, et terram, et mare, et omnia quae in eis sunt") echoes Exod 20:11 and Ps 146:6. The Hebrew rendering preserves this OT allusion well: אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אֶת-הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאֶת-הָאָרֶץ וְאֶת-הַיָּם וְאֶת-כָּל-אֲשֶׁר-בָּם.

### 14:22 — Eschatological suffering
- **Note**: The teaching that entrance to the kingdom requires suffering (per multas tribulationes) is one of the clearest statements of the theology of the cross in Acts. This is apostolic catechesis, not direct divine speech, so it is not red-text material, but it is a key doctrinal verse.

### 14:23 — Ecclesial structure
- **Note**: The appointment of πρεσβύτεροι/presbyteros/זְקֵנִים (elders) in every church is the first clear reference in Acts to the establishment of local church governance structure in the Pauline mission churches. The Vulgate's language of "constituting" presbyters supports the Catholic understanding of apostolic appointment rather than congregational election.
