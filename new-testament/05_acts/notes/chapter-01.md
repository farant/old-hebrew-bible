# Acts Chapter 1 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 26 verses align with Vulgate numbering.

## Content Alignment

### 1:4 — "convescens" vs "being assembled"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "convescens" (eating together / sharing a meal), while the Greek "synalizomenos" is ambiguous — it can mean "eating salt together" or "being assembled." The Hebrew renders it as "eating with them" (ובאכלו עמהם), following the Vulgate sense.
- **Vulgate**: "Et convescens, praecepit eis ab Jerosolymis ne discederent"
- **Greek**: "kai synalizomenos" — ambiguous
- **Note**: The Hebrew aligns with the Vulgate reading here. The Vulgate's "convescens" likely follows the etymological reading of synalizomenos from "syn" + "hals" (salt), i.e., sharing a meal. This is the preferred reading.

### 1:4 — "per os meum" absent from Greek
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "per os meum" (through my mouth) to clarify the promise. The Greek has only "which you heard from me" (en ekousate mou). The Hebrew renders this as "which you heard from me" (אשר שמעתם ממני), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "quam audistis (inquit) per os meum"
- **Note**: The Vulgate's parenthetical "inquit" and "per os meum" are clarifying additions in the Latin tradition. The Hebrew omits "per os meum."

### 1:13 — Apostle list order
- **Issue**: The Vulgate and Greek have different apostle orderings. The Vulgate reads "Petrus, et Joannes, Jacobus, et Andreas" while the Greek SBLGNT has "Peter and John and James and Andrew." The Hebrew reverses James and John to read "Petros and Jacob and Yochanan" (פטרוס ויעקב ויוחנן).
- **Vulgate**: Petrus, Joannes, Jacobus, Andreas
- **Greek**: Petros, Ioannes, Iakobos, Andreas
- **Hebrew**: Petros, Yaakov, Yochanan, Andrai
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows neither the Vulgate nor the SBLGNT order. It should follow the Vulgate sequence.

### 1:14 — "in oratione" vs "in oratione cum mulieribus"
- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds "and supplications" (ובתחנונים) which is absent from both the Vulgate and the Greek critical text. Some Greek manuscripts (Textus Receptus tradition) add "kai te desei" (and petition/supplication).
- **Vulgate**: "perseverantes unanimiter in oratione cum mulieribus"
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Byzantine/TR tradition rather than the Vulgate here. The addition should be noted as non-Vulgate.

### 1:18 — "suspensus" unique to Vulgate
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "suspensus" (having been hanged) before "crepuit medius" (burst in the middle). The Greek says only "falling headlong" (prenes genomenos). The Hebrew renders "and he fell on his face to the ground" (ויפל על פניו ארצה), which follows the Greek sense rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "et suspensus crepuit medius"
- **Greek**: "kai prenes genomenos elakesen mesos"
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "suspensus" harmonizes with Matthew 27:5 (Judas hanged himself). The Hebrew should reflect this Vulgate addition.

### 1:20 — "commoratio eorum" vs "his habitation"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "commoratio eorum" (their habitation, plural) citing Psalm 68:26 LXX. The Greek has "he epaulis autou" (his habitation, singular). The Hebrew uses singular "his encampment" (טירתו). This is a Septuagint-relevant divergence — the Vulgate follows the LXX plural.
- **Vulgate**: "Fiat commoratio eorum deserta"
- **Greek**: "Genethetho he epaulis autou eremos"
- **Note**: The Vulgate follows the LXX Psalm text more closely. The Hebrew follows the MT Psalm text (Ps 69:26) with singular.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 1:7 — Truncated text
- **Hebrew**: "אשר קבע האב בשלטנו של" — ends abruptly with "shel" (של), apparently cut off.
- **Note**: The Hebrew text appears corrupted or truncated. The word "shello" (שלו, "his own") seems incomplete.

### 1:8 — "virtutem" rendered as "gevurah"
- **Hebrew**: "תשאו גבורה" (you will bear might)
- **Note**: The Vulgate "accipietis virtutem" (you will receive power) uses "virtutem" which the Hebrew renders as "gevurah" (might/strength). This is a good biblical choice echoing the language of the judges and warriors. The verb "tis'u" (bear/carry) is less precise than "teqablu" (receive) would be for "accipietis/lempsesthe."

### 1:10 — "levushei badim" for "vestibus albis"
- **Hebrew**: "לבושי בדים" (clothed in linen)
- **Note**: Excellent biblical choice. "Badim" (linen) evokes priestly and angelic garments (cf. Ezekiel 9:2, Daniel 10:5, 12:6-7). The Vulgate has "vestibus albis" (white garments), and the Greek "esthesesin leukais" (white clothing). Delitzsch chose the biblical idiom over a literal color translation — a strong decision for a Hebrew NT.

### 1:12 — "techum shabbat" for "sabbati habens iter"
- **Hebrew**: "כדרך תחום שבת" (as the distance of a Sabbath boundary)
- **Note**: Excellent use of rabbinic terminology. "Techum shabbat" is the technical halakhic term for a Sabbath day's journey (approximately 2000 cubits). This accurately conveys the Vulgate's "sabbati habens iter."

### 1:16 — "tzarikh hayah" (Mishnaic construction)
- **Hebrew**: "צריך היה שימלא" — uses "tzarikh" (need), a characteristically Mishnaic/later Hebrew word.
- **Note**: Biblical Hebrew would more naturally use "nakohn" or the impersonal "dei" construction through a verbal form. "Tzarikh" appears in late biblical texts but is primarily Mishnaic.

### 1:20 — Psalm quotation accuracy
- **Hebrew**: "תהי טירתו נשמה ואל יהי בה ישב" — closely follows Psalm 69:26 MT.
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly renders the Psalm quotation in its MT form. The second quotation from Psalm 109:8 is also rendered accurately: "פקדתו יקח אחר."

## Other Notes

### 1:16-20 — Death of Judas
The Vulgate's addition of "suspensus" in 1:18 is theologically significant as it harmonizes the two accounts of Judas's death (Matthew 27:5 — hanging; Acts 1:18 — falling/bursting). The Vulgate tradition reads these as complementary: Judas hanged himself, and subsequently fell and burst open. This harmonization is absent from the Greek text of Acts alone.

### 1:20 — Dual Psalm citation
Peter cites Psalm 69:26 (LXX 68:26) and Psalm 109:8 (LXX 108:8). Both are applied typologically to Judas. The connection to the Septuagint text is significant for the project's broader goals.

### 1:24 — "Domine" / "YHVH"
The Hebrew renders "Domine" as "YHVH" (יהוה). The prayer in 1:24 is addressed to the Lord who knows hearts. Whether this is addressed to the Father or to Jesus is debated. The Vulgate's "Domine" is ambiguous; the Hebrew's use of the Tetragrammaton commits to a specific reading (addressed to God the Father).
