# Acts Chapter 25 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 27 verses align with the Vulgate. No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 25:2 -- "Principes sacerdotum" (plural) vs. "ha-kohen ha-gadol" (singular)

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "principes sacerdotum" (chief priests, plural). The Greek has hoi archiereis (chief priests, plural). The Hebrew renders this as ha-kohen ha-gadol ("the high priest," singular). This is a reduction from plural to singular.
- **Vulgate**: "principes sacerdotum et primi Judaeorum"
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-kohen ha-gadol ve-rashei ha-Yehudim
- **Note**: The Vulgate and Greek both use the plural, referring to the priestly aristocracy as a group. The Hebrew's singular ha-kohen ha-gadol misrepresents this. The Hebrew should use rashei ha-kohanim or ha-kohanim ha-gedolim.

### 25:5 -- "Potentes" vs. "ha-atzumim"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "potentes" (the powerful/influential among you). The Greek has dunatoi (the able/prominent). The Hebrew has ha-atzumim ("the mighty"), which carries more of a military or physical connotation in biblical Hebrew.
- **Vulgate**: "Qui ergo in vobis potentes sunt"
- **Hebrew**: ha-atzumim she-bakhem
- **Note**: While atzum can mean "powerful" in a general sense (cf. Num 22:6, Deut 26:5), the more political/social sense of the Latin potentes might be better served by ha-nikh'badim or ha-sarim. Minor issue.

### 25:19 -- "Superstitione" vs. "avodat Eloheihem"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "superstitione" (superstition/religion -- the word is ambiguous in Latin and can be neutral or pejorative). The Greek has deisidaimonias (religious observance/superstition). The Hebrew renders this as avodat Eloheihem ("the worship of their God"), which is decidedly neutral and respectful. This is a significant interpretive choice by the translator.
- **Vulgate**: "quaestiones de sua superstitione"
- **Hebrew**: divrei rivot alav be-inyan avodat Eloheihem
- **Note**: Festus is speaking to Agrippa, a Jew, so a somewhat respectful tone is contextually appropriate, but the Latin superstitione carries at minimum an outsider's dismissive tone that avodat Eloheihem entirely removes. The Hebrew has essentially interpreted the passage rather than translating it.

### 25:26 -- "Domino" (the Lord/Emperor)

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "domino" referring to the Emperor (Nero). The Greek has to kurio. The Hebrew renders this as la-adoneinu ("to our lord"). The Hebrew adoneinu is used both for God and for human lords. In context, this refers to Caesar, and the Hebrew is clear from context.
- **Vulgate**: "scribam domino"
- **Hebrew**: likhtov alav davar nakhon la-adoneinu
- **Note**: No issue. The Hebrew adoneinu is appropriate for a political lord/sovereign, though readers should note this is not a divine title here.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 25:11 -- Paul's appeal to Caesar

- **Hebrew**: im hare'oti ve-yesh bi devar mishpat mavet bal ekhsokh nafshi mi-mavet ve-im ein bi davar mi-kol asher hem onim bi ish lo yukhal le-hasgireni el yadam et Qeisar ani qore le-dini
- **Note**: Outstanding Hebrew. The phrase bal ekhsokh nafshi mi-mavet ("I will not withhold my soul from death") is powerful biblical language (cf. Ps 78:50, Eccl 2:10 for the idiom of khasakh). The overall structure of the conditional protasis-apodosis is natural Hebrew legal rhetoric.

### 25:12 -- "Caesarem appellasti? ad Caesarem ibis"

- **Hebrew**: et Qeisar qarata? el Qeisar telekh
- **Note**: The crisp parallelism of the Hebrew perfectly mirrors the Latin's memorable formulation. The use of qarata (you have called) and telekh (you will go) creates effective assonance.

### 25:16 -- Roman legal custom

- **Hebrew**: ki ein derekh ha-Romiim le-hasgir ish be-terem ya'amod ha-nit'an bifnei to'anav ve-nittan lo maqom le-hitztaddeq min ha-sitnah
- **Note**: The legal vocabulary is well-constructed. The word nit'an ("the accused/defendant") and to'anav ("his accusers/prosecutors") use the same root t-'-n, creating an elegant Hebrew legal register. The word sitnah ("accusation/prosecution") is excellent -- it appears in Ezra 4:6 in precisely this legal sense.

### 25:27 -- "Sine ratione" / "lo yittakhen"

- **Hebrew**: ki lo yittakhen be-einai lishlokh assir ve-lo odia gam et alilot ha-devarim asher samu lo
- **Note**: The phrase lo yittakhen be-einai ("it is not fitting/proper in my eyes") is good biblical Hebrew (cf. Esth 8:5 for the construction im tov be-einei ha-melekh). The word alilot ("charges/pretexts") from the root '-l-l is significant -- it can mean both "deeds" and "false charges" (cf. Deut 22:14, 17), adding legal nuance.

## Other Notes

### 25:13 -- Agrippa and Bernice

The arrival of Agrippa II and his sister Bernice is reported neutrally. The Vulgate's "cum multa ambitione" (with great pomp, 25:23) is rendered in Hebrew as be-hod gadol ("with great majesty/splendor"), using hod, a word typically reserved for divine glory (cf. Ps 8:2, 1 Chr 29:11). This elevates the description somewhat beyond the Latin's ambitione, which can carry negative connotations of ostentation.

### 25:19 -- "De quodam Jesu defuncto"

Festus's dismissive description of Jesus as "a certain dead man named Jesus" (de quodam Jesu defuncto) is a pivotal narrative moment. The Hebrew met ehad Yeshua shemo ("a certain dead man, Jesus his name") preserves the dismissive tone well. The contrast with asher amar alav Polos ki hu hai ("whom Paul said is alive") sharply frames the resurrection claim.
