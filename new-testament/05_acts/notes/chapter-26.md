# Acts Chapter 26 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 32 verses align with the Vulgate. No versification discrepancies.

## Content Alignment

### 26:7 -- Restructured syntax

- **Issue**: The Vulgate presents 26:7 as a single continuous statement: the twelve tribes serve God night and day hoping to attain the promise, and "de qua spe accusor a Judais, rex" (concerning which hope I am accused by the Jews, O king). The Hebrew breaks this into a different structure, placing the tribal service clause and the accusation clause together in a way that reads awkwardly: ha-melekh Agrippas ha-Yehudim mevaqshim lehayeveni ("King Agrippa, the Jews seek to condemn me"). The address to the king and the accusation are somewhat run together.
- **Vulgate**: "De qua spe accusor a Judais, rex"
- **Hebrew**: ha-melekh Agrippas ha-Yehudim mevaqshim lehayeveni
- **Note**: The Hebrew loses the rhetorical elegance of the Vulgate's punctuated address. The Latin's placement of "rex" at the end of the clause creates a dramatic pause before the incredulity question of 26:8. The Hebrew compresses this.

### 26:14 -- "Hebraica lingua"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "hebraica lingua" (in the Hebrew language). The Greek has te Hebraidi dialekto (in the Hebrew dialect/language). The Hebrew renders this as be-lashon ivrit ("in the Hebrew tongue"). This is a precise and appropriate rendering.
- **Vulgate**: "hebraica lingua"
- **Hebrew**: be-lashon ivrit
- **Note**: No discrepancy. The phrase be-lashon ivrit is exact. This detail is significant because Jesus's words to Paul on the Damascus road are explicitly stated to be in Hebrew/Aramaic, connecting the risen Christ to the Hebrew-speaking tradition.

### 26:18 -- "Per fidem quae est in me" vs. missing pronoun

- **Issue**: The Vulgate ends 26:18 with "per fidem quae est in me" (through faith which is in me). The Greek has pistei te eis eme (by faith in me). The Hebrew renders this as ve-he'eminu ve-nislah le-hatotam ("and they believed and their sins were forgiven"), omitting the crucial "in me" (bi/eli). This removes the explicit Christological self-reference.
- **Vulgate**: "per fidem quae est in me"
- **Greek**: pistei te eis eme
- **Hebrew**: ve-he'eminu -- simply "and they believed" with no object
- **Note**: This is a significant omission. The risen Christ says that remission of sins comes through faith "in me" -- a direct claim to divinity. The Hebrew's truncation to mere "belief" without specifying the object removes this Christological emphasis. The Hebrew should include bi ("in me") to match both the Vulgate and Greek.

### 26:28 -- "Christianum" vs. "notzri"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "Christianum fieri" (to become a Christian). The Greek has Christianon poiesai (to make a Christian). The Hebrew renders this as lihyot notzri ("to be a Nazarene/Christian"). The term notzri is the standard Hebrew designation for a Christian, derived from Nazareth.
- **Vulgate**: "In modico suades me christianum fieri"
- **Hebrew**: od me'at u-fittitani lihyot notzri
- **Note**: The Hebrew notzri is the natural equivalent and is used consistently throughout the Delitzsch translation. The verb pittah ("to persuade/entice") for the Vulgate's "suades" is interesting -- it carries a sense of being enticed or seduced (cf. Exod 22:15, Judg 14:15), slightly stronger than mere persuasion.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 26:14 -- "Sha'ul Sha'ul" and "darbonot"

- **Hebrew**: Sha'ul Sha'ul lammah tirdifeni qasheh lekha liv'ot ba-darbonot
- **Note**: Superb. The Hebrew form of the name Sha'ul (rather than a Greek transliteration) is exactly right, since the voice explicitly speaks "in the Hebrew language." The word darbonot ("goads") appears in Eccl 12:11 (darbenot) and 1 Sam 13:21 (dorban), giving this saying deep biblical resonance. The phrase is idiomatic and memorable.

### 26:18 -- "Li-fqoah et eineihem"

- **Hebrew**: li-fqoah et eineihem le-ma'an yashuvu me-hoshekh le-or u-mi-yad ha-Satan el ha-Elohim
- **Note**: The phrase li-fqoah einayim ("to open eyes") is a direct echo of Isa 42:7 (li-fqoah einayim ivrot, "to open blind eyes"), the Servant Song. The contrast of hoshekh/or (darkness/light) and the movement mi-yad ha-Satan el ha-Elohim ("from the hand of Satan to God") is powerful. However, see the content alignment note above regarding the omission of "in me."

### 26:22 -- "Qaton ve-gadol"

- **Hebrew**: u-me'id lifnei qaton ve-gadol
- **Note**: The merism qaton ve-gadol ("small and great") is a classic biblical idiom (cf. 1 Sam 30:19, 2 Kgs 23:2, Est 1:5). Paul's claim to testify before all ranks is rendered in perfect biblical style.

### 26:26 -- "Be-qeren zavit"

- **Hebrew**: ki lo be-qeren zavit ne'estah zot
- **Note**: The phrase be-qeren zavit ("in a corner") is idiomatic Hebrew for "in secret/in obscurity." While the exact phrase is post-biblical (common in rabbinic Hebrew), it effectively renders the Vulgate's "in angulo" and Greek's en gonia. The rhetorical force is preserved.

## Other Notes

### 26:14-18 -- Damascus road speech (red-text candidate)

This passage contains the direct speech of the risen Christ to Paul. The words from Sha'ul Sha'ul (26:14) through the commission in 26:18 are spoken by the Lord. This is the most complete version of the commission across the three accounts in Acts (9, 22, 26), and this version includes the fullest description of Paul's apostolic mission. All words from 26:14b through 26:18 should be marked as red text.

### 26:22-23 -- Moses and the Prophets

Paul's claim that he says nothing beyond what "the prophets and Moses" foretold is a crucial hermeneutical statement. The Hebrew correctly renders this with the construction ha-devarim asher higgidu ha-nevi'im u-Moshe ("the things which the prophets and Moses declared"). The Vulgate's word order (prophetae... et Moyses) places the prophets before Moses, which is unusual but preserved in the Hebrew.

### 26:29 -- Paul's chains

Paul's wish that all his listeners might become as he is "except for these chains" (praeter vincula / zulati ha-moserot) is rendered with the word moserot (bonds/fetters), a word found in Jer 27:2 and Ps 2:3. This is strong prophetic vocabulary.
