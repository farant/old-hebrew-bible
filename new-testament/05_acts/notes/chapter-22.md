# Acts Chapter 22 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 30 verses align with the Vulgate.

## Content Alignment

### 22:9 -- Companions' perception of the voice

- **Issue**: The Hebrew adds "and they were afraid" (vayyira'u), which has no basis in the Vulgate or Greek. The Vulgate says they saw the light but did not hear the voice. The Hebrew inserts fear, which may be imported from Acts 9:7 or from a general tendency to dramatize.
- **Vulgate**: "lumen quidem viderunt, vocem autem non audierunt ejus qui loquebatur mecum"
- **Hebrew**: adds vayyira'u ("and they feared") between seeing the light and not hearing the voice
- **Note**: The Hebrew should follow the Vulgate without the interpolated fear response. This is a notable discrepancy between Paul's accounts in Acts 9:7 and 22:9 (heard the voice but saw no one vs. saw the light but did not hear the voice) -- the Hebrew should preserve the distinction rather than harmonize.

### 22:16 -- "Invocato nomine ipsius"

- **Issue**: The Greek SBLGNT has autou ("his," i.e. Christ's name), while some manuscripts read "the name of the Lord." The Vulgate has "invocato nomine ipsius" ("invoking his name"). The Hebrew renders this as be-shem ha-adon ("in the name of the Lord"), which is ambiguous -- it could mean "the Lord" generically rather than "his name" referring to Christ specifically.
- **Vulgate**: "invocato nomine ipsius" -- calling upon His [Christ's] name
- **Hebrew**: be-qor'akha be-shem ha-adon -- "when you call on the name of the Lord"
- **Note**: The Hebrew ha-adon is acceptable as a reverential rendering, but ipsius in the Vulgate is a pronoun referring back to Christ. The Hebrew could be clearer by using shemo ("his name") to match the Vulgate's pronomial reference.

### 22:20 -- Stephen as "testis tui"

- **Issue**: The Greek has "tou marturos sou" (your witness/martyr). The Vulgate has "testis tui" (your witness). The Hebrew renders this as edekha ("your witness"), which is correct.
- **Vulgate**: "Stephani testis tui"
- **Hebrew**: Istfanos edekha
- **Note**: The Hebrew transliteration of Stephen as Istfanos is unusual. The standard Hebrew form is Stefanos or the Hebraized form Istayfanos. Minor issue.

### 22:25 -- "Indemnatum" (uncondemned)

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "hominem Romanum et indemnatum" (a Roman man and uncondemned). The Hebrew renders this fully as be-lo din u-ve-lo mishpat ("without judgment and without trial"), which is actually more expansive than the Latin but captures the legal force well.
- **Vulgate**: "hominem Romanum et indemnatum"
- **Hebrew**: ish romi be-lo din u-ve-lo mishpat
- **Note**: The Hebrew double construction is a good biblical idiom (cf. Job 36:6) and effectively conveys indemnatum.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 22:1 -- Opening address

- **Hebrew**: akhai va-avotai shim'u na va-adabbera ve-etztaddeq lifneikhem
- **Note**: The hitpael of tz-d-q (ve-etztaddeq, "and I will justify myself") is a good biblical Hebrew choice, found in Job 9:20. However, the Vulgate's "rationem reddere" (to give an account) is less adversarial than "self-justification." The opening akhai va-avotai ("my brothers and fathers") is excellent biblical style.

### 22:3 -- "Secus pedes Gamaliel"

- **Hebrew**: le-raglei Gamliel -- "at the feet of Gamaliel"
- **Note**: Excellent. This is direct biblical idiom, echoing the relationship of disciples to masters.

### 22:17 -- "Fieri me in stupore mentis" (ecstasy/trance)

- **Hebrew**: va-tehi alai yad YHWH -- "the hand of the LORD was upon me"
- **Note**: This is a significant interpretive rendering. The Vulgate has "stupor mentis" (ecstasy of mind), which the Greek renders as ekstasis. The Hebrew translator chose the prophetic idiom "the hand of the LORD came upon me" (cf. Ezek 1:3, 3:22, 8:1), which is theologically richer and situates Paul's experience within the Hebrew prophetic tradition. This is an excellent biblical parallel, arguably superior to a literal translation, though it diverges from both the Greek and Latin literal sense.

## Other Notes

### 22:3 -- OT allusion: Exodus 22:27 (LXX 22:28)

Paul's quotation of "you shall not speak evil of a ruler of your people" in 23:5 (see next chapter) is set up by his claim here to be educated "according to the strict manner of the law of our fathers." The narrative arc from 22:3 to 23:5 establishes Paul as a faithful Torah observer.

### 22:14 -- "The Just One" (ton dikaion / justum)

The title "the Just One" for Christ is a significant Christological designation, connecting to Acts 3:14, 7:52, and the Isaianic Servant (Isa 53:11 -- "my righteous servant"). The Hebrew ha-tzaddiq is precise.

### 22:17-21 -- Temple vision

This vision of Christ in the Temple is unique to Acts 22 (not in chs. 9 or 26) and has deep theological significance for Jewish-Christian relations. The Lord commands Paul to leave Jerusalem because the Jews will not receive his testimony -- spoken in the Temple itself. This passage contains direct speech of the risen Christ.
