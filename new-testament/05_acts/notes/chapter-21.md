# Acts Chapter 21 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 40 verses align between the Vulgate, SBLGNT, and Hebrew text.

## Content Alignment

### 21:7 — "Ptolemaidam" vs. "Akko"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "descendimus Ptolemaidam" (we came down to Ptolemais). The Greek reads "eis Ptolemaida" (to Ptolemais). The Hebrew reads "el-Akko" (to Akko), using the ancient Hebrew name for the city rather than its Hellenistic name.
- **Vulgate**: "Ptolemaidam"
- **Hebrew**: "Akko"
- **Note**: Delitzsch replaces the Hellenistic name Ptolemais with its Hebrew equivalent Akko (Judg 1:31). This is historically sound — the city was called Akko before the Ptolemaic renaming — but departs from the Vulgate's form. A transliteration or note would better preserve the Vulgate text while acknowledging the Hebrew identity of the city.

### 21:8 — Bracketed "Paulos va'asher itto" (Paul and those with him)
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes a bracketed phrase "[Polos va'asher itto]" (Paul and those with him) that is absent from both the Vulgate and the SBLGNT.
- **Vulgate**: "Alia autem die profecti, venimus Caesaream"
- **Hebrew**: "umimochorat nasa'nu [Polos va'asher itto] vannavo el-Qeisarya"
- **Note**: The bracketed addition is a Western text reading (Codex D). Since neither the Vulgate nor the critical Greek includes this specification, the brackets appropriately signal its secondary status.

### 21:11 — "Haec dicit Spiritus Sanctus" — prophetic formula
- **Issue**: The Vulgate, Greek, and Hebrew all agree on the prophetic formula. The Hebrew "koh amar Ruach haQodesh" (thus says the Holy Spirit) directly mirrors the OT prophetic formula "koh amar YHWH" (thus says the LORD), substituting the Holy Spirit for the divine name.
- **Note**: Good alignment across all three texts. The prophetic formula echoes Isa 44:6, Jer 2:2, etc. The substitution of "Ruach haQodesh" into the prophetic messenger formula is theologically significant.

### 21:22 — "utique oportet convenire multitudinem" — Vulgate plus
- **Issue**: The Vulgate includes "utique oportet convenire multitudinem" (the multitude must certainly assemble). The Greek reads "pantos akousontai hoti elelythas" (they will certainly hear that you have come). The Hebrew reads "hinneh ye'asef haqahal ki yishme'u ki-vata" (behold the assembly will gather, for they will hear you have come), which combines elements of both.
- **Vulgate**: Emphasizes that the multitude must assemble.
- **Hebrew**: Includes the assembly gathering and the hearing of Paul's arrival.
- **Note**: The Hebrew captures both the Vulgate's concern about the assembly and the Greek's statement about news spreading. Adequate synthesis.

### 21:23-24 — Verse division
- **Issue**: The Hebrew splits the content of Vulgate 21:23-24 differently from the Vulgate. The Vulgate 21:23 ends with "votum habentes super se" (having a vow upon them) and 21:24 begins with "His assumptis" (take these men). The Hebrew 21:23 ends simply with "oto aseh" (do it) and 21:24 begins with "hinneh arba'ah anashim ittanu" (behold four men are with us), moving the description of the four men with the vow to 21:24.
- **Note**: The content is all present but distributed differently across the verse boundary. Minor structural issue.

### 21:25 — "idolis immolato" — Apostolic Decree recap
- **Issue**: This verse recaps the Apostolic Decree from chapter 15. The Vulgate lists: (1) idolis immolato, (2) sanguine, (3) suffocato, (4) fornicatione. The Hebrew follows a similar order. The Hebrew adds a qualifier "raq asher yishmeruu" (only that they should guard themselves) that reframes the decree as solely negative prohibitions.
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate content. The Hebrew preserves all four prohibitions from the Apostolic Decree consistently.

### 21:38 — "quatuor millia virorum sicariorum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "quatuor millia virorum sicariorum" (four thousand men of the sicarii). The Greek "tetrakischilious andras ton sikarion" (four thousand men of the sicarii). The Hebrew "arba'at alafim anshei damim" (four thousand men of blood).
- **Vulgate**: "sicariorum" (of the dagger-men/assassins)
- **Hebrew**: "anshei damim" (men of blood/bloodshed)
- **Note**: The Hebrew renders "sicarii" (Latin loanword for dagger-men) as "anshei damim" (men of blood), a biblical idiom for violent men (2 Sam 16:7, Ps 5:7). This loses the specific historical reference to the Sicarii movement — Jewish assassins who used concealed daggers — but captures their violent character in biblical idiom.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 21:5 — "vannikhra al-birkenu al-chof hayyam" (we knelt on the shore of the sea)
- **Hebrew**: "vannikhra al-birkenu al-chof hayyam vannithpallal"
- **Note**: Beautiful rendering. "Chof hayyam" (shore of the sea) is good biblical geography language (Judg 5:17, Deut 1:7). The image of kneeling in prayer on the seashore has a vivid biblical quality. The Vulgate "positis genibus in littore, oravimus" is captured precisely.

### 21:11 — "koh amar Ruach haQodesh" (thus says the Holy Spirit)
- **Hebrew**: "koh amar Ruach haQodesh kakhah ya'asru haYehudim biYerushalayim"
- **Note**: The prophetic formula "koh amar" followed by the prophetic action (binding his own hands and feet) creates a scene that directly parallels the OT prophets' symbolic actions — Ezekiel's bound ropes (Ezek 3:25), Isaiah's nakedness (Isa 20:2-3), Jeremiah's yoke (Jer 27:2). The Hebrew preserves this prophetic register perfectly.

### 21:13 — "mah-lakhem ki tivku vetamesu et-levavi" (why do you weep and melt my heart)
- **Hebrew**: "mah-lakhem ki tivku vetamesu et-levavi"
- **Note**: "Tamesu et-levavi" (melt my heart) is an excellent rendering of the Vulgate "affligentes cor meum" and Greek "synthryhtontes mou ten kardian." The verb "masas" (to melt) in the sense of weakening courage is well attested: Josh 2:11 "vayyimmas levavenu" (our hearts melted), Josh 14:8 "himsiu et-lev ha'am" (made the people's hearts melt). Paul's rebuke is rendered in vivid biblical Hebrew.

### 21:14 — "retson YHWH ye'aseh" (the will of the LORD be done)
- **Hebrew**: "retson YHWH ye'aseh"
- **Note**: The Vulgate "Domini voluntas fiat" (the Lord's will be done) is rendered with YHWH, mapping "Domini" to the Tetragrammaton. The phrase echoes the Lord's Prayer in a Hebrew form. Clean, solemn language.

### 21:30 — Arrest at the Temple
- **Hebrew**: "vayyochezu et-Polos vayyischavuhu el-michutz lammikdash ukhrega sugru haddelatot"
- **Note**: Excellent biblical narrative pace. "Vayyischavuhu" (they dragged him) is a strong verb. "Ukhrega sugru haddelatot" (and immediately the doors were shut) creates a vivid, clipped narrative rhythm reminiscent of 2 Kings prose style. The Vulgate "statim clausae sunt januae" is well rendered.

### 21:39 — "ezrach be'ir ba'alat shem" (a citizen of a city of renown)
- **Hebrew**: "ish Yehudi anokhi miTarsos ezrach be'ir ba'alat shem asher beQiliqia"
- **Note**: "Ezrach" (native citizen) is excellent — the biblical term for a native-born citizen (Exod 12:19, Lev 16:29). "Ba'alat shem" (possessing a name, i.e., of renown) elegantly renders the Vulgate "non ignotae civitatis" (of no obscure city) and the Greek litotes "ouk asemou poleos." The Hebrew turns a double negative into an affirmative expression of distinction.

## Other Notes

### 21:1-7 — Sea voyage itinerary
The Hebrew narrative maintains a strong waw-consecutive chain throughout the voyage passage, creating a sense of purposeful movement. The itinerary (Cos, Rhodes, Patara, past Cyprus, Tyre, Ptolemais/Akko) is rendered with clear geographical progression.

### 21:20-26 — The Nazirite vow controversy
This passage, where Paul undertakes Temple purification with four men under a vow, is one of the most discussed in Acts for its implications about Paul's relationship to Torah observance. The Hebrew vocabulary is precisely chosen from Numbers 6 (Nazirite vow): "nezirot" (Nazirite status), "tithhar" (purify yourself), "tiglachet" (shaving). The narrative places Paul squarely within the Torah-observant world of Second Temple Judaism. The Hebrew reads naturally here because the concepts are native to biblical legislation.

### 21:37-40 — Linguistic identity
Paul's ability to speak Greek surprises the tribune, who assumed he was the Egyptian rebel. Paul then addresses the crowd in "lashon Ivrit" (the Hebrew language) — the Vulgate "lingua hebraea." The distinction between Paul's Greek literacy and his Hebrew/Aramaic address to the Jerusalem crowd is preserved across all three texts.
