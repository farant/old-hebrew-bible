# Acts Chapter 5 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 42 verses align with Vulgate numbering.

## Content Alignment

### 5:3 — "tentavit" vs "mille'a"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "cur tentavit Satanas cor tuum" (why has Satan tempted/tried your heart). The Greek has "dia ti eplerosen ho Satanas ten kardian sou" (why has Satan filled your heart). The Hebrew renders "lammah zeh mille' haSatan et-levavekha" (why has Satan filled your heart), following the Greek "filled" rather than the Vulgate's "tentavit" (tempted/tried).
- **Vulgate**: "cur tentavit Satanas cor tuum"
- **Greek/Hebrew**: "why has Satan filled your heart"
- **Note**: Significant divergence. The Vulgate's "tentavit" (tempted) implies Satan's external action on the will; the Greek's "filled" implies Satanic possession or domination of the heart. The Hebrew follows the Greek.

### 5:4 — "venundatum" vs "nimkar"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "venundatum in tua erat potestate" (once sold, it was in your power). The Hebrew faithfully renders "gam-acharei nimkar hayah beyadekha" (also after it was sold, it was in your hand).
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate.

### 5:9 — "Spiritum Domini" vs "Ruach YHVH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "tentare Spiritum Domini" (to tempt the Spirit of the Lord). The Greek has "peirasai to pneuma Kyriou" (to test the Spirit of the Lord). The Hebrew renders "lenasot et-Ruach YHVH" (to test the Spirit of YHVH), using the Tetragrammaton.
- **Note**: Consistent with the Hebrew NT's practice. The identification of the Holy Spirit as "Ruach YHVH" has Old Testament precedent (Judges 3:10, Isaiah 11:2, etc.) and is appropriate.

### 5:15 — Vulgate expansion "et liberarentur ab infirmitatibus suis"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "et liberarentur ab infirmitatibus suis" (and they would be freed from their infirmities) at the end of v.15. This clause is absent from the Greek critical text. The Hebrew does not include this addition.
- **Vulgate**: "ut, veniente Petro, saltem umbra illius obumbraret quemquam illorum, et liberarentur ab infirmitatibus suis"
- **Hebrew**: ends at "and only his shadow fell upon one of them"
- **Note**: The Hebrew omits the Vulgate's concluding clause. To follow the Vulgate, an addition such as "venirpu mecholam" (and they would be healed of their sickness) is needed.

### 5:28 — "doctrina vestra" vs "toratkhem"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "replestis Jerusalem doctrina vestra" (you have filled Jerusalem with your doctrine/teaching). The Hebrew renders "milletem et-Yerushalayim toratkhem" (you have filled Jerusalem with your Torah/teaching).
- **Vulgate**: "doctrina vestra"
- **Hebrew**: "toratkhem" (your Torah/teaching)
- **Note**: "Torah" for "doctrina" is a loaded translation. While "torah" can simply mean "teaching/instruction," it carries heavy connotations of the Mosaic Law. "Limudkhem" or "mishnaткhem" would be more neutral for "doctrina."

### 5:30 — "suspendentes in ligno" vs "vatitlu oto al-ha'etz"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "quem vos interemistis, suspendentes in ligno" (whom you killed, hanging on a tree). The Hebrew renders "asher shelachtem yedkhem bo vatitlu oto al-ha'etz" (whom you stretched your hand against and hung him on the tree).
- **Vulgate**: "interemistis" (you killed)
- **Hebrew**: "shelachtem yedkhem bo" (you stretched your hand against him)
- **Note**: The Hebrew paraphrases "interemistis" with the idiom "stretched your hand against," which is a biblical expression (cf. Exodus 24:11) but less direct than the Vulgate's blunt "you killed." The crucifixion language "on the tree" (al-ha'etz) correctly renders "in ligno" and echoes Deuteronomy 21:22-23.

### 5:39 — "Consenserunt autem illi" placement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate places "Consenserunt autem illi" (they agreed with him) at the end of v.39. The Greek also has "epeisthesan de auto" here. The Hebrew omits this clause from v.39 and instead begins v.40 with "vayishme'u elav" (and they listened to him).
- **Vulgate v.39**: ends with "Consenserunt autem illi"
- **Hebrew v.40**: begins with "vayishme'u elav"
- **Note**: The content is preserved but shifted across the verse boundary. Minor rearrangement.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 5:1 — "achuzato" for "agrum"
- **Hebrew**: "makhar et-achuzato" (sold his possession/inheritance)
- **Note**: "Achuzah" (inherited possession) is stronger than the Vulgate's simple "agrum" (field/land). "Achuzah" implies ancestral inheritance (cf. Genesis 47:11, Leviticus 25:10), adding weight to the sin — Ananias sold covenantal land and lied about the price.

### 5:11 — "ha qahal" for "ecclesia"
- **Hebrew**: "kol-haQahal" (all the congregation)
- **Note**: First occurrence of "ecclesia" (church) in Acts being rendered as "Qahal" (assembly/congregation). This is the standard and best Hebrew equivalent, echoing the "qahal YHVH" (congregation of the Lord) from Deuteronomy and Chronicles.

### 5:19 — "mal'akh YHVH" for "Angelus Domini"
- **Hebrew**: "mal'akh YHVH" (angel of YHVH)
- **Note**: Standard and correct. "Mal'akh YHVH" is the classical biblical term (Genesis 16:7, Exodus 3:2, etc.).

### 5:29 — "Obedire oportet Deo" rendering
- **Hebrew**: "halo alenu lehaqshiv beqol Elohim mehaqshiv beqol benei adam" (is it not upon us to hearken to the voice of God more than to hearken to the voice of men)
- **Note**: The Hebrew expands the concise Vulgate formula "Obedire oportet Deo magis quam hominibus" (one must obey God rather than men) into a longer comparative construction with "hearkening to the voice" (shome'a beqol), which is a good biblical idiom (cf. 1 Samuel 15:22). However, it adds "benei adam" (sons of man) where the Vulgate simply has "hominibus" (men). The elaboration is stylistically biblical but adds words not in the source.

### 5:34 — "moreh hatorah" for "legis doctor"
- **Hebrew**: "moreh hatorah" (teacher of the Torah)
- **Note**: Excellent rendering of "legis doctor." This is the natural Hebrew equivalent. Gamaliel I (Rabban Gamaliel the Elder) is well known from rabbinic literature, and Delitzsch's phrasing aligns with the rabbinic idiom.

### 5:41 — "kelimmah" for "contumeliam"
- **Hebrew**: "al-ki zakhu laset al-shemo kelimmah" (because they merited to bear shame upon his name)
- **Note**: "Kelimmah" (shame/disgrace) renders the Vulgate's "contumeliam" (insult/outrage) well. The verb "zakhu" (merited/were worthy) correctly captures "digni habiti sunt" (were deemed worthy). The expression has a paradoxical beauty appropriate to the text.

## Other Notes

### 5:3-4 — Ananias lied to God
Peter's declaration "non es mentitus hominibus, sed Deo" (you have not lied to men but to God) with reference to the Holy Spirit in v.3 is a key Trinitarian proof text. The Hebrew renders "lo shiqqarta livnei adam ki im-le'Elohim" (you did not lie to men but to God). The equation of lying to the Holy Spirit (v.3) with lying to God (v.4) is preserved.

### 5:34-39 — Gamaliel's counsel
This speech by Gamaliel is the only extended speech by a Pharisee in Acts. The references to Theudas and Judas the Galilean provide historical anchoring. The Hebrew rendering of this passage is smooth and idiomatic, using good narrative Hebrew.

### 5:36 — Theudas identification
The Vulgate's "dicens se esse aliquem" (saying himself to be someone) is rendered "vayyiten et-nafsho ke'achad hagedolim" (and he presented himself as one of the great ones). The Hebrew amplifies the vague "someone" into "one of the great ones," which is interpretive.
