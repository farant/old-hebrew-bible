# Acts Chapter 23 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

All 35 verses align with the Vulgate.

### 23:25 -- Vulgate parenthetical vs. Greek content

- **Issue**: The Vulgate text of 23:25 is entirely different from the Greek. The SBLGNT has: "He wrote a letter having this form" (grapsas epistolen echousan ton tupon touton). The Vulgate instead has a parenthetical: "(Timuit enim ne forte raperent eum Judaei, et occiderent, et ipse postea calumniam sustineret, tamquam accepturus pecuniam.)" -- "For he feared lest the Jews might seize and kill him, and he himself afterward sustain the charge of having taken a bribe." The Greek content about the letter appears instead in the Vulgate at 23:26.
- **Hebrew**: follows the Greek ("and he wrote a letter, and in it as these words"), not the Vulgate
- **Note**: The Hebrew should reflect the Vulgate's parenthetical content here. This is a significant content misalignment where the Delitzsch follows the Greek textual tradition rather than the Vulgate. The Vulgate's parenthetical about the tribune's fear of being accused of bribery is absent from the Hebrew entirely.

## Content Alignment

### 23:5 -- "Principem populi tui non maledices"

- **Issue**: Paul quotes Exodus 22:28 (LXX 22:27). The Vulgate has "Principem populi tui non maledices," the Greek has "Archonta tou laou sou ouk ereis kakos." The Hebrew has nasi ve-ammekha lo ta'or, which directly quotes the Masoretic text of Exodus 22:27.
- **Vulgate**: "Principem populi tui non maledices"
- **Hebrew**: nasi be-ammekha lo ta'or -- quoting MT Exodus 22:27
- **Note**: The Hebrew rightly uses the MT form of the quotation. This is proper since the speaker (Paul) is addressing a Hebrew audience. No correction needed.

### 23:9 -- Pharisees' declaration and truncated ending

- **Issue**: The SBLGNT text ends with an aposiopesis (dash): "if a spirit spoke to him or an angel--" (leaving the thought unfinished). The Vulgate completes the thought as a question: "quid si spiritus locutus est ei, aut angelus?" The Hebrew adds the bracketed phrase al nilkhamah b'Elohim ("let us not fight against God"), which comes from the Textus Receptus/Byzantine tradition (cf. Acts 5:39), not from the Vulgate or SBLGNT.
- **Vulgate**: ends with the question "what if a spirit spoke to him, or an angel?"
- **Hebrew**: adds [al nilkhamah b'Elohim] in brackets
- **Note**: The bracketed addition in the Hebrew follows the Textus Receptus but not the Vulgate. Following our Vulgate principle, this addition should be removed or clearly flagged as non-Vulgate. However, the Vulgate's question form is also not quite the SBLGNT's aposiopesis.

### 23:11 -- The Lord's nighttime appearance

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "assistens ei Dominus" (the Lord standing by him). The Greek likewise has "ho kurios." The Hebrew renders this as ve-ha-Adon nitzav alav ("and the Lord stood over him"). The Hebrew appropriately uses the narrative wayyiqtol form.
- **Vulgate**: "Sequenti autem nocte assistens ei Dominus, ait: Constans esto"
- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-Adon nitzav alav ballailah hahu vayyomer khazaq Polos
- **Note**: This is a direct speech of Christ -- significant for red-text marking. The words from khazaq ("be strong") through the end of the verse are the Lord's words.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 23:1 -- "Omni conscientia bona"

- **Hebrew**: be-tummat levavi -- "in the integrity of my heart"
- **Note**: Excellent biblical idiom. Cf. Gen 20:5, 1 Kgs 9:4 (be-tom levavo). The choice of tummat levav over a more literal "good conscience" rendering shows sensitivity to biblical Hebrew style.

### 23:3 -- "Paries dealbate" (whitewashed wall)

- **Hebrew**: ha-qir ha-mesuyad -- "the whitewashed wall"
- **Note**: Good rendering. The metaphor of a whitewashed wall is found in Ezekiel 13:10-15 (qir... taphel), though the exact Hebrew word for whitewash differs. Delitzsch uses mesuyad from the root s-y-d (to plaster with lime), which is the appropriate term.

### 23:12 -- "Devotione devovimus nos"

- **Hebrew**: vayyasru issar al nafsham im yokhlu ve-im yishtu -- "they bound a binding on themselves: if they eat and if they drink"
- **Note**: The Hebrew issar asru construction (cognate accusative with the oath formula) is superb biblical Hebrew, echoing Numbers 30:3 (ki yidor neder... o hishava shevu'ah le-esor issar al nafsho). This is among the best Hebrew renderings in these chapters.

### 23:23 -- Military terminology

- **Hebrew**: anshei tzava matayim... u-farashim shiv'im u-moshkhei qeshet matayim -- "soldiers two hundred... horsemen seventy and bowmen two hundred"
- **Note**: The Vulgate has "lancearios" (lancers/spearmen). The Greek dexiolabous is a difficult word, possibly meaning "spearmen" or "light-armed troops." The Hebrew renders it as moshkhei qeshet (bowmen/archers), which is a common biblical phrase (cf. 1 Chr 8:40, Isa 66:19) but does not match the Vulgate's "lancearios." This is a minor semantic difference.

## Other Notes

### 23:5 -- Exodus 22:28 quotation

Direct OT quotation. Paul cites this commandment after insulting the high priest. The LXX of this passage uses archon, matching the Greek of Acts. The Hebrew appropriately uses the MT wording nasi.

### 23:6 -- Resurrection as central dispute

Paul's declaration "de spe et resurrectione mortuorum ego judicor" makes the resurrection the pivot of the narrative. The Hebrew tiqvat ha-metim u-tekhiyyatam uses two nouns (hope of the dead and their resurrection), whereas the Latin has one concept (spe et resurrectione -- hope and resurrection). The Hebrew pairing is effective.
