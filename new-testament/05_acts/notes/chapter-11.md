# Acts Chapter 11 — Translation Notes

## Versification

No significant versification differences between the Vulgate and the Greek/Hebrew in this chapter. The recapitulation of Peter's vision (11:5-17) tracks the narrative of chapter 10 without verse-boundary shifts.

## Content Alignment

### 11:12 — "Nihil haesitans" vs. Hebrew
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "nihil hæsitans" (nothing doubting/hesitating). The Greek has μηδὲν διακρίναντα (making no distinction / without hesitation). The Hebrew renders this as וּלְבִלְתִּי הִתְמַהְמֵהַּ (and not to delay/linger).
- **Vulgate**: "nihil hæsitans" — emphasizes absence of doubt
- **Greek**: μηδὲν διακρίναντα — can mean "making no distinction" or "not doubting"
- **Hebrew**: הִתְמַהְמֵהַּ — to delay, linger, tarry
- **Note**: The Hebrew shifts the meaning from internal doubt/distinction to external delay. The theological point of Peter not discriminating between Jew and Gentile is lost. A closer rendering would be וּלְבִלְתִּי הַרְהֵר (and not to hesitate/doubt), using הרהר as in rabbinic usage.

### 11:20 — "Graecos" vs. "Hellenists"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ad Græcos" (to the Greeks), while the SBLGNT has πρὸς τοὺς Ἑλληνιστάς (to the Hellenists, i.e. Greek-speaking Jews). Some Greek manuscripts read Ἕλληνας (Greeks/Gentiles). The Hebrew has הַיְּוָנִים (the Greeks).
- **Vulgate**: "Græcos" — ethnic Greeks/Gentiles
- **Greek (SBLGNT)**: Ἑλληνιστάς — Hellenists (Greek-speaking Jews)
- **Hebrew**: הַיְּוָנִים — Greeks
- **Note**: The Vulgate and Hebrew agree against the SBLGNT reading. This is one of the theologically significant variants in Acts: were the Antiochene preachers speaking to Greek-speaking Jews (Hellenists) or to ethnic Gentiles (Greeks)? The Vulgate reading supports the latter, which fits the context of the Gentile mission. The Hebrew follows the Vulgate correctly here.

### 11:25-26 — Condensed verses
- **Issue**: The Hebrew combines material from 11:25 and the beginning of 11:26 into a single flowing sentence. The Vulgate clearly separates: 11:25 is the journey to Tarsus and finding Saul; 11:26 begins with their year in Antioch.
- **Vulgate**: 11:25 ends at "quem cum invenisset, perduxit Antiochiam." 11:26 begins with "Et annum totum..."
- **Hebrew**: 11:25 includes finding Saul and bringing him to Antioch all in one verse.
- **Note**: The content is preserved but the verse division is slightly irregular. No substantive issue.

### 11:28 — "Universo orbe terrarum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in universo orbe terrarum" (in the whole world). The Greek has ἐφʼ ὅλην τὴν οἰκουμένην (upon the whole inhabited world). The Hebrew renders this as עַל-כָּל-ישְׁבֵי תֵבֵל (upon all inhabitants of the world).
- **Vulgate**: Universal scope — the whole world
- **Hebrew**: כָּל-ישְׁבֵי תֵבֵל — all dwellers of the world
- **Note**: Good rendering. תֵבֵל is the biblical word for the inhabited world (Ps 24:1, Isa 14:17), corresponding well to the Latin "orbis terrarum."

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 11:5 — Peter's retelling of the vision
- **Hebrew**: וָאֵרָדַם וָאֵרֶא מַרְאֶה
- **Note**: The Hebrew uses וָאֵרָדַם (and I fell into deep sleep), the same root r-d-m used for Adam's sleep (Gen 2:21). The Vulgate has "in excessu mentis" (in ecstasy of mind). See note on 10:10 regarding תַּרְדֵּמָה — the same issue applies here in Peter's retelling.

### 11:18 — "Poenitentiam ad vitam"
- **Hebrew**: הַתְּשׁוּבָה לַחַיִּים
- **Note**: Excellent. תְּשׁוּבָה (repentance/return) is the classic Jewish theological term for turning back to God. The construct תְּשׁוּבָה לַחַיִּים (repentance unto life) mirrors the Vulgate's "poenitentiam ad vitam" precisely and is idiomatic Hebrew.

### 11:21 — "Manus Domini"
- **Hebrew**: יַד-יְהוָֹה
- **Note**: The construct יַד-יְהוָֹה (hand of the LORD) is a classic OT expression (cf. Exod 9:3, 1 Sam 5:6, 2 Kgs 3:15). The choice to use the Tetragrammaton here for "Domini" is appropriate — the Vulgate "manus Domini" corresponds to the OT formula.

### 11:23 — "In proposito cordis"
- **Hebrew**: בְּלֵב נָכוֹן
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "in proposito cordis" (in the purpose of the heart) is rendered as בְּלֵב נָכוֹן (with a right/firm heart). The word נָכוֹן echoes Ps 57:8 (לִבִּי נָכוֹן, my heart is steadfast). Good biblical register.

### 11:26 — "Christiani"
- **Hebrew**: מְשִׁיחִיִּים
- **Note**: The coinage מְשִׁיחִיִּים for "christiani" is a natural Hebrew formation from מָשִׁיחַ + the gentilic suffix ־ִיִּים. This mirrors the Latin/Greek pattern of forming a group name from the title.

## Other Notes

### 11:16 — Dominical logion
- **Note**: Peter quotes the Lord's words: "Joannes quidem baptizavit aqua, vos autem baptizabimini Spiritu Sancto." This is a citation of the risen Christ's teaching (cf. Acts 1:5) and is red-text material.

### 11:28 — Agabus and the famine under Claudius
- **Note**: The mention of the famine under Claudius (quæ facta est sub Claudio) is one of the anchoring chronological references in Acts. The Hebrew קְלוֹדְיוֹס is a reasonable transliteration of "Claudius."
