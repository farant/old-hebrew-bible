# Acts Chapter 17 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 34 verses align between the Vulgate, SBLGNT, and Hebrew text.

## Content Alignment

### 17:4 — "de colentibus gentilibusque" vs. Greek "sebomenon Hellenon"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "de colentibus gentilibusque multitudo magna" (of the God-fearing and Gentiles, a great multitude), distinguishing two groups. The Greek reads "ton te sebomenon Hellenon plethos poly" (a large number of God-fearing Greeks), treating them as one group. The Hebrew "yir'ei Elohim min-haYevanim" (God-fearers from the Greeks) follows the Greek single-group reading.
- **Vulgate**: Separates "colentibus" (God-fearers) and "gentilibus" (Gentiles).
- **Note**: The Vulgate's reading suggests two distinct categories; the Greek and Hebrew treat them as one. For Vulgate alignment, the Hebrew should distinguish these groups.

### 17:5 — "zelantes" vs. Greek "zelosantes de hoi Ioudaioi"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "Zelantes autem Judaei" attributes jealousy to "the Jews." The Greek similarly says "zelosantes de hoi Ioudaioi." The Hebrew reads "hasorarim shebaYehudim" (the rebellious ones among the Jews), which adds a qualifier not present in either the Vulgate or Greek — "those who were rebellious among the Jews" rather than simply "the Jews."
- **Vulgate**: "Zelantes autem Judaei" (the Jews, being jealous)
- **Hebrew**: "hasorarim shebaYehudim" (the rebels among the Jews)
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds an interpretive qualifier ("rebellious ones among") that is not in either source text. While pastorally understandable, this is an editorial insertion. Both Vulgate and Greek simply say "the Jews."

### 17:18 — "seminiverbius" — seed-picker
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "seminiverbius" and Greek "spermologos" both mean "seed-picker" (i.e., someone who picks up scraps of ideas). The Hebrew "hamefatpet" (the prattler/babbler) captures the dismissive tone but loses the specific imagery.
- **Note**: The Hebrew captures the sense adequately. The Greek metaphor of a bird picking up seeds is culturally specific; "prattler" conveys the intended insult.

### 17:19 — "Areopagum" rendering
- **Issue**: The Hebrew renders Areopagus as "giv'at hamishpat haniqra'ah beshem Aryopagos" (the hill of judgment called by the name Areopagus). The Vulgate simply has "ad Areopagum."
- **Hebrew**: Adds the explanatory gloss "hill of judgment."
- **Note**: The Hebrew expansion is interpretive but helpful. The Areopagus was indeed the judicial hill of Athens. The explanatory gloss does not conflict with the Vulgate but goes beyond it.

### 17:26 — "ex uno" — from one
- **Issue**: The Vulgate "fecitque ex uno omne genus hominum" (made from one every nation of men). The Greek "epoiesen te ex henos pan ethnos anthropon." The Hebrew renders "middam echad" (from one blood).
- **Vulgate**: "ex uno" (from one)
- **Greek**: "ex henos" (from one)
- **Hebrew**: "middam echad" (from one blood)
- **Note**: The Hebrew adds "blood" (dam), which is present in the Byzantine/TR tradition but absent from the critical Greek text and the Vulgate. Since we follow the Vulgate, which simply says "ex uno" (from one), the addition of "blood" should be noted. However, this reading is widespread in the manuscript tradition and theologically innocuous.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 17:11 — "nedivei ruach" (noble of spirit)
- **Hebrew**: "vehem hayu nedivei ruach me'anshei Tasloniki"
- **Note**: "Nedivei ruach" is a beautiful biblical construction. Cf. Exod 35:5 "nediv libo" (generous of heart). The Greek "eugeneseroi" means "more noble/well-born." Delitzsch's choice elevates physical nobility to spiritual nobility — a defensible interpretive choice that reads well in biblical Hebrew.

### 17:16 — "vatitchammetz rucho beqirbo" (his spirit was embittered within him)
- **Hebrew**: "vatitchammetz rucho beqirbo"
- **Note**: Excellent biblical Hebrew. The verb "chametz" in the hitpa'el means to be soured/embittered. This echoes Ps 73:21 "ki yitchammetz levavi" (when my heart was embittered). The Greek "paroxyneto" (was provoked) is well rendered by this strong biblical idiom.

### 17:22 — "yir'ei elohot" (fearers of divinities)
- **Hebrew**: "yir'ei elohot attem me'od"
- **Note**: Paul's description of the Athenians as "deisidaimonesterous" (very religious/superstitious) is rendered with "yir'ei elohot" — using the rare plural "elohot" (divinities) rather than the standard "Elohim." This is a creative choice that captures the polytheistic context. The Vulgate's "superstitiosiores" (more superstitious) has a more negative connotation.

### 17:23 — "la'El hane'lam" (to the Hidden God)
- **Hebrew**: "la'El hane'lam" (to the Hidden God)
- **Note**: The Greek "agnoste theo" (to an unknown god) and Vulgate "Ignoto Deo" both mean "unknown." The Hebrew "ne'lam" means "hidden/concealed" rather than "unknown" (lo noda). This is a theologically loaded choice — "hidden" implies God conceals Himself (cf. Isa 45:15 "aken attah El mistatter" — truly You are a God who hides Himself), while "unknown" implies mere ignorance. The choice of ne'lam over noda creates a stronger theological resonance with Isaiah but departs from the literal meaning.

### 17:28 — Pagan poets citation
- **Hebrew**: "bimshorereichem yesh omrim ki-yelidav anachnu"
- **Note**: "Bimshorereichem" (among your poets/singers) is good biblical Hebrew — "meshorer" is the biblical word for poet/singer (2 Sam 23:1, Ps title-vocabulary). The phrase "ki-yelidav anachnu" (for we are His offspring) is a clean rendering of "tou gar kai genos esmen."

## Other Notes

### 17:22-31 — The Areopagus Speech
This is Paul's most significant address to a Gentile philosophical audience. Several features are noteworthy:
1. The Hebrew translation navigates the challenge of rendering Greek philosophical concepts in biblical Hebrew vocabulary.
2. The speech contains no direct OT quotations but alludes to creation theology (Gen 1-2), divine providence (Deut 32:8), and eschatological judgment (Ps 96:13).
3. The citation of Greek poets (17:28) is preserved in the Hebrew, showing the universality of Paul's argument.

### 17:31 — Judgment and Resurrection
The Vulgate "judicaturus est orbem in aequitate" (will judge the world in equity) connects to Ps 96:13 / 98:9 (LXX 95/97). The Hebrew "lishpot tevel betzedek" mirrors the Psalms language precisely.
