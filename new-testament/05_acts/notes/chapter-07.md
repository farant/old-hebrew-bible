# Acts Chapter 7 — Translation Notes

## Versification

All 60 verses align with Vulgate numbering. However, the Vulgate combines content from Greek vv.55-56 into Vulgate v.55, and content from Greek vv.57-58 into Vulgate vv.56-57. The Hebrew follows the Greek verse divisions rather than the Vulgate's. See note below.

### 7:55-58 — Verse content displacement
- **Issue**: The Vulgate's v.55 contains Stephen's vision AND his spoken declaration ("Ecce video caelos apertos, et Filium hominis stantem a dextris Dei"), combining Greek vv.55-56. The Vulgate's v.56 then contains the crowd's reaction (Greek v.57). The Vulgate's v.57 contains the stoning and Saul reference (Greek v.58). The Hebrew follows the Greek division, placing the spoken declaration in v.56 and the crowd's reaction in v.57.
- **Note**: This is a real versification discrepancy. The content is all present but distributed differently. The Vulgate's v.59 contains the Latin corresponding to Greek v.58's stoning scene, and so on.

## Content Alignment

### 7:3 — "de cognatione tua" vs "umimmoladtekha"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Exi de terra tua, et de cognatione tua" (Go out from your land and from your kindred), quoting Genesis 12:1. The Greek has "ek tes ges sou kai ek tes syngeneias sou" (from your land and from your kindred). The MT of Genesis 12:1 has "me'artzekha umimmoladtekha umibbet avikha" (from your land, from your birthplace, and from your father's house). The Hebrew NT has "lekh-lekha me'artzekha umimmoladtekha" — using the MT's "lekh-lekha" opening but omitting "your father's house."
- **Vulgate**: "de terra tua, et de cognatione tua"
- **Hebrew**: "me'artzekha umimmoladtekha" (from your land and from your birthplace)
- **Note**: Both the Vulgate and the Hebrew omit "from your father's house" (which is in the MT Genesis 12:1 and some Greek manuscripts of Acts). The Hebrew reverts to the MT vocabulary ("lekh-lekha," "moladtekha") while matching the shorter form of the quotation.

### 7:3 — Corrupted Hebrew text
- **Issue**: The Hebrew ends with "asher ar'a'e'a" (אשר אראֶָ), which appears to be textually corrupted. This should be "asher ar'ekha" (which I will show you).
- **Note**: Apparent textual corruption in the Delitzsch Hebrew. The final word is garbled.

### 7:5 — "repromisit" vs "hivtiach"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "repromisit dare illi eam in possessionem" (he promised to give it to him as a possession). The Hebrew has "vayyavtach letittah la'achuzah lo ulezar'o acharav" (and he promised to give it as a possession to him and to his seed after him).
- **Note**: Good alignment. "Hivtiach" (promised) for "repromisit" is accurate. "Achuzah" (possession/inheritance) for "possessionem" is excellent biblical vocabulary.

### 7:7 — "dixit Dominus" vs "amar Elohim"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "dixit Dominus" (said the Lord). The Greek has "ho theos eipen" (God said). The Hebrew has "amar Elohim" (God said), following the Greek rather than the Vulgate.
- **Vulgate**: "dixit Dominus"
- **Hebrew**: "amar Elohim" (God said)
- **Note**: The Vulgate identifies the speaker as "Dominus" (Lord), the Hebrew as "Elohim" (God). To follow the Vulgate, "amar YHVH" or "amar ha'Adon" would be needed. This is a quotation of Genesis 15:14, where the MT has "Elohim" — so the Hebrew reverts to the MT source.

### 7:14 — "septuaginta quinque" / Seventy-five souls
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "in animabus septuaginta quinque" (in seventy-five souls). The Greek has "en psychais hebdomekonta pente" (in seventy-five souls). The MT of Genesis 46:27 has "seventy souls." The Hebrew NT follows the LXX/Vulgate number: "shiv'im vechamesh nafesh" (seventy-five souls).
- **Vulgate**: "septuaginta quinque"
- **Hebrew**: "shiv'im vechamesh"
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate. Stephen follows the LXX reckoning (which includes Joseph's grandsons) rather than the MT's seventy. The Hebrew NT correctly preserves the LXX/Vulgate number here despite the MT disagreement.

### 7:16 — Abraham/Shechem land purchase
- **Issue**: This verse presents a well-known historical difficulty. Stephen says Abraham bought the tomb from the sons of Hamor in Shechem, but the MT records Jacob making this purchase (Genesis 33:19) while Abraham bought the cave of Machpelah from Ephron (Genesis 23). The Vulgate preserves "Abraham" as does the Greek. The Hebrew also has "Abraham."
- **Note**: No textual divergence — all three traditions preserve Stephen's conflated reference. This is a content issue within the speech itself, not a translation issue.

### 7:35 — "redemptorem" vs "go'el"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "principem et redemptorem" (prince and redeemer). The Greek has "archonta kai lytroten" (ruler and deliverer/ransomer). The Hebrew renders "lesar vego'el" (as prince and redeemer).
- **Vulgate**: "principem et redemptorem"
- **Hebrew**: "lesar vego'el"
- **Note**: Excellent choice. "Go'el" (kinsman-redeemer) carries deep biblical resonance (Ruth 3:9, 4:14; Isaiah 41:14, 43:14, 44:6). It is theologically richer than the Vulgate's "redemptorem" and connects Moses typologically to Christ as Go'el.

### 7:37 — Deuteronomy 18:15 quotation expanded
- **Issue**: The Hebrew includes "YHVH Eloheikhem elav tishma'un" (YHVH your God, to him you shall listen), expanding the quotation beyond both the Vulgate and Greek of this verse. The Vulgate ends with "tamquam me: ipsum audietis" (like me: him you shall hear). The Hebrew adds the full MT form of Deuteronomy 18:15.
- **Vulgate**: ends with "ipsum audietis"
- **Hebrew**: adds "YHVH Eloheikhem elav tishma'un"
- **Note**: The Hebrew reverts to the full MT quotation. The Vulgate's shorter form should be followed.

### 7:42-43 — Amos 5:25-27 quotation
- **Issue**: The Vulgate follows the LXX form of Amos, which differs from the MT. Key difference: "Rempham" (Vulgate/LXX) vs "Kiyyun" (MT). The Hebrew uses "Ramphan" (רמפן), following the LXX/Vulgate form rather than the MT's "Kiyyun." Also, the Vulgate has "trans Babylonem" (beyond Babylon) where the MT has "beyond Damascus." The Hebrew follows the LXX/Vulgate with "mehal'ah leBavel" (beyond Babylon).
- **Vulgate**: "Rempham... trans Babylonem"
- **Hebrew**: "Ramphan... mehal'ah leBavel"
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate on both points. The Hebrew correctly follows the LXX/Vulgate form of this OT quotation rather than reverting to the MT. This is noteworthy because elsewhere the Hebrew NT tends to revert to MT forms.

### 7:43 — "sukkat Milkom" vs "tabernaculum Moloch"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "tabernaculum Moloch" (tabernacle of Moloch). The Greek has "ten skenen tou Moloch." The Hebrew has "sukkat Milkom" (tabernacle of Milkom), using the name "Milkom" rather than "Moloch."
- **Vulgate**: "Moloch"
- **Hebrew**: "Milkom"
- **Note**: "Milkom" (Milcom) is the Ammonite deity (1 Kings 11:5, 33). "Moloch" (Molek) is the deity associated with child sacrifice (Leviticus 18:21, 20:2-5). These are traditionally understood as distinct deities, though some scholars identify them. The Hebrew should use "Molek" (מלך) to match the Vulgate's "Moloch."

### 7:46 — "Deo Jacob" vs "le'Elohei Ya'aqov"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "ut inveniret tabernaculum Deo Jacob" (to find a dwelling for the God of Jacob). Some Greek manuscripts read "to oiko Iakob" (for the house of Jacob) instead of "to theo Iakob" (for the God of Jacob). The Hebrew follows the "God of Jacob" reading: "le'Elohei Ya'aqov."
- **Note**: Good alignment with the Vulgate's "Deo Jacob."

### 7:55-56 — Vulgate combines Stephen's vision
- **Issue**: As noted in Versification above, the Vulgate's v.55 reads: "vidit gloriam Dei, et Jesum stantem a dextris Dei. Et ait: Ecce video caelos apertos, et Filium hominis stantem a dextris Dei." The Hebrew separates v.55 (the vision) from v.56 (the declaration).
- **Note**: The Hebrew follows the Greek verse division. The content is identical; only the verse boundary differs.

### 7:59 — "Domine Jesu, suscipe spiritum meum"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Domine Jesu, suscipe spiritum meum" (Lord Jesus, receive my spirit). The Hebrew renders "Adoni Yeshua qabbel et-ruchi" (my Lord Jesus, receive my spirit). The Hebrew uses "Adoni" (my lord), which in the MT is the form used for human lords, not the divine "Adonai."
- **Vulgate**: "Domine Jesu"
- **Hebrew**: "Adoni Yeshua"
- **Note**: "Adoni" vs "Adonai" is theologically significant. "Adonai" is reserved for God; "Adoni" is used for human masters. Stephen's invocation of Jesus as "Kyrie Iesu / Domine Jesu" is a prayer addressed to Christ as divine Lord. "Adonai Yeshua" would better capture the Vulgate's theological weight, though Delitzsch may have avoided using the divine name in direct address to Jesus.

### 7:60 — "Domine" vs "YHVH"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate reads "Domine, ne statuas illis hoc peccatum" (Lord, do not hold this sin against them). The Greek has "Kyrie, me steses autois tauten ten hamartian." The Hebrew renders "YHVH al-tishmor lahem et-hachet hazeh" (YHVH, do not keep this sin against them).
- **Vulgate**: "Domine"
- **Hebrew**: "YHVH"
- **Note**: Stephen's dying prayer uses "YHVH" in the Hebrew, identifying the addressee as God the Father. However, given that v.59 addresses "Lord Jesus" and v.60 immediately follows, the Vulgate's ambiguous "Domine" may refer to Jesus (parallel to Christ's "Father, forgive them" in Luke 23:34). The Hebrew's switch from "Adoni Yeshua" (v.59) to "YHVH" (v.60) creates a shift in addressee that may or may not be intended.

### 7:60 — "obdormivit in Domino" / "Saulus autem erat consentiens"
- **Issue**: The Vulgate adds "obdormivit in Domino" (he fell asleep in the Lord) and continues with "Saulus autem erat consentiens neci ejus" (and Saul was consenting to his death). The Greek of v.60 ends with "ekoimethe" (he fell asleep). The Saul reference appears in Acts 8:1 in the Greek. The Hebrew renders "vayyishan" (and he slept), without "in the Lord" and without the Saul reference.
- **Vulgate v.60**: "obdormivit in Domino. Saulus autem erat consentiens neci ejus"
- **Hebrew v.60**: "vayyishan" (and he slept)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "in Domino" (in the Lord) and the Saul transition sentence are absent from the Hebrew. These Vulgate additions should be noted.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 7:2 — "Elohei haKavod" for "Deus gloriae"
- **Hebrew**: "Elohei haKavod" (God of glory)
- **Note**: Directly echoes Psalm 29:3 "El haKavod" (the God of glory). Excellent biblical parallel for the Vulgate's "Deus gloriae."

### 7:8 — "berit hammilah" for "testamentum circumcisionis"
- **Hebrew**: "berit hammilah" (covenant of circumcision)
- **Note**: The standard and correct Hebrew term, used extensively in rabbinic literature. "Berit milah" is the universal Jewish term for the circumcision covenant (cf. Genesis 17:10-14).

### 7:20 — "tov le'Elohim" for "gratus Deo"
- **Hebrew**: "vayehi-tov le'Elohim" (and he was good/pleasing to God)
- **Note**: The Vulgate's "fuit gratus Deo" (was pleasing to God) renders the Greek "asteios to theo" (beautiful/fair to God). The Hebrew "tov le'Elohim" echoes Exodus 2:2 where Moses is described as "tov" (good/beautiful). The phrase "asteios to theo" is an unusual Greek construction, and the Hebrew captures it naturally.

### 7:22 — "kol-chokhmat Mitzrayim" for "omni sapientia Aegyptiorum"
- **Hebrew**: "vaylummad Moshe bekhol-chokhmat Mitzrayim" (and Moses was instructed in all the wisdom of Egypt)
- **Note**: Good rendering. The passive "yeluddad" (was taught/instructed) matches the Vulgate's "eruditus est" (was educated). The phrase echoes 1 Kings 5:10 (ET 4:30) where Solomon's wisdom is compared to "all the wisdom of Egypt."

### 7:38 — "devarim chayyim" for "verba vitae"
- **Hebrew**: "devarim chayyim" (living words)
- **Note**: Renders the Vulgate's "verba vitae" (words of life) and the Greek "logia zonta" (living oracles). "Devarim chayyim" is a powerful expression — the Torah as living speech from Sinai.

### 7:51 — "qeshei oref ve'arlei lev ve'oznayim"
- **Hebrew**: "qeshei oref ve'arlei lev ve'oznayim" (stiff of neck and uncircumcised of heart and ears)
- **Note**: Superb biblical language. "Qeshei oref" (stiff-necked) echoes Exodus 32:9, 33:3, 34:9. "Arlei lev" (uncircumcised of heart) echoes Deuteronomy 10:16, Jeremiah 4:4, 9:25. The combination of all three reproaches is classic prophetic rhetoric.

### 7:53 — "mal'akhot hamal'akhim" for "dispositione angelorum"
- **Hebrew**: "al-yedei mal'akhot hamal'akhim" (by the ministries of the angels)
- **Note**: Unusual construction. "Mal'akhot" (ministries/missions) is derived from the same root as "mal'akh" (angel/messenger), creating a wordplay: "by the missions of the messengers." The Vulgate's "in dispositione angelorum" (in the ordinance/arrangement of angels) refers to the tradition that angels mediated the giving of the Law (cf. Galatians 3:19, Hebrews 2:2).

## Other Notes

### 7:2-53 — Stephen's speech structure
Stephen's speech is the longest in Acts and follows a pattern of salvation history: Abraham (vv.2-8), Joseph (vv.9-16), Moses (vv.17-43), the Tabernacle and Temple (vv.44-50), and the accusation (vv.51-53). The Hebrew rendering maintains good narrative flow throughout, using wayyiqtol (consecutive imperfect) verb forms appropriate for historical narration.

### 7:42-43 — Amos quotation alignment
The Hebrew's decision to follow the LXX/Vulgate form of Amos 5:25-27 rather than reverting to the MT is significant and correct for this project. Key differences preserved: "Ramphan" for "Kiyyun" and "Babylon" for "Damascus." The one exception is "Milkom" for "Moloch" — see note at 7:43.

### 7:59-60 — Stephen's death and Christ parallel
Stephen's two dying prayers ("Lord Jesus, receive my spirit" and "Lord, do not hold this sin against them") parallel Christ's words on the cross (Luke 23:46 "Father, into your hands I commend my spirit" and Luke 23:34 "Father, forgive them"). The Hebrew captures this parallel with appropriate solemnity. The shift from "Adoni Yeshua" to "YHVH" between the two prayers raises the question of whether both are addressed to the same Person.
