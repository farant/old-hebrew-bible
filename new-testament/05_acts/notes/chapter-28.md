# Acts Chapter 28 -- Translation Notes

## Versification

The Vulgate contains 31 verses. Verse 28:29 is Vulgate-only (no Greek in SBLGNT). The Hebrew includes 28:29 in brackets, correctly following the Vulgate.

### 28:29 -- Vulgate-only verse

- **Issue**: 28:29 is absent from the SBLGNT and most critical Greek texts. The Vulgate has "Et cum haec dixisset, exierunt ab eo Judaei, multam habentes inter se quaestionem" (And when he had said these things, the Jews departed, having much dispute among themselves). The Hebrew places this verse in brackets: [va-yehi be-dabbero zot va-ya'avru me-alav ha-Yehudim va-yitvakehu harbeh ish im re'ehu].
- **Vulgate**: "exierunt ab eo Judaei, multam habentes inter se quaestionem"
- **Hebrew**: va-ya'avru me-alav ha-Yehudim va-yitvakehu harbeh ish im re'ehu
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly preserves this Vulgate-only verse in brackets, signaling its textual status. The content is faithfully rendered.

## Content Alignment

### 28:1 -- Vulgate's additional clause

- **Issue**: The Vulgate's 28:1 contains two sentences: "tunc cognovimus quia Melita insula vocabatur" (we learned the island was called Malta) AND "Barbari vero praestabant non modicam humanitatem nobis" (the barbarians showed us no small kindness). The Greek SBLGNT ends 28:1 at the island identification. The Vulgate places the kindness clause in 28:1 as well, while the Greek puts it in 28:2. The Hebrew follows the Greek arrangement, placing the kindness content in 28:2.
- **Vulgate**: 28:1 includes "Barbari vero praestabant non modicam humanitatem nobis"
- **Hebrew**: 28:1 ends with ki shem ha-i Meliti; the barbarians' kindness appears in 28:2
- **Note**: The content is preserved but follows the Greek verse division rather than the Vulgate's. For strict Vulgate alignment, 28:1 should include a note about the islanders' hospitality.

### 28:4 -- "Ultio" vs. "ruah naqam"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "ultio non sinit eum vivere" (vengeance/justice does not allow him to live). The Greek has he dike (Justice, personified as a goddess). The Hebrew renders this as ruah naqam ("spirit of vengeance"), which is more explicitly supernatural than the Latin ultio but avoids the pagan deity reference. The Hebrew adds the concept of ruah ("spirit"), which is not in either source text.
- **Vulgate**: "ultio non sinit eum vivere"
- **Hebrew**: ruah naqam lo hinniakh lo likhyot
- **Note**: The Hebrew's ruah naqam imports the concept of a "spirit" of vengeance, which goes beyond both the Latin and Greek. The Latin ultio is an abstract noun (vengeance/retribution), and the Greek dike is a personified concept (Justice). The Hebrew could simply use naqam ("vengeance") without ruah.

### 28:11 -- "Insigne Castorum" vs. "ha-te'omim"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "insigne Castorum" (with the sign of the Castors, i.e. Castor and Pollux). The Greek has parasemo Dioskourois (marked with the Dioscuri, sons of Zeus). The Hebrew renders this as ot diglah ha-te'omim ("its flag-sign: the Twins"), avoiding both the Latin mythological names and the Greek reference to "sons of Zeus."
- **Vulgate**: "insigne Castorum"
- **Hebrew**: ot diglah ha-te'omim
- **Note**: The Hebrew's ha-te'omim ("the Twins") is a neutral astronomical/descriptive term (cf. the constellation Gemini). This avoids pagan theological content while preserving the identification. This is a reasonable editorial choice by the Delitzsch translator.

### 28:16 -- Bracketed addition

- **Issue**: The Hebrew contains a bracketed addition in 28:16: [he'evir sar ha-me'ah et ha-asirim el sar ha-tzava u-] ("the centurion transferred the prisoners to the captain of the guard and..."). This material is not in the SBLGNT but corresponds to the Western text tradition. The Vulgate's 28:16 simply says "permissum est Paulo manere sibimet" (Paul was permitted to stay by himself) without the prisoner-transfer detail.
- **Vulgate**: "permissum est Paulo manere sibimet cum custodiente se milite"
- **Hebrew**: adds bracketed clause about the centurion handing prisoners to the camp commander
- **Note**: This bracketed material is from the Textus Receptus/Western tradition, not the Vulgate. Following our Vulgate principle, it should be flagged as non-Vulgate. However, the brackets already signal its secondary status.

### 28:25 -- "Ad patres nostros" vs. "la-avoteinu"

- **Issue**: The Vulgate has "ad patres nostros" (to our fathers). The Greek manuscripts vary between humon (your) and hemon (our). The SBLGNT has humon ("your fathers"). The Hebrew has la-avoteinu ("to our fathers"), matching the Vulgate's nostros rather than the SBLGNT's humon.
- **Vulgate**: "ad patres nostros"
- **Hebrew**: la-avoteinu
- **Note**: The Hebrew correctly follows the Vulgate here. The difference is theologically significant: "our fathers" (Vulgate) includes Paul among the Jewish community, while "your fathers" (SBLGNT) distances him.

## Hebrew Translation Quality

### 28:2 -- "Lo'azim" for "Barbari"

- **Hebrew**: ve-ha-lo'azim lo him'itu hasdam immanu
- **Note**: The word lo'azim (literally "speakers of a foreign tongue") is an excellent Hebrew rendering of barbari. In rabbinic Hebrew, lo'ez means "one who speaks a foreign language" (cf. Ps 114:1, am lo'ez -- "a people of foreign tongue"). This avoids the pejorative connotation of "barbarian" while preserving the linguistic-outsider sense of the Greek/Latin term.

### 28:4 -- "Rotze'ah ha-ish ha-zeh"

- **Hebrew**: akhen rotze'ah ha-ish ha-zeh ha-nimlat min ha-yam
- **Note**: The adverb akhen ("surely/indeed") is well-chosen for the Vulgate's "Utique" (certainly). It appears in Gen 28:16 and Exod 2:14 in contexts of sudden realization, fitting the islanders' snap judgment.

### 28:20 -- "Tiqvat Yisrael" and "keevel"

- **Hebrew**: ki bi-gelal tiqvat Yisrael asur ani ba-kevel ha-zeh
- **Note**: The phrase tiqvat Yisrael ("the hope of Israel") is a rich theological expression (cf. Jer 14:8, 17:13 where God Himself is called miqveh Yisrael, "the hope of Israel"). The word kevel ("chain/fetter") appears in Ps 105:18 and Job 36:8, situating Paul's imprisonment within the tradition of righteous sufferers.

### 28:26-27 -- Isaiah 6:9-10 quotation

- **Hebrew**: shim'u shamoa ve-al tavinu u-re'u ra'o ve-al teda'u ki shamen lev ha-am ha-zeh ve-oznav kavdu ve-et einav hesha pen yir'eh be-einav u-ve-oznav yishma u-levavo yavin ve-shav u-refativ
- **Note**: This is a near-exact quotation of Isaiah 6:9-10 from the Masoretic text. The Hebrew translator rightly uses the MT form rather than attempting to back-translate from the Vulgate or LXX. The infinitive absolute constructions (shamoa, ra'o) and the word hesha (from sha'a, "to be smeared shut") are all directly from Isaiah. This is the appropriate approach for an OT quotation in a Hebrew NT translation.

## Other Notes

### 28:23 -- Paul's final teaching

Paul's final teaching in Rome -- expounding the kingdom of God and persuading about Jesus from the Law of Moses and the Prophets -- is rendered in the Hebrew as va-ya'ad bahem va-yeva'er et malkhut Elohim va-yokhiah lahem inyenei Yeshua mi-torat Moshe u-min ha-nevi'im. The word yokhiah ("he proved/demonstrated") from the root y-kh-h is judicial/argumentative (cf. Isa 1:18, Job 13:15), appropriate for Paul's persuasive mode.

### 28:26-27 -- Isaiah 6:9-10 as OT quotation

Direct quotation of Isaiah 6:9-10. This is the final major OT quotation in Acts and serves as a programmatic conclusion to the book. The Hebrew naturally reproduces the Masoretic text of this passage. The Vulgate's Latin follows the LXX tradition, but since the Hebrew translator is working with the MT, the quotation aligns with the Hebrew Bible directly. This is proper practice.

### 28:30-31 -- Closing summary

The Hebrew rendering of Acts' final words -- be-li khol pahad ve-ein mone'a ("without any fear and no one hindering") for the Vulgate's "cum omni fiducia, sine prohibitione" -- is effective. The word pahad ("fear/dread") for fiducia ("confidence/boldness") is inverted: the Latin says "with all boldness" while the Hebrew says "without any fear." The sense is equivalent but the framing differs. The Hebrew's positive-negative inversion (from "with boldness" to "without fear") slightly changes the emphasis.
